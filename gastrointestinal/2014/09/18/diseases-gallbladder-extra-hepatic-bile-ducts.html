      Diseases of the Gallbladder and Extra-Hepatic Bile Ducts

      Kevan Lu Thursday, September 18, 2014

      Anatomy of the Extra-Hepatic Biliary Tree

      • The gallbladder connects to the biliary tree via the cystic duct.
      • The cystic duct connects with the common hepatic duct to form the common bile duct.
      • The common bile duct and the pancreatic duct meet at the Ampulla of Vater.
      • The bump where this enters the duodenum is called the duodenal papilla; it’s kept closed by the Spincter of Oddi.

      Bile Secretion and Return

      • Bile is used to break down fat.
      • It’s comprised mainly of bile salts and phospholipids.
      • Smaller components include protein, cholesterol, and bilirubin.
      • The liver doesn’t make much bile, but it secretes much more.
        • This is because a great deal of the bile is reabsorbed at the terminal ileum (95%), and is sent back to the liver via portal venous return.
        • This also explains why colon cancer metastasizes to the liver; due to portal venous return!

      Extrahepatic Biliary Obstruction

      • Obstruction of the biliary tree, outside of the liver.
      • There are 4 main causes:
        1. CBD stones.
        2. Previous surgery.
        3. Non-neoplastic stricture.
        4. Tumour.
      • Note that prolonged bile duct obstruction (6 months - years) can lead to secondary biliary cirrhosis.
        • This is different from Primary Biliary Cirrhosis (recall that this was an autoimmune disease that attacks the small and medium bile ducts).
        • Here, the damage to the bile ducts is secondary to prolonged obstruction.
      • Incidence of SBC depends on the degree of obstruction, and whether it’s intermittent or constant.

      Remember

      • Prolonged bile duct obstruction can lead to secondary biliary cirrhosis.

      Gallstones

      • Stones = anything that ends in -lithiasis
        • Nephrolithiasis = kidney stones.
        • Cholelithiasis = GB stones.
        • Choledocholithiasis = BD stones.
      • Gallstones in particular are seen in 10-20% of adults, but >80% are asymptomatic.
      • There are 3 main types of gallstones:
        • Cholesterol (most common in Western countries)
        • Black pigment.
        • Brown pigment.
      • Complications of cholelithiasis (stones in gallbladder):
        • Obstruction only → distended gallbladder. This can either be:
          • Mucocele → distended gallbladder filled with mucous.
          • Hydrops → distended gallbaldder filled with serous fluid.
        • Acute cholecystitis → if short term inflammation.
        • Chronic cholecystisis → if long term inflammation.
        • Choledocholithiasis → if the stone ends up moving into the common bile duct.
      • Complications of choledocholithiasis (stones in common bile duct):
        • May cause no problems → the stone might pass out of the bowel with no effect.
        • Intermittent obstruction → this may be a ‘ball-valve’ type, where the gallstone moves up and down, resulting in partial/intermittent obstruction.
        • Fibrotic stricture → if the gallstone gets stuck in the CBD. Often will have to be removed endoscopically.
        • Ascending bacterial cholangitis → if obstructed near the bottom.
          • Here, bile stasis throughout the CBD becomes the perfect medium through which bacteria can sneak up and cause an ascending infection.
        • Acute pancreatitis.
        • 2° Biliary Cirrhosis → if chronic obstruction occurs.

      Cholesterol Gallstones

      • >50% cholesterol. F>M.
      • Seen in Europe, Americas, Native Americans (anywhere where people eat lots of fatty food).
      • Pathogenesis
        1. Cholesterol supersaturation → too much cholesterol (or too little phospholipid or bile acid) prevents the formation of mixed micelles to transport them, resulting in the crystallization of excess cholesterol.
        2. Nucleation → these cholesterol crystals will aggregate on a seed, such as a bacteria/mucous/calcium/parasite ova.
        3. Crystal growth → often as a result of GB motility ↓, or inflammation.
      • Risk Factors:
        • Female → particularly if you have high estrogen (eg. early menopause, premenopausal, PCOS).
        • Obesity, rapid weight loss → increases cholesterol secretion.
        • Pregnancy, rapid weight loss → reduces gallbladder emptying.
        • Ileal disease, bypass, resection → interferes with the circulation of bile acids.
        • Age, FHx, etc.
      • An easy way to remember the risk factors is as the 4 F’s (but don’t say this in front of the patient in question):
        1. Fat.
        2. Fertile.
        3. Female.
        4. Forties.

      Remember

      • Risk factors for cholesterol gallstones are the 4 F’s:
        • Fat.
        • Fertile.
        • Female.
        • Forties.

      Black Pigment Gallstones

      • Polymerized bilirubin is the main component.
      • These form in the gallbladder, as a result of chronic hemolysis.
        • Hence, anything that increases hemolysis (eg. sickle cell anemia, mechanical heart valves, etc) will increase your risk for having black pigment gallstones.
      • Pathogenesis
        1. Chronic hemolysis.
        2. Unconjugated bilirubin ↑↑uarr;*
        3. Supersaturation of bile.

      Brown Pigment Gallstones

      • Calcium bilirubinate is the main component.
      • Unlike black pigment gallstones, which form in the gallbladder, these tend to form in the ducts, particularly infected ducts.
      • Often as a result of bile stasis an infection.
      • Pathogenesis
        1. Bacteria deconjugate bilirubin → this results in a mixture of biliary lipids, calcium salts, unconjugated bilirubin and cholesterol.
        2. Precipitation → this mix then precipitates as these brown stones.
      • Note that this will often precipitate as a ‘calcium soap’, which may be radiolucent.
      • Often caused by parasites in Asia, due to infection of the biliary tree. The 2 main parasites of note are:
        1. Ascaris lumbricoides.
        2. Clonorchis sinensis.

      Remember

      • Brown pigment stones may not show up on X-ray.

      Conditions of the Gallbladder

      Acute Calculous Cholecystitis

      • ie. Acute cholecystitis with gallstones.
      • 90% of acute cholecystitis.
      • Caused by cystic duct obstruction.
      • Complications of Acute Calculous Cholecystitis (IMPORTANT):
        1. Perforation.
        2. Gangrenous Cholecystitis.
        3. Gallstone Ileus → when a gallstone causes so much irritation that it creates a fistula between the GB and the small bowel. If the stone is big enough, it can be big enough to obstruct the small bowel → ileus.

      Acute Acalculous Cholecystitis

      • ie. Acute cholecystitis without gallstones.
      • 10% of acute cholecystitis.
      • Seen in patients with major surgery, critically ill patients.
      • The cause is multifactorial: bile stasis, ↑ bile viscosity, ↓ blood flow.
      • Worse than acute calculous cholecystitis.
        • Higher incidence of gangrene and perforation.
        • Higher morbidity and mortality.

      Chronic Cholecystitis

      • May or may not have Hx of acute cholecystitis.
      • Pathophysiology: Stones → Low grade chronic inflammation → Fibrosis → Loss of function.

      Adenocarcinoma of the Gallbladder

      • Very rare, seen in elderly females.
      • Gallstones in 60-90% of pts.
      • Probably a result of recurrent trauma + chronic inflammation → cancer.
      • As usually, progression goes from dysplasia → carcinoma in situ → invasive adenocarcinoma.
      • Poor prognosis → usually unresectable at time of Dx.
        • This is because the location of the gallbladder makes it easy for a cancer to invade into the liver.

      Conditions of the Extrahepatic Biliary Tree

      Extrahepatic Biliary Atresia

      • Rare condition, but #1 reason for liver transplant in children.
      • Here, the extrahepatic biliary ducts are destroyed, either in utero, or shortly after birth.
        • It’s not thought to be inherited or genetic; no one knows what mediates the destruction of the ducts.
        • Strangely (if it’s not genetic) 25% of patients have other abnormalities (typically in heart and renal system).
      • As a result of the duct destruction, the liver becomes engorged with bile, and gets damaged.
      • Early diagnosis and treatment is critical, otherwise patients will die early in life (typically between 18 mo - 2 yrs).

      Choledochal Cyst

      • Congenital dilatation of the common bile duct.
      • Results in increased bile stasis, leading to:
        • Infection.
        • Stones.
        • Strictures.
        • Adenocarcinoma.
      • Treatment → excision.
      • How does a choledochal cyst result in adenocarcinoma?
        • Bile stasis leads to the accumulation of bacteria.
        • Bacteria deconjugate bile acids.
        • These deconjugated bile acids irritate the epithelium and cause metaplasia.
        • This increases the risk for dysplasia, and eventually malignancy.

      Adenocarcinoma of the Extrahepatic Bile Duct

      • Used to be called Extrahepatic Cholangiocarcinoma, but the correct term is Adenocarcinoma of the Extrahepatic Bile Duct.
        • Technically speaking, cholangiocarcinoma refers to a carcinoma of the intrahepatic bile ducts.
      • Risk Factors:
        • Ulcerative colitis → especially if there’s PSC.
        • Choledochal cysts.
        • Parasitic infection.
        • Stones? → not proven directly, but indirect evidence exists.
      • The farther up in the bile duct the cancer is, the poorer the prognosis (is, it’s up near the hepatic ducts, it’s harder to resect than if it’s closer to the ampulla).
      • Klatskin Tumour (IMPORTANT) → where there’s a tumour at the intersection of the right and left hepatic ducts.
