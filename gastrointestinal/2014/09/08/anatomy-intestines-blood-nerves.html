                              Anatomy – Intestines, Blood Supply and Nerve Supply

                              Omer Dost Monday, September 08, 2014

                              Overall Objectives

                              • Use knowledge of surface landmarks, planes, quadrants, regions to determine the position of abdominal viscera and. interpret pain presentation.
                              • Predict the pathway infection/inflammation/exudate/transudate may take within the abdomen, spreading to/from lesser and greater sacs.
                              • Use knowledge of blood supply to GI viscera to determine the extent of ischemia from ligation or obstruction.
                              • Describe the correlation between GI visceral development, blood supply, innervation and lympathic drainage.
                              • Extrapolate the anatomical structures, found on the cadaver and images, to histological, CT and radiographic images in order to interpret clinical findings of a given scenario.

                              Branches of abdominal aorta

                              UNPAIRED spleen, duodenum - L1 - S uperior Mesenteric - jejunum, ileum, 2/3 colon - L3 - I nferior Mesenteric - descending, sigmoid, rectum
                              > ####TIP: Blood supply travels in mesentery and follows development of gut.

                              SMA comes off at the level of L1 - transpyloric plane IMA comes off at L3 - umbilicus

                              Superior Mesenteric Artery

                              SMA can become occluded if there is abnormal growth of the pancreas.

                              • Comes off at L1, behind the pancreas

                              • Between the neck and uncinate of pancreas

                              • Gives branches to the pancreas, duodenum (inferior pancreaticoduodenal)

                              If the midgut rotates around the SMA too much in development, we can get twisting of the SMA.

                              Branches of SMA

                              • Middle Colic: 1st branch coming off the SMA. Supplies transverse colon.

                              • Right Colic: Supplies ascending colon.

                              • Intestinal: Small branches supply the small intestine.

                              • Ileocolic: Supplies cecum, ileum, appendix.

                              Inferior Mesenteric Artery

                              • L3 level, above bifurcation of common iliac artery

                              • Supply to Hindgut structures

                              Descending colon, sigmoid, and rectum.

                              • Gives branches to the left side of the colon, anastomosis with right sided vessels

                              • Blockage of IMA does not result in ischemia of this region due to right sided anastomosis.

                              Branches of IMA

                              IMA Radiographs

                              Important: Ischemia is a concern in low-flow areas, especially around the watershed/splenic flexure area.

                              Diagram on right: 1. Catheter putting dye in IMA 3. Superior rectal artery 4. Sigmoidal branches 5. Left colic
                              7. Marginal artery of Drummond is followed to 7. which is the kidney.

                              Jejunal vs Ileal branches of SMA

                              • The jejenum and ileum can be differentiated based on the arrangment of blood supply
                                • The jejenum has few arterial arcades (1-2 rows) and long vasa recta
                                • The ileum has many arterial arcades (4-5 rows) and short vasa recta

                              Venous System

                              • Mesenteric venous drainage goes to the portal vein

                              • Does not drain directly to the IVC

                              • Inferior Mesenteric Vein: Drains the descending and sigmoid colon

                              • Splenic Vein: Drains the spleen, posterior to stomach

                              • Superior Mesenteric Vein: Drains the right side of colon

                              • Inferior Mesenteric Vein: Drains the left side of colon. Dumps into either splenic vein, SMV, or directly into portal vein.

                              • Junction of Splenic Vein and SMV form Portal Vein entering the liver

                              Jejenum and Ileum

                              • intraperitoneal

                              • visceral fat vs. superficial (subcutaneous) fat

                              -Duodenojejunal Flexure differentitates upper and lower GI bleeds.

                              Large Intestine

                              • Large intestines begin at ileocaecal junction

                              • Appendix is typically inferior to cecum and indicates the large intestine

                              • Tenea coli are longitudinal strands of muscle that are characteristic large intestine

                              • Position of appendix is variable

                              PATIENT CASE

                              Case Summary

                              19 year old female presents with dull but intense umbilical pain that spreads to the right inguinal region. Hip extension increases umbilical pain and deep pelvic pain is experienced as well.

                              • Umbilical pain means L3

                              • Important: R/O Pregnancy should be the first thing you do.

                              • This is a case of appendicitis.

                              • The patient presented with Psoas Sign
                                • Hip extension stretches the psoas muscle. The appendix is inflamed and is tucked in the pelvic area, attaching itself to the overlying fascia of the psoas muscle. Pulling of the inflamed appendix during extension results in pain. this is individual with inflamed appendix.
                              • The Obturator Sign is another sign of an inflamed appendix

                              The Colon

                              • Teniae coli are under chronic contraction causing puckering appearance of large intestine. These indentations are known as haustra and are very typical of the large intestine.

                              - Apendices epiploicae are fat tags hanging off the colon. They are very typical of the large intestine as well.

                              Lymphatics

                              • Lymphatic drainage follows the vessels

                              • Peripheral nodes are located on the gut wall

                              • Intermediate nodes - arterial branch

                              • Preaortic (celiac, mesentaric & lumbar)

                              • Cisterna chyli and thoracic duct

                              • Common iliac

                              • Inguinal

                              Radiographs

                              Remember

                              -Always look for leaks, obstructions, masses

                              • Look for diameter, plicae, haustra

                              • Most common cause of obstruction is adhesions

                              Post-op ensure the patient is passing gas to confirm there is no obstruction.

                              • Post-op surgery, hernias, tumours = adhesions

                              • Splenic flexure is identified. The wall of the colon is thickened. Some sort of colitis is occuring.

                              • A cyst is identified in the pancreas

                              • Diaphragmatic hernia Large intestines are pushed up through diaphragm.

                              Abdominal Visceral Innervation

                              Somatic vs. Visceral pain

                              Autonomic innervation follows development and blood supply

                              Innervation to abdominal viscera

                              Sympathetic Chain Splanchnics

                              • T5-T9 supply foregut

                              • T10-T12 supply midgut

                              • L1, S2-S4 supply hindgut

                              Parasypmathetic - Vagus does the foregut and hindgut

                              • S2,3,4, pelvic splanchnics do the hindgut

                              Autonomic Efferents & Visceral Pain

                              Application of Abdominal Pain - Pain originally felt in epigastric or umbilical region Early stage: Dull unlocalized pain. Conveyed by visceral affarents (viscera) Late stage: Sharp localized pain. Conveyed by somatic afferents (parietal peritoneum)

                              Case Summary

Denies changes in bowel function. Physical Exam findings include a soft and distended abdomen, high-pitched bowel sounds, tender firm mass in left lower quadrant, mobile uterus, and tender nodulars in rectouterine pouch. Imaging indicated an obstruction of large bowel.
