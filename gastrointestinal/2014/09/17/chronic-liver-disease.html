      Chronic Liver Disease

      Kevan Lu Wednesday, September 17, 2014

      Chronic Liver Disease Notes

      • See Chronic Liver Disease Notes

      Etiology

      • Long and complicated.
      • So it’s easier to split the etiology into conceptual groups:
        1. Viral → most common.
        2. Autoimmune.
        3. Drug / Toxin.
        4. Genetic / Metabolic.
        5. Infiltrative.
        6. Vascular.
      • We’ll go through each category in more detail.
      • However, regardless of etiology, the end result is similar → cirrhosis.

      Viral Causes

      • Chronic Hepatitis
        • Seen with Hepatitis B, C, D.
        • The important ones are Hepatitis B and Hepatitis C, since Hepatitis D requires a concominant Hepatitis B infection.
        • Recall that Hepatitis A doesn’t become chronic, and Hepatitis E behaves like Hepatitis A.
      • Acute viral infections, that don’t lead to liver disease, include Hepatitis A, E, F, GB (used to be called Hepatitis G, but was renamed to GB), CMV, and EBV.

      Hepatitis B

      • Most common cause of chronic liver disease world-wide.
      • Parenteral/vertical transmission:
        • Parenteral → IVDU, needle-stick injury, blood. Also transmitted sexually.
        • Vertical → maternal transmission.
        • Recall the rule of 3’s for needlestick infection → 30% for Hepatitis B, 3% for Hepatitis C, 0.3% for HIV.
      • Increased risk of Hepatocellular Carcinoma (HCC).
        • Highest risk of HCC in this group, since it’s thought that Hep B is oncogenic (since it’s a DNA virus).
        • What’s unique about Hepatitis B is that you can get liver cancer without cirrhosis → in all other conditions, you need to have cirrhosis first before being at risk for liver cancer.
      • Serology can be confusing (review this from Introduction to Liver Disease).

      Remember

      • You can get HCC with Hepatitis B without first getting cirrhosis.

      Hepatitis C

      • Common → 1-3% of the Canadian population.
      • Parenteral transmission → IVDU is biggest in Canada, transfusion infection is less likely now with better monitoring.
      • Not really sexually transmitted, unlike Hepatitis B.
        • This is because transmission of Hepatitis C is via blood-to-blood contact.
        • With Hepatitis B, you actually have the virus in all your secretions.
      • Once you get it, 90% of people get chronic hepatitis.
        • High rate of HCC, but only once they develop cirrhosis (again, this is different from Hepatitis B).
        • Leading cause of transplantation in North America.

      Remember

      • Hepatitis B can be transmitted by fluid secretions.
      • Hepatitis C can only be transmitted by blood.

      Autoimmune Causes

      • There are 3 main ones that you really need to know well:
        1. Autoimmune Hepatitis (AIH) → attack of hepatocytes.
        2. Primary Sclerosing Cholangitis (PSC) → attack of cholangiocytes, the epithelial cells of the bile duct.
        3. Primary Biliary Cirrhosis (PBC) → attack of the intrahepatic bile duct.

      Autoimmune Hepatitis

      • Much like most other autoimmune disease, AIH typically attacks middle aged women.
      • Typically have a high rate of cormobidity with other autoimmune diseases.
      • Diagnosis:
        1. High IgG (>2x)
        2. (+) ANA → very non-specific.
        3. (+) Anti-smooth muscle Ab → slightly more specific than ANA.
        4. Liver Biopsy → confirmatory evidence.
      • Treatment: Immunosuppression:
        1. Prednisone → induction therapy.
        2. Azathioprine (Imuran) → maintenance therapy.
          • Recall that this was an immunomodulator that was used to try and taper patients off of Prednisone, because of the risk of long-term side effects with steroids.
      • With immunosuppression, this typically resolves very well → AIH is very treatable.

      Primary Sclerosing Cholangitis

      • Seen in young males.
      • Affects the large bile ducts.
        • Either large intra-hepatic, or extra-hepatic ones.
      • 80% associated with IBD.
        • Predominantly Ulcerative Colitis rather than Crohn’s disease.
        • Patients with UC have their liver profiled regularly, to look for PSC.
        • Could it be a toxin from the inflamed gut?
      • Also genetic factors → HLA B8, and DR3.

      • We still aren’t completely sure of the etiology.

      • Diagnosis:
        • ↑ alkaline phosphatase → suggesting a cholestatic problem.
        • ERCP or MRCP (MRI equivalent of ERCP) → showing obstruction, corroborating the ALK-P.
          • Fibrotic pearls will be seen on ERCP, as seen on the right.
        • Presence of IBD → IBD + ERCP showing obstruction is pretty highly suggestive of PSC.
        • Liver Biopsy → not very useful, because this is affecting the bile ducts, and will only have indirect effects on the liver.
        • Here, the gold standard is actually imaging, since the biopsy won’t give you a good look at the area that’s actually affected.
      • Treatment: This is a plumbing problem → you can’t use meds to treat it!
        • Biliary stents.
        • Transplant.
      • Steroids are contraindicated:
        • Because they have bile stasis, they’re at increased risk for infection.
        • Infection + immune suppresion = bad.

      Primary Biliary Cirrhosis

      • Again females > males by 9:1.
      • Affects the small & medium intrahepatic bile ducts.
        • So it only affects the intrahepatic biliary tree.
      • Patchy disease, doesn’t affect the liver homogeneously. Often will have normal liver enzymes.
      • Presents with itch, fatigue.
        • Fatigue is the most common findings.
        • Sometimes there’s intractable itching → this is actually the cause of patients needing transplants. Unstoppable itching!
      • Diagnosis:
        1. Anti-mitochondrial Ab (AMA) → extremely specific test for PBC.
          • Unlike ANA, which is highly non specific, a (+) AMA is pretty much diagnostic for PBC.
        2. Liver biopsy → not really needed as much for Dx because AMA is so good, but good for staging.
      • Treatment:
        • Ursofalk → Ursodeoxycholic acid.
          • Biliary salt that’s more hydrophilic than hydrophobic, so it changes the consistency of the bile acids.
          • It’s thought that this makes it less toxic by making the bile less ‘sticky’.
          • This treatment isn’t curative, but it prolongs the condition.
            • Since the natural history of disease is already so long (measured in decades), slowing it down is usually sufficient.
            • However, it doesn’t work for everyone.
          • As a result, most people with PBC die with the disease, rather than from the disease.

      Drugs/Toxic Causes

      • Alcohol.
      • Acetaminophen.
      • Alpha‐methyl dopa.
      • Nitrofurantoin.
      • Clavulinic acid.
      • Herbal products.

      Alcohol

      • Alcohol induced CLD.
      • There seems to be a genetic component to this:
        • Some people seem predisposed to developing liver disease, and others don’t.
        • Only <10% of EtOH abusers have chronic liver disease.
      • Unlike all the other chronic liver diseases, cirrhosis here is reversible.
      • Treatment:
        • Don’t drink alcohol.
        • If you can remain abstinent, the liver disease will resolve itself.
          • Even if you have portal hypertension, varices, caput medusae → the whole gamut!
          • Note → if there’s fibrosis, it’ll be permanent, but everything else will.

      Remember

      • Alcohol-induced chronic liver disease is the only one that is reversible.
      • Pathogenesis:
        • Due to fat accumulation in the hepatocyte.
        • This is due to a number of mechanisms:
          1. Increased Production of Lipids:
            • Ethanol is converted to acetaldehyde via alcohol dehydrogenase.
            • This is converted into acetic acid by aldehyde dehydrogenase.
            • This is finally oxidized to CO2 and H2O.
            • This process generates NADH, increasing the NADH/NAD+ ratio, which results in lipid biosynthesis.
          2. Impaired Fat Export form the Liver.
            • Alcohol impairs lipoprotein assembly and secretion.
          3. Movement of fat from the periphery into the liver.
        • Alcohol metabolism also involves Cytochrome P-450, which produces reactive oxygen species, which cause damage.
      • Progression of Alcoholic Liver Disease → there’s a spectrum of ALD:
        1. Fatty Liver → Steatosis → earliest form.
        2. Alcoholic Hepatitis → acute inflammation.
        3. Alcoholic Cirrhosis → when there’s fibrosis.

      Genetic/Metabolic Causes

      • Non‐alcoholic steatohepatitis (NASH).
        • The number one condition leading to abnormal liver tests.
        • Basically seen in the obese.
        • Will probably replace Hepatitis C as the number one reason for liver transplantation soon.
      • There are also 3 classic metabolic problems that lead to chronic liver disease:
        • Alpha‐1‐antitrypsin deficiency → too little α-1-antitrypsin.
        • Hemochromatosis → too much iron.
        • Wilson’s disease → too much copper.
      • Also, there’s a disease called Gilbert’s syndrome that may be misleading:
        • This isn’t a cause of chronic disease, but it will result in abnormal unconjugated bilirubin levels.
        • Recall that this was a problem with the conjugation of bilirubin, because of a rate-limiting step in the enzymatic processes.
        • This isn’t serious, but it’s a common genetic abnormality (3-5% of the population), that results in abnormal test values.

      Infiltrative Causes

      • Anything that forms masses → granulomas, tumours, etc.
        • Granulomatous Hepatitis.
        • Lymphoma.
        • Amyloidosis.
        • Cholangiocarcinoma.
        • Hepatocellular Carcinoma.
        • Metastasis.
