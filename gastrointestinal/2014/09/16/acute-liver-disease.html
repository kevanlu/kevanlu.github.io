          Acute Liver Disease

          Tehmina Ahmad Tuesday, September 16, 2014

          Lecture Objectives

          1. Describe the pathogenesis of Hepatitis C.
          2. List the causes for acute hepatitis as defined pathologically.
          3. Describe the mechanism of injury for acute hepatitis as defined pathologically.
          4. Describe the clinical and pathological features of alcoholic hepatitis.
          5. List five common drugs that may cause acute liver disease.
          6. Describe the clinical features and principles of diagnosis and treatment for autoimmune hepatitis.
          7. Classify vascular diseases of the liver.
          8. Define Budd Chiari syndrome.
          9. Describe the spectrum of disease in Budd Chiari syndrome.
          10. Describe the etiology and pathology of passive congestion of the liver.

          Hepatitis C Virus (HCV)

          • small RNA blood-borne virus discovered in 1989.
          • not present in tears, saliva, or semen; hence blood-borne.
          • Major cause of post-transfusion hepatitis prior to 1992.
          • Major cause of chronic liver disease, cirrhosis, and hepatocellular carcinoma.
          • 3% of the population (170 million) world-wide are infected.

          HCV Risk Factors

          • IV Drug-use.
          • Blood transfusion prior to 92’.
          • Tattoos .
          • Body-piercings.
          • “High-risk” sexual behaviour (deemed as more than 35 partners) .

          HCV Epidemiology

          • Affects males more than females.
          • Prevalence: 300,000 Canadians are living with HCV.
          • Incidence: 8000 new cases/yr.
          • About 2000 cases will present as “acute” viral hepatitis.

          Natural History of HCV Infection

          Slower rate of progression if: 1. Female 2. Young age at infection.

          Faster rate of progression if: 1. Male 2. > 40 yrs of age 3. Alcohol use 4. Co-Infection (HIV/HBV)

          HCV Pathogenesis

          1. HCV replicates in hepatocytes.
          2. T-cell mediated attack on hepatocytes.
          3. Cytokines produced, leading to mass inflammation.
          4. Over time, tissue destruction and remodeling lead to cirrhosis.
          5. These factors combine to produce chronic liver disease.

          Liver Transplantation

          • Hepatitic C is the most common indication for liver transplantation world-wide.

          Treatment for HCV

          • Expensive $$$.

          • 76% cure-rate with PEG-Interferon and Ribavirin.

          Hepatitis D Virus

          • Must co-infect or super-infect with Hepatitis B virus (HBV).
          • Requires HBV machinery to replicate and thus survive.
          • Aggravates and worsens active HBV.
          • Diagnosis: Anti-HDV IgM
          • Treatment: Same as Hepatitis B (supportive care)
          • Prevention: HBV immunization

          Hepatitis E Virus

          • Fecal/oral contamination in the Mediterranean Basin.
          • Similar to HAV.
          • Acute self-limited disease.
          • Only fatal to pregnant women in their 3rd trimester.
          • Diagnosis: Anti-HEV (CDC)
          • Treatment: Supportive care
          • Prevention: No vaccine; wash your hands/food.

          Hepatitis F Virus

          • Not too much is really known about this one.

          Hepatitis G Virus

          • Flavivirus
          • Originally discovered in a surgeon.
          • Transfusion associated.
          • Parental (vertical) & sexual transmission (horizontal).
          • Not a significant cause of chronic liver disease.

          CLINICAL NOTE

        • All Hepatitis Virus infections A-G can present with acute viral hepatitis.
        • ONLY Hepatitis B-D lead to chronic liver disease aka chronic hepatitis.

          • Toxic/Drug Causes of Acute Hepatitis

          ####1. Alcoholic Hepatitis

          • History of heavy drinking.
          • Not usually biopsied.

          • Mallory bodies and swollen hepatocytes with inflammation (not T-cell mediated) and fibrosis are seen if biopsied.

          • Acute Alcoholic Hepatitis: ↑2x AST compared to ↑ALT, but < 500 overall.
          • Chronic Alcoholic Cirrhosis: < 20% of all drinkers develop this. Responds to abstinence.

          2. Other Drugs

          • Acetaminophen (Tylenol) overdose, > 3-10g is UNSAFE.
          • **Statins, if ↑ALT > 3x normal. **
          • NSAIDS
          • Antibiotics (typically clavulin)
          • Herbal medications
          • Phenytoin
          • Isoniazid (TB)
          • Amanita phalloides (Mushrooms)

          Other Causes Acute Hepatitis

          ####1. Autoimmune Hepatitis

          • Middle aged females (usually in late 30s or 40s).
          • Often known to have pre-existing/other autoimmune disorders.
          • Diagnosis:
            1. High IgGs (> 2x)
            2. +ANA
            3. +Anti-Smooth Muscle Antibody
            4. Liver Biopsy (confirmatory)

          • Marked necroinflammatory activity and numerous plasma cells.

          • Treatment: Immunosuppress on presentation with Prednisone. Maintenance therapy with Azothioprine (Imuran).

          2. Vascular Liver Disease

          • Arterial/Hypoxic vs. Venous Congestion.
          • Essentially blood cannot get in or cannot get out of the liver.
          • Arterial: Circulatory Collapse.
          • Venous: Budd Chiari Syndrome or Right-sided Heart Failure.
            1. Budd Chiari Syndrome (BCS): A spectrum of diseases characterized by obstruction of hepatic venous OUT flow. Obstruction can be at any level from small hepatic veins to the junction of the IVC or R. Atrium. Presentation can be fulminant/acute/sub-chronic/chronic. 50% have underlying malignancy. Acute BCS = hepatitic vein thrombosis Histology of BCS = centrilobular and sinusoidal congestion, with centrilobular necrosis, fibrosis and hemmorage. Occurs in zones 2 & 3 (around central venule and just outside of it).
          1. Passive Congestion of Liver: Increased hepatic vein pressure leads to centrilobular congestion with ischemic necrosis in zone 3 (central venule). Results in “NutMeg” Liver. #1 cause of passive congestion of the liver is R. Heart Failure

          3. Metabolic/Genetic

          • Iron, copper, obesity/over-eating, Acute Fatty Liver of Pregnancy (AFLP), Reye’s syndrome.

          Summary of Acute Hepatitis Causes

          ####Infectious - Hep A to G, EBV, CMV, Parvo, syphilis, HIV

          Toxic/Drugs

          • Alcohol, Acetaminophen , amiodarone, chlorpromazine, clavulin, dideoxyinosine, gold, halothane, isoniazid , ketoconazole, MAOI, methyldopa, NSAIDs, phenytoin, PTU, rifampin, statins, sulpha, tetracycline, TCAs, valproic acid, herbals, mushrooms Amanita phalloides

          Autoimmune

          • Middle aged females.

          Vascular

          • Arterial: Circulatory collapse.
          • Venous: Budd Chiari Syndrome or R. Heart Failure.

          Other: Metabolic/Genetic

          • Iron, copper, obesity/over-eating, Acute Fatty Liver of Pregnancy (AFLP), Reye’s syndrome.
