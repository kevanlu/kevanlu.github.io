                            Pathology & Pharmacology – Stomach & Duodenum

                            Pei Jun Zhao Friday, September 05, 2014

                            Overview

                            • Gastric anatomy
                            • Physiology
                            • Gastritis
                            • Peptic ulcer disease
                            • Pathophysiology
                            • Clinical approach
                            • Pharmacology

                            Stomach

                            Anatomy Cardia, body, antrum, pylorus, duodenal cap, duodenum Blood supply - left/right gastric arteries, gastroepiploic artery

                            Function Upper half: reservoir, acid secretion - receptive relaxation Lower half: pump/grinder, peristalsis, control of acid secretion

                            Physiology of Gastric Motility and Acid Secretion

                            Gastric motility Fed state - mixing, peristalsis Fasted state - MMC (Interstitial cells of Cajal)

                            Acid secretion

                            By parietal cells Basal acid secretion Stimulated acid secretion

                            • Cephalic: 30% thinking about food
                            • Gastric: 60% gastrin mediated
                            • Intestinal: 10%, mechanism unknown

                            Stimulants

                            • histamine: from ECL cells, acts on parietal cell H2 receptors
                            • gastrin: stimulates ECL cells to release histamine
                            • Acetylcholine: also stimulates ECL cells

                            Side note: vomiting results in hypocholoremic alkalosis

                            Mucin protect gastric mucosa from low pH, lubricates stomach

                            Intrinsic Factor Parietal cells, vitamin B12 absorption Pernicious anemia: auto-antibodies against parietal cells lack of acid, intrinsic factor, vitamin B12 absorption

                            Gastrin released by antrum G cells in response to amino acids and polypeptides, vagal activity, and stomach distension inhibited by acid (negative feedback)

                            Gastritis

                            Acute

                            Chemical injury - NSAIDs, alcohol, steroids, bile

                            • decreased mucin production
                            • prostaglandin insufficiency

                            Physiologic stress - burns, shock, sepsis

                            • microvessel ischemia

                            Case 24 year old involved in motor vehicle accident upon autopsy of the stomach - erythematous mucosa - petechial hemorrahages - erosions

                            Chronic

                            H. pylori - #1 cause of gastritis, duodenal ulcers

                            • Gram negative bacillus
                            • chronic active gastritis
                              • peptic ulcer
                              • MALT lymphoma
                              • mutifocal atrophic gastritis -> adenocarcinoma

                            Chemical Auto-immune

                            Peptic Ulcer Disease

                            ulcer - breach in the mucosa

                            Pathophysiology Balance between

                            • acid, pepsin, H. pylori
                            • HCO3-, mucus, prostaglandins

                            Gastric

                            • Location: lesser curvature, antrum, pylorus
                            • Causes: NSAIDs, H. pylori
                            • Gastric ulcers may be malignant -> biopsy

                            Typical gastric ulcer - punched out, round, smooth edges, flat margins, radiating folds Malignant ulcer - oval, irregular edge, heaped up margins, folds do not radiate to edge

                            Duodenal

                            • Location: first part of duodenum
                            • Causes: H. pylori, NSAIDs

                            Complications Hemorrhage, obstruction, penetration, perforation

                            Clinical Findings

                            Epigastric burning pain, tenderness

                            Gastric ulcer

                            • older, male = female
                            • during meal, gastric acid production increases

                            Duodenal ulcer

                            • any age, male > female
                            • 2-3 hours after meal, when stomach releases acid into duodenum

                            GERD - heartburn - radiates up to chest - worse lying down, bending, lifting
                            - regurgitation, bad taste

                            Diagnosis Upper GI series Endoscopy - invasive

                            Approach

                            Young patient (< age 40)

                            1st visit: stop NSAIDs, give PPI, obtain H. pylori serology

                            2nd visit (4 weeks later) Feeling better

                            • serology + -> eradicate H. pylori
                            • serology - -> probably reflux

                            Not feeling better

                            • Upper GI series, endoscopy

                            Older patient (>= age 40)

                            Upper GI series, endoscopy Duodenal ulcer

                            • eradicate H. pylori, stope NSAIDs
                            • PPI 4-8 weeks

                            Gastric ulcer

                            • stop NSAIDs, eradicate H. pylori
                            • PPI 4-8 weeks
                            • biopsy to rule out malignancy

                            Tests for H. pylori

                            • serology
                            • biopsy
                            • 13C urea breath test

                            To reduce ulcer, take NSAID with food Cytoprotection: PPI > H2 antagonist > misoprostol

                            Pharmacology

                            Proton pump inhibitors

                            • Chemical class: substituted benzimidazoles
                            • Mechanism of action: inhibit H/K ATPase on apical membrane of parietal cells, entric coated
                            • Adverse effects: constipation, diarrhea, headache, dizziness, flatulence, exanthema, elevated liver enzymes, fundic gland polyposis, osteoporotic fractures, C difficile infection, interaction with Plavix

                            Histamine H2 antagonists

                            • Mechanism of action: competitive inhibition
                            • Reduce gastric acid, pepsin secretion
                            • General adverse effects: headache, nausea, constipation, diarrhea, pruritis, myalgia, skin rashes
                            • Example: cimetidine
                              • Adverse side effects: binds to androgen receptors to increase prolactin, gynecomastia, impotence; binds to cytochrome P450 enzymes to affect drug metabolism

                            Chronic increase in gastric pH can cause bezoar formation.

                            Muscarinic antagonists

                            Block muscarinic receptors to reduced acid Non-selective, a lot of side effects, e.g. atropine Historically used

                            Antacids

                            • Mechanism: neutralize acid
                            • Systemic
                              • capable of producing systemic alkalosis
                              • NaHCO3
                              • not recommended
                            • Non-systemic
                              • Poorly absorbed cation
                              • Mg2+, Al3+, Ca2+
                              • Adverse side effects: Mg laxative, Al/Ca constipation, Al reduced phosphate absorption and bind pepsin, Ca produce acid rebound and bind tetracyclines
                            • Milk-alkali syndrome
                              • taken with milk may produce hypercalcemia, hypercalciuria, calcinosis, azotemia, hypophosphatemia

                            Cytoprotectants Prostaglandinds: decrease acid, pepsin, gastrin; increase bicarbonate, mucin, mucous Misoprostol: prostaglandin analogue; side effects: diarrhea, cramp, uterotonic actions, metrorrhagia, abortifacient

                            Bismuth Pepto-Bismol Increase mucin produciton, decrease pepsin secretion, antimicrobial against H. pylori

                            Treatment of Gastritis and Peptic Ulcer Disease

                            Pathology PPI: parietal cell and G cell hyperplasia, enlarged irregular glands, fundic gland polyp

                            H. pylori Eradication

                            Amoxicillin, clarithromycin, PPI 2x per day, 7-10 days (If penicillin allergy, substitute using metronidazole)

                            Failed eradication Quadruple therapy: tetracycline, fluoroquinolone, bismuth, PPI Longer duration
