      Physiology of the Liver & an Approach to Jaundice

      Sulara Guruge Monday, September 15, 2014

      Liver Physiology

      Liver functions

      • Second largest organ in the body.
        • Largest is the skin.
      • Multiple Regulatory functions.
        • Concentration of solutes in blood.
        • Energy metabolism.
        • Synthesis and storage of glucose, fatty acids and proteins.
        • Metabolism, biotransformation, and elimination of endogenous and exogenous compounds.

      Vascular Physiology of the Liver

      • Dual blood supply:
        • Hepatic Artery 30%.
        • Portal Vein 70%.
      • 1.5L/min (25% of Cardiac output).
      • 10% of circulating blood volume.
      • 50% of lymph is formed in liver.

      Cell Types of the Liver

      • There are 3 main cell types:
        1. Hepatocyte: largest in total mass.
        2. Sinusoidal cells: These include Kupffer, endothelial and stellate cells.
        3. Bile ductular cells.

      Hepatocytes

      • 60-80% of a normal liver.
      • Functional unit of the liver.
        • The ones near the arterial supply of the liver are more active than those near the venous supply, because there’s more oxygen.
      • Detoxify, modify and excrete wastes.
      • Synthesis, storage, and transformation of:
        • Carbohydrate, fatty acids, amino acids.
        • α-1-antitrypsin, cholesterol, bile salts, and phospholipids.
      • Initiates formation and secretion of bile.

      Liver Metabolism

      • Protein Synthesis
        • Only site able to manufacture:
          • Albumin, fibrinogen, and the Vitamin K dependant clotting factors (II, VII, IX, X).
          • Best way to remember this is ‘1972’ → Factor 1, 9, 7, and 2.
          • Recall from Blood that Factor VIII was the only one that was also produced outside the liver.
        • Main site of synthesis of:
          • Ceruloplasmin → carries Copper. Problems with this lead to Wilson’s Disease.
          • Lipoproteins, transferrin, glycoproteins.
      • Carbohydrate Metabolism
        • Stores and releases carbohydrate as needed.
        • Converts sugars to glucose and glycogen.
        • The liver is the only organ that can make glucose other than the brain.
          • It can do this by converting glyogen, fatty acids, or amino acids, into glucose.
          • Remember learning about Gluconeogenesis in undergrad? Yeah, me neither.
      • Lipid Metabolism
        • Fatty acids are formed from CHO (carbohydrates).
        • Triglycerides formed from free fatty acids and glycerol.
        • Apolipoproteins synthesized needed for HDL formation.
        • Cholesterol is made and converted to bile salts
        • All lipid metabolism is controlled by the liver

      Detoxification

      • The liver’s first pass metabolism, where it processes everything you absorb, it critical for detoxification.
        • Some compounds are altered to an inactive form, like steroids.
        • Other compounds are altered to a more active form, like Vitamin D.
      • Main site for drug metabolism.
        • Sedatives, steroids, ethanol, opiates, antibiotics, analgesics.
        • Recall from Inflammatory Bowel Disease Day that Budesonide had fewer side effects than Prednisone. This was because the first pass metabolism of Budenoside is much stronger, meaning that there’s less systemic circulation of it!
      • When you have liver disease, you have lower clearance.
      • Also, since the liver is the ‘first-pass’, it usually takes the first hit when you ingest something toxic.

      Sinusoidal Cells

      • Cells that reside in the sinusoids of the liver.
      • As mentioned before, these include:
        1. Endothelial Cells.
        2. Kupffer Cells.
        3. Stellate Cells.
      • Endothelial Cells
        • Fenestrated to act as a graded barrier → lets small particles in, but not large ones.
        • Mediates the exchange of fluids and particles into and out of the Space of Disse.
        • Actively keeps macromolecules and other particles from the circulation.
      • Kupffer Cells
        • Liver macrophages. The policemen of the sinusoids.
        • They line the sinusoids, and attach to the epithelium.
        • Kupffer cells form the largest group of macrophages in the body.
        • Vital for filtering function, by removing stuff like old cells, bacteria, particles, etc.
      • Stellate Cells.
        • Liver fibroblasts. Also function as lipocytes.
        • They store (1) fat and (2) Vitamin A.
        • Play a primary role in fibrosis in chronic hepatitis → when they get injured, they change their cell type and start laying down collagen.

      Bile Duct Cells

      • These line the bile duct.
      • Create a tubular passage for the excretion of bile.
      • Function to alter bile constitution, water and salt content.

      Bile Secretion

      • Bile is a conglomerate of bile salts, bile pigments, phospholipids, bicarbonate, and electrolytes
      • Hepatocytes secrete their stuff into the biliary canaliculi.
      • Bile release is controlled by:
        1. Bile salt concentration.
        2. Vagal stimulation.
        3. Gastrointestinal hormones.
          • Secretin, Gastrin, Cholecystokinin and Glucagon ↑ flow.
      • All of us produce about a litre of bile a day.
      • Since the Sphincter of Oddi is normally closed, when the bile comes down the common bile duct, it’ll reflux back into the gallbladder.
        • The hepatic bile is then concentrated 10x to form concentrated gallbladder bile via water and electrolyte (Na) absorption.
      • GB contraction and Oddi relaxation then allows flow to duodenum for fat digestion.

      Laboratory Tests

      • We can measure:
        1. Hepatobiliary transport (in/out of cells)
          • Measure organic compounds that are removed from the plasma or are excreted into the bile.
        2. Hepatobiliary injury (release of contents)
        3. Hepatic function via synthesis

      Hepatobiliary Transport

      1. Bilirubin
        • Bilirubin is the degradation product of heme.
        • Heme is contained in:
          • RBC’s = 80%.
          • Heme containing enzymes (Cytochrome P450, catalase, peroxidase) 20%.
        • Intuitively, Bilirubin can be elevated if you:
          1. Make too much.
          2. Can’t get it into the liver.
          3. Can’t process it in the liver.
          4. Can’t get it out of the liver.
        • Bilirubin is a marker of hepatobiliary function + excretion.
        • Clinical build-up, leading to yellowing of the skin, is termed jaundice.
        • Unconjugated bilirubin is unprocessed:
          • It hasn’t been processed by the liver yet, and is not water soluble.
          • It’s floating around in your blood, and can’t dissolve in it.
        • Conjugated bilirubin is processed
          • It has been processed by the liver, and is water soluble.
        • Unconjugated bilirubin enters the sinusoid, gets processed, and is released as conjugated bilirubin into the bile duct.
        • Suppose we had a blockage of the biliary tree, resulting in a backflow of conjugated bilirubin.
          • It can actually go back into the bloodstream, and since it’s water soluble, it’ll be secreted into the urine → dark urine
          • Since it doesn’t go into the GI tract, you end up with a pale stool (bilirubin is what makes your stool dark).
          • One common cause of these is gallstones.
      2. Bile Salts
        • Synthesized from cholesterol in the liver.
        • Conjugated to glycerine or taurine.
        • If you have an increase in bile salts in the plasma, it’s profoundly itchy.
        • Sensitive indicators of liver dysfunction.
        • Aids digestion.

      Hepatobiliary Injury

      • When hepatocytes are injured, intracellular enzymes are released.
      • These enzymes, and their rate of increase, are specific for the site of injury.

      Aminotransferases

      • Aminotransferases → sign of Hepatocellular injury → ‘Hepatitis’.
        1. Aspartate Aminotransferase (AST) → Normal <40.
        2. Alanine Aminotransferase (ALT)
      • AST can be found in organs other than the liver, but the ALT is specific to the liver.
      • There are only 4 things that will make these go over 1000:
        1. Ischemia.
        2. Viral hepatitis.
        3. Toxic injury.
        4. Autoimmune Liver Disease.
      • People can have alcoholic hepatitis, but their AST will never go over 500.
        • Double the AST vs the ALT, but both < 500
      Alkaline Phosphatase
      • Alkaline phosphatase (ALP) found in: liver, kidney, bone, placenta, and small bowel
      • Induced in bile ductule injury
        • It’s not something that’s released when bile duct cels are damaged, it’s actually made in response to injury.
        • This means that it’ll take a while to go up → around 24 hours.
      • Especially elevated in bile duct injury.
      • γ-glutamyl transpeptidase (GGT) parallels ALP elevations in biliary injury.

      Hepatic Function

      • When you’re asked for ‘liver function tests’, you usually mention ALT, AST, etc.
        • But these are actually liver injury tests.
      • True liver function tests include:
        1. INR → because Vitamin K dependent clotting factors are manufactured in the liver.
          • The half life of these clotting factors is short (6-24 hours).
          • This means that the patients INR will elevate shortly after any impairment to liver function.
        2. Albumin
          • This has a longer half life of 17 days.
          • So if this has fallen, so it’s a slower indicator of liver function → takes longer to fall when liver function falls, takes longer to recover when the liver recovers.
      • These are the only TRUE liver function tests.

      An Approach to Jaundice

      • Why are they Yellow?
      • Identify the nature of the bilirubin disturbance.
      • Conjugated vs unconjugated.
      • Hepatocellular (liver cell problem) vs biliary/cholestatic (excretion problem).

      Conjugated vs Unconjugated

      • So if a patient has a high bilirubin, it can be:
        1. ↑ Unconjugated Bilirubin:
          • Do they have increased production? → Hemolysis.
            • Other causes, like hereditary spherocytosis, or G6PD deficiency are also possible.
          • Do they have decreased conjugation? → eg. Gilbert’s disease.
            • Patients with this have a conjugation problem, where there’s a rate limiting step in bilirubin conjugation, making it very slow and sluggish.
        2. &urarr; Conjugated Bilirubin:
          • Is it not getting out of the liver? → Cholestasis.
          • Is there something wrong with the liver itself? → Hepatitis.
      • If there’s elevated bilirubin on the urine dipstick, then it has to be conjugated bilirubin, because that’s the only one that’s water soluble.

      Hepatocellular vs Cholestatic

      • Hepatocellular markers are ALT and AST, because they show hepatocellular injury.
        • Elevated 10x normal ALT, AST.
        • ↑INR because the liver cells aren’t working normally, as well.
      • Cholestatic markers are ALP and GGT → they show obstruction of the biliary tree.
        • Elevated 5x normal ALP, GGT.

      Hepatocellular vs Cholestatic Findings

      1. Hepatocellular → ALT and AST >10x normal.
      2. Cholestatic → ALP and GGT >5x normal.

      Intrahepatic vs Obstructive

      • If you have cholestasis due to a blockage, you need to know whether it’s:
        1. Obstructed in the Liver.
          • Can be caused by (1) Primary Biliary Cirrhosis, or (2) Sepsis.
        2. Obstructed in the Duct.
          • Can be caused by (1) Primary Sclerosing Cholangitis, or (2) Stones.

      Investigations for the Jaundiced Patient

      • Things to look at:
        1. Age of patient.
        2. History.
        3. Physical.
      • Diagnostic Tests
        1. Liver/biliary ultrasound
        2. CT
        3. Nuclear medicine imaging
        4. Liver chemistries
        5. ERCP/PTC
        6. Liver biopsy
      • Clinical manifestations of liver disease:
        • Jaundice → less bilirubin conjugation.
        • Bruising → less clotting factor.
        • Muscle Wasting → not making albumin, so your body will steal protein from the rest of your body.
        • Spider Angiomas
        • Ascites → due to less albumin and increased interstitial pressures.
