                              Case 1

                              Case Summary

                              A 60 year old man presents to you with trouble swallowing. The dysphagia appeared three months ago and has been progressive, starting with solids but now occurring with thick liquids. He has lost 10 kg in weight.

                              Notes

                              • Dysphagis = difficulty swallowing
                              • You can diagnose dysphagia with 3 questions:
                                1. Solid or Solid/Liquid
                                  • This helps you determine if it’s a mechanical problem or a motility problem.
                                  • Solid → mechanical problem.
                                  • Solid/liquid → motility problem.
                                  • These make sense, if you have
                                  • For instance, if there’s a problem with how we coordinate movement down the GI tract, then it’s more
                                2. Progressive or Intermittent
                                  • Some things won’t spontaneously get better (eg a tumour).
                                  • Other things may fluctuate (eg. an allergy).
                                3. Associated Symptoms
                                  • For instance, some problems may cause acid backflow, causing heartburn.
                                  • With a tumour, you would be looking at potential B-symptoms (fever, night sweats, unexplained weight loss).
                              • Difference between a ring and a

                              Dysphagia -> Esophageal | Oropharyngeal (DES, also eosinophilc esophagitis)

                              • How do we know if it’s not oropharyngeal dysphasia?
                              • They could have problems with actually just getting it into
                              • On Hx, may have some neurological disorders.
                              1. Discuss the clinical features that help to distinguish:
                                1. Oro-pharyngeal vs. esophageal causes.
                                  • This would likely be esophageal.
                                  • If it was oropharyngeal, you would see some choking, or regurgitation.
                                  • No history of neurological disorders as well.
                                  • Patient sensation localization with the esophagus is somewhat inaccurate → there is a lot of referred sensation in the esophagus.
                                2. Motility problems vs mechanical obstruction.
                                  • Mechanical, since we see problems with mostly solids.
                                  • If it was a motility problem (eg. a spasm), then you would also see chest pain.
                                  • Also, he’s not actually regurgitating, showing that some food probably is going through, unlike with a motility disorder.
                                3. Benign vs malignant strictures.
                                  • Probably malignant, since we’re rapidly progressive, he’s elderly, and there’s weight loss.
                                  • With a malignancy, we’ll more likely see a week → month history.
                                  • With something benign, we’ll usually have a history on the scale of years.
                                  • Potential risk factors include age, smoking, Barrett’s esophagus, HPV, caustic ingestions (eg. alcohol).
                                  • There are 2 cancers of the esophagus:
                                    1. Adenocarcinoma → Barrett’s is a risk factor.
                                    2. Squamous carcinoma → Tobacco, and alcohol is a risk factor.
                              2. *What is the most likely diagnosis? What tests need to be done?
                                • Probably an adenocarcinoma or a squamous carcinoma.
                                • A barium swallow could be used for imaging.
                                • Endoscopy would also be done too, because you need to take a biopsy.
                                • Since this is a pretty clear-cut case, you might as well directly proceed to the endoscopy + biopsy, because you’ll need to do a CT later for staging anyway.

                              Case Summary

                              A 25 year old male presents with trouble swallowing. The dysphagia occurs with both solids and liquids. It is worse as the meal progresses. There is no pain. If the patient lies down he frequently experiences regurgitation of non-acidic material into his throat. An upper GI series is shown at right.

                              1. What is the most likely diagnosis and why?
                                • Solids or solids/liquids? Solids/liquids.
                                • Intermittent/progressive? Doesn’t say.
                                • Associated symptoms? Regurgitation of non-acidic material.
                                • The most likely diagnosis is achalasia.
                                  • There’s regurgitation of non-acidic material, suggesting that the material hasn’t reached the stomach before it got regurgitated.
                                  • The image is also pretty obvious. The lower esophageal sphincter can’t relax here, so you esophagus proximal to it begins to dilate.

                              Case 3

                              Case Summary

                              A 45 year old female comes to your office with a 2 year history of a burning epigastric abdominal pain radiating to the chest. It occurs typically 15 minutes after she eats and is associated with regurgitation of acid material into her throat.

                              1. What is the most likely diagnosis?
                                • GERD → Gastroesophageal Reflux Disease.
                                  • We see this after meals (where more acid is secreted).
                                  • There’s regurgitation of acidic material into the throat.
                                  • We don’t see any signs suggesting otherwise (there’s a 2-year history, so it’s likely not malignant, and we see no oropharyngeal signs).
                              2. List symptoms that suggest complications:
                                • Barrett’s.
                                • Strictures.
                                • Hemorrhage. (anemia).
                                • Cough.
                                • Pharyngitis, laryngitis, hoarse voice.
                                • Damage to teeth, pneumonia.
                                • Dysphagia.
                              3. List potential causes / exacerbating factors:
                                • Any drugs you use for achalasia! (Anything that relaxes the sphincter).
                                • Anything that reduces LES pressure.
                              4. When is endoscopy indicated?
                                • Only scope if complications. (eg. dysphasia)
                                • Everyone gets heartburn once in a while.
                                • Also if refractory after PPI Tx. PPI is very effective, so if it doesn’t work, suspect something else.
                                • 10 year Hx to get scope, since progression to Barrett’s takes a LONG time.
                                • ‘Once in a lifetime endoscopy’ for Barrett’s -> eg once at 50, or 10 year Hx.
                                • Or with new-onset symptoms -> never had it before, suddenly having it 3x a week.
                              5. What is your approach to GERD therapy?
                                • Step up or step down Tx.
                                • PPI, H2 antagonists (less effective, less $).
                                • Lifestyle modifications (see slide).
                                  • spicy foods, coffee, alcohol, chocolate, peppermint.
                                  • acidic foods.

                                • Young, otherwise well, so no reason for other diagnosis.
