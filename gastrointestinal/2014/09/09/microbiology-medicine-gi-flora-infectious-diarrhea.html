      Microbiology and Medicine – GI Flora & Infectious Diarrhea

      John Paul Larkin Tuesday, September 09, 2014

      GI Flora

      • The gut is colonized soon after birth. Your mother’s flora actually plays a significant role in determining your flora!

      • Your gut flora actually varies depending on:
        • Vaginal delivery vs. C-section.
        • Breast-fed vs. formula fed.
      • Once established, your colonization remains stable:
        • You have approximately the same distribution for the rest of your life!
      • Your normal flora protects you against outside pathogens.

      Normal GI Flora

      • From top to bottom, there’s a transition from anaerobic organisms to gram negative organisms.
      • Near the mouth, you have more anaerobic organisms.
        • The mouth is actually the dirtiest part of the body!
      • Near the stomach, there isn’t much bacteria, due to the high pH.
        • H. pylori is an exception.
      • Past the stomach, bacterial concentration increases as we move further down (stomach < ileum < colon).
      • There’s actually more bacteria in your colon than cells in your body!

      Other Bacterial Defense Mechanisms

      • We have a number of other bacterial defense mechanisms:
        1. Saliva → washes bacteria away.
        2. *Gastric acid → kills bacteria in the food once it enters the stomach.
        3. Peristalsis → gut movement prevents colony formation.
        4. Gut associated lymphoid tissue (GALT) → recall those Peyer’s patches!
        5. Bacterial inhibitors → for instance, E. coli produce colicins.
      • If any of these are affected, we may have a disturbance of our normal flora.

      Infectious Diarrhea

      So What’s Diarrhea?

      • The definitions of diarrhea are pretty vague and not always well agreed on but may include:
        1. “An abnormal frequency and liquidity of fecal evacuations” (Normal frequency being anywhere from 3/day to 1 per 3 days”
        2. Stools too watery or frequent for the patients liking
        3. 3 bowel movements in a day
        4. Stool weight >200g per day
      • At the end of the day → shit’s happening too much.

      Classifying Infectious Diarrhea

      • It can be useful to classify infectious diarrhea into various groups. A simple classification is based on whether it is inflammatory or not.
        • Non-inflammatory diarrhea is characterized (unsurprisingly) by a non inflamed portion of the GI tract.
          • Typically in the small bowel.
          • The diarrhea results from issues with fluid/electrolyte absorption/secretion.
          • Organisms causing this will often produce enterotoxins leading to cell dysfunction.
          • These organisms include:
            1. Cholera.
            2. Enterotoxigenic E. Coli (traveller’s diarrhea)
            3. Staph aureus.
            4. Bacillus cereus.
          • The last 2 organisms (Staph aureus, Bacillus cereus) are common cause of rapid onset food poisoning due to preformed toxins in the food.
          • Some organisms will cause non-inflammatory diarrhea without enterotoxins.
            • These organisms include Rotavirus, Norwalk virus, and Parasites such as Giardia and Cryptosporidium.
        • Inflammatory is characterized by bacteria inflaming a portion of the GI tract, leading to fluid malabsoption and increased secretion
          • Typically in the colon.
          • The GI tract cells can be damaged through either:
            1. Cytotoxins.
            2. Direct cell invasion.
          • This diarrhea is often characterized by abdominal cramping, fever, and bloody stools (in general it appears more severe than non-inflammatory infectious diarrhea).
          • Anti-motility agents are contraindicated with bloody diarrhea.
          • These organisms include:
            • C. Difficile (cytotoxin)
            • E. Coli 0157:H7 (cytotoxin)
            • Salmonella (direct cell invasion)
            • Campylobacter jejuni (direct cell invasion)
            • Shigella (both mechanisms)
            • Entameoba histolytica (invasion of colonic mucosa and spreading through bloodstream)

      We will now examine the pathogenesis and specifics of some of these organisms.

      Cholera

      • A very classical cause of non-inflammatory diarrhea. Due to contamination of water supplies in developing countries.
      • Gram negative rod, Vibrio cholerae.

      Pathogenesis

      • The cholera toxin activates adenylate cyclase, leading to cAMP production.
      • This leads to increased Cl secretion, and water osmotically follows the Cl into the lumen of the small bowel resulting in the “rice water diarrhea” typical of cholera.
      • Note that Enterotoxigenic E. Coli produces the same toxin and therefore has a similar pathogenesis.
        • That’s why the Dukoral vaccine for Cholera has some cross-compatibility for Traveller’s diarrhea.
      • Treatment modalities include:
        1. Rehydration (Oral if possible, IV if needed)
        2. Antibiotics → if severe, cholera will resolve on its own. The antibiotics help to fight transmission and speed up remission.
          • Doxycycline.
          • Azithromycin.
          • Ciprofloxacin.
          • Prevention
          • Sanitation (SURPRISE!).
          • Dukoral vaccine.

      Clostridium Difficile

      • This gram positive rod causes inflammatory diarrhea by cytotoxin production.
      • Most people may have low levels of C. Diff in their flora, but sometimes it will ‘take over’ the GI flora due to antibiotic use or other stressors.
      • Risk factors include:
        1. Antibiotic use.
        2. Hospitalization.
        3. PPIs (unable to kill the C. Diff if ingested).
        4. Inflammatory Bowel Disease.
        5. Chronic disease.

      • If you suspect C. Diff a diagnosis can be made through:
        • Stool testing for C. diff toxin.
        • Sigmoidoscopy which will show ‘pseudomembranes’ (white plaques).
      • Treatment modalities include:
        • Supportive therapy (fluids, lytes, nutrition)
        • Stop previous antibiotics if possible
        • Metronidazole (1st choice) and Vancomycin (PO, second line) are antibiotics of choice to kill off the C. Diff.
        • DO NOT give anti-diarrheals-this could precipitate toxic megacolon.

      Remember

      • Metronidazole and Vancomycin are antibiotics of choice for C. difficile.
      • Do not give antidiarrheals → may precipitate toxic megacolon.

      E Coli O157:H7

      • Also known as Enterohemorrhagic E. coli.
      • Produces shiga toxin, a cytotoxin that causes cell destruction.
      • This gram negative rod usually causes bloddy diarrhea and will commonly resolve without specific treatment.
      • In fact, treatment may increase rates of Hemolytic Uremic Syndrome, and is a part of the reason while not all infectious diarrheas should be empiracally given antibiotics.

      Remember

      • Don’t give antibiotics to patients with E. coli O157:H7.
        • May precipitate HUS because all the shiga toxin in the bacteria will be released at once.

      Shigella

      • This gram negative rod both produces a cytotoxin and direct cell invasion.
      • This organism spreads incredibly easy, and as few as 100 organisms to colonize a new host.
      • This will resolve on its own, but antibiotics can be given to reduce risk of transmission and speed remission

      Entamoeba Histolytica

      • This intestinal parasite is spread due to contaminated water or fecal-oral transmission, but can also be sexually transmitted (with a higher rate among homosexual men).

      • There are 2 main points to the pathogenesis of entamoeba histolytica:

        • Invasion of colonic mucosa (this leads to ameobic dysentery → bloody diarrhea with fever).
        • Spreading to other organs via blood stream (ie liver) where it may form cysts. As a result of this the treatment is two part:
        • Metronidazole will kill the organism.
        • Paramomycin will kill cysts.

      Remember

      • You need 2 medications for Entamoeba Histolytica:
        1. Metronidazole to kill the organism.
        2. Paramomycin to kill the cysts.

      Salmonella enterica serotype Typhi (Typhoid Fever)

      • This infection is endemic to many countries, due to contaminated food or water.
      • The Salmonella bacteria will penetrate small bowel and cause sepsis.
      • Diagnosis: Positive stool or blood culture.
      • There are 4 stages to the illness which may help in directing clinical suspicion. Each stages will last approximately a week:
        1. Fever, bradycardia, headache, cough.
          • Normally, with a fever, you have tachycardia so bradycardia is a key finding.
        2. High fever, bradycardia, delirium, rose spots on chest, abdo pain, diarrhea or constipation.
        3. Bleeding, perforation, septicemia, delirium.
          • After week 3, you either die or get better.
        4. Fever abates, recovery begins.
      • Treatment:
        • Rehydration.
        • Ciprofloxacin or Ceftriaxone.
        • Prevent transmission by sanitation and vaccination.
      • Prognosis:
        • Without treatment there is a 10-30% mortality rate.
        • With treatment this rate dropes to about 1%
        • 5% will become chronic carriers (Think of Typhoid Mary)

      An Approach to a Patient with infectious Diarrhea

      History

      • Look for prior history of GI disease.
      • Travel.
      • OPQRST of symptoms.
      • Other people with symptoms.
      • Antibiotic use.
      • Systemic symptoms.

      Physical Exam

      • Weight/nutritional status (with attention to any change).
      • Vital signs (with attention to hydration status).
      • Abdominal exam (looking for tenderness).

      Investigations

      • CBC, lytes, BUN, Cr (standard blood work).
      • Blood cultures if febrile.
      • Abdo X-rays if severe symptoms.
      • Micro studies
        • Stool culture & sensitivity.
        • Stool ova + parasites.
        • Stool C. Diff toxin.
      • Consider sigmoidoscopy if symptoms persist and are severe enough to warrant a biopsy

      Treatment

      • Supportive care is most important.
      • Need oral rehydration → isotonic.
      • Some infections cause transient lactose intolerance, so you may need to avoid milk products afterwards.

      Hospital Admission

      • Not needed for most patients, but consider if:
        • Severe systemic signs.
        • Hydration difficult PO.
        • Infants/elderly.
        • Immunocompromised.

      Anti-motility agents

      • Can use for watery diarrhea w/o fever (mostly).
      • Never give in presence of bloody stools or if C. Diff is a consideration:
        • This may precipitate toxic megacolon.
        • This is massive dilation of the colon which requires emergent colectomy to repair.

      Antibiotics

      • Not required for most. Consider if:
        • Shigella.
        • C. Diff.
        • E. Histolytica.
        • Typhoid fever.
        • Patients in hospital (but try to avoid for E. Coli 0157:H7).
        • Failure to resolve after 1 week.
      • In general, Ciprofloxacin is a good choice, since it’s broad spectrum.
      • The only exceptions are with C. diff and Entamoeba histolytica → Metronidazole is the first line treatment.

      Antibiotics for Infectious Diarrhea

      • Typically, Ciprofloxacin is first line, since it’s broad spectrum.
      • The only exceptions are with C. diff and Entamoeba histolytica → Metronidazole is the first line treatment.
