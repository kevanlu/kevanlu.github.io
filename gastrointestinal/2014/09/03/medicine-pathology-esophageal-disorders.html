          Medicine & Pathology of Esophageal Disorders

          Jeremy Chitpin Wednesday, September 03, 2014

          Topics Covered

          1. Esophagitis
            • GERD → Gastroesophageal Reflux Disease.
            • Barrett’s esophagus.
            • Other.
          2. Dysphagia & Motility Disorders

          Esophagitis

          • Esophagitis (inflammation of the esophagus) can be caused by:
            • GERD → by far the most common.
            • Eosinophilic.
            • Infections → Candida, HIV, CMV. Recall that kayexelate was a potassium-binding resin that’s sometimes used when dealing with hyperkalemia.
            • Drugs → NSAIDs, bisphosphonates, iron, kayexelate.
            • Chemical.
            • Physical.

          Gastroesophageal Reflux Disease (GERD)

          • GERD = most common cause of esophagitis.
          • injury due to acid/pepsin/bile reflux into the esophageal lining causing damage
          • Damages the mucosa of the esophagus.

          What prevents GERD?

          1. A competent lower esophageal sphincter. Decreased LES makes sense as a preventative agent, because it lowers the strain on the sphincter.
          2. Decreased LES Pressure → reduces strain on the LES.
            • Diet → alcohol, chocolate, peppermint, fatty foods.
            • Drugs → relaxants like calcium-channel blockers, and benzodiazepines.
            • Smoking
            • Pregnancy → either due to hormonal changes, or just increases in abdominal pressure.
            • Hiatal Hernia.
            • If any of these mechanisms are faulty, then GERD can occur.

          What is a Hiatal Hernia?

          Hiatal Hernia → when the stomach ends up moving out of the abdominal cavity through the esophageal hiatus in the diaphragm. - Sliding Hiatal Hernia → the stomach gets pulled up through the esophageal hiatus. - Paraesophageal Hiatal Hernia → the stomach prolapses alongside the esophagus through the esophageal hiatus.

          GERD Symptoms

          • Heartburn → retrosternal burning pain.
            • Post-prandial pain → after eating.
            • relieved with antacids → gravity doesn’t help keep things down anymore.
            • aggravated by lying down.
          • Regurgitation → spontaneous vomit.
          • Atypical chest pain → ‘non-cardiac chest pain’.
          • Signs of gastric contents affecting other parts of the body:
            • Dental erosions
            • Cough, pharyngitis.
          • When these are mild and infrequent, then it’s not serious → it happens all the time in the general population.
            • GERD is common, but most is mild, and isn’t treated.
            • Those with more moderate symptoms seen by FPs.
            • Those with severe symptoms seen by gastroenterologists.

          Diagnosing GERD

          • mostly on history
          • Physical exam to exclude other illnesses
          • Barium swallow to assess anatomy (see other lectures)
          • Esophageal manometry to assess LES pressure and competence
          • pH monitoring studies - an acidic esophagus indicates reflux

          To assess complications, do an upper GI endoscopy.

          Complications

          • hemorrhage (acute and chronic)
          • repeated damage → fibrosis → stricture
          • Barrett’s esophagus (repeated damage) → adenocarcinoma

          Endoscopic Screening for Barrett’s Esophagus and Adenocarcinoma

          • Anyone middle aged with GERD should undergo endoscopic screening
          • Disease progression:
          • Metaplasia → low grade dysplasia → high grade dysplasia → adenocarcinoma

          Barrett’s Esophagus replacement of normal squamous mucosa with columnar epithelium containing goblet cells Gross pathology reveals a colour change where cells are not uniform 0.3% of developing adenocarcinoma

          Management

          ####First line lifestyle modification#### - elevate head of bed, gravity assisted - dietary modification, lower fatty foods, chocolate, alcohol - weight loss - stop smoking - discontinue medications i.e. benzos, CCBs

          Pharmacological therapy

          • Antacids
          • H2 Antagonists i.e. raniditine
          • PPIs i.e. pantoprazole, omeprazole
          • prokinetics i.e. domperidone (very strong)
            • useful for regurg to stimulate gastric emptying

          Surgery

          • Nissen Fundoplication
            • Hernia is repaired by pulling stomach down
            • also wrapping stomach around bottom near esophagus to reinforce sphincter

          Other Causes of Esophagitis

          Eosinophilic esophagitis

          • Such as in cases of asthma
          • upon endoscope, shows vertical furrows, corrugated rings
          • upon microscopic pathology, squamous tissue is filled with eosinophils

          Candida Esophagitis

          • upon endoscope, can see candida plaques
          • microscopic pathology shows pseudo hyphae

          Herpes (HSV) Esophagitis

          • notable herpes lesions in esophagus

          CMV Esophagitis

          • notable exudates
          • microscopic inclusions in endothelial cells diagnostic of CMB

          Dysphagia & Motility Disorders

          Dysphagia - difficulty swallowing Oropharyngeal - the transfer of food from the mouth into the esophagus

          • often a muscular or neuromuscular problem

          Esophageal - the transfer of food down the esophagus to the stomach

          • stuck around “sternal notch”

          Clinical Correlate: Patients will point to where they feel food gets stuck The actual pathology is anywhere downstream from where they point see achalasia below, where loss of peristalsis at bottom of esophagus causes swallowing dysfunction

          Diagnosis of Esophageal Disorders

          Problems with swallowing solids?

          • mechanical problem, like stricture or tumour

          Problems with swallowing liquids?

          • motility problem

          Swallowing problems are unpredictable?

          • an event, like spasm

          Progressive?

          • chronic condition

          Do a barium swallow as a screening tool for dysphagia, looks at anatomy and function >Schatzki’s Ring - a radiological finding, 2-3mm mucosal ring shows as narrowing Note: Upon radiology, peptic strictures look very geometric, while malignant structures are more convoluted or asymmetric

          Esophageal Motility Disorders

          • Primary vs secondary
          • Primary
            • achalsia, spasm, nutcracker esophagus
          • Secondary
            • connective tissue disorder (scleroderma or CREST syndrome)
            • weakness of the sphincter due to CT disease

          Primary Esophageal Disorder: Achalasia

          • spastic LES, does not relax normally
          • acts as a functional obstruction
          • loss of peristalsis at bottom of esophagus
          • inappropriate reservoir
          • leads to weight loss since you feel full
          • Diagnosis
            • manometry shows high pressure at level of LES
          • Treatment:
            • smooth muscle relaxants → balloon dilatation or Botox → surgical myotomy

          Primary Esophageal Disorder: Diffuse Esophgeal Spasm

          • intermitted unpredictable dysphagia
          • mimics angina, squeezing central chest pain
          • Diagnosis
            • manometry shows unpredictable spasms which prevent peristalsis
          • Treatment
            • Reassurance and smooth muscle relaxants

          Primary Esophageal Disorder: Nutcracker Esophagus

          • characterized by high amplitude, peristaltic contractions
          • contractions are so powerful swallowing becomes painful
          • Treatment
            • Reassurance and smooth muscle relaxants

          Secondary Esophageal Disorder: Scleroderma Esophagus

          • Bad reflux due to hypotensive lower sphincter, CT weakness
            • loss of peristalsis Diagnosis
            • manometry shows little pressure at LES
