                              Physiology & Pathophysiology of Motility

                              Kevan Lu Wednesday, September 03, 2014

                              Functions of Motility

                              • Propulsion from oral to anal.
                              • Mixing → mechanical dispersion, increase contact with digestive secretions and absorbing cells.
                              • Reservoir/storage → stomach, gallbladder and colon.

                              Clinical GI Motility Problems

                              • Motility is important → problems with motility can cause issues anywhere in the GI tract:
                                • Esophagus → GERD, spasm and achalasia .
                                • Stomach → gastroparesis, dumping. syndrome, functional dyspepsia.
                                • Small bowel → diarrhea, bacterial overgrowth.
                                • Large bowel → constipation, IBS.
                                • Anus → fecal incontinence.
                                • Generalized GI → pseudoobstruction, diabetes and CT disorders (scleroderma).

                              Control of GI Motility

                              • Complex and multi-faceted process
                              • Multiple levels of control:
                                • CNS (brain and spinal cord)
                                  • Top level control.
                                  • For instance motility changes when you’re prepping for an exam, or running a marathon.
                                • Enteric NS (myenteric plexus)
                                  • The GI tract has its own brain.
                                  • More neurons in the GI tract than in the spinal cord actually!
                                • Myogenic (unique smooth muscle)
                                  • Some are pre-programmed in the muscle itself.
                                  • Just like with cardiac muscle!
                                • Hormonal (endocrine and paracrine)
                                • Luminal contents

                               Innervated from enteric nervous system  Muscle fibres exhibit BER or slow wave (spontaneous variations in membrane potential)  Slow wave is independent of innervation (part of the muscle itself)  Slow wave is propagated aborally (phase lag) and coordinates contractions

                              ￼Smooth Muscle Interstitial cells of Cajal  Interstitial cells of Cajal (ICC) are specialized cells found in the smooth muscle of the GI tract. - Attach to the outside of smooth muscle cells. - Act like a pacemaker to set the rate of depolarization and coordinate between smooth muscle cells. - So they play an important role in the generation of the slow wave electrical activity.

                              ￼GI Sphincters - Divide the luminal gut into functional components. - They act like the valves in veins → they prevent backflow and ensure the proper progression of stuff from gum to bum.  GI tract is divided into functional units by sphincters  Act as valves to regulate forward flow and prevent backwards flow  Muscular high pressure zones  Relax to allow aboral flow  Contract to prevent oral movemeny

                              Constant tonic contraction. Proximal distension causes the sphincter to relax. Distal distension causes the sphincter to tighten. This makes it easier for stuff to go in, but harder for stuff to go back.

                              ￼Patterns of GI Muscle Contractions  Tonic contraction (sphincter)  Peristaltic (propagated) wave – ring of contraction moves aboral direction  Segmental contraction – stationary ring of contraction to brake and mix contents - Random segmental contractions shake stuff around to mix stuff around. - Instead of moving things cranially to caudally, movecs from side to side.

                              The Esophagus

                              Esophageal Motility  Striated muscle proximally (complete CNS control) (since same as throat) -> ALL DEPENDENT ON CNS  Smooth muscle distally (Enteric NS control)  Swallow induces Primary Peristalsis  Distension (retained food) induces Secondary Peristalsis

                              The Stomach

                              Control of Gastric Motility

                              Receptive relaxation – vagal reflex - Patients who have a vagotomy have a dumping syndrome. - This is because the patient can no longer accomodate extra contents in the stomach, so it just dumps everything into the duodenum, overwhelms things.  Gastric contractions – myogenic control coordinated by gastric pacemaker (sets frequency) and peristalsis is generated by “phase lag” of BER  Rate of gastric emptying is affected by luminal contents (pH, osmolarity, fat and protein content) working through local reflexes and hormone release - Fat slows gastric emptying, to maximize absorption in the small bowel.

                              Lose this, get dumping.

                              The Small Bowel

                              • From a functional component, this is more important, because this is where most of the absoprtion takes place.
                              • Motility here is designed to maximize absoprtion
                                • Lots of mixing (sort of like kneading).
                                • Lots of circulation too (to increase contact with absorptive cells)
                                • Propulsion occurs in a controlled fashion so that there’s still forward movement.

                              Mix of propagated or localized. Localized contractions mix. Propagated propel.

                               Segmentation  Short peristaltic contractions  Migrating myoelectric complex (MMC FED pattern. MMC = after fasting. Migrating myoelectric complex. Very important role as the housekeeper of the small bowel. - Used to clear out undigested materials, leftover food, bacteria. - Special fasting event where everything gears up and gets swept out.

                              The Colon - Acts as a reservoir. - Takes out water and electrolytes. - Takes out remaining good stuff. - Makes things solid so they’re easier to pass.

                               Segmentation  Peristalsis over short segments  Mass movements = taking a shit! - Propagated front from colon to rectum -> SHIT! - 1-2 a day
