      Nutritional Supplementation

      Tehmina Ahmad Wednesday, September 24, 2014

      Lecture Objectives

      1. Describe a basic nutritional assessment
      2. Review the role that each party of the GI tract has in nutrition.
      3. Describe the indications and techniques for gastric feeding, jejunal feeding and total parenteral nutrition.
      4. Describe the nutritional formulae one has to augment nutrition in patients with diseases.
      5. Review kwashiorkor and marasmus.

      Causes for Nutritional Support

      • As hospital stays progresses, nutritional status worsens in medical and surgical patients.
      • Prolonged NPO (nil per os) status -Hospital-based testing requiring NPO/clear fluid status
      • Restrictive diets
      • Incidence: occurs in 30-55% of inpatients.

      Consequences of Malnutrition

      • Increased risk of infection due to compromised immunity.
      • Impaired wound healing.
      • Increased risk of skin breakdown.
      • Weakness, decreased activity and delayed rehabilitation.
      • Increased morbidity and mortality.
      • All these consequences result in slower recovery, increased length of hospital stay and higher health care costs. Inflammatory states induce anorexia because people feel too ill to eat.

      Benefits of Nutrition Support

      • Improve immune function.
      • Promote wound healing.
      • Stabilize or increase body weight.
      • Stabilize or increase visceral proteins (Albumin & Transferrin).
      • Improved strength and tolerance to therapy.
      • Hasten recovery and decrease length of stay.

      How do we detect malnutrition in our patients?

      There are 4 key steps to nutritional assessment that we must consider:

      1. History
      2. Physical Examination
      3. Anthropometric measurements
      4. Laboratory Investigations

      History

      • Dietary history.
      • Alcohol history.
      • Significant weight loss within the last 6 months
        1. >10% loss of body weight
        2. Either % ideal weight or % usual weight
        3. Beware of patients with ascites/oedema, which adds weight (think of cirrhotic patients with portal hypertension)

      Physical Examination

      • Evidence of muscle wasting
      • Depletion of subcutaneous fat
      • Peripheral oedema, ascites (due to hypoalbuminemia)
      • Features of Vitamin deficiency (low iron can lead to koilonychia; the spooning of nail beds)
      • Easy to detect > 10% loss in muscle wasting
      • Most obviously, look at deltoid and quadracep muscles for signs of muscle wasting

      Anthropometry

      • Weight for Height comparison
      • Body Mass Index (<18.5, or >10% decrease)
      • Triceps, measuring skin fold
      • Mid-arm muscle circumference.

      Laboratory Investigations for Nutritional Assessment

      • albumin < 35 mg/dL
      • pre-albumin <.18 mg/dL
      • transferrin < 150 mmol/L
      • total lymphocyte count < 1800 / mm3
      • tests reflecting specific nutritional deficits ie) Prothrombin time

      Consider Where GI Tract is Effected

      Methods of Giving Nutrition IV Feeding

      1. Total Parenteral Nutrition (TPN)
        • Infusion of fluid, salts, and nutrients directly into the systemic circulation
        • Given to patients with abnormal gut function
        • These patients cannot consume adequately enough by enteral feeding and will not be feeding orally by 5-7 days.
        • TPN will not be started unless normal BMI but has not fed by mouth for >7 days
        • OR low BMI and has had nothing by mouth for >5 days
        • Peripheral IV (peripheral parenteral nutrition) 10% glucose, most of the calories are provided by fat (intralipid)
        • Central line (total parenteral nutrition) 25% glucose, most of the calories are provided by glucose and it is easier to maintain and control nutrition. This is the preferred method.
        • Need venous access to a “large” central line with fast flow to avoid thrombophlebitis from a hypertonic solution

      Complications of TPN:

      • Mechanical Complications → Pneumothorax, bleeding, air embolism, thrombosis
      • Metabolic Complications → Electrolyte abnormalities “Hypo/hyper everything”
      • TPN liver disease → 15% of long term TPN
      • Infectious Complications → Septicemia
      • Expensive – $250/day

      Methods of Giving Nutrition TUBE Feeding

      1. Oral Feeding
        • BEST method of feeding
        • Uses all the control systems the GI tract has to offer
        • Gut mucosa is preserved (food is trophic to the gut)
        • Cheaper
        • Socially important, no synthetic tube required
        • Can only be used if gut is functioning
        • Avoids complications of other modalities
      2. Naso-Gastric Tube (NG Tube)
        • Indicated in strokes
        • Requires a functioning stomach
        • Complications of NG Tube:
        • Food Aversion
        • Sinusitus
        • Aspiration
      3. Naso-Jejunum Tube (NJ Tube)
        • Indicated in strokes
        • Indicated in Pancreatitis to let the pancreas rest
        • Complications of NJ Tube:
        • More complex issues
        • Food Aversion
        • Sinusitus
        • Aspiration
      4. Gastrotomy Tube (G Tube)
        • Indicated in long term feeding & lowers aspiration risk.
        • Easy to change and relatively painless
        • Complications of G Tube:
        • Requires a functioning stomach (no motility issues)
        • Tube blockage
        • Bleeding
        • Peritonitis
        • Pulling out the tube (as patients maybe in a confused state/malnutritioned)
      5. Gastro-Jejenal Tube (GJ Tube)
        • Indicated in long term feeding, no/low aspiration risk because nutrition is getting delivered directly from stomach to jejenum.
        • Used when stomach is not functioning properly.
        • Complications of GJ Tube:
        • Insertion
        • Pulling out/peritonitis
        • Tube blockage
        • Bleeding
        • Need continuous feeds (not bolus feeds) 12 ‐ 16 hours/day

      BOLUS Feeds

      • Infusion of a specified volume of formula at defined intervals over short period of time
      • Administered by gravity or with a syringe
      • Feedings usually given over a period of several minutes (150 – 400 ml)
      • Exclusively used for gastric feedings
      • GI tract must be normal
      • Used when there are swallowing problems

      CONTINUOUS Feeds

      • Same amount is delivered slowly over a period of 8 ‐ 24 hours
      • Infusion pump enables accurate flow
      • Benefits those who cannot or have not received food through the GI tract for a considerable time
      • Allows for maximal absorption
      • Usual way for overnight supplementation
      • Majority of hospitalized patients are fed this way

      Types of Feeding Formulas

      1. General Purpose

      • Isotonic (300 ‐ 500 mOsm) – close to the osmolality of body fluids
      • Caloric density: 1.0 – 1.4 cal/cc
      • Protein content: 35 ‐ 45 gm/L intact protein
      • Fats: 30 ‐ 40% of calories
      • Majority are lactose and gluten free
      • Examples: Jevity1.0 (contains fibre), Osmolite (Low fibre)

      2. Nutrient Dense

      • Designed for patients requiring fluid restriction or very high calories
      • Typically high osmolality
      • Caloric density ranges 1.5 – 2.2 cal/cc

      3. High Nitrogen

      • Used for patients with high protein needs due to catabolism or patients at risk for protein losses (trauma, sepsis, critical illness, burns or fistulas)
      • Caloric density 1.0 – 2.0 cal/cc
      • Protein 50 ‐ 80g/L
      • Examples: Promote, Isosource VHN, Jevity 1.2

      4. Elemental Feeds (Chemically Defined)

      • Designed to optimize nutrient absorption in patients with compromised GI function
      • Components given in digested form
      • No digestion needed
      • Very low in fat
      • Indications: IBD, short bowel syndrome, pancreatic or bile salt insufficiency
      • Examples: Vital HN, Vivonex TEN

      5. Semi-Elemental Feeds

      • Used more often than Elemental feeds but serve similar purposes
      • Protein source is peptide source
      • Mixture of long and short chain fatty acids
      • Requires some digestion
      • Examples: Peptinex, Peptamen prebio, Peptamen 1.2, 1.5
      • Lower osmolarity than elemental

      Disease Specific Feeding Regimens

      • Renal (NEPRO) – kidney failure o Low electrolyte & low protein
      • Diabetic (GLUCERNA) – Low in carbs (~35% of calories), added fiber
      • Hepatic (NUTRIHEP) – hepatic failure, Protein content is low, but high in BCAA (this is from old literature and no longer used)
      • Sepsis induced ARDS (OXEPA) – provides omega 3 fatty acids and antioxidants

      Summary of Types of Feeding Regimens

      General Purpose

      • Begin feeding to supply all nutritional deficiencies, hence general purpose.

      Nutrient Dense

      • Designed for patients requiring fluid restriction or very high calories

      High Nitrogen

      • Used for patients with high protein needs due to catabolism or protein losses (trauma, burns, fistulas, sepsis, critical illness)

      Elemental

      • Designed to optimize nutrient absorption in patients with compromised GI function

      Semi-Elemental

      • Used more often than Elemental, but requires some protein digestive function.

      Refeeding Syndrome

      The metabolic and physiologic consequences of the depletion, repletion, compartmental shifts and interrelationships of the following:

      • Phosphorus
      • Potassium
      • Magnesium
      • Glucose metabolism
      • Vitamin deficiency
      • Fluid resuscitation

      Refeeding Syndrome may result in:

      • Hypophosphatemia
      • Hyperglycemia
      • Fluid retention
      • Cardiac arrest

      Go SLOW When refeeding!!!

      • 0.8 Basal energy requirements and increase as tolerated
      • Monitor – phosphate, glucose, potassium, magnesium

      Patients at Risk for Refeeding Syndrome

      • Anorexia nervosa
      • Classic kwashiorkor
      • Classic marasmus
      • Chronic malnutrition ‐ underfeeding
      • Chronic alcoholism
      • Morbid obesity with massive weight loss
      • Patient unfed in 7 ‐ 10 days with evidence of stress and depletion
      • Prolonged fasting
      • Prolonged intravenous hydration

      Clinical Note: Kwashiorkor vs. Marasmus

      Kwashiorkor

      • The word is taken from the Ga language in Ghana & used to describe the sickness of weaning.
      • Due to inadequate protein intake +/ ‐ catabolism secondary to infection
      • An example of a maladaptive physiological response to unbalanced deficiency where the body utilizes proteins and conserves subcutaneous fat stores.
      • Features of Kwashiorkor include:
        1. Edema
        2. Psychomotor Changes
        3. Growth Retardation
        4. Muscle Wasting
        5. Skin Depigmentation (Flaky Paint Dermatitis)
        6. Anemia
        7. Moon Face

      Marasmus

      • The term marasmus is derived from the Greek marasmos, which means wasting.
      • Due to inadequate intake of protein and calories and is characterized by emaciation
      • Marasmus represents the end result of starvation where both proteins and calories are deficient
      • An example of an adaptive response to starvation, where the body utilizes all subcutaneous and visceral fat stores before using muscles (protein stores)
      • Features of Marasmus include:
        1. Severe wasting of both muscle & subcutaneous fats
        2. Severe growth retardation
        3. Child looks older than his age (fat in cheeks is gone)
        4. No edema or hair changes
        5. Alert but miserable
        6. Hungry
        7. Diarrhea & Dehydration
