      Inflammatory Bowel Disease Day

      John Paul Larkin Friday, September 12, 2014

      IBD Notes

      • See Inflammatory Bowel Disease Notes.
      • First very important thing to not mess up:

      Important

      • IBD is NOT IBS. Not even remotely close. Become very very very very sure of the fact that they are different.
      • Recall that IBS is a functional disorder meaning there is no organic cause for the symptoms. As you’ll soon discover IBD has lots of fun pathophysiology to find.
      • Patients with IBD are the main patient group with chronic disease that are monitored by gastroenterologists.
      • There are two disorders within IBD (which may exist more on a continuum than on a dichotomy by some standards). They are:
        1. Crohn’s Disease (CD).
        2. Ulcerative Colitis (UC).
      • These affect different areas of the gut, but share key clinical and pathological features.
      • Even though they may present similarly, management differs.

      Pathogenesis of IBD

      • For details on the pathogenesis you can refer to the following diagram:

      • The pathogenesis of IBD is complicated!
      • A simplified approach is to consider commensal microbes, environmental factors, and defective barrier function causing an unregulated, exaggerated host immune response in the GI tract.
      • There is a genetic component to IBD as 15% of patients have first degree relatives with IBD, and monozygotic twins have a 30-50% concordance rate for CD. There are several gene candidates for this including:
        • HLA associations.
        • NOD2 gene (a protein expressed in leukocyte and epitehlial cells).
        • NF-κB pathway can be triggered by microbes.

      Intestinal Flora plays a Role

      • The role of the intestinal flora has been inferred from animal studies with mice where they knockout the flora in mice that generally get IBD, and the disease disappears.
        • There does not appear to be a dose dependence however (reducing the microbe load did not affect course of disease, only eliminating it does).
        • The Microbes can gain access to mucosal lymphoid tissue due to the defective barrier function (mentioned above) and trigger an exaggerated immune response by providing antigens.

      The Abnormal Immune Response

      Recall that CD4+ T-cells were the T-helper cells, and CD8+ T-cells were cytotoxic T lymphocytes.

      • The abnormal immune response is attributed to CD4+ T-cell over-activation, with insufficient control by regulatory T cells.
      • With Crohn’s Disease, there’s an additional element:
        • There’s a delayed-type hypersensitivity reaction, where there’s granulomatous inflammation (which we will see more about later).

      Environmental Factors

      • Several Environmental factors also affect IBD including:
        • Smoking → which actually can relieve UC while paradoxically aggravating CD.
        • Diets → there really hasn’t been any great evidence so far suggesting that special diets change disease progression.
        • Drugs → will be discussed later.

      Remember

      • Smoking with Crohn’s Disease has a damaging effect.
      • Smoking with Ulcerative Colitis has a protective effect.

      Epidemiology

      • Prevalence is similar between UC, and CD.
      • Incidence of CD used to be increasing but has now tapered off to be similar to UC.
      • There seems to be no gender trends between CD and UC.
      • While IBD can most commonly show up in 20s or 30s, there is a second rise in incidence in the 50-70s.

      Remember

      • The peak incidence of IBD is in the 20s to 30s.

      Disease Distribution

      • This has come up several times in discussions, lectures, small groups, so be sure to have this down pat:
      • UC has continuous colonic involvement beginning in the rectum and almost always stays within the colon (although it may sometimes ‘backwash’ into the terminal ileum).
      • In contrast CD has skip lesions where some areas of small/large bowel may be unaffected, but bordered by affected parts.
      • For CD involvement common places are:
        • 30% ileum and cecum.
        • 30% colon/rectum alone.
        • 40% small bowel alone.
        • Can also affect esophageal, gastroduodenal areas as well (just less frequently)
      • This is roughly a rule of 1/3s → pretty even distribution between the (1) ileum and cecum, (2) colon and rectum, and (3), small bowel.

      Remember

      • With ulcerative colitis, there is continuous colonic involvement.
      • With Crohn’s disease, there are skip lesions across the small and large bowel.

      Contrasting Pathological Features between CD and UC

      • Memorize this table. A lot of the differences in presentation, pathology and complications can be explained from these differences.

      Pathological Features of Crohn’s and Ulcerative Colitis

      Feature Crohn’s Disease Ulcerative Colitis
      Continuity Discontinuous Continuous
      Depth of Inflammation Transmural Mucosal only
      Ulcers Fissuring Shallow
      Granulomas Yes No

      Crohn’s Disease

      • Early Crohn’s presents with apthous ulcers → canker sores that you can have anywhere along the GI tract.
        • These are superficial erosions of the mucosal surface.
      • As these progress, they penetrate the mucosa, into the submucosa.
        • This was one of our differences from Ulcerative Colitis → UC stays in the mucosa only!
      • These ulcers take on a characteristic long, linear, snake-like appearance. Hence these are called serpiginous ulcers.

      Above we can also see how, grossly, Crohn's has skip lesions. We see alternation between normal bowel and roughened, inflamed bowel.

      This skipping can even be seen pathologically. Here we have two adjacent tissue samples where one appears normal, and the other shows inflammation and a loss of architecture.

      Local Complications of CD

      • It’s easy to think of all of these as complications of the transmural inflammation seen with Crohn’s disease.
        • There are several consequences of the fissuring ulcers that can form in CD:
          • Perforation into the peritoneal free space.
          • Fistulization with other parts of the GI tract, perianal region, urethral/vaginal tracts.
          • Peri-enteric/perianal abscesses can form.
        • The inflammatory process also causes fibrosis which can lead to obstruction:
          • Strictures (which can obstruct the bowel and may be a surgical emergency)
          • Adhesions (which may also obstruct the bowel).
        • Developing dyplasia and cancer is also a risk of CD (although the risk is higher with UC).
          • To screen for this most guidelines recommend a regular biopsy starting at 8 years and then every 1-3 years.
          • Can detect the dysplatic mucosal changes which are precursors to adenocarcinoma.

      Remember

      • When considering the complications of Crohn’s, think of the transmural inflammation that it causes.
        • It can cause ulcers that go through the bowel wall (perforation, fistulation, abcesses).
        • It can also cause fibrosis that leads to strictures or adhesions.
        • It can cause dysplastic changes that lead to cancer.

      Above is a fissuring ulcer. It can be described as a knife like cleft cutting through granulomatous tissue.

      Above is a summary of some of the complications of Crohn's disease.

      Signs and Symptoms of CD

      • Can include:
        • Abdominal pain.
        • Diarrhea.
        • Weight loss.
        • Rectal bleeding.
        • Fever.
        • Nausea and vomiting.
      • Note that these symptoms are ridiculously non-specific.
      • So we have a relatively large differential and CD is considered to be a great ‘mimicker’ in medicine for this reason. The DDx can include:
        • Colitis from other causes (UC, ischemic colitis, infective colitis).
        • Appendicitis, diverticulitis.
        • NSAIDS.
        • Malignancy.
        • IBS.
        • Celiac.
        • Radiation enteropathy.
      • There will be exacerbations and remissions over the course of the disease, and in general the disease course is unpredictable.

      Typical Presentation

      • There are several aspects of CD pathology you should recognize clinically.
      • Ileo-colonic inflammation:
        • RLQ abdominal pain.
        • Diarrhea.
      • Strictures:
        • Intermittent abdo pain, bloating, nausea, vomiting.
        • Since there’s an anatomic problem, they won’t respond to medical management.
        • Treated with balloon dilation or surgery.
      • Fistulas will present differently depending on location:
        • If they are perianal they will present with pain, mucous, discharge.
        • If they are between segments of the GI tract you tend to get diarrhea (this is due to digested material skipping segments of reabsorption).
        • Treated with an enteral diet to minimize GI tract usage.
          • Medically can be treated with Metronidazole, Methotrexate, Azathioprine, Infliximab.
          • Surgery also works.
          • Important point: steroids will not work to treat fistulas.
      • Abscess:
        • Pain.
        • Swelling.
        • Fever.
      • Be aware that CD can also cause anemia and diarrhea through several mechanisms, and these can have systemic effects.
        • Anemia is obvious → the inflammation and ulceration causes GI bleeding and malabsorption.
          • Iron deficiency anemia if the proximal small bowel is affected.
          • Megaloblastic anemia if the terminal ileum is affected (where B12 is absorbed).
        • Diarrhea is due to reduced absorption → either due to inflammation of the bowel, bacterial overgrowth, or loss of bowel due to surgical resection of affected areas.
      • Systemically you can also get osteoporosis. This is caused by:
        • Glucocorticoids (iatrogenic!).
        • Malabsorption.
        • Smoking.
        • Immobility.
        • Chronic inflammatory disease.
      • It can be detected with bone mineral density screening, and treatment with Calcium, Vitamin D, Bisphosponates.

      Extraintestinal manifestations of IBD

      • We’ll now bridge the gap between CD and UC by discussing several other manifestations of these diseases.

      • While there is overlap between the two diseases, we will list each manifestation with the disease it is more commonly associated with.

      • Skin manifestations:
        • CD: Erythema nodosum, Apthous ulcers in the mouth.
        • UC: Pyoderma gangrenosum.

      • Eye lesions:
        • CD: Iritis, uveitis, episcleritis, conjunctivity.
        • UC: Similar to above but far less common. Recall from Infection & Immunity (so long ago!) that Ankylosing Spondylitis was one of the only auto-immune diseases which was more common in males than females!
      • MSK manifestations:
        • CD: peripheral arthritis (classic in cases!), gallstones, Kidney stones
        • UC: Ankylosing spondylitis, Primary sclerosing cholangitis, Hypercoagulable.

      Investigations in IBD

      • There are no specific lab findings for IBD but several things can be suggestive:
        • CBC → increased WBC, platelets, anemia.
        • ESR, CRP for signs of inflammation.
        • Electrolyes, Urea, Creatinine abnormalities.
        • Liver enzymes.
        • Albumin → marker for nutrition.
        • INR.
      • Radiology can also play a role in diagnosing/managing IBD:
        • X Rays can be used in emergencies to detect perforation or obstruction.
        • Small Bowel follow through can detect mid bowel disease.
          • Like an Upper GI Series (Barium swallow), except you follow it further.
        • U/S can detect abscesses or ileal thickening.
        • CT scan can detect most complications.
      • Colonoscopy:
        • 90% of the colon can be reached.
        • Biopsies to rule out malignancy and determine severity should be performed.

      Managing CD

      • Nutrition:
        • There are NO diets that have been shown to induce remission or decrease risk of relapse.
        • In CD avoid foods that could cause a partial obstruction (ie uncooked veggies, salads, skins).
        • In UC avoid foods that worsen diarrhea (such as excessive dairy, caffeine).
      • Treatment → dependent on severity/location of disease:
        • Mild ileitis and right sided colitis.
          • Budesonide
            • Glucocorticoid with a significant first pass effect from the liver.
            • Almost as effective as prednisone, but with far fewer side effects.
          • Oral 5-ASA
            • An ASA analogue which must be enterically coated (or given by suppository) to be effective.
            • This is very safe but only works in a smaller portion of patients.
        • Moderate to severe disease.
          • Prednisone on a tapering regimen
            • Mainstay therapy for a flare.
            • Very effective, but has side effects
              • Cushingoid (like what you see with Cushings → bloated face, etc.), weight gain, acne, depression, insomnia, osteoporosis just to name a few.
          • Immunomodulators (azathioprine)
          • Biologics (infliximab)
            • Remicade is the trade name for Infliximab.
      • Antibiotics may play a role by helping to control the intestinal flora.
        • Only really used for infectious complications.
        • Commonly used antibiotics are Ciprofloxacin and Metronidazole.
        • These also help to treat abscesses and peri-anal disease.

      Immunomodulators

      • These have several functions including suppressing resistance to biologics, helping to treat steroid resistant disease, and inducing/maintain remission.
      • Typically used when a patient is steroid-dependent → symptoms are controlled with a high dosage of steroids, but they flare up when we try to taper the dosage down.
        • At this point, we would use Azothioprine (Imuran), or Methotrexate, to try and taper the patient off of prednisone (since there are so many side effects).
      • The main options are:
        • Azathioprine → Effective but has a 3% chance of causing pancreatitis.
        • Methorextate → Again effective but can cause liver/lung fibrosis (recall from Interstitial Lung Diseases), and is teratogenic.
      • Also, since these suppress the immune system, risks include:
        1. Bone marrow suppression.
        2. Infection.

      Biological agents

      • These are new drugs (hence, $$$) that have revolutionized the treatment of Crohn’s Disease.
      • They target cytokines involved in the inflammatory response.
        • Most target TNF (infliximab, adalimumab) or Adhesion molecules.
      • Infliximab (Remicade) is the archetypical example.
        • Probably our most effective treatment for fistulas.
        • However, has a risk of reactivating TB if the patient has a latent infection.
        • More effective when used with an immunomodulator (Azothioprine, Methotrexate) → prevents an immune response to the biologic.
      • Adalimumab (Humira) is another example.
        • Considered a little less immunogenic than Remicade, so it doesn’t necessarily need to be coupled with an immunomodulator.

      Remember

      • If a patient with Crohn’s presents with a fistula, then Remicade (Infliximab) is our first line treatment.
      • Works better when coupled with an immunomodulator (Azothioprine, Methotrexate).

      Surgery

      • 50% of patients with Crohn’s will require surgery in 10 years.
      • Will be considered for major complications or failure to respond to medical treatment.
      • Its not curative, so you can remove the diseased bowel, but it may recur in other locations.
      • So there is an aim to preserve as much of the bowel as possible

      Ulcerative Colitis

      • Recall that a major difference between UC and CD is the distribution.
        • UC doesn’t have skip lesions like Crohn’s → it’s continuous!
        • UC starts from the ‘end’ → from the rectum to the left colon and can continue proximally along the bowel. The farther reaching the colitis the more severe it tends to be.
      • Pseudopolyps → ‘islands’ of healed mucosa along an ‘ocean’ of inflammatory damage, form these finger-like projections.
        • These can be seen to the right.

      Here you see resected bowel showing ulcerative colitis. Notice the loss of the typical intestinal folds, and how the affected bowel is continuous. Also notice how the inflammation worsens as you go further along the bowel.

      • Its important to be aware of major complications of UC that can arise that have not been previously mentioned: - Hemorrhage → because the mucosa is susceptible to bleeding.
        • Toxic megacolon ± perforation.
          • Muscosal inflammation now spreads through the muscularis.
          • Precipitated by constipating medications (Loperamide, opiates).
        • Dysplasia and Cancer.

      Clinical Features of UC

      • Hematochezia (more common in UC than CD)
        • Bright red rectal bleeding.
        • Pretty much always seen with UC.
      • Diarrhea.
        • Also extremely common.
        • Hence, bloody diarrhea is archetypical of UC.
      • Abdominal pain.
        • Cramping with bowel movement.
        • Sharp localized pain is suggestive of inflammation.
      • Dehydration, malnutrition, fatigue.
        • Due to chronic bleeding.
      • In general severity of clinical features correlates well with disease severity.

      • Acutely it can mimic an infection, but when it persists chronically, a diagnosis can be made.

      • Severe colitis:
        • Severe bloody diarrhea (gt;10 movements per day).
        • Tachycardia.
        • Fever.
        • Low albumin.
        • Anemia.

      Prognosis of UC

      • 80% of patients will have intermittent flares.
      • 10-15% will have a chronic continuous course.
      • 5-10% will present acutely requiring emergent surgery.

      Investigations for UC

      • If acute rule out infection with Stool Culture and Sensitivity, Ova and Parasites, and C diff toxin tests.
      • Sigmoidoscopy and biopsy can be used for diagnostic purposes, but a colonscopy will determine the extent of colon involved.
        • With severe colitis, you don’t want to do a colonoscopy, for fear of causing a perforation.
        • Hence why a sigmoidoscopy is done for diagnosis.

      Management of UC

      • Medical Management includes:
      • For mild/moderate disease: Mesalamine was discovered years ago when it was found that patients on Sulfasalazine (used for Rheumatoid Arthritis) would have their diarrhea resolved. It turned out that it was actually the Mesalamine component in the drug that was doing this!
        1. Mesalamine: (anti-inflammatory, kinda like a topical inflammatory to the bowel)
          • This is the key difference in the treatment between CD and UC.
          • Sulfasalazine:
            • Mesalamine with a Sulfa drug.
            • Increases side effects due to Sulfa drug. The colonic bacteria will metabolic the bond.releasing the mesalamine.
          • 5-ASA medications:
            • Asacol, Salofalk, Pentasa, Mezavant.
            • These are all pure Mesalamine, without the Sulfa drug seen with Sulfasalamine. So better tolerated.
            • Effective for induction of remission in mild disease.

      Remember

      • Mesalamine (especially 5-ASA drugs) is the key difference in the treatment between UC and CD.
        • With mild/moderate Crohn’s Disease, we use budesonide.
        • With mild/moderate Ulcerative Colitis, we use mesalamine (5-ASAs).
      • For Moderate-Severe UC → similar drugs as with Crohn’s.
        • Steroids:
        • Azathioprine:
          • For steroid dependent disease, helps to induce remission
          • Not much evidence for Methotrexate, unlike CD.
        • Infliximab:
          • For severe colitis not responding to steroids.
          • Adalimumab (Humira) is less effective.
      • Indications for Surgery include:
        • Severe colitis not responding to medical treatment.
        • Complications due to an acute attack (perforation, toxic megacolon).
        • Chronic continuous disease with impaired quaity of life.
        • Dysplasia or carcinoma.
          • Note that, unlike Crohn’s, you take out the entire colon with surgery, as diseased bowel is difficult to anastomose and there is a high recurrence risk.
      • The surgery performed depends on conditions
        • Emergency: Subtotal colectomy with ileostomy
        • Planned: Proctocolectomy with ileostomy, or with pouch formation
