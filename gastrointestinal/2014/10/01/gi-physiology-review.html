      GI Physiology Review

      Kevan Lu Wednesday, October 01, 2014

      Case #1

      63 year old driver, from Albania.

      • Most older people from Albania are H. pylori positive. How does HP increase gastric acid secretion?
        1. Gastrin from G-cells stimulates acid secretion.
        2. Somatostatin from D-cells inhibits gastrin secretion.
        3. HP inhibits somatostatin release.
        4. This stimulates gastrin production, increaing acid production from the parietal cells.
        5. Over time, excess acid contributes to ulcers.
      • What stimulates acid secretion in the stomach?
        1. Histamine → paracrine control.
        2. Vagus Nerve → neural control.
        3. Gastrin → endocrine control.
      • What inhibits acid secretion in the stomach?
        1. Somatostatin → by inhibiting gastrin.
      • What’s the treatment for H. pylori?
        1. Amoxicillin.
        2. Clarithromycin.
        3. PPI.

      ####Additional Information - The patient was a little better, but still had pain. - Was on twice daily PPI when an endoscopy was performed. - Reflux esophagitis was still seen.

      • Why does he still have reflux esophagitis, even with double-dose PPIs?
        • Years of reflux has caused damage to his LES.
        • This causes reflux of corrosive liquids into the esophagus (acid, bile).

      ####Additional Information - To track his recovery, they measured his serum gastrin. - This is a good measure of acid production, since gastrin is primarily responsible for acid production. - Found a serum gastrin of 1190 (normal gastrin < 100).

      • What are 2 reasons why the gastrin can be so high?
        1. Excess gastrin → perhaps due to a tumour producing gastrin.
        2. PPIs → PPIs reduce gastrin secretion, causing the pH rises, feedback cycle, long term PPI actually increases gastrin.

      GASTRIN hormonal contril of gastric acid secretion. Acts on parietal cells and causes ECL cells to release histamine. Trophic to the acid producing gastric mucosa. Released by G-cells in antrum in response to amino acids and polypeptids, by vagal acitvity, and by stomach distension. Gastrin release is inhibited by acid (pH < 3) and somatostatin.

      Had Zollinger Ellison syndrome. Very rare syndrome. Highly functioning endocrine tumour that secrets gastrin, usually very small. Essentially raw G-cells. duodenum, liver, pancreas. → ectopic location (typically in the stomach). These have improper feedback mechanism.s Severe ulceration, resistant to usual doses of meds. Often has diarrhea. - Acid is caustic, so irritates the bowels, leading to increased secretions. - This increases diarrhea. Benign-malignant borderline.

      TX. 1. Keep increasing dose of PPIs, enough to get pH above 4. 2. Find the tumour. - U/S, CT, MRI, normal. - But what natural substance could you label with a radioactive substance that could ‘light up’ the tumour? - Based on acid secretion. - You’re looking for G-cells, secreting acid. - Recall that they have a SOMATOSTATIN receptor, so if you radio-label stomatostatin, you’ll be able to find it! Octreotide (somatostatin) scan. - Showed an extra speck in the liver. - 1st CT scan = normal. - Then octreotide scan positive. - Then 2nd CT scan based on octreotride, found tumour. - Tumour was still growing, serum gastrin kept going up.

      How can we get rid of the tumour? - Right in the centre of the liver, so it’s sort of hard to remove. - In this case, it’s basically impossible to resect (in both the right and left hepatic lobe), so your only other option would be a transplant. - Surgery, BIG TIME. - Local destruction. - Embolization. - Chemotherapy -> chemoembolization (clot off the artery).

      AST and ALT are signs of hepatocellular damage ALK phosphatase and gamma someshit is cholestatic.

      What should Craig be eating and why?

      • Electrolytes.
        • Water
        • NaCl
        • Calcium, magnesium -> absorbed proximally in the small bowel.
          • Bile flow, pancreatic flow, lots of Ca and Mg there.
          • He’s going to be actually losing Ca and Mg, so you gotta manage this.
        • Others
      • Carbohydrates.
        • Complex carbs best, since slowly broken down over time.
      • Protein
        • Variable, takes longer than carbohydrates to absorb.
        • So he may have protein loss because he’ll be unable to absorb.
      • Fat
        • No terminal ileum.
        • Won’t be able to reabsorb bile salts.
        • So won’t be able to form mixed micelles.
        • So he won’t be able to absorb any fat!
        • But he NEEDS fat → essential fatty acids!
        • His poop is going to be fatty as hell!
      • Vitamins
        • Water soluble
          • Folic acid in duodenum, so fine.
          • B12 won’t be absorbed, need to inject.
        • Fat soluble
          • ADEK. Big problem, won’t absorb them.
      • How many meals per day?
        • Frequent small meals (grazing) to maximize absorption.
        • high salt, ihigh fluid, high calcium, high Mg, high complex carbs, low lactose, high protein, low fat.
        • With supplements of esential fatty acids, calories, vitamins ADEK, and B12.
