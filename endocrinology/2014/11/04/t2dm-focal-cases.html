          T2DM Focal Cases

          Kevan Lu Tuesday, November 04, 2014

          Case #1

          Case Summary

          You are asked to see a 21-year-old woman with a history of oligomenorrhea (5 cycles in the past year), acne and excess facial hair. Her parents were not born in Canada. Her menses started at age 10 and came monthly until she left high school and went to UWO. She has had recurrent yeast infections and is up at night with nocturia more than once. On examination she has a height of 1.5 m, weight 90 kg; BP 135/85; mild acanthosis and a few skin tags on the neck. She and her partner want to conceive a pregnancy in the next year. She has not had a period in 4 months. You send her for a 75 gram OGTT and the results are:

          • Fasting sugar: 6.1 mM
          • 2 hour sugar: 11.2 mM
          1. What things in the story above are consistent with insulin resistance?
            • Obesity, skin tags, acanthosis, skin tags, high blood pressure.
          2. Suggest a few reasons that her periods became irregular after leaving high school?
            • Stress can cause amenorrhea.
            • She might be pregnant! Always do a βHCG.
            • Causes of hypothalamic hypogonadism:
              • Sudden discontinuation of OCP → it takes a while for the pituitary to start up again.
          3. What is her BMI and what is her ideal body weight (IBW)?
            • BMI = 40kg, IBW ~= 55kg.
          4. What is IBW in this context?
            • Anything where you don’t have metabolic complications (high BP, diabetes).
          5. What are the criteria for diabetes?
            1. HbA1C > 6.5%.
              • Things that affect hemoglobin such as anemia will under-represent this.
              • Hence you should do a CBC with this as well.
            2. Fasting glucose > 7%.
            3. Random blood glucose of > 11.1% with SYMPTOMS:
              • Polyuria, polydipsia, weight loss.
            4. 2 hour 75 g Oral Glucose Tolerance Test > 11.1%
              • Must confirm the test, often by doing an HbA1C.
          6. Does she have diabetes?
            • Yes → she’s symptomatic and has 1 positive test.
            • If she wasn’t symptomatic, you’d need 2 tests on 2 separate days.
          7. What other tests would you like to do?
            • β-HCG for pregnancy.
            • LH, FSH, Estradiol → look for a primary hypogonadism or secondary hypogonadism.
            • Urine Albumin:Creatinine Ratio → look for proteinuria.
            • HbA1C → look for long-term severity, as well as a corrobating test.
            • Testosterone → patient has hirsutism.
            • Prolactin → recall that Prolactinomas result in amenorrhea in women.
            • TSH → hypo- or hyper-thyroidism can affect menstrual cycles.
          8. What ethnic groups are more prone to diabetes?
            • Southeast Asians, Middle-easterns, First Nations.
          9. What pre-conceptual advice would you give her that would improve her chances of a good pregnancy outcome?
            • Get her diabetes under control → aim for a HbA1C < 7.
            • Lose weight → improves fertility.
          10. What components of a typical UWO students’ lifestyle may have predisposed her to her present personal biochemical difficulties?
            • Bad diet, sedentary lifestyle.
            • Excessive alcohol intake.

          Case #2

          Case Summary

          A family doctor calls you at 4:30 Friday afternoon before your 3-day weekend off. She has a 55-year-old man with a random BG of 55 mM. The patient has thirst, blurred vision, and polyuria every 1/2 hour. She asks for some advice on what to do (while secretly wanting you to see him at 4:45 pm).

          You ask her for the serum creatinine and bicarbonate level and these are normal.

          1. Why did you ask for a Cr and bicarbonate?
            • You’re worried about DKA → would result in a metabolic acidosis, resulting in a low bicarbonate.
            • You would also look for electrolytes.
            • Would also look for ketones, although recall that this may be negative early in DKA.
          2. What type of diabetes is most likely in this man? What is the most common form of diabetes?
            • Most common cause of diabetes → T2DM.
            • This is probably T2DM since this is an older patient.
            • DKA is less likely in T1DM, but it still happens.
          3. What diet advice would you give for the immediate future of the weekend?
            • The patient needs (1) hydration, (2) less glucose.
            • This patient should probably go to the ER.
            • In the case of patient without DKA, their treatment would be IV fluids. Insulin usually isn’t used.
          4. What diabetes medication would you recommend and give an advantage and disadvantage of each:
            • Metformin → usually first line treatment.
              • Suppresses glucose production by the liver.
              • Advantages → no risk of hypoglycemia, no weight gain.
              • Disadvantages → GI side effects (diarrhea, nausea, bloating). To improve tolerance, titrate the dosage up. Also a rare risk of lactic acidosis (especially with renal failure).
              • Usually continued even if patients go on other medications, or even insulin, since it’s generally well tolerated, and pretty effective.
            • Sulfonureas → eg. Glaburide, Diamicron (Gliclazide).
              • Advantages → can be used on top of metformin; functions as a Secretagogue.
              • Disadvantages → can cause hypoglycemia, weight gain (always a side-effect related to insulin).
              • Secretagogue → causes your β-cells to secrete more insulin.
            • Thiazolidinediones (TZDs) → the glitazones.
              • Used less frequently nowadays, don’t worry too much about this.
              • Disadvantages → causes water retention, so not for use in patients with CHF.
            • Dipeptidyl Peptidase-4 (DPP4) Inhibitors → the Gliptins (eg. Januvia).
              • Prevent metabolic breakdown of GLP1, so it increases body levels of GLP1
              • Advantages → generally well tolerated, weight neutral, covered by the government.
              • Disadvantages → A1C lowering effect is less strong.
            • GLP1 Agonists
              • GLP1 increases insulin sensitivity, increases insulin secretion, and decreases glucagon secretion.
              • Advantages → causes weight loss.
              • Disadvantages → nausea, not covered by the government.
              • Good choice for patients who don’t want to go on insulin, and have a drug plan.
            • Sodium-glucose Co-transporter 2 (SGLT2) Inhibitors → Invokana.
              • New, very effective, and trials for T1DM are even underway now.
              • Advantages → lets you pee out sugar, so there’s low risk of hypoglycemia on its own. Also causes you to lose weight.
              • Disadvantages → urogenital infections (1/20), slightly increased urination.
            • Acarbose → alpha glucosidase inhibitor.
              • Rarely used.
              • Advantages → functions by preventing people from absorbing their carbs.
              • Disadvantages → GI upset.
            • Insulin
              • Advantages → most powerful and effective.
              • Disadvantages → high learning curve, tedious, risk of hypoglycemia, causes weight gain.
          5. What role could the dietitian play in caring for this man? When would you have him see her?
            • Improving her diet → duh.
            • Should probably see the patient as soon as possible.
            • If patients go on insulin, dietitians can help them with carbohydrate counting.
          6. What role could the diabetes nurse educator have in caring for this man? When would you have him see her?
            • Counseling patients about hypoglycemia, and how to deal with it.
            • If patients end up on insulin, they could help teach them how to use it.
            • Telling them about glucose monitoring before driving → over 5 to drive.

          Case #3

          Case Summary

          You are asked to see 45-year-old A. Davis, newly diagnosed with Type 2 diabetes. He wants to discuss how he can prevent diabetes complications. You go on line in the back room and review these things so that you will look “uptodate” and then sit down to speak with him.

          1. What things have been shown to prevent the following? (primary prevention)
            1. Kidney failure → 3 big ones.
              1. ACEIs/ARBs.
              2. Glucose control.
              3. Lowered blood pressure → < 130/80.
            2. Heart disease and stroke:
              • Exercise.
              • LDLs → < 2.
              • Lowered blood pressure.
              • Smoking cessation.
            3. Amputation:
              • Good foot care → especially in patients with peripheral vascular disease.
              • Glucose Control → to help prevent neuropathy.
              • Smoking cessation → can cause peripheral vascular disease.
            4. Retinopathy:
              • Glucose control is the big one.
              • Lowered blood pressure as always.
              • Diebetic eye exams once a year.
              • TL;DR → check your blood pressure, check your glucose, stop smoking, take care of your feet/eyes.

          Additional Information

          He then asks about his identical twin brother. His brother is overweight and drives long haul trucks.

          1. What is the risk of this brother developing diabetes?
            • His brother is at higher risk → sedentary lifestyle, and he has a family member with diabetes (T2DM is more strongly genetically linked than T1DM).
          2. *How can his brother prevent diabetes? Indicate how effective these prevention modalities are. *
            • Exercise and weight loss → probably the best choice.
            • Metformin. 850 BID → 30% decreased risk.
            • Avandia (TZE, Rosiglitazone) → 60% decreased risk.
            • Acarbose → 20-30% decreased risk.
          3. What is metabolically toxic about the long distance trucker lifestyle?
            • Sedentary lifestyle.
            • Poor diet.

          Case #4

          Case Summary

          You end up seeing your patient A. Davis’ twin truck-driving brother B. Davis, who has also been diagnosed with T2DM. He currently has polyuria every 3 hours, making driving long distances very difficult. You have already started him on two oral agents, metformin and gliclazide (a sulfonylurea), but his sugars have been climbing despite better diet, weight loss and exercise. His BMI is 40.

          His glucose levels over the past week are:

            Breakfast Lunch Supper Bedtime
          Monday 9.8 6.8 5.1 8.0
          Tuesday 8.5 5.6 7.9 5.2
          Wednesday 11 9.1 4.6 5.6
          Thursday 10.1 4.4 6.6 6.3

          You order a random blood glucose and an HbA1c:

          • Random BG = 10 mM.
          • HbA1c = 8.5%

          He comes to see you 4 weeks later, with the following values:

            Breakfast Lunch Supper Bedtime
          Monday 16 15 12 22
          Tuesday 12 17 16 19
          Wednesday 10 19 9.9 18
          Thursday 13 14 15 14
          1. Why are his sugars especially high in the mornings?
            • Dawn Phenomeon → cortisol levels are higher in the morning, resulting in increased serum glucose.
            • The Liver also produces glucose overnight.
          2. List considerations for initiating insulin.
            • Failure on other medications → usually when you’re on 3 oral agents, you begin to consider going on insulin.
            • Very high HbA1C → guidelines say that HbA1C > 8.5% upon diagnosis suggests the use of multiple oral agents, or insulin.
            • Symptomatic → polyuria, polydipsia, weight loss.
          3. What medication regime would you start him on and why?
            • Keep him on Metformin → since it’s an insulin sensitizer, it reduces the amount of insulin required.
            • DPP-4 inhibitors are usually used in conjunction with Metformin (Januvia).
              • There are even combination meds with both Metformin and Januvia → Janumet.
            • Take the patient off the Sulfonylurea → the patient is a truck driver, so you worry about hypoglycemia, especially if you’re starting insulin. You can’t be on insulin if you are in the US → so you can’t give him insulin if he drives to the US.
            • Give the patient insulin:
              • Long-acting basal insulin at bedtime (Lantus, Levamir).
              • Rapid-acting insulin with each meal (eg. Novorapid).

          Case #5

          You are asked to see a 24-year-old woman who has had Type 1 diabetes since she was 8 years old. Her BG control was not great until a couple of years ago when she started QID insulin and started to carbohydrate count. Now sugars run 4-7 most of the time before meals and her A1C is 6.5%.

          She is now pleased to be 6 weeks pregnant. However morning sickness is taking a bit of the excitement off the pregnancy so far.

          1. What do you predict will happen to her insulin needs during the first trimester?
            • Insulin requirements usually decrease somewhat in the first trimester.
            • They increase in the second and third trimester because the placenta increases insulin resistance.
          2. What would you do in the office to check that she is healthy for the pregnancy?
            • Regular BP checkups → diabetics are at higher risk for preeclampsia.
          3. What lab tests would you order?
            • Urine Microalbumin:creatinine ratio.
            • Serum Creatinine.
            • TSH → higher risk for hyper-/hypo-thyroidism in diabetics.
          4. What specialists would you involve and when?
            • Ophthamologist to have eyes checked in the first semester.
            • Refer to nephrology if issues with urine protein or if creatinine changed.
          5. She asks you if the child will be born with diabetes. (a) What is your answer? (b) What is the lifelong risk of Type 1 DM developing in the child?
            • NO. Not as strongly genetically linked as T2DM.
            • The lifelong risk is not very high. 3%.

          Additional Information

          She comes back for her 30 week of gestation visit. A1C is still good at 5.8%. She is not feeling her low blood sugar symptoms as well now.

          1. (a) What are the normal symptoms of low blood sugar? (b) Why has this change occurred now?
            • Normal Symptoms:
              • Sweating, tremors, dizziness.
              • Neuroglycopenic symptoms → confusion, coma.
            • Why does this happen?
              • Seen in patients with tighter control → they tend to feel their lows less.
              • Increased hypoglycemic episodes are a worrisome but unavoidable side-effect of tight glucose control.
              • You should counsel patients, since hypoglycemia unawareness is dangerous, especially when driving.
          2. *What do you expect might happen to her insulin needs in the first few days after delivery? *
            • It should drop suddenly after delivery.
            • This is because the placenta causes a lot insulin resistance.
            • This is also why patients often have gestational diabetes.

          Case #6

          Case Summary

          You are seeing a 35-year-old man, self-employed. He has significant hypertension and hyperlipidemia despite running the Boston Marathon and having a BMI of 21. He cannot afford the medications for his conditions. You have some medications in the clinic “drug cupboard” and consider giving him some.

          1. You hand him 2 months of the antihypertensive Norvasc and the lipid-lowering drug Lipitor. What should you record in the chart?
            • You gave him a sample.
            • Make sure they’re not outdated. Discuss possible side-effects.
          2. He calls in 90 days and requests some more medication. You care not in the clinic that day. Can you have the receptionist give him the pills?
            • No.
          3. The same drug company who makes these free drugs asks you to give a talk to a Family Practice seminar about lipid-lowering agents. They will pay you for your time. The company provides a powerpoint slide kit for this talk, and a pharmaceutical representative will drive you to the talk. What ethical issues should come up for you about this situation?
            • Ha.

          Quickie Questions

          1. Name one mechanism whereby weight loss might prevent diabetes
            • Less visceral fat, less FFA flooding liver, less insulin resistance.
            • Less food intake → less strain on pancreas.
          2. Name one mechanism whereby activity might prevent diabetes
            • Exercise is an insulin sensitizer.
            • And you can lose weight, leading back to the benefits described above.
          3. People with Type 2 DM often experience the need for more and more medications to keep their glucose values under control. Why?
            • T2DM is chronic and progressive (progressive β-cell failure).
            • Many diabetes medications also lead to weight-gain, which can cause a vicious cycle.
          4. People without DM who run the Boston Marathon are really insulin sensitive. Why don’t they pass out from hypoglycemia?
            • They produce counter-regulatory hormones like glucagon to keep your sugars up.
            • So patients with a total pancreatectomy will be both diabetic and have a high risk of hypoglycemia. They also can’t get DKA because they won’t have an increase in counter-regulatory hormones since they can’t produce them!
