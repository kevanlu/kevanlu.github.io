        Pregnancy & Diabetes

        Tehmina Ahmad Tuesday, October 28, 2014

        Lecture Objectives

        1. Identify maternal and fetal risks of having Types 1 and 2 diabetes as well as gestational diabetes in pregnancy
        2. List the expected changes in insulin needs that occur during pregnancy
        3. List 2 treatments that have lowered the risk of adverse fetal outcomes in pregnancy

        Pregnancy and Diabetes stats now?

        • *Risk of baby congenital malformations 1.7-9.4% *
        • Risk of baby death: 0.6-4.8%
        • Risk of maternal death: low
        • Risk of maternal morbidity: moderately low
        • 1-2% of pregnancies involve pre-existing diabetes (Types 1 or 2)
        • 2-3% of pregnancies involve GDM (Gestational Diabetes)

        Congenital Malformations seen in Diabetes Types 1 & 2

        • Neuro: anencephaly; neural tube defects
        • CV: transposition of the great vessels; coarct of aorta; ASD, pulmonary stenosis
        • GI: duodenal atresia, imperforate anus (exam alert!) , anorectal atresia, situs inversus
        • GU: duplex ureters, renal agensis, hydronephrosis
        • Skeletal: caudal equinal regression

        What has worked to lower the risk for fetal anomalies in Type 1 and 2 Diabetes

        • Entering pregnancy with great Blood Glucose (BG) control (A1C normal at 7% or at least 10% or less)
        • Keeping BG controlled in pregnancy
        • Good OB care

        Other baby morbidities that have been associated in pregnancy with pre-exisiting diabetes

        1. C-Section rate: 32-45%
        2. Prematurity: 15% before 34 weeks
        3. Fetal distress: 9-28%
        4. Macrosomia (weight over 4kg): 9-28%
        5. Intra Uterine Growth Restriction (IUGR): 2-8%
        6. Low calcium, high RBC, high bilirubin, low glucose etc.

        Changing Insulin Needs during Pregnancy

        • 1st trimester: insulin needs may fall, so more risk for hypoglycemia
        • 2nd trimester: insulin needs rise slowly
        • 3rd trimester: insulin needs go up more
        • Immediately post-partum (after birth): insulin needs decrease dramatically (exam alert!)

        Maternal Risks of Pregnancy when Type 1 or 2

        • Pre-eclampsia: women with Types 1 or 2 diabetes have twice the risk of pre-eclampsia than in normal pregnancy (10% vs 5%)
        • Retinopathy: increased risk of worsening eye disease
        • Nephropathy: increased risk of worsening kidney function (proteinuria may increase)
        • Cardiovascular: increased risk for angina and also small for dates baby if maternal atherosclerosis is present. Poor placental blood supply due to atherosclerosis will lead to poor fetal nutrition.
        • NB: Usually retinopathy settles and doesn’t progress to permanent worsening. Usually nephropathy regresses after delivery.

        Gestational Diabetes (GDM)

        • GDM is diabetes discovered during pregnancy; Insulin resistance develops in 2nd and 3rd trimester
        • Can encompass a new onset Type 1 or 2 DM
        • Most commonly however, GDM is a mild, transient glucose intolerance that occurs late in pregnancy and disappears after delivery
        • Causes: increased prolactin, leptin, cortisol, adiponectin (much of this is due to the placenta and adipose tissue) (exam alert!)
        • Normally insulin output from the pancreas rises in 2nd and 3rd trimester GDM occurs when pancreatic insulin output is inadequate
        • Women entering pregnancy already overweight will be at more risk for GDM
        • Since GDM usually occurs in 2nd or 3rd trimester, infants of these pregnancies do not have congenital malformations. Recall that organ development occurs in the 1st trimester (exam alert!)

        So What are GDM Pregnancies at Risk for?

        • Macrosomia
        • C-Section
        • Stillbirth
        • Hypoglycemia and hypocalcemia

        Diagnosis of GDM

        • At weeks 24-26 of pregnancy
        • Pregnant woman is given 50 gr glucose to drink at any time of day
        • One hour later a BG is drawn and sent to lab
        • The normal result: < 7.8 mM
        • If screening test is abnormal, then 75 gr glucose test is done after a 12 hour fast and after consuming a diet high in carbohydrate for a few days.
        • 75 gr gluose test goals: Fasting glucose: < 5.3 mM 1 hour glucose: < 10.6 mM 2 hour glucose: < 9.0 mM if 1 value is abnormal, then GDM is formally diagnosed

        Glucose targets for women with GDM

        Before Breakfast (ac): < 5.3 mM 1 hour After Meals (pc): < 7.8 mM

        GDM Treatment

        • Frequent small meals with up to 60% of diet being carbohydrate
        • Avoid starvation
        • Exercise is usually safe
        • Test BG levels
        • Insulin if BG values go over targets
        • Insulin is very safe during pregnancy and rarely crosses the placenta!
        • Currently, Glyburide & Metformin are not recommended (though they are cautiously used in GDM.)

        Treatment outcomes for GDM Mothers

        • Acute outcomes are fine—the mother’s will not feel much different with treatment (this is since the blood sugars in “normal” GDM are not dreadfully high
        • But life-time risk of developing DM is as high as 80%
        • Those most at risk for DM are: heavier, developed GDM earlier in pregnancy, used insulin

        Hyperglycemia and Pregnancy Outcomes (HAPO) STUDY & Australian Carbohydrate Intolerance Study in Pregnant Women (ACHOIS) Trial

        • Purpose: to determine BG threshold(s) for adverse outcomes in GDM
        • Results:
          1. Screening for GDM is worthwhile
          2. Maternal glucose is a continuous risk for fetal macrosomia, so there are consensus decisions still coming that may alter the defining BG levels for GDM No threshold level was determined.
          3. Treating GDM does not harm the mother and results in fewer delivery and neonatal complications.

        GDM Offspring Risk (the big worry)

        • Imprinting: Exposure in the womb to abnormal glucose environment increases risk of:
          1. childhood obesity
          2. hypertension
          3. impaired glucose tolerance

        Some Things Women Ask About…

        1. Women don’t become dependent or “addicted” to insulin just because they have to use insulin during pregnancy
        2. Infants of mothers with any type of DM in pregnancy will not automatically be born with diabetes
        3. Risk of diabetes later in life follows the genetic predisposition of their parents (with the one extra worry we discussed about the uterine environment being potentially toxic and increasing the lifelong diabetes risk) <hr style="border:none; height:15px">
