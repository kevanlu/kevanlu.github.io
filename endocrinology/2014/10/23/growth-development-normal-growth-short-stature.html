        Growth & Development – Normal Growth & Short Stature

        Bill Huang Thursday, October 23, 2014

        Objective: Describe normal growth and the use of growth charts

        Normal Growth

        • Normal growth has 3 phases: infant, childhood, and pubertal
        • Infant phase is marked by very rapid growth rate
        • During the childhood phase, growth is slower compared to other phases but lasts longer and is consistent
          • The longer the childhood phase lasts, the greater the final height
          • GH is the primary growth factor during this phase, but thyroid hormone also plays an important role
          • Average growth rate is 5-6 cm/year
        • Pubertal phase is characterized by a growth spurt due to a combination of sex steroids and increased frequency and amplitude of growth hormone pulses
        • In general, growth rate is fastest in the beginning and slows down to 5-6 cm/year between the age of 4 and the onset of puberty
        • Growth charts and growth velocities can be used to track development
          • In the first 18 months of life, children are allowed to cross growth percentiles due to genetics manifesting after an in utero environment
          • Children are also allowed to cross percentiles during puberty
        • Length should be measured instead of height for the first 2 years of life
          • Note that length is longer than height for the same person
        • Target height (aka. midparental height) for an individual is calculated by:
          • (Height of mother + height of father +/- 13 cm) / 2
            • Add 13 for boys, subtract 13 for girls
          • This is the 50th percentile height; actual results are on a bell curve
        • Height age is the age at which a child’s height is at the 50th percentile for the reference standard population for gender
        • Bone age (x-ray of left hand and wrist) can be used to determine potential for further growth

        Objective: Describe normal variants of growth Objective: Summarize the causes of short stature

        Abnormal Growth Parameters

        • Low growth velocity is having less than 4.5 cm/year from age 4 to puberty
        • Crossing percentile channels on the growth chart after age of 18 months and prior to puberty
        • Height significantly below genetic potential (>2 SD below midparental height)
        • Abnormal growth can be evaluated using:
          • Genetic potential – target height
          • Growth velocity – accurate measurements of annual growth plotted on chart
          • Growth potential – bone age

        Classification of Short Stature

        • Normal growth variants:
          • Familial
          • Constitutional
        • Abnormal growth variants:
          • Intrauterine
          • Genetic
          • Skeletal
          • Nutritional
          • Chronic systemic disease
          • Endocrine

        Familial Short Stature

        • Annual growth rate is normal
        • Height is at or below 3rd percentile
        • No evidence of systemic or endocrine disease
        • Puberty growth spurt occurs at normal age
        • Bone age equal to chronologic age
        • Family members are relatively short
        • Not pathologic; give reassurance

        Constitutional Delay of Growth and Puberty

        • Height and weight at or below 3rd percentile
        • Normal growth velocity
        • Age of puberty is delayed
        • Bone age is delayed (ie. bone age younger than chronologic age)
        • Positive family history of late bloomers – siblings, parents
        • Again, not pathologic; give reassurance

        Intrauterine

        • Events such as maternal infection, smoking, alcohol, systemic illness can affect growth
        • Fetal malnutrition
        • Placental insufficiency

        Genetic

        • Down’s syndrome marked by loss in growth
        • Prader Willi syndrome:
          • Microdeletion of chromosome 15
          • Hypotonia of muscles
          • Difficulties feeding, FTT in infancy
          • Subsequent hyperphagia and severe obesity
        • Note that syndromes have their own growth charts

        Primary Skeletal Bone Disorders

        • Skeletal dysplasias – most common are achondroplasia and hypochondroplasia
          • Disproportionate bone growth
          • Due to mutations in FGFR-3 gene
        • Osteogenesis imperfect
          • Fragile bones that fracture easily
          • May be associated with blue sclerae, easy bruising, deafness
          • Majority of cases caused by dominant mutation in type 1 collagen genes

        Nutritional

        • Inadequate intake
          • Mechanical cleft palate preventing proper feeding
          • Psychosocial deprivation (abuse, neglect)
          • Eating disorders
        • Impaired absorption – typically weight is the dominant issue, and height keeps up (resembles FTT picture)
          • Celiac disease
          • Crohn’s disease
          • Liver or pancreatic disease
          • Lactose intolerance
          • Mechanical/obstructive lesions of the bowel

        Chronic Systemic Disease

        • Any chronic disease can affect growth
        • Renal – chronic renal disease
        • GI – celiac disease
        • CNS – cerebral palsy
        • Cardiac – cyanotic congenital heart disease
        • Respiratory – cystic fibrosis
        • Malignancy – leukemia

        Endocrine

        • GH deficiency
        • Hypothyroidism – significant hypothyroidism can cause growth delay
          • Look for associated features – weight gain, puffy features, goitre, cold intolerance, constipation, dry skin, brittle hair, fatigue
        • Metabolic bone disease
        • Glucocorticoid excess
          • Cushing’s is associated with poor growth in children
          • In the pediatric population, Cushing’s is most often iatrogenic
        • (discussed more below)

        Objective: Describe the etiology, diagnosis, and treatment of a child with growth hormone deficiency

        Introduction to Growth Hormone Deficiency

        • GH deficiency is the most common endocrine cause of short stature
        • Estimated incidence at 1 in 4000 children in general population
        • May be isolated, or be associated with other pituitary hormone deficiencies
        • Occurs due to abnormalities in the growth hormone axis
        • Typical appearance on growth hormone is a hyperbolic curve – growth simply slows down and stops
        • Genetic causes:
          • Mutations of GHRH, GH-1, Hesx-1, Prop-1, Pit-1
            • Hesx-1 required for development of all AP cell types
            • Prop-1 required for development of all AP cell types except corticotropes
            • Pit-1 required for somatotropes, lactotropes, thyrotropes
          • GH receptor defects
          • IGF-1 gene deletions or mutations
          • IGF receptor defects
          • Growth plate disorders
        • Congenital causes:
          • Structural defects (ectopic or hypoplastic pituitary)
          • Usually midline and associated with multiple pituitary hormone deficiencies
        • Acquired causes (frequently, GH axis is first one disrupted; other deficiencies may follow):
        • CNS tumours
        • Cranial irradiation
        • Head injury

        Assessment of GH Status

        • Random GH levels of no value due to pulsatile release of GH
        • Serum IGF-1 is screening test
        • Provocative testing:
          • Arginine, clonidine, or glucagon to stimulate AP secretion of GH
          • Insulin hypoglycemia is used in adults (avoided in children due to risk of hypoglycemic seizures)
        • Diagnostic criteria of GH deficiency:
          • Short stature – height below 3rd percentile
          • Growth velocity <25th percentile
          • Bone age delayed by more than 2 SD below chronologic age
          • Inadequate serum GH response to 2 provocative tests (<8 ug/L)

        Clinical Characteristics of GH Deficiency

        • Birth weight and length is normal
        • Neonatal hypoglycemia and hyperbilirubinemia
        • Decreased linear growth
        • Face – poor development of nose bridge, protrusion of frontal bone
        • Teeth – irregular, delayed eruption
        • Anterior fontanelle – delayed closure
        • Thin hair, poor nail growth
        • High-pitched voice
        • Micropenis
        • Other midline defects

        Therapy of GH Deficiency

        • Biosynthetic GH
        • Administered by SC injection 6-7 times per week until growth is complete
        • Expensive – thousands of dollars annually

        Objective: Summarize the clinical features of Turner’s syndrome

        Turner’s Syndrome

        • Caused by total or partial absence of X chromosome
        • Incidence is 1 in 2000 girls
        • Short stature affects all girls with Turner’s
          • GH is insufficient, skeletal dysplasia occurs
            • Reduced amplitude and frequency of GH pulses
        • Gonadal failure (complete in 90%, no true puberty)
          • Streak ovaries
          • Pubarche occurs in all, but only 10-15% undergo thelarche and 1% have menarche
          • More than 99% of patients are infertile
        • Dysmorphic features
          • Neck webbing
          • Short metacarpals
          • Increased carrying angle
          • Nail dysplasia
        • Congenital heart disease
          • Coarctation of the aorta
        • Renal anomalies (eg. horseshoe kidney, duplex ureters)
        • Hypothyroidism (10%)
        • Normal range of IQ, but greater rate of learning difficulties and ADD
        • Treatment: Turner’s responds well to synthetic GH
        • Long-term health issues:
          • Infertility
          • Cardiovascular disease
          • Osteoporosis (related to gonadal failure)
          • Insulin resistance, T2DM

        Metabolic Bone Disease

        • Rickets: hypophosphatemic poor growth and bowing of bones (esp. legs)
          • Increasing prevalence of Vitamin D deficiency
        • Pseudohypoparathyroidism
          • Short stature
          • Obesity, round face
          • Shortening of the metacarpals
          • Developmental delay

        Evaluation of the Short Child

        • History:
          • Duration of slow growth
          • Birth history
          • Family history – parental heights, Hx of delayed puberty
          • Specific inquiry about features of GH deficiency and hypothyroidism
        • Physical exam:
          • Accurate measurement of height and weight
          • Tanner staging of pubertal development
          • Features of GH deficiency
          • Palpation of thyroid gland
          • In girls, assess for signs of Turner’s syndrome
        • Investigations:
          • CBC, ESR, electrolytes, Cr, BUN
          • Albumin, calcium, phosphate
          • TSH
          • X-ray of hands for bone age
          • In girls, karyotype to rule out Turner’s
          • Consider IGF-1 and provocative GH testing for suspected GH deficiency
          • Consider transglutamase antibodies for celiac disease
        • Most useful parameters:
          • Good growth curve
          • Parental heights
          • Bone age (growth potential)
