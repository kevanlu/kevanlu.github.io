        Physiology of Normal Puberty

        Kevan Lu Thursday, October 23, 2014

        Lecture Objectives

        • State a definition of puberty and how its “arrival” is known.
        • Describe some of the physical changes that occur during puberty.
        • Summarize the current understanding of the mechanisms by which hormonal changes associated with puberty are regulated.
        • List factors which may trigger the mechanisms by which hormonal changes associated with puberty are regulated.

        Introduction & Key Concepts

        • Dealing with the hypothalamic-pituitary-gonadal axis.
        • The hypothalamus secretes GnRH (Gonadotropin Releasing Hormone), in a pulsatile fashion.
          • This pulsatility is important, because constitutively active stimulation would lead to a decrease in the number of receptors at the cell surface.
        • GnRH acts on receptors on the pituitary, and leads to the pulsatile release of (1) FSH (Follicle Stimulating Hormone) and LH (Luteinizing Hormone).
          • FSH acts on the ovary to help the follicles develop.
          • LH drives steroidogenesis in both the ovaries and the testes.
        • Steroids feed back to the hypothalamus and pituitary to inhibit the release of GnRH, FSH, and LH.
        • This hypothalamic-pituitary-gonadal axis is fully functional during fetal development, but it becomes repressed after birth.
          • We’ll be discussing the mechanism of this supression, and how it gets relieved again during puberty.
        • FSH is subject to additional regulatory mechanisms outside of the HPG axis, namely through (1) inhibin, and (2) activin:
          • Inhibin → inhibits FSH secretion.
          • Activin → activates FSH secretion.
        • These hormones are mainly secreted by the ovary, and gives the ovary some control over GnRH levels (by modulating FSH levels, the ovary can modulate negative feedback to GnRH).

        Key Features of Puberty

        • Puberty → behavioural, physiologic and anatomic changes a child experiences as the gonads change from the juvenile to adult condition.
          • In girls, menarche (the first menstruation) provides a clear sign that this change is underway.
            • The ovary secretes sufficient estrogen and progesterone to permit endometrial proliferation and then shedding.
            • Many of the first menstrual cycles are irregular and anovular, and are not reflective of full reproductive capacity.
          • In boys, there’s no obvious counterpart → the first ejaculation is more difficult to date precisely.
            • First ejaculations may also be azoospermic, so they may not indicate reproductive capacity.
            • Spermarche (the first appearance of sperm in ejaculates/urine) would be a better measure of this.

        Growth Rates

        • Highest rates of growth are seen in the first year of life.

        • The next highest rate of growth is during the adolescent growth spurt.

        • The peak height velocity and total height gained during puberty, between boys and girls is not very different.
        • In that case, why are boys generally taller than girls?
          • The biggest reason for this is because boys are generally older and taller when the onset of puberty begins.

        Male vs. Female Differences

        • Males → broadening of the shoulders.
        • Females → an analogous broadening of the pelvis occurs.
        • Pre-puberty, the lean body mass and percentage of body fat between males and females are virtually identical.
          • However, adult men have 1.5 times the lean body mass of women; the skeletal mass of men is 1.5 times that of women.
          • Women also have about twice as much body fat as men.
        • These changes in body composition can begin to occur as early as 6 in females, and 9 in males; these dates do not necessarily coincide with menarche or spermarche respectively.
        • Why is this important?
          • Historically, there’s a lot in the literature about reaching a critical metabolic mass → this is hypothesizes as when a child is able to support the changes associated with puberty.
          • This is something that’s still an area of active research.

        Adrenarche vs Gonadarche

        • Adrenarche → initiation of an increase in androgen secretion from the adrenals.
        • Gonadarche → initiation of hormone secretion from the gonads.
        • The earliest signs of puberty in males and females (development of pubic and axillary hair), are actually secondary to adrenarche.
        • The mechanisms underlying adrenarche and the real importance of this phenomenon are not known.
        • Adrenarche seems to be a wholly independent process from gonadarche (although they do often overlap in time):
          • Adrenarche even occurs in agonadal individuals.
          • Premature adrenarche is not associated with premature gonadarche.
          • Children with absent adrenarche (as a result of primary adrenal insufficency) experience normal gonadarche.
        • Hence, the development of pubic or axillary hair does not necessarily suggest that puberty is proceeding normally, or that gonadarche is iminent.

        Chemistry and Secretion of Steroid Hormones

        • There are two important steroid products of the ovary, progesterone and estradiol.
        • Testosterone is also synthesized in the ovary, but under normal circumstances less leaves the gonad compared to the male.
        • The first few key steps of steroidogenesis are as follows:
          1. Cholesterol Import into the Mitochondria
            • Mediated by STAR → Steroidogenic Acute Regulatory Protein.
            • This protein is inserted into the mitochondrial membrane, and allows for cholesterol transport into the mitochondria for further conversion.
          2. Conversion of Cholesterol to Pregnenolone
            • P450 Side Chain Cleavage is the enzyme that converts cholesterol to pregnenolone.
            • This is the rate limiting step in steroid synthesis.
          3. Further Conversion
            • Pregnenolone then leaves the mitochondria, into the cytoplasm, where all further steroid synthesis occurs.
            • Of importance is the fact that Estrogens are derived from Androgens.

        The HPG Axis Over Time

        The above summarizes the action of the gonadotropins over time.

        1. During pregnancy, the fetus does secrete gonadotropins → HPG is active.
        2. In the 3rd trimester, this is inhibited because the mom’s steroid levels are quite high.
        3. Shortly after birth, the steroid effect is removed, and the pathway is fairly active until around 6 months of age.
        4. During the childhood years, the HPG axis is suppressed.
        5. During puberty, this repression begins to disappear, and cyclic levels of hormone begin to be released.
        6. This ends (in women), at menopause, where gonadotropin levels go through the roof.
          • Since there’s no estradiol, there’s no negative inhibition, so the GnRH levels go crazy high.

        Diurnal Changes in LH/FSH Secretion During Puberty

        • Before puberty LH secretion is fairly constant during an entire 24 hour period.
        • Early- to mid-puberty there is a nocturnal increase in the amount of LH secretion.
          • Hence the HPG axis initially turns on at night.
          • This manifests as a change in behaviour → adolescents seem to require more sleep.
        • Late puberty this increased LH and FSH secretion occurs both during the sleep period and during the waking hours.
        • This pattern continues similarly throughout adulthood.

        Neural Mechanisms of Pubertal GnRH Release

        • Studies done in primates on how GnRH Release is regulated during Puberty.
        • Hypothalamus is dominated by GABA neurotransmission during pre-puberty.
          • GABA is inhibitory, so it acts to inhibit GnRH secretion.
          • GABA is also thought to inhibit glutamatergic neurons, which are thought to be stimulatory.
          • Hence GABA not only directly inhibits GnRH secretion, but it also inhibits a stimulator of secretion.
        • As we transition through puberty, the GABAergic neurons begin to wane, resulting in an increase in glutamatergic activity.
          • We’re not sure about the exact mechanism of this → is this because the number of glutaminergic neurons increases?
          • Hence we have increased secretion of GnRH.
        • In a mature individual, the GABAergic suppression is almost completely eliminated, leaving the primary neurotransmission via glutamateric neurons.
          • Thus we have activation of complete HPG function.
        • Note → GABA is thought to be the inhibitor is females, and NPY (Neuropeptide Y) is thought to be the inhibitor in males.

        Triggers for Puberty

        • The onset of puberty has gotten earlier and earlier in developed countries.
        • What accounts for this change?
          • Changes in nutrition have been suggested, since the weight range at which the adolescent growth spurt has remained constant.
          • This suggests that there’s a ‘critical metabolic mass’ (as has been mentioned before), which then leads to the initiation of pubertal events.
          • Early occurrence of puberty is then explained by earlier attainment of this critical metabolic mass due to improvements in living conditions.
        • What supporting evidence is there?
          • Moderately obese girls experience an early menarche, and malnutrition is associated with a delayed menarche.
          • However, there is a great deal of controversy regarding this hypothesis.
          • Critically, it is extremely difficult to predict the age of menarche in an individual solely from the knowledge of weight and weight gain.

        Potential Neurological Triggers of Puberty

        • We’ve mentioned how the HPG axis is suppressed before puberty due to GABAergic inhibition, but what triggers the removal of this inhibition?
        • In the past, we’ve believed that Leptin was what triggered this.
        • The Leptin Hypothesis:
          • Leptin is produced by adipose tissue, suppresses appetite, and targets neurons in the brain.
          • It seems to be able to regulate hypothalamic activity and GnRH release.
          • However, studies have shown that Leptin is NOT the ‘magical trigger’.
          • Although Leptin levels do (1) increase as body mass increases, and (2) regulate hypothalamic activity and GnRH release, it seems that this effect is permissive, rather than essential.
        • In other words, although Leptin seems to be a role in pubertal development, it’s not absolutely necessary for inducing puberty.
        • The essential trigger seems to be Kisspeptin: Kisspeptin is named as such because it was discovered in Hershey, Pennsylvania, the home of ‘Hershey’s Kisses’.
          • This was the result of exciting new research (2005), where a hormone called Kisspeptin was discovered.
          • Research has shown that Kisspeptin directly leads to an increase in GnRH hormone, and that this increase does not occur if you add a GnRH receptor antagonist beforehand.
          • There also seems to be a correlation between the stage of puberty, and the level of KiSS-1 mRNA and GPR54 mRNA (GPR54 is the Kisspeptin receptor).
          • In fact, constitutive activation of just the GPR54 receptor in the hypothalamus leads to precocious GnRH release.
        • Kisspeptin is now the major candidate for the upstream signal GnRH release.
          • However, we still don’t know what regulates the GPR54 and Kisspeptin.
          • That’s the next step of the pathway that we’re interested in.
