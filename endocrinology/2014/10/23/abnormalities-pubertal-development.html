          Puberty

          “Puberty is the sickest joke god plays on us. So you’re just noticing members of the opposite sex: ‘Girls, girls, ooo!’ Naturally you want to look your best, and God says ‘No! You will look the worst you’ve ever looked in your life!” – Eddie Izzard (British comedian)

          Characteristics

          • Secondary sexual
          • Growth spurt (including bone density and muscle mass)
          • Psychosocial/psychosexual maturation

          Control (HPG Axis)

          GABA and Neuropeptide Y (NPY) neurons inhibit the axis, and Glutamate neurons stimulate it. Effects are mediated by estrogen and testosterone.

          Estrogen Effects Androgen Effects
          ↑ Growth rate ↑ Growth rate
          Mood swings (“mean”) Mood swings (“stupid”)
          Breast development Acne
          Vaginal discharge Oily skin/hair
          Menses Body odour
            Deeper voice
            Enlargement of penis/clitoris


          #Staging — ##Sequence | Girls | Boys | |- | Thelarche (breasts) | Testicular enlargement | Growth spurt | Penile enlargement | Pubarche (pubic hair) | Pubic/axillary hair | Menarche (menses) | Growth spurt | | Deep voice | | Muscle bulk | | Facial hair Notice that in boys, growth spurt comes after pubic hair

          Tanner Stages

          "Prepubertal" is same for boys and girls, thus puberty begins at T2. "Secondary mound", in other words, mean that the areolas get their own mounds.

          Remember that girls enter their growth spurts before pubic hair and boys enter it after pubic hair begins

          Remember >3 ml as the threshold for T2 testicular size

          Milestones

          | Girls | | Boys | | |- | Breasts T2 | 10 ± 1 yr | Testicular T2 | 11.5 ± 1.3 yr | Pubic hair | 11 ± 1 yr | Menarche | 12.7 ± 1 yr

          Note

          Axillary hair is not emphasized, but you can stage it if you want:

          1. No axillary hair
          2. Some axillary hair
          3. Full axillary hair


          #Delayed Puberty — > #####Three main causes > 1. Constitutional delay of growth > 2. Primary hypogonadism > 3. Secondary hypogonadism

          Constitutional delay of growth

          • Growth and pubertal delay
            • Height/weight below 3rd percentile
            • Bone age delay
          • Normal growth velocity
          • Positive family history

          Primary Hypogonadism - Girls

          #####Three main causes 1. Turner’s syndrome 2. Autoimmune → primary ovarian failure 3. Acquired → gondal disease, radiation, chemotherapy

          Turner’s Syndrome
          • Total (60%) or partial (40%) absence of one X chromosome (XO)
            • Partial can be mosaic, deletion, ring chromosome, or even a bit of Y chromosome
          • 1/2000 liveborn girls
          • Presentation
            • Short (143 ± 11 cm final height)
            • Primary hypogonadism (from “streak” gonads ← gonadal failure)
              • Delayed puberty
              • Thelarche 15%, pubarche 100%, menarche 1%, infertility 99%
            • Dysmorphic features
              • neck webbing, short metacarpals, increased carrying angle (arms bend outwards at elbow), nail dysplasia
            • Congenital heart disease
            • Renal anomalies (e.g. horseshoe kidney)
            • Hypothyroidism 10%

          Primary Hypogonadism - Boys

          #####Three main causes 1. Klinefelter’s syndrome 2. Congenital anorchia → testes absent at birth 3. Acquired → gondal disease, radiation, chemotherapy

          Klinefelter’s syndrome
          • One or more extra X chromosome (XXY)
            • 80% 47XXY, 20% mosaic (46XY/47XXY)
          • 1/500 liveborn boys
          • Presentation
            • Tall
            • Testicular failure (most common cause of)
              • Seminiferous tubular dysgenesis (no spermatogenesis → infertile)
              • Variable Leydig cell function (inconsistent testosterone)
                • Small testes/penis
                • Pubertal delay
                • Testicular failure
            • Learning difficulties
            • Aggressive behaviour

          Secondary Hypogonadism

          1. Bad brain things
            • Trauma, infection, radiation, tumour
            • Congenital pituitary defects
            • May cause other pituitary effects (or not)
          2. Kallmann’s syndrome
            • Presents with anosmia (no sense of smell)
            • Congenital isolated (no other pitutary effects) gonadotropin deficiency
          3. Other genetic syndromes
            • Laurence-Moon syndrome
            • Bardet–Biedl syndrome
            • Prader-Willi syndrome
          4. Functional gonadotropin deficiency
            • Chronic systemic disease (CF, leukemia, cerebral palsy, CHD, etc.)
            • Eating disorder/excessive exercise (stops periods)
          5. Hyperprolactinemia (prolactin can suppress GnRH)


          #Precocious Puberty — > #####Introduction > - Girls → onset before 8 (boys → 9) > - More common than delayed puberty > - Onset getting earlier and earlier in developed nations > - Most won’t have pathology, but still treat for social/psychological reasons > - The younger at onset, the more likely to see pathology > - Can be gonadotropin dependent or independent

          Diagnosing Gonadotropin Dependence

          • Random serum LH/FSH
            • LH > 5, FSH > 7 is dependent (since in kids you’d expect 0)
            • But gonadotropins are pulsatile (since GnRH is pulsatile), so…
          • Make sure with GnRH stimulation test
            • Inject GnRH, measure LH/FSH at half-hour intervals
            • Peak LH:FSH ratio > 1 (LH dominance)
            • Peak LH > 8

          Gonadotropin Dependent Precocious Puberty

          • Girls mostly idiopathic, boys usually pathological
          • Referral bias → easier to tell puberty onset in girls (breast buds) vs. boys (larger testicles), also as a society more anxious about girls undergoing puberty
          1. Idiopathic
            • Only abnormality is timing (of onset) or tempo (of progression)
              • Everything else normal, e.g. sequence of events normal
            • Common in girls, rare in boys (referral bias?)
            • Adult height shorter 5-6 cm
            • Psychosocial effects (bullying, etc.)
          2. Primary CNS disorders
            • Bad things in brain (tumour, trauma, radiation)
            • Congenital abnormalities (stuff affecting the pituitary not including tumours?)

          Gonadotropin Independent Precocious Puberty

          Remember, this means the precocious puberty is not due to higher FSH/LH #####Causes 1. Gonadal - Ovarian - Cysts, granulosa cell tumours - Remember granulosa (follicular) cells produce estrogen - McCune Albright syndrome - Precocious puberty, bone problems (polyostotic fibrous dysplasia), cafe-au-lait spots - Due to activating mutation of α subunit of G protein - Testicular - Leydig cell tumours - Remember Leydig cells make testosterone, Sertoli cells make sperm - Familial male precocious puberty (A.K.A. testetoxicosis) - LH receptor constitutionally stimulated → make lots of testosterone 2. Adrenal - Congenital adrenal hyperplasia (21 hydroxylase deficiency) - Refer to previous notes about CAH - Virilizing/feminizing tumours (virilizing in boys/feminizing in girls) - Cushing’s (adrenal androgens) 3. Iatrogenic - Exogenous sex hormones (most often accidental exposure to parents’ medication) - E.g. Getting at mom’s BCP - E.g. Dad’s testosterone cream, despite hand-washing, can get on his towel. If child uses this towel, could get exposure to testosterone


          #Approach to Assessing Pubertal Development —

          Variants of Pubertal Development

          • Premature thelarche
            • Isolated breast development with some estrogenization of vaginal mucosa
              • Isolated = no other signs
            • 6 month - 2 years old
              • Estrogen from mom → prenatal, breast-feeding
            • Usually spontaneous regression (occasionally early puberty)
          • Premature pubarche (A.K.A. adrenarche)
            • Isolated pubic hair development with some acne/body odour
            • 6 - 8 years old
              • Adrenal androgens or polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS)
            • Usually no problem, normal puberty
            • Maybe insulin resistance or later PCOS
          • Premature menarche

          Assessing Pubertal Development

          #####History - Sex → boys usually present with pathology due (referral bias?) - Timing → < 8 girls/9 boys, but maybe < 7g/8b in developed countries, and certainly < 5 yo = bad - Tempo → fast progression = bad - Sequence → anything out of turn = bad - Growth → hit the spurt yet? before/after pubic hair? - CNS symptoms → headaches, vision suggests pituitary problem - Meds → e.g. corticosteroids for asthma → adrenal androgens - Family history → how early were parents’ puberties? how tall are they? → familial/constitutional delay of growth

          Physical
          • Height & weight → track them on growth curves, look for percentile/crossing percentiles
          • Pubertal staging → helps in assessing sequence and tempo
          • CNS symptoms → test vision, etc., suggests pituitary problem
          Investigations
          • Basal serum LH/FSH → assess gonadotropin dependence vs. independence
          • Serum estradiol/testosterone → assess gonadal vs. other cause
          • TSH → severe hypothyroidism can cause early puberty, less severe hypothyroidism can cause delayed puberty (this area is clearly not well understood)
          • X-ray of hands → assess bone age
          • If suspicious for pathology (using all above), consider the following further investigations
            • Karyotype → Turner’s/Klinefelter’s
            • Pelvic U/S → ovarian failure/DSD (disorder of sexual development)
            • GnRH stimulation test → in precocious puberty, determine gonadotropin dependence (“primary” vs. “secondary” precocious puberty)
            • MRI of head → check for pituitary tumours (gonadotropin dependent precocious puberty / secondary hypogonadism delayed puberty)
