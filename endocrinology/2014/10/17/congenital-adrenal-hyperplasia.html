        Congenital Adrenal Hyperplasia

        Bill Huang Friday, October 17, 2014

        Objective: Describe the 3 major adrenal hormone pathways and state the most common enzyme deficiency in CAH

        Introduction to Congenital Adrenal Hyperplasia (CAH)

        • Group of disorders of adrenal steroid biosynthesis
        • Autosomal recessive, occurs 1 in 10,000 births
        • Over 90% of cases are due to 21 hydroxylase deficiency
        • 11-beta hydroxylase deficiency in 5%, 3-beta HSD deficiency in 1%
        • Mineralocorticoids, cortisol, and androstenedione (precursor of testosterone) are all made by the adrenals from cholesterol
        • If 21 hydroxylase is deficient, neither mineralocorticoid nor glucocorticoid can be made, and the precursors are shunted into androstenedione synthesis
          • 17-OH progesterone is a precursor that builds up with 21 hydroxylase deficiency, and this can be used in diagnostic testing
        • If 11 beta-hydroxylase is deficient, 11 deoxycortisol is a precursor that accumulates; corticoids are still deficient
        • With lack of cortisol, there is no negative feedback of ACTH
        • ACTH excess results in hyperplasia of adrenal cortex tissue
        • Androgen excess results in genital ambiguity in females, and hyperandrogenic effect in both sexes (often unnoticed in neonatal boys)

        Variable Presentation of 21 Hydroxylase Deficiency

        • A broad spectrum of clinical presentations are possible depending on the particular gene mutations affecting 21 hydroxylase activity
        • Salt wasting is the most severe mutation, and occurs when the enzyme has close to no activity.
          • Severe mutations in both copies of gene
          • Deficiency of glucocorticoid, mineralocorticoid
          • Androgen excess
          • Accounts for 75% of 21 hydroxylase deficiency cases
        • Simple virilising presentation occurs when the amount of mineralocorticoid activity is sufficient
          • One relatively mild mutation in gene, one severe
          • Some cortisol deficiency occurs, and androgen excess is still present
          • Significant salt loss does not occur, but plasma renin may be elevated
        • Non-classic, also known as late-onset presentation
          • Two mild mutations in 21 hydroxylase gene
          • Can present pre-puberty as premature pubarche, or during puberty as hirsutism, acne, oligomenorrhea

        Diagnosis of CAH

        • With a 21 hydroxylase deficiency, look for high serum 17-OH progesterone
        • Often, serum Na+ is low and K+ high due to absence of aldosterone; background setting of metabolic acidosis
        • Hypoglycemia can occur with a salt-losing crisis
        • With an 11 hydroxylase deficiency, there is a cortisol deficiency and androgen excess
        • There is also an excess of deoxycorticosterone (a precursor of aldosterone), which is a very potent mineralocorticoid
          • Hypertension, hypokalemia possible
        • Ultrasound should be performed in girls to examine presence and state of uterus, ovaries
        • Retrograde urethrogram can assess separation of urogenital openings

        Objective: Identify the presentation of CAH in a female and male infant

        Presentation of CAH in Neonatal Period

        • Females tend to present with virilisation of the external genitalia, with clitoral hypertrophy and variable fusion of the labia
        • Most severe effect is a single urogenital opening
        • Males tend to present with enlargement of penis and scrotal pigmentation
          • Salt-losing adrenal crisis at 1-3 weeks of age
            • Vomiting, loose frequent stools
            • Poor feeding, inability to gain weight
            • Dehydration, low body temperature, hyperpigmentation
            • Widened pulse pressure, diastolic hypotension
          • Neonatal death if condition not recognized and treated
        • In 2007, newborn screening for CAH was introduced to prevent salt-losing crises

        Presentation of CAH in Childhood

        • Does not apply to salt-wasters, who do not survive beyond infancy if undiagnosed
        • Late-onset presentation does not have salt-wasting and kids are protected from absence of glucocorticoids
        • Excess androgen leads to:
          • Tall stature in first few years of life
          • Precocious puberty in boys
          • Progressive virilisation in girls
        • Precocious puberty is marked by low LH and FSH (which should be elevated in normal puberty)
        • Precursors (17-OHP, DHEA) can be elevated
        • Bone age may be older than chronological age, indicating bone maturation stimulated by excess androgen

        Objective: Outline the management of an infant in a salt losing crisis

        Management of Salt-Losing Crisis

        • Fluid replacement with a combination of dextrose and normal saline
        • Potassium resuscitation
        • IV hydrocortisone bolus immediately (cross-reactivity with mineralocorticoid receptors), continue with steady infusion after initial bolus
        • Switch over to replacement fludrocortisone and taper hydrocortisone once electrolytes stabilize

        Long-Term Management

        • Steroid replacement will be required for life
        • Glucocorticoid replacement (hydrocortisone) is based on the normal rate of cortisol secretion
        • In older adolescents, hydrocortisone is replaced by prednisone or dexamethasone
        • Maintenance mineralocorticoid (fludrocortisone) is also required daily
        • To prevent future adrenal crises:
          • Increase glucocorticoid dose for illness, vomiting/diarrhea, surgery, trauma
          • May require emergency IM or IV hydrocortisone
          • Medic-alert bracelet can help with identification
        • Monitoring effects of long-term therapy:
          • Measure linear growth and weight gain (prevent precocious puberty)
          • Assess for progress virilisation
          • Check for signs of excess glucocorticoids (eg. Cushingoid features) or excess mineralocorticoids (eg. hypertension)
            • Also check 17-OHP, DHEA, androstenedione, testosterone levels to monitor efficacy of glucocorticoid therapy
            • Check electrolytes and plasma renin to monitor mineralocorticoid therapy
          • Check bone age to ensure maturation is not proceeding too quickly
        • Surgery may be considered to reduce clitoral size
        • For labial fusion, reconstruction of the vagina at or after puberty is an option

        Future Pregnancies

        • Risk of CAH is 1 in 4, risk of affected female is 1 in 8
        • Virilisation can be prevented in a female fetus to optimize gender identity
        • Therapy is daily dexamethasone for the mother as soon as mother knows she is pregnant, and no later than 8 weeks
        • Fetal diagnosis:
          • Chorionic villus sampling at 10 weeks gestation
          • Amniocentesis at 15-18 weeks gestation
        • Continue treatment to term in pregnancies where fetus is affected female; stop therapy in all other cases (including affected male)
        • Side effects of dexamethasone include weight gain, gestational diabetes
