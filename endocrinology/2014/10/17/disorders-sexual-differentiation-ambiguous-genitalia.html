        Disorders of Sexual Differentiation & Ambiguous Genitalia

        Tehmina Ahmad Friday, October 17, 2014

        Lecture Objectives

        • List the causes of incomplete virilization in an individual with an XY karyotype
        • List the causes of virilization in an individual with an XX karyotype
        • Describe the appropriate clinical and laboratory assessment of an infant with ambiguous genitalia
        • Describe the management of a newborn with ambiguous genitalia

        Ambiguous Genitalia

        • The phenotype is sufficiently abnormal to cause uncertainty as to which gender should be assigned at birth
        • Genital abnormalities sufficient to warrant endocrine and genetic evaluation occur in 1 in 4,500 births

        Disorder of Sexual Development (DSD)

        • Definition: A congenital condition in which development of chromosomal, gonadal, or phenotypical/anatomical sex is atypical.
        • DSDs are characterized based on the discrepancy between one or more of the following:
          1. Karyotype
          2. Gonadal histology/type
          3. Internal genitalia
          4. External genitalia
        • NB: DSDs are not always associated with ambiguous genitalia but may present at puberty with abnormal pubertal development.
        • Revised Nomenclature regarding DSDs

        Principles of Sexual Differentiation

          • Essentially if an individual has SRY (rather by having XY or XX with a translocation of SRY) then the gonads that develop will be Testes.
          • If no SRY region is present, internally ovaries and externally female genitalia will develop
          • If SRY present and Testes develop, cells within the testes will undergo differentiation into Leydig cells and Sertoli cells.
          • Leydig cells will drive the production of testosterone, which drives the development of the Wolffian system (seminal vesicles, vas deferens) and DHT which drives the development of male external genitalia.
          • Sertoli cells will drive the production of Anti-Mullarian substance, which inhibits female internal genitalia from being produced.

        Female Genital Examination

        • Genitalia to note:
          1. Labia minor and majora
          2. Presence of labia fusion
          3. One or two urogenital openings
          4. Clitoral size (cliteromegaly)

        Male Genital Examination

        • Genitalia to note:
          1. Phallus length and width (Stretched penile length)
          2. Position of urethral orifice (Hypospadias 65% occur on anterior position of glans)
          3. Scrotal rugation
          4. Palpable testes (palpable gonads are never ovaries)

        DSD and Ambiguous Genitalia Classifications

        46, XX DSD - chromosomes = 46XX - gonads = ovaries - external genitalia = virilized 46, XY DSD - chromosomes = 46XY - gonads = testes - external genitalia = incompletely virilized Ovotesticular DSD - chromosomes = variable - gonads = mixed Mixed gonadal dysgenesis - chromosomes = 45X/46XY

        46 XX, DSD

        Increased fetal androgen - CONGENITAL ADRENAL HYPERPLASIA (CAH) - 21 hydroxylase deficiency - Androgen secreting tumor (from ovaries or adrenals) - Placental Aromatase deficiency Transplacental maternal androgen - Virilizing tumor - Androgenic drugs

        46 XY, DSD

        Disorders of testicular development - Complete or partial gonadal dysgenesis - Testes regression Disorders of androgen synthesis - Leydig cell hypoplasia (LH deficiency/LH receptor defect) - Testosterone biosynthesis, (non-virilizing CAH), 3ßHSD, 17, 20 lyase, 17ßHSD - Deficient synthesis or action of AMH - 5-alpha-reductase deficiency End organ unresponsiveness - Incomplete androgen insensitivity (present at birth with ambiguous genitalia)

        Clinical Note: Complete Androgen Insensitivity

        • Complete androgen insensitivity would present during puberty, where the individual is 46XY but phenotypically female, as there would be no virilizing effect of testosterone or DHT.
        • Internally, their gonads are testes with immature Wolffian structures, no mullerian structures but externally FEMALE genitalia.
        • This person likely would present with primary amenorrhea ~ 16 years of age.
        • These individuals are not fertile.

        46 XY, DSD, Disorder of Androgen Synthesis: 5-α-reductase deficiency

        • Impaired conversion of testosterone to dihydrotesterone.
        • Genetics: autosomal recessive disease
        • Dx = Elevated Testosterone:DHT after BHCG stimulation test.
        • chromosomes = 46XY
        • gonads (internal) = testes, abdominal, inguinal or labioscrotal
        • external genitalia = incompletely virilized and ambiguous
        • puberty = sparse facial and pubic hair, no acne, excess testosterone production during puberty may cause testes to descend leading to a phenotypic “female XY”–> male conversion ####46 XY, DSD, End organ unresponsiveness: Incomplete androgen insensitivity
        • Partial androgen receptor defect
        • Genetics: X-linked recessive
        • Dx = DNA analysis
        • chromosomes = 46XY
        • gonads (internal) = testes
        • external genitalia = ambiguous, micropenis or normal male
        • puberty = incomplete virilization, gynecomastia, azoospermia
        • These individuals often run into problems at puberty, depending on the effect of androgen insensitivity.

        46 XY, DSD, End organ unresponsiveness: Complete androgen insensitivity

        • Complete androgen receptor defect
        • Genetics: X-linked recessive
        • Dx = DNA analysis
        • Phenotypically female
        • chromosomes = 46XY
        • gonads (internal) = testes - bilateral inguinal hernia, absent fallopian tubes, uterus and upper third of vagina
        • external genitalia = totally female
        • puberty = normal breast development, sparse pubic and axillary hair, primary amenorrhea ####Ovotesticular DSD
        • Disorder of gonadal differentiation
        • 1/60000 births
        • chromosomes = 46XY or XX or mosaic
        • gonads (internal) = bilateral ovotestes is most common, ovaries/testes/uterus maybe present
        • external genitalia = ambiguous
        • puberty = virilization and/or feminization (really could go either way at puberty)

        Mixed Gonadal Dysgenesis

        • Disorder of gonadal differentiation
        • typically short stature and somatic stigmata of Turner Syndrome
        • chromosomes = 45XO, 46XY or 46XX
        • gonads (internal) = unilateral testis (dysgenetic), contralateral streak gonad
        • external genitalia = ambiguous
        • variety of genitalia presentations associated with this > ###Clinical Note: DSD & Ambiguous Genitalia Summary

        Assessment of a Newborn with Ambiguous Genitalia

        History
        - Family history of ambiguous genitalia or virilization - Pregnancy-maternal medications and health - Neonatal crises (Hx of crib deaths, CAH or adrenal problems) Physical Exam
        - General examination - Dysmorphic features (look for midlines defects such as cleft lip/palate as well) - Palpation of gonads - Describe the genitalia: open vs. fused folds, phallus length, urogenital openings (number and position) Prader Classification: Five stages of female infant virilization NB: A palpable gonad in the labia, scrotum or inguinal canal is either a testis, an ovotestis, or a dysgenetic testis. It is never an ovary.

        • Investigations
          1. Karyotype - first thing to do in workup but takes 2 days; do this with a virilized female or incompletely virilized male
          2. Pelvic U/S - second thing to do in workup; assess the presence and location of uterus and gonads
          3. Na, K, glucose - to assess potential for adrenal crisis/CAH
          4. 17 Hydroxyprogesterone (17OHP) - high levels are DIAGNOSTIC for CAH
          5. cortisol and testosterone
          6. Genitogram - this is an imagine test to define urethra, vagina, bladder (looking at internal structures with more detail)

        Further Investigations (confined to XY DSD)
        1. 11-deoxycortisol (CAH) 2. BHCG test - mimics LH/FSH, if you give this and testosterone does not rise, than the gonads are not working (primary hypogonadism) 3. Check the response to a course of testosterone 4. Mutation analysis 5. Laparascopy and gonadal biopsy

        Diagnostic Evaluation

        • A specific molecular diagnosis is identified in only 20% of cases with DSD
        • The majority of 46XX DSD = CAH
        • Only 50% of 46XY DSD will receive a definitive diagnosis

        Gonadal Neoplasm Formation

        High Risk Groups - Dysgenetic gonads - Undescended testes Gonadoblastoma - Most common type of gonadal tumor - Usually develops in the presence of a Y chromosome - Usually < 8 mm, may calcify - May secrete androgens/estrogens - 50 % associated with dysgerminomas - 10 % associated with other malignant tumors - this must be taken out

        Gender Assignment & Gender Identity

        • No evidence that chromosomes directly influence gender identity
        • Consideration of prenatal androgen brain exposure should be cautious
        • Gender identity development is not necessarily linked to genital appearance at birth
        • If genital assignment is opted for, it may require multiple procedures often required after childhood surgical intervention

        Outcome of Gender Assignment

        • 46XX DSD CAH - 90% assigned female in infancy identify as females. Evidence supports the current recommendation to raise markedly virilized 46XX infants with CAH as female.
        • 46XY DSD Complete Androgen Insensitivity - 100% assigned female in infancy identify as females.
        • 46XY DSD 5-α-Reductase Deficiency - 60% assigned female in infancy and virilizing at puberty (and 100% assigned male) identify as males
        • 46XY DSD Incomplete Androgen Insensitivity - androgen biosynthetic defects, gonadal dysgenesis, 25% whether assigned male or female have gender dissatisfaction

        Gender Assignment in Newborns

        • Initial gender uncertainty is extremely unsettling and stressful for families
        • Factors influencing gender assignment include: underlying diagnosis, genital appearance and surgical options, need for lifelong replacement therapy, potential for fertility, views of the family, and, sometimes, circumstances relating to cultural practices
        • Involvement of multidisciplinary team including psychology/social work essential
        • Delay recommendation until completion of evaluation of parameters of biological sex based on: chromosomes, gonads, hormones, internal genitalia, external genitalia

        European Society for Paediatric Endocrinology (ESPE) and the Lawson Wilkins Pediatric Endocrine Society (LWPES) Consensus

        • Sex assignment does not inevitably mean surgical intervention
        • Evidence justifying treatment methods is lacking
        • Individual consideration essential
        • Consider clitoral reduction only in severe virilization, especialyl in Prader 3-5 Classification
        • Only surgeons who are expert in the complex procedures of genital reconstruction and aware of the longer term outcome should be involved in the early management of DSD

        Management of Ambiguous Genitalia at Birth

        • Refer to a tertiary centre for assessment by multiple services, including endocrinology , neonatology , genetics, gynecology , urology and social work/psychology support
        • Delay assignment of sex of rearing until all investigations are complete
        • Do not assign a name
        • Do not register the birth
        • Optimal early management of infants with intersex reduces the likelihood of anomalies of gender identity in adult life
