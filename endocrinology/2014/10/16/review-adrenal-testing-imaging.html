            Review of Adrenal Testing & Imaging

            Kevan Lu Thursday, October 16, 2014

            Adrenal Testing

            • Most testing is done nowadays with immunoassays, which have replaced older and more cumbersome tests.
            • These tests are still sometimes used, but don’t order them. For historical interest, these methods include liquid chromatography, mass spectrometry, or colorimetric assays for specific steroids.

            Adrenal Cortex Tests

            • Adrenal Glucocorticoids → from the zona fasciculata.
              • Baseline Tests.
              • Dynamic Tests.
            • Adrenal Mineralicorticoids → from the zona glomerulosa.
              • Baseline Tests.
              • Dynamic Tests.
            • Adrenal Androgens → from the zona reticularis.
            • An easy way to remember which hormones are released from where is to remember the GFR (Glomerulosa, Fasciculata, Reticularis) corresponds to salt, sugar, and sex.
            • Baseline → we’re not doing anything to the patient when we’re performing the test.
            • Dynamic → we are doing something (eg. giving them a hormone), and are monitoring their response.

            Adrenal Glucocorticoids

            Baseline Tests

            1. AM Serum Cortisol.
              • Used for Adrenal Insufficiency.
              • Measured at 8 or 9 AM.
              • Recall that this was because Cortisol is normally high in the morning, so a AM cortisol would be more sensitive for low values.
                • <100 → likely AI.
                • >500 → unlikely AI, unless their CBG↑ (OCP, pregnancy).
                • In-between, it’s a gray-zone, where you would then try dynamic testing.
              • Recall that this is a total cortisol measurement, including bound cortisol (typically bound to CBG), and free cortisol.
                • Only the free cortisol is biologically active, but we don’t have a serum assay for it.
                • Also, CBG can be elevated with birth control or with pregnancy, so you may have an elevated total cortisol with a normal free cortisol, resulting in a false increase.
                • As a result, this test is not effective for looking for cortisol overproduction → which is why it’s only used for Adrenal Insufficiency.
            2. 24 Hour Urine for Free Cortisol.
              • Used for Adrenal Overproduction (Cushing’s Syndrome).
                • Recall that Cushing’s Syndrome = Glucocorticoid Overproduction, while Cushing’s Disease = Glucocorticoid Overproduction from a Pituitary Cause.
                • This is the best test for high levels of corticosteroids, and is diagnostic if >3x the upper limit of normal.
                • This test doesn’t look at the lower limit → so it’s not good for adrenal insufficiency.
                • One negative however is that it’s a pain-in-the-ass for patients.
              • Measures free cortisol.

            Baseline Tests for Adrenal Glucocorticoids

            • The 8 AM Serum Cortisol is the best test for Adrenal Insufficiency.
            • The 24 Hour Free Cortisol is the best test for Adrenal Overproduction.
            • The 11 PM Salivary Cortisol is also useful with Adrenal Overproduction, to look for loss of diurnal rhythm, but it isn’t widely available in Canada (at the moment).
            • What about 11 PM Salivary Cortisol?
              • Currently not freely available in Canada.
              • Recall that this was used in suspect Adrenal Overproduction, to look for loss of diurnal rhythm in cortisol excretion (normally it should be low at night).
              • This is lost in pathological conditions.
              • Why not evening serum cortisol? It’s not very practical, since labs are closed at night. Also, salivary free cortisol isn’t affected by OCP/pregnancy.

            Remember

            • Serum Cortisol → Total Cortisol (= Free + Bound).
            • 24 Hour Urine → Free Cortisol.
            • Salivary Cortisol → Free Cortisol.
            • Bound cortisol will increase in OCP and Pregnancy, due to an increase in Corticosteroid Binding Globulin (CBG). This may result in falsely high values.
            • What about the ACTH Level?
              • The ACTH is useful in diagnosing primary adrenal insufficiency → ACTH would be high.
              • It’s also useful in determining the cause of Cushing’s Syndrome → whether it’s ACTH dependent (↑ pituitary production) or ACTH independent (loss of regulation).
              • Note → ACTH is very labile, so if it’s improperly collected, it may be degraded, and will thus be falsely low. So if the clinical syndrome doesn’t fit with the ACTH, verify if it was properly collected.

            Dynamic Tests

            • If hyposecretion (insufficiency) is suspected, then you would want see if production can still be stimulated.
              • Done with the 250 µg ACTH (cosyntropin) Stimulation Test → uses an ACTH analogue to stimulate the adrenals.
                • IV or IM bolus → serum cortisol increases >500 (minimum 250 increase).
                • Doesn’t help you determine if it’s a primary or secondary adrenal insufficiency; it only helps you make a diagnosis if the 8 AM serum cortisol was in that gray area (100-500).
            • If hypersecretion (overproduction) is suspected, then you would want see if production can still be suppressed.
              • Done with the 1 mg Dexamethasone Suppression Test → uses a corticosteroid to try and suppress ACTH production.
                • Given at 11 PM, measured the next morning at 8 AM.
                • Cortisol levels the next morning should normally be < 50 nM/L.
                • If it’s > 50 nM/L, then they have an abnormal response → Cushing’s Syndrome.
                • Since we’re measuring serum cortisol with this, it can be confounded by:
                  1. ↑ CBG (OCP, Pregnancy). → if they’re on birth control or pregnancy, do the 24 hour urine free cortisol.
                  2. ↑ Dexamethasone metabolism → if it’s metabolized quickly, it won’t be able to suppress the HPA axis.
                  3. Lack of compliance → the patient has to take the pill!
              • If either the 24 hour urine free cortisol or the 1 mg dexamethasone suppression test are abnormal, then the next test is a high dose dexamethasone suppression test (8 mg).
                • The purpose of the high dose one is to help determine the etiology of the Cushing’s Syndrome.
                • If you have Cushing’s Disease, you should suppress → the pituitary still has feedback, it’s just set at a higher level.
                • If it’s not Cushing’s Disease, then you won’t have suppression on high dose dexamethasone.

            Dynamic Testing For Adrenal Glucocorticoids

            • Hyposecretion → stimulation (250 µg ACTH Stimulation Test).
            • Hypersecretion → suppression (1 mg Dexamethasone Suppression Test).

            Adrenal Mineralicorticoids

            • Very weakly affected by the HPA axis, through cross-reactivity of cortisol to the mineralicorticoid receptor.
            • Primarily regulated by the RAAS System, which we’ve seen a million times.

            Hypoaldosteronism

            • With primary adrenal insufficiency (eg. with Addison’s Disease), you have Aldosterone ↓, Na+ ↓, and K+ ↑.
            • Because you lose Na+, and water follows the salt, you end up hypotensive.
            • Hence we end up with:
              1. Hyponatremia.
              2. Hyperkalemia.
              3. Hypotension.
            • Tests are usually not necessary for hypoaldosteronism → can usually be seen from the clinical picture.
              • Also, if it’s a primary adrenal problem, then it’ll affect the glucocorticoids too, so you can use the diagnostic algorithm for adrenal insufficiency shown previously.

            Hyperaldosteronism

            • We do perform tests for hyperaldosteronism.
            • Why do we need to do tests?
            • It’ll help direct medical/surgical/radiological treatment for:
              1. Primary hyperaldosteronism.
              2. Primary renal artery stenosis (secondary hyperaldosteronism)
              3. Primary hypertension.

            Primary Hyperaldosteronism

            • Also known as Conn’s Syndrome.
            • Feedback is intact, so renin is low. We saw this in GU with the functions of the principal cell and the α-intercalated cell. Review Reabsorption & Secretion from GU.
            • However, since aldosterone is high, we have ↑ Na+ retention, ↑ K+ secretion, and ↑ H+ secretion. resulting in hypertension, hypokalemia, and metabolic alkalosis.
            • Here, the aldosterone:renin ratio is high → recall that with ectopic aldosterone production, it’s no longer renin-dependent, so the ratio should be high.

            Secondary Hyperaldosteronism

            • Here, the kidney is secreting excess renin, leading to increased aldosterone, and the same clinical effects as before.
              • This can be seen in renal artery stenosis, where the juxtaglomerular cells sense low flow due to the stenosis, triggering renin production.
            • However, since there isn’t ectopic aldosterone production, aldosterone is still renin dependent here.
            • Hence, the aldosterone:renin ratio is normal, or sometimes even low.

            Primary vs Secondary Hyperaldosteronism

              Primary Secondary
            Na+ ↑ ↑
            K+ ↓ ↓
            H+ ↓ ↓
            Aldosterone:Renin Ratio High Normal/Low
            • Since you need to look at the relationship between aldosterone and renin to properly know what’s going on with hyperaldosteronism, the best baseline test is the aldosterone:renin ratio.
              • Measuring the serum renin individually, or the serum aldosterone individually, is insufficient.
              • Aldosterone itself is also suppressed whtn the K+ is low, so K+ must be > 4 for the aldosterone component to be reliable.

            Baseline Tests for Adrenal Mineralicorticoids

            • The Aldosterone:Renin Ratio is the best test for Hyperaldosteronism.

            Dynamic Testing

            1. Suppression Tests
              • Used to confirm primary hyper-aldosteronism.
              • Done via a saline infusion, or via exogenous mineralicorticoid (fludrocortisone) → normally this should suppress the RAAS system; if there’s primary hyperaldosteronism, there will be a failure to suppress.
            2. Stimulation Tests
              • Used to confirm primary hypo-aldosteronism.
              • Furosemide +/- salt restriction cause salt loss and hypotension, so they should increase renin to maintain BP.
              • In patients with a primary hypoaldosteronism, the adrenals will be unable to respond adequately to the increased renin.

            Dynamic Testing for Adrenal Mineralicorticoids

            • Saline Infusion Suppression Test can be used to confirm primary hyper-aldosteronism.
            • Furosemide +/- Salt Restriction Stimulation can be used to confirm primary hypo-aldosteronism.

            Adrenal Androgens

            Baseline Tests

            • Not too exciting.
            • Only really tested in women with virilization.
              • This is because most of the testosterone in women is from the peripheral conversion of androgens.
            • These tests include:
              1. Serum Dehydroepiandrosterone Sulphate (DHEAS)
                • Specific for virilizing tumours → most virilizing tumours add a sulphate onto the DHEA.
              2. Serum Androstenedione
            • Both are baseline tests, although they can also be used as dynamic tests by measuring them in serum after suppression with dexamethasone.
              • Dynamic tests with these are extremely rarely done.
            • There are also indirect tests:
              1. Serum Testosterone
                • Not directly secreted by the adrenal, but adrenal androgens can be converted to testosterone.
                • Total = free + bound to sex-hormone binding globulin.
              2. 24 hour urine for 17-Ketosteroids
                • Extremely old test; not used anymore.
                • Indirect colorimetric method for measuring adrenal androgens.
                • Don’t order this.

            Baseline Tests for Adrenal Androgens

            1. Serum Dehydroepiandrosterone Sulphate (DHEAS)
            2. Serum Androstenedione
            3. Serum Testosterone → indirect test, since adrenal androgens are converted to testosterone.

            Example Case

            Case Summary

            • Mrs. Mary Oliver is a pleasant 35-year-old woman.
            • She presents to urgent medical clinic with a 6 month history of a 30 pound weight loss.
            • At first her appetite was increased.
            • However, over the past month she has become anorexic and has experienced recurrent nausea and vomiting, crampy abdominal pain and diarrhea.
            • Food seems to be tasteless unless she puts extra salt on it.
            • She has also felt lightheaded on standing, has frequent palpitations, and has become shaky and irritable.
            • She also noted increased sweating and heat intolerance.
            • Her friends frequently ask her if she has been down south (hyperpigmentation.
            • She has lost her axillary and pubic hair.
            • Mary also noticed she was thirstier than usual.
            • She has been more anxious, nervous, and tired with trouble getting to sleep and waking frequently.
            • Her menses have become irregular over the past year with the last one occurring 2 months ago.

            Physical Examination

            • She has a good tan with some pigmentation of her knees and elbows and buccal mucosa, but no other abnormal pigmentation.
            • Her blood pressure is 100/60 lying and 70/40 standing and her pulse increases from 100 to 130/min.
            • Fine tremor of the outstretched hands.
            • Skin is warm and moist but her mucous membranes appear dry.
            • Thyroid gland is diffusely enlarged and meaty with bruits over both lobes.
            • Axillary and pubic hair are absent.

            Findings

            • Here the patient actually has 2 disorders.
              1. Hyperpigmentation, orthostatic hypotension, nausea, vomiting, salt-craving, loss of pubic hair, abdominal pain suggests Addison’s Disease, a cause of primary ad‘mors, sweating, heat intolerance, and diffuse thyroid enlargement suggest Graves’ Disease.

            Additional Information

            • Electrolytes show low sodium, high potassium.
            • AM Cortisol was 50.
            • ACTH is pending.
            • The low sodium, high potassium, and low BP are suggestive of hypoaldosteronism.
            • The AM Cortisol is < 100, so this is positive for adrenal insufficiency.
            • What would you expect the ACTH to be?
              • It should be high, to try and stimulate the adrenals.
            • The patient’s Addison’s must be treated before her Graves’. Why?
              • Her Addison’s could lead to an adrenal crisis, which can be fatal.

            Acute Adrenal Insufficiency (Adrenal Crisis)

            • Acute Treatment
              • Monitor ABCs, electrolytes, glucose.
              • IV fluids to resolve hypotension.
              • Hydrocortisone 100 mg IV bolus, followed by 50-100 mg Q 6-8 h.
              • Mineralicorticoid supplementation isn’t necessary acutely because we have such a high dose of the hydrocortisone, which is cross reactive.
            • Subacute Treatment
              • Taper fluids, corticosteroids.
              • Now you need to start mineralicorticoids (Florinef, aka Fludrocortisone), because we can’t rely on cross-reactivity here.
            • Maintenance therapy.
              • Glucocorticoid + Mineralicorticoid Replacement.
              • Hydrocortisone for the glucocorticoid.
              • Fludrocortisone for the mineralicorticoid.
            • This patient actually had Polyglandular Autoimmune Syndrome Type 1
              • We saw this briefly in the Adrenal Insufficiency lecture.
              • The take home point here is that auto-immune endocrine disorders can run together! Be cognizant of this in the future!
