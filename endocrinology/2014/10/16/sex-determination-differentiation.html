        Sex Determination & Differentiation

        Kevan Lu Thursday, October 16, 2014

        Introduction & Key Concepts

        • The key differentiating factor between a male and a female is the presence of a Y chromosome.
        • What on the Y chromosome predisposes you to being a male?
          • The SRY gene (Sex-determining Region on Y Chromosome).
          • This is a tiny genetic locus found on the anterior end of the short arm on the Y chromosome.
        • So if you have SRY, and it’s expressed at the right time at development, you’ll develop testes.
          • In particular, they trigger the formation of Sertoli cells, which as we saw in GU, were the epithelial cells that line the seminiferous tubules.
          • Sertoli cells support the development of the germ cells into spermatogonia during puberty.
        • In other words, the Y Chromosome → SRY → Sertoli Cells → Testes.
        • Once you have testes, what do they produce (aside from spermatozoa)?
          • They produce 2 key hormones:
            1. Testosterone → produced by Leydig Cells.
            2. Mullerian Inhibiting Substance (MIS) → produced by Sertoli Cells.
        • In early development, the fetus is completely undifferentiated → it can develop into either a female or a male depending on what gonad forms.
          • If testes form (in the presence of SRY), androgens are produced, which act on the male primordial structures to produce a male reproductive tract.
          • In addition, the Sertoli cells in the testes produce MIS, which prevents the female primordial structures from developing.
        • What about the development of the penis? (heh heh heh)
          • Testosterone is converted to Dihydrotestosterone (5α-dihydrotestosterone, DHT) by 5α-reductase, which is a much stronger androgen.
            • Testosterone, which is produced by the Sertoli cells of the testes, gets diluted on the way to the urogenital sinus, and isn’t potent enough to induce penile formation.
            • To resolve this, 5α-reductase is expressed in the urogenital sinus, and amplifies the testosterone by converting it to a more active form → DHT.
          • This induces the penis to grow off of the urogenital sinus.

        Summary of Male Sexual Differentiation

        • The SRY gene on the Y chromosome predisposes you to being a male.
        • It triggers the formation of the testes, in particular Sertoli Cells.
        • The testes secrete two hormones:
          1. Testosterone → secreted by Leydig Cells.
            • Testosterone (and a stronger analogue, DHT), act on the male primordial structures to produce a male reproductive tract.
          2. Mullerian Inhibiting Substance (MIS) → secreted by Sertoli Cells.
            • MIS inhibits the development of the female primordial structures.
        • In order for the external genitalia to develop, testosterone must be converted to a more potent form called dihydrotestosterone (DHT).
          • This conversion is mediated by 5α-reductase.

        The Jost Paradigm for Sexual Differentiation

        • Jost Paradigm → chromosomal sex (genetic) determines gonadal sex (primary sex differentiation), which determines phenotypic sex (secondary sex differentiation).
        • This matches well with what we’ve discussed above:
          • Chromosomal Sex → Y chromosome, SRY gene.
          • Gonadal Sex → development of the testes.
          • Phenotypic Sex → development of the external genitalia.

        Discovery of the ‘Testis Determining Factor’

        • We didn’t actually know that the Y chromosome was required for male differentiation until the late 1950’s, when karyotyping was developed.
          • In individuals with only the short arm of the Y chromosome, there was no phenotypic change.
          • In individuals with only the long arm of the Y chromosome, they were completely phenotypically female.
        • This suggested that there was something on the short arm of the Y chromosome (later discovered to be the SRY gene), that was critical to sexual diferentiation.
          • They proposed to name this the Testis Determining Factor (TDF).
          • It was postulated that this was a single gene, that regulated a cascade of other genes, which would cause sertoli cell differentiation, leading to testicular formation.

        Primary Sex Reversal

        • When the X and Y chromosome line up during meiosis and crossing-over occurs, it’s possible for the end of short arm of the Y-chromosome (where SRY is located), to end up on the X-chromosome.
          • This either results in an XX individual that is phenotypically male, or an XY individual that is phenotypically female, depending on which chromosome is passed on.
          • This is known as Primary Sex Reversal, and it occurs in approximately 1/20,000 births.

        SRY as a Repressor/Inhibitor

        • McElreavey et al., 1993 PNAS Study.
        • In this study, the investigators performed PCR on over 100 XX males, and looked for the presence of the SRY gene.
          • Of the 35 XX males with no ambiguous genitalia, 32 were SRY positive.
            • This is the primary sex reversal we discussed above.
          • Of the 38 XX males with ambiguous genitalia, only 2 were SRY positive.
          • Of the 46 XX hermaphroditic males, only 4 were SRY positive.
        • This suggests that most cases of sex-reversal are not SRY related, and involve the function of other genes.
          • They postulated that their results were due to the presence of another gene, ‘Z’, that SRY had effects on.
          • They suggested that SRY’s main role was as an inhibitor of this ‘Z’ gene.
            • With SRY, Z is inhibited, allowing for the formation of the male gonads.
            • Without SRY, Z is active, allowing for the formation of the female gonads.
          • In the above cases where SRY is absent, and there’s virilization, the researchers postulated that there was a functional mutation in ‘Z’.

        Other Sex Determination Genes

        • It turned out that this hypothesis was correct, and there is a particular gene that fits the model:
          • Dax-1 → DSS dosage sensitive sex reversal: X-linked.
            • Located on the X-chromosome, and is inhibited by SRY.
              • Dax-1 is turned off during testis formation.
              • Dax-1 is turned on during ovary formation.
            • In patients with a Dax-1 duplication, you can actually have sex-reversal in XY individuals (it’ll exceed the inhibition by SRY).
            • So Dax-1 is the gene Z described in our previous model.
        • Other genes were discovered that add to this model:
          • SOX9
            • Autosomal gene, mutations in it result in campomelic dysplasia.
            • Campomelic dysplasia → severe dwarfism; bone disorder with XY reversal in 3/4 of males.
            • SOX9 is required for sertoli cell development.
            • Dax 1 may repress SOX9 during ovary development, so SRY represses Dax-1, which represses SRY.
        • Nowadays, we know that there are over 30 critical sex determining genes. However, the basic conceptual framework that we need for understanding most sexual differentiation disorders is the basic one with SRY, Dax-1, and SOX9.

        Important

        • SRY inhibits Dax-1, which inhibits SOX9.

        Gonad Development

        • Primordial germ cells migrate from the yolk sac into the developing embryo during the 4th week of development.
        • At the 5th week of gestation, we have what’s known as an ‘indifferent gonad’ → something that can become either the ovary or the testes. It consists of the following:
          1. Germ Cells → these were the ones that migrated from the yolk sac.
          2. Supporting Cells → these either become Sertoli Cells (male) or Granulosa Cells (female).
          3. Stromal Cells
        • The indifferent gonad also has two rudimentary systems, which can develop into the male or female reproductive system:
          • The male system is the mesonephric duct, also known as the Wolffian duct.
          • The female system is the paramesonephric duct, also known as the Mullerian duct.
        • Classical experiments by Alfred Jost showed how the fetus ‘decides’ between these two rudimentary systems.
          • In female rabbit fetuses, he grafted a testis right next to one of the 2 developing ovaries.
            • On the side where the testis was grafted, a fallopian tube did not develop; instead, male Wolffian structures formed.
            • The other side was perfectly normal.
          • Now, interested in determining what factors in the testis contribute to male reproductive system development, he grafted a testosterone crystal instead of a testis.
            • On the side with the crystal, both a Mullerian duct and Wolffian structures formed.
        • This led him to the following conclusions:
          1. The testis produces Testosterone which stimulates the formation of the male reproductive system.
          2. The testis must produce something else that inhibits female reproductive system development → later found to be MIS.
        • Mullerian Inhibiting Substance
          • Member of the TCF-β family; acts locally (autocrine).
          • Inhibits formation of the female reproductive system, but only in a very short time window.
          • If it’s blocked over this interval, eg. with monoclonal antibodies to MIS, then the Mullerian duct will persist.
          • This is known as Persistent Mullerian Duct Syndrome.

        Androgen Disorders

        • As mentioned before, Testosterone is converted to Dihydrotestosterone (DHT) by the enzyme 5α-reductase.
          • There are 2 isoforms of 5α-reductase.
          • The main form is expressed in the urogenital tract; the other form is expressed in hair follicles and play a large role in male pattern baldness.
        • Testosterone virilizes the primordial Wolffian Duct, however it’s at too low a concentration at the urogenital sinus to stimulate formation of the external genitalia.
        • DHT stimualtes differentiation of the male urethra, prostate, and external genitalia.
        • Below are two androgen disorders along the same pathway, that affect (1) conversion of testosterone to DHT, or (2) sensitivity to androgens.
        • 5α-reductase deficiency:
          • Will result in 46 XY males presenting as phenotypic females, or severely undervirilized males, depending on the mutation.
          • All the internal machinery is normal, but it ends in a ‘pseudovagina’ instead of a penis.
          • This makes sense, as DHT is necessary for the development of the external genitalia.
        • Testicular Feminization:
          • Typically result from mutations in the androgen receptor.
          • These patients have testes, and normal androgen levels, but they’re either partially or completely insensitive to androgens.
          • In patients that have CAIS (Complete Androgen Insensitivity Syndrome), they will phenotypically look entirely like a normal female.
          • The only difference is that they will lack normal female internal genitalia (they will still have testes), and will thus be unable to have children.
          • This also makes sense:
            • Since they still have SRY, they still repress Dax-1, which represses SOX9, which leads to Sertoli Cell formation and testes development. Sertoli cells also secrete MIS, which inhibits formation of the internal female reproductive system.
            • However, since they aren’t sensitive to testosterone or DHT, they don’t have formation of the Wolffian duct, nor any male external genitalia.
