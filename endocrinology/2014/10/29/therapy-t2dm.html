        Therapy for T2DM

        Kevan Lu Wednesday, October 29, 2014

        T2DM Therapy Notes

        • See T2DM Therapy Notes.

        Lecture Objectives

        1. Identify effective lifestyle treatments for Type 2 diabetes.
        2. Identify the major medications used to treat hyperglycemia.
        3. Integrate medication interventions for Type 2 DM that are additional to glucose lowering options.

        Treatment Goals

        • You want to help your patient with diabetes:
          1. Feel well day-to-day (immediate goal).
            • This is the absence of thirst, polyuria, fatigue, yeast infections, blurred vision, pruritus (these are the acute effects of hyperglycemia).
          2. Feel well as long as possible (prevention goal).
            • This is the avoidance of the chronic complications of DM.

        Treatment Challenges

        • Patients with T2DM don’t only have hyperglycemia; they also have a body chemistry that gives them a multitude of cardiovascular risks.
        • Diabetes is a chronic disease; successful treatment requires the patient to ‘buy-in’.
        • There are many challenges to meeting their goals:
          • Inertia and resistance → it’s hard to change your habits.
          • Financial costs → medications cost money.
          • Guilt → try to avoid guilt tripping your patients.

        Treatment Toolkit

        1. Lifestyle for everything → by far the most important; medications can only do so much.
        2. Patient Education for everyone.
        3. Medications for glucose.
        4. Medications for lipids, BP, complications.

        Diet

        • High carbohydrate, low fat, high fibre.
        • Three meals a day and snacks.
        • Important collaboration with DM dietitians.
        • We try to match the diet to what the pancreas can make; if we can’t get things to match, we use medication to help.

        Lifestyle

        • Regular meals.
        • Regular exercise (“exercise is medicine”).
        • Diabetes thrives on boring routines.
        • Weight control → patients may not need medications, even if they lose a little bit of weight.
        • Ideal patient should be medically literate.

        Patient Education

        • There are specific Registered Dietitians certified for Diabetes → RD (CDE).
        • There are also specific Registered Nurses for Diabetes → RN (CDE).
        • Physicians.
        • The patient plays a critical role as well → especially in monitoring their own blood glucose.

        Medications for Glucose Control

        • These are separated into two major categories:
          1. Insulin secretagogues → chemicals that make your pancreas produce more insulin.
          2. Insulin sensitizers → chemicals that make your body respond better to insulin.

        Categories of Glucose Medications

        1. Insulin secretagogues → chemicals that make your pancreas produce more insulin.
        2. Insulin sensitizers → chemicals that make your body respond better to insulin.

        DPP4 inhibitors, incretin mimics: Chemicals that boost your internal incretin response • SGLT inhibitors: Chemicals that increase glucose excretion in renal tubules - Just came out in June. ! Chemicals that give you gas (alpha- glucosidase inhibitors)—well actually, things that inhibit the absorption of glucose from the GI tract - No one orders them anyway, it’s hard to get on them w/o uncomfortable bowel gas, and they’re less effective anyway. - GLITAZONES!? also makes you gain weight - DPP4s are much better; don’t make you gain weight.

        Insulin

        • There are many many forms, but don’t panic.
        • To put things succinctly, insulin comes in two basic forms:
          1. Fast Acting.
          2. Longer Acting.
        • The insulin that Banting & Best originally made is now called regular insulin → in Ontario, it’s also known as ‘Toronto’ insulin.
          • Starts in about 1/2 an hour.
          • Lasts about 4 hours.
        • Later on, longer acting insulins were developed from Regular by adding chemicals to make a depot preparation.
          • Example → NPH (neutral protamine Hagadorn).
          • Onsets → in about 4 hours.
          • Lasts → about 8 hours.
        • Some newest insulins include:
          • Fast Acting → Lispro, aka. Humalog, or Insulin Aspart.
            • Onset in 10 minutes, last 2 hours.
            • More convenient, because patients can pop a pill right before they started eating.
          • Long Acting → Glargine or Detemir.
            • Onset in a few hours, lasts 24 hours.
            • Convenient because you can pop 1 pill, and make it last a day.
            • Has less of a ‘peak’, so it’s less likely to make you hypoglycemic.
