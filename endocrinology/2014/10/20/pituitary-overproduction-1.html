          Pituitary Overproduction I

          James Jae Monday, October 20, 2014

          Examples of Pituitary Overproduction

          1. Acromegaly
          2. Prolactinoma/Hyperprolactinemia
          3. Cushing’s Syndrome

          Acromegaly

          • A clinical syndrome caused by persistent Growth Hormone (GH) secretion
            • GH normally affects growth, repair and appetite
          • Overproduction of GH can be traced to the anterior pituitary usually
            • Most acromegaly cases are due to GH-producing tumour of the pituitary gland (Pituitary Adenoma)
              • These tumors produce excess GH and, as they expand, compress surrounding brain tissues, such as the optic nerves
              • This expansion may cause headaches and visual disturbances among many other symptoms
            • Overproduction of GHRH by the hypothalamus or ectopic GH production are rare
          • GH elevation results in an increase in IGF-1 production by the liver
            • Based on this knowledge, we measure serum GH and IGF-1
            • However, GH fluctuates a lot, making interpretation difficult
            • IGF-1 is more stable and reliable in a screening test; only downside is that patients have to pay out of pocket
            • Hence the best screening test for acromegaly is the serum IGF-1

          Confirmation of Diagnosis

          • Glucose Tolerance Test
            • GH normally increases blood glucose level, much like cortisol.
            • When you give glucose, it should suppress GH level
            • Try to suppress GH production through the Glucose Tolerance Test
            • In Acromegaly, expect minimal suppression of GH level.

          Tests for Acromegaly

          1. Screening Test → IGF-1.
          2. Confirmatory Test → Glucose Tolerance Test.

          Symptoms

          • Acral overgrowth
          • Skin thickening
          • Change facial features → eg. frontal bossing.
          • Macrolossia → often the tongue is so large that you can teeth markings on the side of it.
          • Enlarged hand size
          • Carpal tunnel syndrome
          • Fatigue, Weakness
          • Obstructive sleep apnea
          • Visceral enlargement
          • Hypertension and Heart Failure
          • Insulin Resistance
            • GH antagonizes insulin
          • Diabetes Mellitus
          • Colonic polyps, increased risk of colon cancer
            • regular colonoscopy indicated
          • Skin tags
          • x1.72 Mortality Rate (if left untreated)

          Cardiovascular Complications of Acromegaly

          • Hypertension (43%)
          • LVHT
            • if muscle growth excessive, muscle becomes less functional (Cardiomyopathy)
            • Heart Failure in 3-10%
          • Diabetes Mellitus in 10-15%
            • Impaired Glucose Tolerance (i.e. pre-diabetes) in a further 50%
          • Arthropathy (joint problems)
            • faster development of hip and knee problems

          Case A: 34-year old industrial technician

          • Referred by GP for large hands suggestive of acromegaly
          • PMHx:
            • Asthma since childhood
            • New diagnosis of HTN and T2DM few months ago
              • The fact that HTN and Diabetes presented recently aroud the same time should make you suspect a common underlying cause
          • Unaware of changes to his body over the past few years
            • most patients and family members do not notice slow, gradual changes over time
          • Average time between first symptoms of acromegaly and time of diagnosis = 8-10 years
            • We still fail to pick up symptoms early
            • Often first picked up by GP, ER, and ENT specialists
          • Patient’s shoe size also changed. More commonly, the change in the shoe width is more pronounced
          • Patient also noticed changes in skin
            • GH affects both internal and external organs
            • Increased hair growth on his back and shoulders
            • Oily sweaty skin
          • More prominent forehead and large jaw
            • When mandible increases in size, but teeth stay same in size
            • Results in larger gaps between teeth
            • Often results in easier flossing; can be picked up by dentists

          • Patients may also report arthralgia, change in bowel habits, and FHx of pituitary hormone pathology

          • GH increases circulating glucose levels
            • Therefore, diabetes can be a sign of developing acromegaly
            • This patient presented with elevated BG at 19 [Normal: 4-8]
            • Unresponsive to treatment with Metformin BID
              • If Diabetes is unresponsive to treatment, suggestive of an underlying driving factor
            • This patient initially gained weight (weight gain is typical of acromegaly), but as the diabetes got out of control, he lost weight (negative energy balance from severe diabetes results in weight loss).
          • Skin tags on neck and under arms
          • Deepening of voice
            • Changes in shape of mouth and sinuses result in resonant changes
            • Deeper voice
          • Ask for any changes in vision
            • The Optic nerve travels to the optic chiasm. A tumour on the pituitary gland may press on the optic chiasm
          • Positive Tinel Sign
            • The nerves from the arms go under ligaments in order to innervate the hands and fingers
            • With increased bone growth, however, the nerves become entrapped and sensitive
            • Percussing on wrists elicits a sensation of tingling in the distribution of the nerve (i.e. fingers)
            • This also serves as a good test for Carpal Tunnel Syndrome in which the median nerve is compressed at the wrist

          Clinical relevance in a pair of Identical twins

          • Two twins are shown to the right; one develops acromegaly and gigantism, the other one did not.
          • There’s a marked height difference when GH overproduction occurs before epiphyseal plate closure
          • Also note: difference in hand size, width of feet, tongue size (macroglossia) and extra space between teeth

          Tests in Mr. A

          • IGF-1 > 1600
            • normal levels are age- and gender-dependent
            • In this patient, normal range is 115-307
          • GH = 93.3 (Normal adult male = 0.02-1.70)
          • Glucose Tolerance Test: GH down to 74
            • GH went a bit down, but normally it should go below 1
            • NB: GTT is less useful here, as patient already presents with HIGH glucose (HbA1c = 10.3%).
          • Patient presents with poorly controlled diabetes, symptoms suggestive of acromegaly, High GH, and High IGF-1
            • Leading to a diagnosis of Acromegaly
          • Since the source of the problem is usually in the pituitary, perform a pituitary MRI to explore treatment options

          Therapeutic Options for Acromegaly

          • Goal is to control tumour growth and IGF-1 and GH levels
          • Transsphenoidal Surgery involves resecting the GH-producing adenoma
            • Results vary based on the size of the adenoma; the larger it is, the worse the outcomes.
          • Medical Therapy
            • initiated if surgery is contraindicated. For example, if the adenoma has grown around a major artery
            • Somatostatin analogues (suppression of GH production)
            • Pegvisomant (GH Receptor antagonist)
              • if GH production cannot be adequately controlled, you can still limit effect of GH on liver, and thereby reduce IGF-1 production
              • it binds to GH Receptor
              • Will still have high GH, but it does not result in high IGF-1
          • Radiotherapy
            • not selective; a crude treatment.
            • Side effects:
              • patient often sustain diffuse damage to pituitary cells, and develop pituitary insufficiency.
              • may also affect blood vessels around, thereby increasing the risk for stroke.

          Treatment of Acromegaly

          • Transsphenoidal Surgery is first line treatment.
          • If surgery is contraindicated, then medical treatment is second line:
            1. Somatostatin analogues → suppress GH production.
            2. Pegvisomant → GH receptor antagonist, reducing effect of GH.
