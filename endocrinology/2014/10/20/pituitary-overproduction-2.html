        Pituitary Overproduction II

        James Jae Monday, October 20, 2014

        Examples of Pituitary Overproduction

        1. Acromegaly
        2. Prolactinoma/Hyperprolactinemia
        3. Cushing’s Syndrome

        Prolactinoma

        Normal Physiology

        • Prolactin (PRL) is a reproductive hormone synthesized by Lactotroph cells, distributed throughout pars distalis of the anterior pituitary.
        • Normal values: <20μg/l in adults (usually <15 in men).
        • PRL secretion is under inhibitory control from the hypothalamus (Controlling agent = Dopamine)
          • Dopamine works by stimulating the pituitary lactotroph D2 receptor to inhibit adenylyl cyclase and consequently inhibits both PRL synthesis and secretion.

        • Normal physiological hyperplasia of Lactotrope cells occurs in pregnancy
          • PRL is involved in secretory cell differentiation for milk production
          • PRL axis is further activated by suckling
          • Suckling increases estrogen (E2), which plays a feed forward effect to further increase TRH/PRF and increase prolactin secretion from anterior pituitary
          • Therefore, lack of suckling (Hypothalamic and Pituitary inhibition) and lack of dopamine release (Hypothalamic inhibition) result in diminished PRL secretion

        Clinical Corollary

        Moms with postpartum depression have elevated levels of dopamine. This excess dopamine impairs prolactin release from anterior pituitary, which in turn, reduces their milk production.

        • Lactotroph Hyperplasia may also occur in:
          • Estrogen Treatment → by increasing PRL gene expression
          • Some cases of Cushing’s Disease
          • Primary Hypothyroidism

        Prolactinoma

        • Most common functional adenoma of the pituitary.
        • A condition characterized by a grossly elevated serum PRL
        • Most common in women and peak in child-bearing years
        • In women, 95% are Microadenomas (<1cm diameter)
        • Macroadenomas (>1cm diameter) are more frequent in men.
          • This is not because of any underlying physiological difference; rather, it’s because these are typically diagnosed later.
        • What is the clinical relevance of distinguishing between Microadenomas and Macroadenomas?
          • Recall that there is a 8-10 mm distance between the pituitary and optic chasm.
          • Therefore, you need significant growth in the pituitary before it can reach optic nerves and cause visual field defects.
          • Usually, Microadenomas won’t be able to reach the optic chasm.
          • However, Macroadenomas can reach the optic chasm or start compressing other hormone-active tissues within the pituitary and cause hormonal insufficiencies.

        Symptoms

        • Can be sub-divided into symptoms related to compression, problems related to a decrease in the secretion of other hormones, or problems related to an increase in PRL.
        • Post-menopausal women: usually asymptomatic
        • Pre-menopausal women: Hypogonadism (oligomenorrhea or amenorrhea), Galactorrhea (30‐70%), Infertility.
        • Prolactinomas may have variable presentation, depending on their activity:
          • 20-50 μg/l: decreased progesterone and short luteal phase
          • 50-100 μg/l: amenorrhea or oligomenorrhea
          • 100 μg/l: overt hypogonadism, (E2 decrease, symptomatic)
          • >200 μg/l: often a sign of macroprolactinoma

        Mechanism of Amenorrhea in Premenopausal Women

        • Elevated PRL → Inhibition of GnRH → Inhibition of LH and FSH
        • Lab results will reveal no compensatory increases of LH and FSH in response to low estrogen levels. This is suggestive of secondary hypogonadism.
        • Men: Low testosterone symptoms such as decreased libido, impotence, infertility, gynaecomastia, osteopenia, galactorrhea
          • Similar to in women, in men, testosterone is low because elevated PRL inhibits GnRH, which reduces stimulation of LH/FSH.
        • Both sexes:
          • Visual disturbances and headache
          • Problems related to decrease of other hormones

        Causes

        • Hypothalamic Disease (Tumor, infiltrative disease, cranial radiation)
        • Pituitary Disease
          • Prolactinoma (MEN1), acromegaly, pituitary stalk section, other tumors, infiltrative disease, germinoma (intrasellar)
        • Pituitary adenoma (microprolactinoma or macroprolactinoma)
        • Interference with portal blood flow in pituitary stalk
        • Drugs (i.e. Dopamine Receptor Antagonists)
        • Chronic renal failure or Cirrhosis
        • Primary Hypothyroidism (TRH stimulates prolactin secretion)
        • Idiopathic

        Diagnosis

        • Serum Prolactin → this can be take at any time, although the PLC level can be increased due to stress.
          • If the PRL is > 200, then it’s suggestive of a lactotroph macroadenoma (or pregnancy).
        • Based on history, a pituitary MRI should be considered.

        Diagnosis of a Prolactinoma

        • Serum Prolactin.
        • Based on history, a pituitary MRI should be considered.

        Treatment

        • Rationale for Treatment:
          • Existing or impending neurologic symptoms due to adenoma
          • Hypogonadism or other symptoms due to excess PRL
        • Unlike with most other pituitary adenomas, surgery is not the first line treatment for a prolactinoma.
        • Dopamine-Agonists are the first choice of therapy. Start on it with gradually increasing dose. Dopamine agonists include:
          • Bromocriptine (Parlodel) → less expensive, should be used first in patients without drug coverage.
          • Cabergoline (Dostinex) → Cabergoline is marginally more effective than Bromocriptine, but much more expensive.
          • Quinagolide (Norprolac)
        • Since dopamine inhibits prolactin secretion, dopamine agonists are very effective!
        • Surgery is second line treatment, and involves removal of hormone producing tissue.
        • Radiation therapy may also be considered.

        Treatment of Prolactinomas

        1. Dopapine Agonists (Bromocriptine, Cabergoline, etc)
        2. Surgery is second line.

        NB: If prolactin increase is due to stalk compression by a non-functioning pituitary adenoma, the therapy will control symptoms related to prolactinoma, but the pituitary tumor will continue to grow.

        Cushing’s Syndrome

        • Review the Adrenal Overproduction lecture on Oct 15 for more thorough coverage of the topic.

        Pathophysiology

        • Overproduction of cortisol resulting in excess tissue exposure to cortisol
        • Reduced sensitivity to feedback inhibition by glucocorticoids
        • Disappearance of normal diurnal rhythm
        • Excess cortisol production in adrenal glands can be due to:
          1. Adrenal Cushing
            • Primary ACTH-Independent production of cortisol from adrenal glands
            • A compensatory decrease in CRH and ACTH in response to excess cortisol in circulation
          2. ACTH overproduction due to a pituitary pathology
            • elevated cortisol and ACTH with normal or diminished CRH response from the hypothalamus
          3. CRH overproduction stimulating ACTH due to a hypothalamic pathology (uncommon)

        Symptoms

        • Increased fatigue, Decreased libido, Weight gain, Irritability, Insomnia, Decreased concentration, Impaired short‐term memory, Lethargy, Menstrual changes

        NB: Know the most important presenting symptoms based on likelihood ratios of positive result

        • Ecchymoses (x 10‐11)
        • Osteopenia or fracture (x 8-14)
        • Weakness of proximal muscles (x 8-13)
        • Hypertension (x 4-5)

        Diagnostic steps for Cushing’s syndrome

        • Diagnose Cushing’s Syndrome by assessing cortisol level
        • Once cortisol over-production is confirmed, find the source of the problem based on ACTH level:
          • If ACTH Dependent, consider pituitary adenoma, ectopic ACTH or ectopic CRH production
          • If ACTH Independent, consider exogenous cortisol administration, adrenal adenoma, adrenal hyperplasia, or adrenal carcinoma

        Important

        • Most common cause of CS = exogenous cortisosteroids.
        • Most common cause of endogenous CS = pituitary adenoma.

        Cushing-related clinical entities (Ddx)

        • Sub-clinical Cushing’s syndrome or Preclinical Cushing’s syndrome
          • Adrenal adenomas showing autonomous cortisol secretion without specific endocrine symptoms
          • Typically discovered in patients with adrenal incidentalomas
        • Pseudo-Cushing
          • Presents with all or some clinical features of Cushing’s Syndrome, but not caused by a problem with the HPA axis
        • Metabolic Syndrome
          • A cluster of conditions (increased blood pressure, a high blood sugar level, excess body fat around the waist and abnormal cholesterol levels) that occur together.

        Pseudo Cushing States

        • Many features of Cushing’s syndrome
        • Hypothesized to be caused by increased neuronal CRH secretion
        • Heterogeneous group including:
          • chronic alcoholism
          • alcohol withdrawal syndrome
          • depression
          • poorly controlled diabetes mellitus
          • obesity

        *Prognosis of Cushing’s**

        • Left untreated, 5-year survival <50% due to cardiovascular complications (especially hypertension), thromboembolic processes, and infections
        • Symptoms disappear after 2-12 months of successful treatment (including osteoporosis)
        • Hypertension and impaired glucose tolerance may persist
        • 20% recurrence after successful initial treatment

        Screening Tests for Cushing’s

        1. 24 hour urine cortisol may pick up cortisol overproduction
        2. 1 mg overnight Dexamethasone-suppression test (DST) may pick up lack of normal suppression of ACTH/cortisol to dexamethasone
        3. 11 PM plasma/saliva cortisol may pick up lack of normal diurnal rhythm of cortisol
        • At least 2 out of 3 above screening tests have to be positive in order to establish a diagnosis of Cushing’s

        In the presented case, there is a high clinical suspicion for Cushing’s syndrome. Her ACTH level indicates a ACTH-dependent Cushing’s Syndrome. Therefore, a pituitary MRI is in order.

        Imaging in Cushing’s Syndrome

        • Recall that Cushing’s Disease = Cushing’s Syndrome due to a pituitary cause.
        • Non-functioning adenoma present on MRI or CT in 10% of healthy subjects
        • Cushing’s disease: presents as a micro-adenoma in 80-90% of patients (In 40 %, no abnormalities are even visible on CT or MRI).
        • MRI has higher specificity and sensitivity than CT, making the former imaging modality more useful for neurosurgeons.

        Treatment Plan

        • Ketoconazole (most effective inhibitor of steroid hormone biosynthesis in Cushing’s) helps reduce endogenous cortisol production
        • Request urgent transsphenoidal pituitary surgery
        • Post-operatively, start steroids in order to avoid adrenal crisis from acute glucocorticoid withdrawal

        Treatment of Cushing’s Disease

        • Transsphenoidal Pituitary Surgery
          • Ketoconazole should be used leading up to the surgery to inhibit cortisol production.
          • Corticosteroids should be given post-operatively to avoid the risk of an adrenal crisis.
