            Pituitary Overproduction II

            James Jae Monday, October 20, 2014

            Pituitary Overproduction II

            Examples of Pituitary Overproduction:

            1. Acromegaly
            2. Prolactinoma/Hyperprolactinemia
            3. Cushing’s Syndrome

            2. Prolactinoma

            Normal Physiology

            • Prolactin (PRL) is a reproductive hormone synthesized by Lactotrope cells, distributed throughout pars distils of the anterior pituitary
            • Normal values: <25μg/l in adults (usually <15 in men)
            • PRL secretion is under inhibitory control from the hypothalamus (Controlling agent = Dopamine)
              • Dopamine works by stimulating the pituitary lactotroph D2 receptor to inhibit adenylyl cyclase and consequently inhibits both PRL synthesis and secretion.
            • Normal physiological hyperplasia of Lactotrope cells occurs in pregnancy
              • PRL is involved in secretory cell differentiation for milk production
              • PRL axis is further activated by suckling
              • Suckling increases estrogen (E2), which plays a feed forward effect to further increase TRH/PRF and increase prolactin secretion from anterior pituitary
              • Therefore, lack of suckling (Hypothalamic and Pituitary inhibition) and lack of dopamine release (Hypothalamic inhibition) result in diminished PRL secretion >Clinical Implication of PRL Loop >Moms with postpartum depression have elevated levels of dopamine. This excess dopamine impairs prolactin release from anterior pituitary, which in turn, influences mom’s nursing with babies
            • Lactotroph Hyperplasia may also occur in:
              • Estrogen Treatment – by increasing PRL gene expression
              • Some cases of Cushing’s Disease
              • Primary Hypothyroidism

            Prolactinoma

            • A condition characterized by a grossly elevated serum PRL
            • Most common in women and peak in child-bearing years
            • In women, 95% are microadenomas
            • Macroadenomas are more frequent in men

            Symptoms

            • Post-menopausal women: usually asymptomatic
            • Pre-menopausal women: Hypogonadism (oligomenorrhea or amenorrhea), Galactorrhea (30‐70%), Infertility

            Variable Presentation of Prolactinoma

            20-50 μg/l: decreased progesterone => short luteal phase 50-100 μg/l: amenorrhea or oligomenorrhea 100 μg/l: overt hypogonadism, (E2 decrease, symptomatic)

            Mechanism of Amenorrhea in Premenopausal Women

            Elevated PRL => Inhibition of GnRH => Inhibition of LH and FSH Lab results reveal no compensatory increases of LH and FSH in response to low estrogen levels. This is suggestive of secondary hypogonadism

            • Men: Low testosterone symptoms such as decreased libido, impotence, infertility, gynaecomastia, osteopenia, galactorrhea
            • Both sexes:
              • Visual disturbances and headache
              • Problems related to decrease of other hormones

            Causes

            • Hypothalamic Disease (Tumor, infiltrative disease, cranial radiation)
            • Pituitary Disease
              • Prolactinoma (MEN1), acromegaly, pituitary stalk section, other tumors, infiltrative disease, germinoma (intrasellar)
            • Pituitary adenoma (microprolactinoma or macroprolactinoma)
            • Interference with portal blood flow in pituitary stalk
            • Drugs (i.e. Dopamine Receptor Antagonists)
            • Chronic renal failure or Cirrhosis
            • Primary Hypothyroidism (TRH stimulates prolactin secretion)
            • Idiopathic

            Diagnosis

            -Based on plasma prolactin that can be take at any time, although the PLC level can be increased due to stress -Based on history, a pituitary MRI should be considered

            Treatment

            Indicated for: - Existing or impending neurologic symptoms due to adenoma - Hypogonadism or other symptoms due to excess PRL

            First Choice of Therapy: Dopamine-Agonists – Bromocriptine (Parlodel) – Cabergoline (Dostinex) – Quinagolide (Norprolac)

            Surgery involves removal of hormone producing tissue

            - NB: if prolactin increase is due to stalk compression by a non-functioning pituitary adenoma, use of dopamine-agonists can control symptoms related to prolactinoma, but the pituitary tumor will continue to grow regardless

            3. Cushing’s Syndrome

            -Review the Adrenal Overproduction lecture on Oct 15 for more thorough coverage of the topic

            Pathophysiology

            • Overproduction of cortisol resulting in excess tissue exposure to cortisol
            • Reduced sensitivity to feedback inhibition by glucocorticoids

            • Disappearance of normal diurnal rhythm

            • Excess cortisol production in adrenal glands can be due to:
              1. Adrenal Cushing
                • Primary ACTH-Independent production of cortisol from adrenal glands
                • A compensatory decrease in CRH and ACTH in response to excess cortisol in circulation
              2. ACTH overproduction due to a pituitary pathology
                • elevated cortisol and ACTH with normal or diminished CRH response from the hypothalamus
              3. CRH overproduction stimulating ACTH due to a hypothalamic pathology (uncommon)

            Symptoms

            – Increased fatigue – Decreased libido – Weight gain – Irritability – Insomnia – Decreased concentration – Impaired short‐term memory – Lethargy – Menstrual changes

            NB: Know the most important presenting symptoms based on likelihood ratios of positive result

            • Ecchymoses (x 10‐11)
            • Osteopenia or fracture (x 8-14)
            • Weakness of proximal muscles (x 8-13)
            • Hypertension (x 4-5)

            Diagnostic steps for Cushing’s syndrome

            • Diagnose Cushing’s Syndrome by assessing cortisol level
            • Once cortisol over-production is confirmed, find the source of the problem based on ACTH level:
              • If ACTH Dependent, consider pituitary adenoma, ectopic ACTH or ectopic CRH production
              • If ACTH Independent, consider exogenous cortisol administration, adrenal adenoma, adrenal hyperplasia, or adrenal carcinoma >- Most common cause of CS: exogenous cortisol >- Most common cause of endogenous CS = pituitary adenoma ###Cushing-related clinical entities (Ddx)###
            • Sub-clinical Cushing’s syndrome or Preclinical Cushing’s syndrome
              • Adrenal adenomas showing autonomous cortisol secretion without specific endocrine symptom
              • Discovered in patients with adrenal incidentalomas
            • Pseudo Cushing
              • presents with all or some clinical features of Cushing’s Syndrome, but not caused by a problem with the HPA axis
            • Metabolic Syndrome
              • a cluster of conditions (increased blood pressure, a high blood sugar level, excess body fat around the waist and abnormal cholesterol levels) that occur together

            Pseudo Cushing States

            • many features of Cushing’s syndrome
            • hypothesized to be caused by increased neuronal CRH secretion
            • Heterogeneous group including:
              • chronic alcoholism
              • alcohol withdrawal syndrome
              • depression
              • poorly controlled diabetes mellitus
              • obesity

            Prognosis

            • Left untreated, 5-year survival <50% due to cardiovascular complications (especially hypertension), thromboembolic processes, and infections
            • Symptoms disappear after 2-12 months of successful treatment (including osteoporosis)
            • Hypertension and impaired glucose tolerance may persist
            • 20% recurrence after successful initial treatment

            Screening Tests for Cushing’s

            A. 24 hour urine cortisol

            • cortisol overproduction

            B. 1 mg overnight Dexamethasone-suppression test (DST)

            • lack of normal suppression of ACTH/cortisol to dexamethasone

            C. 11 PM plasma/saliva cortisol

            • lack of normal diurnal rhythm of cortisol

            In the presented case, there is a high clinical suspicion for Cushing’s syndrome. Her ACTH level indicates a ACTH-dependent Cushing’s Syndrome. Therefore, a pituitary MRI is in order

            Imaging in Cushing’s Syndrome

            • Non-functioning adenoma present on MRI or CT in 10% of healthy subjects • Cushing’s disease: presents in 80-90% micro adenoma (In 40 %, no abnormalities visible on CT or MRI) • MRI has higher specificity and sensitivity than CT, making the former imaging modality more useful for neurosurgeons

            Treatment Plan

            • Ketoconazole (most effective inhibitor of steroid hormone biosynthesis in Cushing’s) helps reduce endogenous cortisol production
            • Request urgent transsphenoidal pituitary surgery
            • Post-operatively, start steroids in order to avoid adrenal crisis from acute glucocorticoid withdrawal
