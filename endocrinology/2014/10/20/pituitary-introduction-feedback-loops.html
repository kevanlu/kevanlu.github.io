        Pituitary Introduction & Feedback Loops

        Kevan Lu Monday, October 20, 2014

        Pituitary Anatomy, Embryology, Histology, & Common Pathology Notes

        • See Pituitary Anatomy, Embryology, Histology, and Common Pathology Notes.

        Introduction

        • Pituitary problems require a different type of thinking compared to primary problems.
        • This lecture will review the feedback loops that we’ve seen previously, and will discuss how to integrate them into pituitary pathologies.

        Pituitary Anatomy

        • The pituitary gland is small → 10-15 mm, and 0.5 grams.
        • It’s connected to the hypothalamus by the pituitary stalk, though which hypothalamic hormones are secreted.
          • These hormones are present in very low levels peripherally, which is why we always measure the pituitary hormone (eg. ACTH, TSH), versus the hypothalamus hormone (eg. CRH, TRH).
        • Located at the base of the brain in the sella turcica (a depression formed by the sphenoid bones).
        • Lies close to the optic chiasm and cavernous sinuses.
          • Pituitary adenomas can compress the optic chiasm and cause vision problems.
          • Generally, faster growing tumours are higher risk; slower growing ones give time for the bone to remodel to compensate.

        • The pituitary is divided into anterior and posterior lobes:
          • The Anterior lobe has a portal venous vascular supply.
          • The Posterior lobe has an arterial vascular supply.
        • Because the anterior lobe is supplied by veins, which are more compressible, it’s more susceptible to ischemia. This is known as Sheehan Syndrome.
          • This is classically seen with low blood pressure due to blood loss after pregnancy.

        Remember

        • The anterior lobe of the pituitary has a portal venous vascular supply.
        • The posterior lobe of the pituitary has an arterial vascular supply.
        • Because the venous system is more compressible with low blood pressure, the anterior lobe is more susceptible to ischemia.

        Pituitary Embryology

        • This is sort of like the pancreas, where we had two distinct components coming together.
          • The anterior pituitary, also known as the adenohypophysis, arises from an upward extension from the developing oral cavity, known as the Rathke pouch.
          • The posterior pituitary, also known as the neurohypophysis, is a downward growth of axons from the hypothalamus.
        • These two growths meet, merge, and form the pituitary gland.

        Remember

        • The anterior pituitary grows up from the oral cavity.
        • The posterior pituitary grows down from the hypothalamus.

        The Pituitary Stalk

        • The magnocellular neurons that project into the posterior pituitary travel from the hypothalamus through the pituitary stalk.
        • The parvicellular neurons that indirectly innervate the anterior pituitary secrete hormones into the hypophyseal portal system. This also travels through the pituitary stalk.
        • Hence the pituitary stalk is comprised of two components → a neuronal component that feeds the posterior pituitary, and a vascular component that feeds the anterior pituitary.

        Pituitary Histology

        Anterior Pituitary

        • Architecturally, the anterior pituitary has an acinar/nested pattern, which can be seen with a reticulin stain.
          • These acini are lost with a pituitary adenoma.
        • The anterior pituitary is full of secretory cells, coloured differently depending on the hormones they produce.
        • Broadly speaking, they can be classified as (1) acidophils (pink cells), (2) basophils (blue cells), or (3) chromophobes (pale cells).
          • Acidophils include:
            1. Somatotrophs → secrete Growth Hormone.
            2. Mammotrophs → secrete Prolactin.
          • Basophils include: This was why Primary Adrenal Insufficiency (with high ACTH) resulted in hyperpigmentation.
            1. Corticotrophs → secrete Pro-opiomelanocortin (POMC).
              • Recall that POMC was the precursor polypeptide that was split into multiple protein products, including (1) ACTH and (2) MSH (Melanocyte Stimulating Hormone).
            2. Thyrotrophs → secrete TSH.
            3. Gonadotrophs → secrete FSH.
          • Chromophobes are any of the above cells, but they have been inactivated so that they produce few/no hormones.

        Cells Types of the Anterior Pituitary

        Acidophils Basophils Chromophobes
        Somatotrophs (GH) Corticotrophs (POMC → ACTH)  
        Mammotrophs (PRL) Thyrotrophs (TSH)  
          Gonadotrophs (FSH)  

        Posterior Pituitary

        • As befitting its embryological origin, the posterior pituitary is composed primarily of (1) neuronal axonal processes extending from the hypothalamus, and (2) pituicytes, which are modified glial cells*.
          • The neuronal processes have neurosecretory granules, which contain the hormones involved in the posterior pituitary axes:
            1. ADH
            2. Oxytocin.
          • The pituicytes function to support the neurons.

        Cell Types of the Anterior Pituitary

        1. Neuronal Cell Processes
          • From magnocellular neurons.
          • Contain neurosecretory granules which release ADH and Oxytocin.
        2. Pituicytes
          • Modified glial cells.
          • Support the neurons.

        Clinical Manifestations of Pituitary Disease

        • This can manifest in 3 ways:
          1. Hyperfunction → too much hormone.
          2. Hypofunction → too little hormone.
          3. Local Mass Effects → if there’s a growth.
        • Much of the material covered after this is a review of the feedback loops within the pituitary, so I’ll only mention the important material:
          1. Prolactin regulation is unique compared to the other pituitary axes because it’s the only hormone that’s negatively regulated by the hypothalamus (via dopamine).
            • So if you have a tumour that leads to stalk compression, reducing dopamine secretion into the pituitary, you’ll end up with elevated prolactin.
          2. Pituitary adenomas are classified as either a micro-adenoma, or a macro-adenoma.
            • Micro-adenoma → < 1 cm.
            • Macro-adenoma → > 1 cm.
            • 1 cm is the approximate threshold for where stalk compression and hormone suppression occurs.
          3. Mass effects from larger adenomas can lead to a number of symptoms:
            • Headache, nausea, and vomiting, due to increased intracranial pressure.
            • Decreased visual fields, due to compression of the optic nerves.
            • If there’s a hemorrhage or infarction, you can have a subarachnoid hemorrhage-like headache.
          4. Histologically, pituitary adenomas will be comprised of a single cell type, with a loss of the normal acinar pattern seen in normal anterior pituitary.
            • Normal pituitary is seen on the top right, with acidophils, basophils, and chromophobes.
            • A pituitary adenoma is seen on the bottom right, with a loss of the normal acinar pattern, and a single cell type.
          5. Very rarely, pituitary adenomas can be more aggressive, and may show signs of central necrosis, brain invasion (very unusual), or malignant transformation (very very rare).

        Remember

        • Micro-adenoma → < 1 cm.
        • Macro-adenoma → > 1 cm.
