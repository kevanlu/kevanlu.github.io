        Adrenal Overproduction

        Vince Le Wednesday, October 15, 2014

        Adrenal Gland


        The adrenal glands control the body’s response to stress, adjust blood pressure, and helps maintain the body’s water, sodium, and potassium levels.

        They consist of an out adrenal cortex (cortex means outer layer) and an inner adrenal medulla (medulla means middle). Functionally and anatomically, the adrenal gland is two glands packaged together. The cells of the cortex and endocrine and the cells of the medulla are neurosecretory. These cells look distinct from one another and embryologically, they come from different tissues. They also secrete different hormones: the adrenal cortex produces steroid hormones and the adrenal medulla produces catecholamines.

        The adrenal cortex consists of three zones:

        • The zona glomerulosa secretes mineralocorticoids such as aldosterone

        • The zona fasciculata secretes glucorticoids such as cortisone

        • The zona reticularis secretes sex steroids such as androgens and estrogens


        Cushing’s Syndrome ===================

        Cushing’s syndrome describes the signs and symptoms resulting from long-term exposure to high levels of cortisol.

        Note: Cushing’s disease is a cause of Cushing’s syndrome characterised by increased secretion of adrenocorticotropic hormone (ACTH) from the anterior pituitary.


        Diagnoses of Cushing’s Syndrome ==============================

        Potential diagnostic tests for Cushing’s Syndrome Include:

        • 24-Hour Urinary Free Cortisol (UFC): Cortisol level above normal limit confirms that cortisol secretion is elevated

        • Overnight Dexamethasone Suppression Test (DST): A cortisol level > 50 nmol/L at 8am shows loss of sensitivity to negative feedback

        Dexamethasone is a steroid that provides negative feedback to the pituitary gland to suppress ACTH secretion

        • Late-night salivary cortisol level testing: Cortisol level > 4 nmol/L shows loss of normal diurnal rhythm of cortisol

        The Late-night salivary cortisol level testing has to account for the patient’s normal day/night cycle. Night shift workers may have a different cycle


        Determining ACTH Dependent vs Independent Cushing’s Syndrome =======

        After diagnosing Cushing’s syndrome, determine if there the cause is ACTH dependent.

        • ACTH dependent causes include ectopic ATCH production by the pituitary or ectopic corticotropin-releasing hormone (CRH) by the hypothalamus.

        • ACTH independent causes include adenomas, hyperplasia, and carcinomas.


        Adrenalectomy ===

        After surgery to remove adrenal mass, give cortisol treatment to prevent an adrenal crisis because the normal feedback system for cortisol will still be suppressed for ~8 months.


        Primary Hyperaldosteronism ===

        Primary hyperaldosteronism is characterized by the overproduction of the mineralocorticoid aldosterone by the adrenal glands.

        This occurs in 5-10% of patients with hypertension. The aldosterone overproduction is not suppressible and can result in hypokalemia.


        Diagnoses of Hyperaldosteronism ===

        To diagnose primary hyperaldosteronism, levels of both aldosterone and renin have to be measured. A high plasma aldosterone to renin ratio suggests that the elevated aldosterone levels are no longer renin dependent.

        A 24-hour urine analysis showing high levels of potassium loss with low sodium secretion is also suggestive of hyperaldosteronism.


        Pheochromocytoma ===

        A pheochromocytoma is a neuroendocrine tumour of the medulla of the adrenal glands that secretes high amounts of catecholamines, mostly norepinephrine and some epinephrine.

        The signs and symptoms of pheochromocytoma include:

        • Spell-related: Headache, palpitations, tachycardia, sweating, pallor, anxiety, diaphoresis, dyspnea, nausea, increased blood pressure

        • Chronic: Sustained hypertension, anxiety, fatigue, cold hands, headache, orthostatic hypertension, fever, nausea, vomiting, sweating


        Diagnoses of Pheochromocytoma ===

        The fractionated plasma free metanephrines (measures norepinephrine and epinephrine) test has a 99% sensitivity for pheochromocytoma and the urinary VMA test has a 95% specificity


        Treatment of Pheochromocytoma ===

        Surgery is the first choice of treatment.

        However, you must give pre-treatment before the surgery otherwise the surgery could cause a huge release of epinephrine and cause a hypertensive crisis. So you have to prevent the patient from being sensitive to the release of catecholamines. One strategy is to block the receptors that the epinephrine would work on. First you give an alpha blockade, then a beta blockade.

        So before surgery of an adrenal mass, you must either exclude a pheochromocytoma or give alpha and beta blockade.
