        Metabolic Bone Disease

        Tehmina Ahmad Thursday, October 09, 2014

        Metabolic Bone Disease Notes

        • See Metabolic Bone Disease Notes.

        Lecture Objectives

        1. Describe the pathophysiology of osteomalacia, osteoporosis and Paget’s Disease.
        2. Describe therapeutic options for osteomalacia, osteoporosis and Paget’s Disease.

        Osteomalacia

        • Definition: A defect in bone matrix mineralization.
          • In childhood, prior to the closure of the epiphyseal plates, this leads to rickets.
          • In adults it is known as osteomalacia.
        • A child with rickets can be seen to the right.

        Clinical Features

        • Mineralization defects result in weak bones and fractures.
        • Proximal muscle weakness (myopathy) can be severe, leading to falls.
        • Malabsorption syndromes (celiac disease patients) often also have or develop osteomalacia.

        Pathophysiology

        • Osteomalacia has several etiologies such as:
          1. Vitamin disorders → Vitamin D deficiency is the most common cause.
          2. Mineral deficiencies
          3. Disorders of bone matrix
          4. Inhibitors of bone mineralization

        1. Vitamin Disorders

        • Vitamin D is essential for calcium absorption.
        • Main source is sunlight.
        • Calcium intake can prevent secondary hyperparathyroidism and reduce bone resorption.
        • Older individuals have difficulties getting enough Vitamin D.
        • Broadly speaking Vitamin D intake can be limited by:
          1. Impaired formation or processing of Vitamin D.
          2. Lack of sunlight exposure.
          3. Inadequate Vitamin D intake .
          4. Inconsistent patient compliance with Vitamin D.
          5. Drug metabolism interference.
        • Causes of decreased Vitamin D include:
          1. Insufficient sunlight exposure.
          2. Nutritional deficiency.
          3. Malabsorption.
          4. Hydroxylation defects.
          5. Nephrotic Syndrome.
          6. Liver Disease (unable to perform the 25-hydroxylation).
          7. Chronic Renal Failure (unable to perform the 1α-hydroxylation).
          8. Anticonvulsant Therapy.

        Clinical Note: Daily intake of Vitamin D

        • The new 2010 recommended daily allowance (RDA) is 600 IU for those 1-70 years of age and pregnant or breastfeeding women, and 800 IU for those over 71 years of age.
        • An adequate blood level of vitamin D is 20ng/mL, which can be achieved through daily skin exposure to sunlight.
        • Multivitamin pills may contain 200-400 IU
        • Calcium tablet containing vitamin D may have from 120 to 400 IU
        • Supplements contain 400 and 1000 IU / tablet

        2. Mineral Deficiencies

        • Calcium deficiency.
        • Adults require 1000mg-1200mg of calcium a day >19 years of age
        • Phosphate deficiency → decreased GI absorption or increased renal loss

        3. Disorders of Bone Matrix

        • Nothing more was said on this topic, just know it can play into the etiology of osteomalacia.

        4. Inhibitors of Bone Mineralization

        1. Aluminum.
        2. Bisphosphonates.
          • Bisphosphonates function to reduce bone turnover, but first generation bisphosphonates like etidronate actually inhibit mineralization if used for too long.

        A Clinical Approach to Osteomalacia

        History

        • Dietary history.
        • Alcohol history.
        • Significant weight loss within the last 6 months.
          1. > 10% loss of body weight.
          2. Either % ideal weight or % usual weight.
          3. Beware of patients with ascites/oedema, which adds weight (think of cirrhotic patients with portal hypertension).

        Investigations for Osteomalacia

        • Laboratory: ↓Ca, ↓ PO4, ↑alkaline phosphatase, ↓ urinary Ca.
        • Radiology: Looser-Milkman pseudofractures → thought to be healed microfractures
        • Bone biopsy: usually not necessary but considered gold standard.

        Treatment for Osteomalacia

        • Depends on the underlying cause.
        • Vitamin D supplementation.
        • PO4 supplements if low PO4.
        • Ca supplements if low Ca.

        Paget’s Disease

        • Definition: A metabolic disease characterized by excessive bone destruction and repair; a localized disorder of bone remodeling.

        Clinical Features

        • Usually asymptomatic → routine X-ray finding or elevated alkaline phosphatase
        • Severe bone pain → pelvis, femur, tibia from lytic lesions that heal into sclerotic areas
        • Skeletal deformities → bowed tibia (saber shins), kyphosis, frequent fractures
        • Skull involvement → headaches, increased hat size, deafness, frontal bossing
        • Increased warmth over involved bones due to increased vascularity

        Some of the key clinical findings in Paget's disease, due to the accelerated breakdown and regrowth of bone. Top left: lytic lesions that heal into sclerotic areas. Top right: skull enlargement. Bottom left: frontal bossing. Bottom right: saber shins (bowed tibias).

        Clinical Note: Paget’s Disease

        • Paget’s disease is very common: affecting 5% of the population as a whole and 10% of the population > 80 years of age.
        • Second most common bone disease after osteoporosis

        Pathophysiology

        • Initiated by increased osteoclastic activity leading to increased bone resorption.
        • Osteoblastic activity increases in response to produce new bone that is structurally abnormal and fragile.
        • Initiated by localized increases in osteoclast-mediated bone resorption with subsequent compensatory osteoblast increases in bone formation.
        • Results in a disorganized mosaic of woven and lamellar bone
        • This structural change produces bone that is:
          1. expanded in size.
          2. less compact.
          3. more vascular.
          4. more susceptible to deformity or fracture.

        Investigations for Paget’s Disease

        • Laboratory:
          • ↑↑↑ alkaline phosphatase
          • Alkaline Phosphatase is a marker of bone resorption!
          • Normal or ↑ Ca, normal PO4.
          • ↑ Urinary hydroxyproline (indicates lots of bone resorption).
        • Imaging:
          • Evaluate extent of disease with bone scan
          • Initial lesion may be destructive and radiolucent.
          • Involved bones are expanded and denser that normal.
          • Multiple fissure fractures in long bones.

        Remember

        • Alkaline Phosphatase is a marker of bone resorption!
        • Hence, patients with Paget’s disease will have very high alkaline phosphatase levels.

        Complications of Paget’s Disease

        • Fractures.
        • Hypercalcemia and nephrolithiasis (kidney stones).
        • Cranial nerve compression if skull involvement → deafness.
        • Spinal cord compression.
        • Osteosarcoma (1-3%) indicated by marked bone pain, new lytic lesions and increased alkaline phosphatase.
        • High output congestive heart failure due to increased vascularity.

        Treatment of Paget’s Disease

        • Treat the symptoms → analgesias for pain relief
        • Bone protection → bisphosphonates → alendronate, risedronate, zoledronate.
          • Unlike osteomalacia, where the issue is that the bones are too soft, here it’s that the bone turnover is too high.
          • Hence, where in osteomalacia, bisphosphonates are contraindicated, since they inhibit mineralization somewhat, here they’re critical, since they reduce turnover.
        • Calcitonin.

        Osteoporosis

        Osteoporosis

        • Definition: A progressive systemic skeletal disease characterized by decreased bone mass and microarchitectural deterioration of bone tissue leading to increased in bone fragility and risk of fracture.

        Clinical Features

        • Commonly asymptomatic
        • Pain, usually backache
        • Height loss → collapsed vertebrae, kyphosis
        • Fractures → hip, vertebrae, wrist

        Clinical Note: Osteoporosis

        • 1 in 4 women and 1 in 6 men > 50 years of age have osteoporosis
        • Total of 1.4 million Canadians are affected by osteoporosis and 2 million people are at increased risk of developing osteoporosis
        • < 20% of people who fracture go on to be assessed for osteoporosis (more than 79% are not diagnosed or treated for osteoporosis)
        • Of the 30,000 hip fractures per year in Canada, 70% are due to osteoporosis
        • Mortality rate is 20% higher than average within one year of hip fracture
        • By age 80, half of all women will have a vertebral fracture
        • << 500 grams of Ca (bone mass) is the threshold for fracture

        Pathophysiology

        • Over time, both men and women lose bone mass.
        • RANK ligand is released from osteoblasts, and it functions to initiate precursor osteoclasts to mature.
        • When PTH increases, it stimulates the release of RANK ligand from osteoblasts, increasing the number of osteoclasts, and hence the resorption of calcium.
        • Estrogen binds RANK ligand, limiting it’s release and hence limiting resorption by osteoclasts.
          • As a result, women lose bone mass faster than men due to menopause, because they no longer produce estrogen.
          • Men are able to maintain bone mass longer than women due to testosterone conversion to estrogen and because they have a larger amount of bone mass to lose.

        Clinical Note: Osteoporosis Primary vs. Secondary

        • Primary Osteoporosis:
          • Usually in women, within 20 years of menopause.
          • Typically due to the mechanism described above; estrogen binds RANKL, so without estrogen production, osteoclast resorption increases.
          • Affects mainly trabecular bone.
        • Secondary Osteoporosis:
          • Endocrinopathies
            1. Hyperparathyroidism.
            2. Hyperthyroidism.
            3. Premature menopause or in men hypogonadism .
            4. Diabetes (this is still in up in the air).
          • Malignancy
          • Gastrointestinal disease
          • Drugs
            1. steroids
            2. phenytoin
          • Other
            1. Rheumatoid arthritis
            2. Renal disease
            3. Poor nutrition

        Fragility Fractures in Osteoporosis

        • Definition: A fracture that occurs spontaneously or following a minor trauma such as:
          1. Fall from standing position (e.g. walking on ice)
          2. Fall from sitting position
          3. Fall from bed (< 1 metre high)
          4. Fall after having missed 1–3 steps in a staircase
          5. After a movement outside of the typical plane of motion
          6. From coughing

        Risk Factors for Fractures

        • Fragility fracture after the age of 40
        • Parental history of hip fracture
        • Premature menopause
        • Glucocorticoid use (> 7.5 mg/d) > 3 months in the prior year
        • Lifestyle factors: smoking, excessive alcohol, and physical inactivity
        • Weight loss since age 25 >10%
        • Poor nutrition, calcium intake, vitamin D status
        • Recurrent falls

        Investigations for Osteoporosis

        • Laboratory: Ca, PO4, alkaline phosphatase are usually normal unless secondary osteoporosis
        • Imaging: Bone densitometry.
          • Dual xray absorptiometry (DEXA) most useful but single energy xray absorptiometry, ultrasound and quantitative CT also used.
          • Lumbar spine and femur sites tested and compared to controls.

        Treatment of Osteoporosis

        1. Prevention and lifestyle modifications:
          • Weight bearing exercise.
          • Calcium → Total daily intake of Ca supplementation to 1200 mg/day.
          • Vitamin D → 400-1000 IU/day if < 50 years of age, 800-2000IU/day. if > 50 years of age*.
          • Limit smoking and alcohol.
        2. Measures to decrease further bone loss or resorption:
          • Bisphosphonates → inhibitors of osteoclast
          • Denosumab → monoclonal antibody against RANK ligand and inhibits osteoclasts
          • Selective estrogen receptor modulators → SERM’s
          • Postmenopusal estrogen replacement.
          • Calcitonin → decreases osteoclast activity.
        3. Measures to increase bone mass:
          • Recombinant parathyroid hormone → teriparatide.
          • Teriparatide is identical to a portion of human parathyroid hormone (PTH) and intermittent use activates osteoblasts more than osteoclasts, which leads to an overall increase in bone.

        Clinical Note: Fracture Risk Assessment

        1. Low peak bone mass → small Caucasian or Asian female (<57kg or BMI <20), family history.
        2. Previous fragility fracture → vertebral, hip, wrist
        3. Estrogen related bone mass → early menopause, oophorectomy, amenorrhea, in men, hypogonadism.
        4. Advanced age.
        5. Secondary to medical disease → Hyperparathyroidism, Hyperthyroidism, Anticonvulsants, Glucocorticoids, Cancer chemotherapy, Malabsorption.
        6. Other → Diet, Smoking, Alcohol, Minimal weight bearing activity
