            Pharmacology of T2DM

            Keegan Guidolin Monday, October 27, 2014

            Type 2 diabetes results from two contributing factors:

            • insulin resistance
            • β-cell dysfunction (impaired insulin secretion)

            Drugs to fight T2DM are targeted towards these factors.

            Antihyperglycemic Drugs

            Insulin Sensitizers

            Biguanides

            Members: Metformin Mechanism:

            • Activates AMP-dependent protein kinase → block fatty acid breakdown, inhibit hepatic gluconeogenesis/glycogenolysis
            • Increases activity of glucose transporters → increases glucose uptake in skeletal muscle and adipose tissue
            • Increases insulin responsiveness

            Adverse Effects:

            • Lactic acidosis - ↓ metabolic acid metabolism = ↑ lactate
              • Don’t use in liver, heart, lung, or kidney failure, severe infection, or alcohol abuse
            • GI - anorexia, nausea, abdominal discomfort, falutulence, diarrhea

            Notes: Does not produce hypoglycemia, reduces weight gain

            Thiazolindinediones

            Members: Pioglitazone, rosiglitazone Mechanism:

            • Binds PPAR-γ →↑ transcription of insulin-sensitive genes
            • Targets adipocyte
            • ↑ glucose uptake, fatty acid uptake/utilization
            • ↓ secretion of adipocytokines (inhibit insulin), glucose output from liver, plasma glucose

            Adverse Effects:

            • Weight gain (via fat deposition and fluid retention)
            • Heart failure in at risk patients (due to fluid retention)

            Notes:

            • Action is indirect/slow - may take ~3 months for max effect
            • Monotherapy or combined with metformin or sulfonylurea

            Insulin Secretatgogues

            ###Sulfonylureas Members:

            • 1st Generation: Chlorpropamide, Tolbutamide
            • 2nd/3rd Generation: Glyburide, Glimepiride, Gliclazide
              • More potent, longer action (due to active metabolites) than 1st Generation

            Mechanism:

            • Blocks K+ channel in pancreatic β-cell → Membrane depolarization → Ca2+ channels open → ↑ Ca2+ → Insulin secretion

            Adverse Effects:

            • Weight gain (due to decreased basal metabolic rate)
            • Hypoglycemia (extension effect)
            • Hasten β-cell loss (due to increased work)
            • Worsened cardiac ischemia (mechanism unknown)

            Meglitinides

            Members: Repaglinide, Nateglinide

            Mechanism:

            • Same as SUs, different binding site on same subunit of K+ channel
            • ↑ insulin release in post-prandial phase
            • Efficacy < SUs

            Adverse Effects:

            • Hypoglycemia, weight gain (< SUs)

            Notes: Produced by molecular manipulation of 2nd Generation SUs

            Incretins

            Direct

            Members: Exenatide, Liraglutide

            Mechanism:

            • Stimulates insulin release
            • Inhibits glucagon release
            • Primes pancreas for insulin release
            • ↓ appetite, gastric motility, acid secretion, weight

            Adverse Effects: Nausea/vomiting, pancreatitis, medullary carcinoma (few reports)

            Notes: Human GLP-1 is effective, but rapidly metabolized by DPP-4; direct incretins are analogs of GLP-1 found in the saliva of Gila Monsters

            Indirect

            Members: Sitagliptin, Saxagliptin, Linagliptin

            Mechanism: Block destruction of endogenous incretins (GLP-1) by DPP-4; mechanism otherwise identical to direct incretins

            Adverse Effects: Few reports of pancreatitis, decreased renal function

            Notes: Less potent than direct incretins with no effect on weight

            α-Glucosidase Inhibitors

            Members: Acarbose

            Mechanism: Prevent absorption of glucose from intestine

            Adverse Effects: Flatulence, abdominal distention, diarrhea (due to fermentation of unabsorbed sugar)

            Notes: Adverse effects result in poor patient uptake

            Sodium Glucose Transporter type 2 Inhibitors

            Members: Canagliflozin

            Mechanism: Prevents glucose reabsorption in the nephron

            Antihypoglycemic Drugs

            • Glucagon
            • Diazoxide
              • Inhibits insulin release (activates K+ channel in pancreatic β-cells)
