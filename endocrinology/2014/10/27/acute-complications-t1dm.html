            Acute Complications of T1DM

            Kevan Lu Monday, October 27, 2014

            Lecture Objectives

            1. Discuss the pathogenesis of Diabetic Ketoacidosis (DKA) and Hyperosmolar Hyperglycemic State (HHS) (prev. known as hyperosmolar nonketotic coma (HONK)).
            2. List the precipitating factors and describe the clinical features of these conditions.
            3. Discuss the management and complications of these conditions.
            4. List the risk factors for the development of cerebral edema during the management of DKA.

            Important

            • Review the Acid/Base and ABG Material!
              • Acid/Base → Acid/Base Physiology.
              • Arterial Blood Gases → Blood Gas Analysis.

            Review of Some Key Equations

            1. Anion Gap
              • Measured cations - measured anions.
              • → normally 12.
            2. Expected osmolality
              • .
              • ‘Two salts and a sugar bun’.
              • If there’s a large gap between the expected osmolality and the measured osmolality, then it’s more suggestive of methanol or ethylene glycol poisoning.
            3. Serum Sodium Correction
              • Increased plasma glucose affects serum sodium levels because it draws out water into the ECF.
              • Recall that this was pseudohyponatremia.
              • To compensate for this, you correct the serum sodium by adding 1.6 mmol/L for every 5 mmol/L ↑ in glucose above 5.
              • So if the glucose is 30, increase the ‘true’ Na+ by 1.6 × 5, or 8 mmol/L.

            Introduction

            • DKA → Diabetes Ketoacidosis.
            • HHS → Hyperosmolar Hyperglycemic State.
            • Both DKA and HHS are diabetic emergencies.
            • They are two extremes in the spectrum of hyperglycemia.
              • DKA is usually a T1DM phenomenon.
              • HHS is usually a T2DM phenomenon.
              • However, there is overlap between the two.
            • The fundamental difference between the two is that DKA arises from an absolute insulin deficiency, while HHS arises from a relative insulin deficiency.
            • Diabetic Ketoacidosis
              • High blood glucose.
              • High anion gap acidosis.
              • Ketonemia.
            • Hyperosmolar Hyperglycemic State (HHS)
              • Very high BG/serum osmolality.
              • Minimal or no ketoacid accumulation.
              • Neurologic Abnormalities.
            • Neurologic abnormalities are seen more in HHS because the higher osmolality pulls more water out of the brain.

            Diabetic Ketoacidosis

            • DKA → Diabetes Ketoacidosis.
            • The most serious metabolic complication of T1DM.
            • Insufficient circulating insulin and excess counter-regulatory hormones.
            • Leads to an overproduction of ketone bodies and an anion gap Metabolic Acidosis.
            • Although it’s more common in T1DM, it’s not pathognomonic of T1DM.
              • This can also occur in T2DM, because a particularly high glucose level can be toxic to β-cells.
              • High catecholamines may also suppress insulin release.
            • Generally has a lower mortality rate than HHS, due to the absence of neurologic symptoms.

            Pathogenesis

            • This should be review.
            • Recall that:
              • Insulin suppresses gluconeogenesis, glycogenesis, increases peripheral glucose uptake, and inhibits glucagon secretion.
              • Glucagon has the opposite effects.
            • Hence, with low insulin, glucagon is less inhibited, resulting in less glucose storage and more plasma glucose → hyperglycemia.
            • Insulin also stimulates GLUT-4 mediated uptake of glucose into tissues, so without insulin, you can’t utilize your glucose.
              • Ketones are then used as an alternate source of energy → oxidized for ATP.
            • Ketogenesis occurs as follows:
              1. Decreased insulin leads to increased fat lipolysis.
              2. This results in increased Free Fatty Acids.
              3. These FFAs are converted to ketones in the liver.

            Hyperosmolar Hyperglycemic State

            • HHS → Hyperosmolar Hyperglycemic State.
            • Less common, on a continuum with DKA (since they differ in their relative levels of insulin).
            • Here, the insulin level is too low to utilize glucose, but high enough to restrain lipolysis and ketogenesis:
              • The insulin level is too low to utilize glucose, so you end up with glycosuria, osmotic diuresis, H2O loss, and an increase in electrolyte concentration.
              • The insulin level is high enough to restrain lipolysis & ketogenesis. Since we don’t have lipolysis, we don’t have the increase in free fatty acids. As a result, we don’t have ketone synthesis.
            • Hence, they key differentiating feature is the absence of ketones.
            • Another key differentiating feature is the presence of neurological complications → seizures, stroke.
              • As mentioned before, this is due to the fact that the osmolarity is higher in HHS, meaning that more water is drawn out of the brain.

            Precipitating Factors

            • Below are some precipitating factors for acute complications of diabetes.
            • A good way to remember these are things that start with I:
              • Insufficient Insulin (new onset, inadequate education, insulin pumps, eating disorders, fear of hypoglycemia, rebellion)
              • Infection
              • Infarction (stroke, MI)
              • Intoxication (alcohol)
              • Iatrogenic (drugs: steroids, thiazides, sympathomimetics)
              • Injury (trauma)
              • Inflammation (pancreatitis)

            Clinical Presentation

            • Split these into symptoms based on (1) hyperglycemia, (2) neurologic complications, and (3) metabolic acidosis.
            • Hyperglycemia:
              • Low insulin → increased plasma glucose.
              • Results in polyuria, polydipsia, weight loss.
            • Neurologic Complications:
              • Due to increased osmolality (especially in HHS), drawing water from the brain.
              • Presents with lethargy, focal signs, obtundatation (decreased level of consciousness), seizures, coma.
            • Acidosis
              • Abdominal pain, nausea, vomiting, delayed gastric emptying.
              • Kussmaul’s breathing due to compensatory respiratory alkalosis.
            • In DKA, you can sometimes smell acetone in the patient’s breath.
              • Will smell fruity, or like nail polish.

            Clinical Approach

            • History & Physical Exam:
              • ABCs → these patients are usually acutely sick.
              • Mental status → neurologic symptoms are suggestive of HHS.
              • Cardiovascular, renal status → especially urine output to assess polyuria.
              • Precipitants → are they sick.
              • Hydration → are they drinking to compensate for their fluid losses?
            • Lab Evaluation:
              • Blood gases.
              • Electrolytes, plsama glucose, BUN, creatinine.
              • Serum & urine ketones.
              • Urinalysis.
              • CBC, EKG.
            • Depending on the history, consider cardiac enzymes, blood/urine cultures, CXR, etc.

            Measuring Ketones

            • There are two methods for measuring ketones:
              1. Serum βOH butyrate → looks for βOH butyrate.
              2. Urine Dipstick Test → looks for acetoacetate/acetone only, but not βOH butyrate.
            • Importantly, βOH butyrate is the predominant ketone in DKA.
            • Over time, it gets metabolized into acetoacetate and acetone.
            • Hence, since the urine dipstick measures a metabolite, there’s a lag time between urine dipstick values and ‘true’ serum ketones. Which means that:
              • Urine ketones may be negative early in DKA.
              • Urine ketones may remain positive early in treatment.
            • Then why not always use the serum βOH butyrate?
              • It’s not available at all labs.
              • But if you’re in a large tertiary care centre, this is the best choice.

            Treatment

            1. Fluid → restore circulation, perfusion.
            2. Insulin → halt ketone production.
            3. Replace → electrolyte deficits.
            4. Treat → precipitating causes.
            5. Monitor → response to treatment and for complications of therapy.

            1. Fluids

            • Give fluids to compensate for the osmotic diuresis.
            • Start with normal saline; go slowly to prevent the risk of cerebral edema or CHF (especially in children).
            • Just like we saw in GU, monitor the osmolality of the urine and compare it with what you’re putting in, to determine whether you’re increasing or decreasing the serum osmolality.

            2. Electrolytes

            • The main issue here is potassium.
            • Potassium is often seen to be high on presentation, but this is due to low insulin. Recall that insulin and catecholamines are used to shift K+ with hyperkalemia.
              • Recall that insulin shifts K+ from the ECF into the ICF.
              • As a consequence, when the insulin is low, you have a false high extracellular K+.
            • Hence, you may have a measured high K+, but an actual low total body K+.
            • Hence, you only supplement low potassium:
              • If K+ > 5, you don’t treat it.
              • If K+ is from 3.3-5, then add 20-30 mEq/L KCl.
              • If K+ < 3.3, then add 40 mEq/L KCl.
            • With high potassium, the insulin should naturally bring it down.

            3. Insulin

            • Lowers blood glucose, stops lipolysis, inhibits glucagon.
            • Intravenous (IM), not SC (patients have hypoperfusion).
            • Monitor hourly.
              • Don’t write nomograms or sliding scales, don’t rely on nursing staff to call you with abnormal values (they are busy too). Check each value yourself and come back to examine the patient frequently.
            • Maintain blood glucose within 7-14.
            • If BG falls low, Do NOT stop insulin infusion; instead increase dextrose in IV.
              • Otherwise DKA could recur.
            • Delay giving the insulin bolus if the serum K is low.
              • As mentioned above, insulin shifts K+ from the ECF into the ICF.
              • So if the serum K is already low, it’ll fall even lower.

            Aside

            • What about using bicarbonate to treat the acidosis?
            • Very controversial; thought to actually slow recovery.
            • In general, don’t do it; consider only if the patient is severely acidotic (pH < 7).

            Stopping Treatment for DKA

            • You can stop IV insulin and return to SC insulin at the following criteria:
              • Glucose < 11, HCO3 > 18, pH > 7.3, normal anion gap.
            • It’s best to use the anion gap to determine when to stop IV insulin → ketones and pH may lag behind.

            Complications with Treatment

            1. Hypoglycemia → if you overdo the insulin.
            2. Hypokalemia → too much insulin, or insufficient replacement of K+ beforehand).
            3. Hyperglycemia/Recurrent DKA → if the IV insulin is stopped too early.
            4. Hyperchloremic Acidosis → NAGMA, excessive NaCl administration, ketones excreted with Na or K in urine → excess Cl.
            5. Cerebral Edema → probably due to rapid osmotic movement of water into the CNS due to rapid drops in plasma osmolality.
              • Much more common in children, rare in adults.
              • Most common cause of death in children with DKA → there’s a 40-90% mortality rate (!).
              • Correct fluids slowly!
              • Typically presents 4-12 hours after the onset of treatment, with headache, deterioration or consciousness, slowing of the HR, or an ↑ BP.

            Risk Factors for Cerebral Edema

            • Younger age.
            • New diagnosis of DM.
            • Severity of the acidosis.
            • ↓ pCO2.
            • ↑ BUN.
            • Treatment with HCO3.
            • Rapid rate of fluid resuscitation.

            Key Points in Treating Acute Complications of Diabetes

            1. The patient needs fluid, but not too much fluid! Give it slowly.
            2. Although the measured K+ may be normal, the patient is K+ depleted, because of ICF→ECF shifts.
            3. Never stop the insulin → if the glucose falls too low, instead add more dextrose to IV.
