        Introduction to T2DM – Physiology & Epidemiology

        John Paul Larkin Monday, October 27, 2014

        Lecture Objectives

        1. Identify associations with the population epidemic of Type 2 DM.
        2. Identify underlying pathophysiologic changes associated with Type 2 diabetes.
        3. Define the glucose values that diagnose diabetes.
        • Type 2 DM is impossible to avoid as a physician.
        • It exemplifies interactions between (1) genetics, (2) insulin resistance and (3) environment that we have been considering.

        Introduction to Diabetes

        Definition

        • Diabetes can be diagnosed with any of the following:
          1. Random blood glucose of ≥ 11.1 mM with hyperglycemic symptoms:
          2. Fasting blood glucose of ≥ 7 mM with hyperglycemic symptoms.
          3. Oral Glucose Tolerance Test (OCTT) with 2 hour BG ≥11.1 mM
          4. HbA1C ≥ 6.5% → used to be 7% (literally last year), now 6.5%.
            • This is a new criteria, and it’s only applicable in adults, and not the pediatric population.
        • Some hyperglycemic symptoms include polyuria, polydipsia, nocturia, etc.
        • You need 2 positive findings, either:
          1. Two different positive tests.
          2. Two positives for the same test.
        • You can’t do these tests when the patient is under stress (ie, when they’re sick.)
        • Also, as a practical matter, it’s important to let your patients get insurance before you diagnose them, otherwise they’re irrevocably fucked!

        Definition of Diabetes

        • Diabetes can be diagnosed with any of the following:
          1. Random blood glucose of ≥ 11.1 mM with hyperglycemic symptoms:
          2. Fasting blood glucose of ≥ 7 mM with hyperglycemic symptoms.
          3. Oral Glucose Tolerance Test (OCTT) with 2 hour BG ≥ 11.1 mM
          4. HbA1C ≥ 6.5%.
        • You need 2 positive findings, either:
          1. Two different positive tests.
          2. Two positives for the same test.

        • These numbers were based on studies looking at the the presence/absence of diabetic retinopathy.
        • That’s because that’s an easily assessible sign, unlike something like vascular damage.
        • The graph on the right shows that the onset of diabetic retinopathy is around an HbA1C of 6.5%.
