            T1DM Focal Cases

            Kevan Lu Thursday, October 30, 2014

            Case #1

            Case Summary

            A 9-year-old girl with Type 1 Diabetes for 3 years is on an insulin regimen of Novorapid 8 units and NPH 12 units before breakfast; Novorapid 6 units before supper and NPH 8 units at bedtime. She has consistently high blood glucose levels in the range of 12 – 20 mmol/l before breakfast with some ketones present in the urine before breakfast. The rest of the day her blood sugars are all around 4-7 mmol/l and her ketones are negative.

            • Novorapid (Insulin Aspart) is a short-acting insulin.
              • Onset → 10 minutes.
              • Lasts → 2 hours.
            • NPH is an intermediate-acting insulin.
              • Onset → 4 hours.
              • Lasts → 8 hours.
            • Lantus (Glargine) is a long-acting insulin.
              • Onset → a few hours.
              • Lasts → 24 hours.

            Questions

            1. Describe the 2 most likely causes of her high morning sugars?
              1. Dawn Phenomenon → cortisol is higher in the morning, raising sugars. Here, it’s because her NPH at bedtime is too low to account for the dawn phenomenon.
              2. Somogyi Phenomenon → rebound hyperglycemia after you have hypoglycemia overnight. Here, it’s because her NPH at bedtime is too high overnight, causing her glucose to rebound in the morning.
            2. What questions about symptoms would you ask to help you determine which of these 2 events is occuring?
              • These two phenomena have the opposite etiology, so you have to come up with differentiating factors to decide your diagnosis.
              • If she’s hypoglycemia overnight, she may have nocturia, diaphoresis, disorientation, nightmares, headaches in the morning.
            3. What would you advise her to do if doubts still exist as to the cause?
              • Check her sugars overnight.
              • If her sugars are low, then it’s the Somogyi Phenomenon; otherwise, it’s the Dawn Phenomenon.
            4. What changes would you recommend to her insulin regimen?
              • Depends on what the etiology is:
                • Dawn Phenomenon → raise her bedtime NPH.
                • Somogyi Phenomenon → drop her bedtime NPH.

            Remember

            • The two most common causes for high morning sugars are:
              1. Dawn Phenomenon → cortisol is high in the morning, which can raise morning glucose. Due to her night-time insulin being insufficient.
              2. Somogyi Phenomenon → rebound hyperglycemia after you have overnight hypoglycemia. Due to her night-time insulin being excessive.

            Case #2

            Case Summary

            A 15-year-old girl is a picky eater. She was diagnosed with T1DM at age 8 and is currently on an insulin pump of rapid-acting insulin with increasing administration at mealtimes. Since starting high school her mother has noticed she has been skipping lunch and she also doesn’t like to check her glucose levels at school in front of her friends. She has been having hypoglycemic spells 3 to 4 times a week due to not completing her meals. Her A1c today is 9.8%.

            • Here, the patient clearly has poor control; HbA1c > 7.
            • She also has phases where she goes hypoglycemic, because she isn’t finishing the meals that correspond to her insulin boluses.

            Questions

            1. How does an insulin pump work? What are its advantages over MDI?
              • Subcutaneous cathether inserted.
              • Basal infusion of insulin over the day.
              • When you’re eating a meal, you input the carbohydrate value into the pump, and it’ll deliver a catered insulin bolus.
              • Advantages of a pump over Multiple Daily Injection (MDI):
                • Less disruption to lifestyle → no need for needles.
                • More flexibility over what you eat.
                • Better control over blood glucose.
              • Diadvantages of an Insulin Pump:
                • You have to carry it around with you.
                • The pump is more expensive → although Ontario does pay for the pump, and subsidize the cost of supplies.
            2. What are some reasons for poor glycemic control in adolescents?
              • Peer pressure → embarrassed about checking her glucose levels in front of friends.
              • Rebellion, resistance to authority.
              • Adolescents think they’re invincible.
              • Puberty increases insulin resistance due to hormonal changes.
              • Patients may have body image issues, changing their diet.
            3. What options are available to prevent hypoglycemia in this teenager?
              • Adjusting dosing to avoid hypoglycemia. It might seem logical, but some patients don’t think of it.
              • Counselling, patient education.
            4. List three rescue options for hypoglycemia.
              1. Glucose Gel → good for patients with gastric emptying because you can hold it in your mouth where it can be absorbed.
              2. Glucagon Kit → solution with a powder that is mixed and injected. When you prescribe it, a pharmacist will teach the patient (and their family) how to administer it.
              3. Glucose tablets → or just some candy or another readily available source of glucose.
            5. Our patient wants to start driving as soon as she turns 16. How would you counsel her?
              • She needs to manage her hypoglycemic spells.
              • Her glucose needs to be over 5 to drive.
                • If it’s less than 5, take some carbohydrates.
                • Re-measure glucose level in 15 minutes.
                • You actually have to wait 1 hour after glucose has normalized before you can drive.
              • Tell her that if she gets into a car accident due to her low sugars, then it could harm not only herself, but also others.

            Case #3

            Case Summary

            Mr. Bruce Holt is a 37-year-old man who has had Type 1 diabetes for 27 years. His current insulin regime is 5 units Novorapid and 22 units NPH at breakfast, 7 units Novorapid at supper and 15 units NPH at bedtime. He is doing blood glucose testing 3-4 times daily and has very variable control with values ranging from 2 to over 20 mmol/l.His HbA1C value is 11.0%.

            Questions

            1. What does the HbA1C value indicate and what is the optimal target range for this man?
              • HbA1C reflects long-term blood sugar levels, so a high level indicates poor compliance.
              • It’s actually weighted towards more recent blood sugar levels.
              • The optimal target range is <7%.
            2. List 3 changes to his insulin regime aimed at optimizing his glycemic control.
              • Patient is taking both fast-acting and long-acting insulin at breakfast.
              • Patient is not taking anything at lunch.
              • This technically isn’t a MDI regimen → an MDI regimen consists of 4 injections/day, with short-acting injections before meals, and long-acting injections before bedtime.
              • Some possible changes:
                1. Patient could consider going on an MDI regimen.
                2. Patient could consider switching to an insulin pump.
                3. Could implement an insulin:carbohydrate ratio to dose-adjust their insulin level.
            3. What characteristics would indicate that an insulin pump would be beneficial for Mr. Holt?
              • He has Type 1 diabetes, so it’ll be subsidized in Ontario.
              • He has suboptimal glucose control.
              • He has a lot of variability in his glucose levels → an insulin pump has finer control.

            Case #4

            Case Summary

            An 11-year-old boy presents with polyuria and polydipsia. A random blood glucose is 25.4 mmol/l. Urine is negative for ketones but 3+ for glucose

            Questions

            1. List and describe 3 demographic factors that may help distinguish Type 1 from Type 2 diabetes.
              1. Age → it’s more likely to be Type 1 if <9, and more likely to be Type 2 in older patients. It does get a bit murkier in older patients; it’s actually possible to be Type 1 in a patient that’s 40.
              2. Ethnicity → First Nations, African Americans, Asians, Hispanics, are at higher risk of Type 2 Diabetes.
              3. Family History → T2DM is more strongly genetically linked.
            2. List and describe 2 clinical features which may help distinguish Type 1 from Type 2 diabetes.
              1. BMI → BMI in the 85th %ile or higher, makes it more likely to be Type II.
              2. Skin Changes → Acanthosis nigricans is a marker of hyperinsulinemia as a result of insulin resistance. Hence, it’s more suggestive of T2DM.
            3. What lab tests can we use to help differentiate T1DM and T2DM?
              1. Anti-islet cells Antibodies.
              2. Glutamic acid decarboxylase autoantibody (GADA).
                • These are also targets of auto-antibodies in patients who develop T1DM.
              3. C-peptide Levels
                • C-peptide is part of the insulin protein, so this is a marker of endogenous insulin protein.
                • If this is low, it’s more suggestive of T1DM.

            Case #5

            The last part of this small group session is designed to give you the opportunity to test your skills at insulin dose adjustments. You are provided with blood glucose records for two patients. What insulin dose adjustments would you recommend for each patient?

            Patient #1

            • 8 y/o boy with T1DM, HbA1c = 8.4%.
            • Current insulin regimen is as follows:
            Breakfast Lunch Supper Bedtime
            Blood Glucose NR NPH NR NPH
            <4 mmol/L 3 16 2 8
            4-8 4 3
            8-12 5 4
            12-16 6 5
            16-20 7 6
            >20 8 7
            • Blood glucose levels over the past week:
              Breakfast Lunch Supper Bedtime
            Mon 4.2 6.8 12.3 16.7
            Tues 3.9 12.5 14.6 18.2
            Wed 6.8 8.5 9.2 12.6
            Thurs 3.2 6.9 8.5 4.7
            Fri 4.1 5.2 11.9 16.5
            Sat 2.8 11.4 13.4 8.4
            Sun 3.9 9.4 14.3 15.9

            Adjustments

            • The patient is on a sliding scale for Novorapid based on his glucose levels. He is on no NR at lunch, and he’s on NPH at bedtime.
            • Is the patient having any lows?
              • Yes, especially at the mornings.
              • Hence, we should reduce the bedtime NPH (we usually drop things by 10%).
            • Is the patient having any highs?
              1. Yes; their glucose levels before supper.
                • We probably can’t give them insulin at lunch because this is a 8-year old child who receives insulin from his parents.
                • So instead, we can increase their NPH at breakfast.
              2. Their glucose is also high at bedtime.
                • Increase supper dose of NR by around 10%.

            Patient #2

            • 15 y/o girl with T1DM; HbA1c = 7.1%.
            • Current insulin regimen is as follows:
              Breakfast Lunch Supper Bedtime
            Blood Glucose NR NR NR Lantus
            <4 mmol/L 6 14 18 42
            4-8 7 15 19  
            8-12 8 16 20  
            12-16 9 17 21  
            16-20 10 18 22  
            >20 11 19 23  
            • Blood glucose levels over the past week:
              Breakfast Lunch Supper Bedtime
            Mon 12.4 8.4 3.2 15.4
            Tues 18.4 2.6 4.5 16.4
            Wed 16.5 3.9 6.2 9.5
            Thurs 8.7 4.5 2.9 11.5
            Fri 6.4 9.7 8.4 7.8
            Sat 13.5 4.1 3.9 18.6
            Sun 15.2 3.2 4.7 13.5

            Adjustments

            • This patient is on a full MDI, with a long-acting insulin (Lantus, aka. Glargine) at night, and rapid-acting insulin before each meal.
            • Is the patient having any lows?
              1. At supper → also note that she’s consistently dropping from lunch to supper. So drop her lunch dose by 10%.
              2. At lunch → drop her breakfast NR by 10%.
            • Is the patient having any highs?
              1. At breakfast → treatment depends on whether it’s the Dawn phenomenon or the Somogyi phenomenon. Increase or decrease the Lantus depending on the cause.
              2. At bedtime → increase her supper NR by 10% (18-23, so by 2 units).

            Note

            • Whenever we’re adjusting a patient’s insulin, we always adjust up/down by around 10%!
