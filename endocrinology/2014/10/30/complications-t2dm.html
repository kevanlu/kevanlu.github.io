            Complications of T2DM

            Vince Le Thursday, October 30, 2014

            Diabetes Mellitus Type 2


            Type 2 diabetes is a metabolic disorder characterized by hyperglycemia in the context of insulin resistance and a relative lack of insulin. This differs from diabetes mellitus type 1 in which there is an absolute lack of insulin due to the destruction of beta cells in the pancreas.

            Common symptoms of diabetes mellitus type 2 include:

            • Polydipsa
            • Polyuria
            • Increased Hunger


            Complications of Diabetes Mellitus Type 2 ===

            Today, very few people die of diabetes and very few people die of the acute complications of diabetes. The problem is now the possibility of developing chronic complications of diabetes.

            There are two main categories of diabetes complications: acute and chronic.


            Acute Complications of Diabetes Mellitus Type 2 ===

            • Hyperosmolar nonketotic coma (HONK - more recently called Hyperosmolar Hyperglycemic State or HHS)
            • Hypoglycemia (when the patient is on insulin)


            Hyperosmolar Hyperglycemia State (HHS) —

            Occurs when a high level of glucose causes severe polyuria, dehydration, increases in osmolarity, and can lead to confusion, coma, or death.

            Ketosis does not occur because there is sufficient insulin to inhibit lipolysis but insufficient insulin to keep glucose under control.

            Patients generally present to the ER with:

            • Elevated blood glucose
            • Slightly decreased pH
            • Decreased Bicarb
            • Normal or minimally elevated ketons
            • Elevated anion gap

            There is usually some underlying cause of trigger such as:

            • Infection
            • MI
            • Stroke
            • Pancreatitis

            To treat for HHS, control ABC’s, give IV fluids, insulin, and look for cause


            Chronic Complications of Diabetes Mellitus Type 2 ===

            The main categories are:

            • Retinopathy
            • Nephropathy
            • Neuropathy
            • Associated Vasculopathies

            Or they can be categorized as:

            1. Microvascular (retinopathy, nephropathy, neuropathy)
            2. Macrovascular (stroke, MI, PVD)


            How does hyperglycemia cause DM Complications? —

            • Advanced Glycation Endproducts (AGEs) > Occurs intracellularly when glucose sticks to aminao acids, causing glycosylation of proteins which result in proteins which don’t function normally. Also the AGEs attach to their receptor and generate O2 species which results in inflammation

            • Oxidative Stress >Hyperglycemia depletes glutathione and forms free radicals (a bad thing)

            • Polyol (sorbitol) pathway > Excess glucose results in sorbitol, which causes intracellular oxidative stress as NADPH is needed in the production of sorbitol. The sorbitol then accumulates in tissues

            • Nitric oxide synthase
            • Cytokines, growth factors, inflammation


            Increased glucose also results in:

            • Increased protein kinase C enzyme
            • stimulates neovascular growth
            • increased cell death
            • increased epithelial cell proliferation
            • increased collagen synthesis
            • increased vascular endothelial growth factor
            • increased angiogenesis
            • increased nitric oxide that generates damage through making free radicals
            • increased IGF-1

            Conclusion: Elevated glucose is bad


            Retinopathy ===

            Occurs in most people with type 1 and 2 DM

            There are 3 types of retinopathy: - Background - Preproliferative - Proliferative


            Background and Pre-Proliferative Retinopathy —

            What is seen:

            • Capillary dilatations (microaneuryms)
            • Dot hemoorhages
            • Retinal infarcts (cotton wool spots)
            • Lipid exudates
            • Macular edema (the baddie in this group)


            Proliferative Retinopathy —

            What is seen:

            • Follows retinal ischemia
            • New blood vessels grow out into the vitreous
            • Very fragile vessels so they may break/blood


            Also, cataracts are more common in people with DM

            Screen for retinopathy with a yearly fundoscopy. Also, aim for a A1C below 7% (normal is up to 6%). This has been proven to decrease the risk of developing retinopathy by 30% and it decreases the risk of retinopathy progressing by 75%.


            Nephropathy ===

            Approximately 40% of people with Type 1 DM will develop nephropathy. The risk in Type 2 DM is less but it is still substantial.

            It starts with the capillary basement membrane thickening. Then the mesangial matrix accumulates in glomerulus and eventually fibrosis develops. This results in a loss of GFR and albuminuria occurs.

            Also, hypertension can occur during this process which further increases the renal destruction.

            Screen for nephropathy with BP checks, urine microalbumin/Cr ratio, and a 24 hour urine for protein if microalb/cr is high. An A1C below 7% decreases the risk of nephropathy by 30-50%.

            Also, ACE inhibitors are ARBs have been shown to decrease progression of nephropathy once it starts. Currently, studies are ongoing to test whether they can stop nephropathy from occurring in the first place.


            Neuropathy ===

            The vasa nervorum may be diseased just like any other vessel in DM. The nerves can partially demylinate and the axons can swell and degeneration.

            Some people with nerve damage may have no symptoms. Others may feel a tingling or numbiness in their feet which can progress to anesthesia of the feet, which is a bad.

            Other symptoms include:

            • Sudden onset diplopia
            • Microinfarct in nerve to eye muscle
            • Carpel tunnel syndrome
            • Impotence
            • Urinary retention
            • Constipation/diarrhea
            • Orthostatic hypotension
            • Decreased sweating
            • Sudden death due to cardiac autonomic neuropathy

            The only proven prevention intervention for neuropathy is glucose control. If A1C is kept at 7% of less, patients have a 60% decreased risk for developing neuropathy.


            Cardiovascular Complications ===

            Patients with DM have an increased risk of MI/Stroke/PVD. The most frequent cause of death in DM is a MI.
