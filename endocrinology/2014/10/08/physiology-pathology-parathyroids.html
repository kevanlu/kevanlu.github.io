                              Physiology & Pathology of the Parathyroids

                              Pei Jun Zhao Wednesday, October 08, 2014

                              Anatomy and Physiology

                              Average 4 glands behind thyroid, each about size of grain; brown/fatty vascularized tissue, encapsulated

                              • Chief cell – produce PTH
                              • Oxyphil (oncocytic) cell – large, eosinophilic (pink), granular cytoplasm; lower activity (but more mitochondria)
                              • Solid nests of cells separated by fibrous/fatty tissue

                              Electrolytes and Hormones

                              Calcium

                              Functions: signal transduction, membrane potential, muscle contraction, co-factor; tightly regulated - PTH receptor (GPCR)
                              Distribution: 99% in skeleton, 0.5% protein bound, 0.5% ionic; intake/loss about 1 g/day; low intracelluar, high extracellular

                              Parathyroid Hormone (PTH)

                              PTH: amino acid 1-34 polypeptide active (teriparatide) Effects

                              • bone turnover (osteoclast)
                              • renal Ca re-absorption (distal convoluted tubule) and phosphate secretion
                              • higher 1α-hydroxylase activity (increase calcitriol) → gut Ca absorption

                              chronic high PTH – bone resorption (osteoclasts)

                              PTH receptor mutations Activating – Jansen’s metaphyseal chondroplasia (dwarfism) Inactivating – Blomstrand’s chondroplasia (defective bone maturation, early death)

                              Ca sensing receptor mutation Inactivating – familial hypercalciuric hypercalcemia (FHH)

                              Vitamin D

                              important in rickets, osteomalacia, hypoPTH, and osteoporosis

                              • Steroid hormone (cholesterol based)
                              • ergocalciferol added to milk
                              • dihydrotachysterol used in nephrology
                              • UV: 85% from sun
                              • cholecalciferol based molecule
                              • 25-hydroxy vit D: by liver, major circulating form
                              • 1,25-dihydroxy vit D (calcitriol): by kidneys, activated vitamin D

                              Effects increase bone resorption/mineralization, Ca/PO4 absorption (GI) recommended 1000 IU/day

                              More Pharmacology

                              Bisphosphonates Incorporates into bone. Inhibits osteoclasts zolendronate – used for malignancy, osteolytic bone lesions Adverse effects: headache, rash, abdominal discomfort, MSK/bone pain, diarrhea, loss of taste, gastric irritation, osteonecrosis of jaw

                              Hormone Replacement Therapy estrogen to treat osteoporosis

                              Selective estrogen receptor modifier (SERM) raloxifene; mediates factors produced by osteoblast

                              Denosumab (Prolia) RANK-L inhibitor (receptor activator of nuclear factor κB ligand) – inhibit osteoclast action

                              Teriparatide (Forteo) recombinant PTH

                              Calcimimetic cinacalcet (Sensipar) – allosteric activator of Ca sensing receptor on parathyroid chief cells leading to increased PTH secretion

                              More

                              • Thiazide diuretics reduce Ca excretion
                              • loop diuretics increase Ca excretion
                              • glucocorticoids increase bone resorption
                              • fluoride in excess can lead to flurosis

                              Hyperparathyroidism

                              Symptoms: “abdominal groan, mental moan, breaking bone, kidney stone”

                              In secondary hyperparathyroidism: Osteititis fibrosa cystica and brown tumour of bone (hemorrhage)

                              1°: high PTH neoplasia (focal): adenoma (80%), carcinoma (<1%)

                              hyperplasia (15%) – multiple glands

                              • age, F:M = 3:1, 80% sporadic, 20% familial (MEN1/2A)
                              • treatment: subtotal parathyroidectomy (leave part of 1 gland)

                              2°: adaptive increase in PTH chronic renal failure → hypocalcemia, hyperphosphate → high PTH other: Ca/vit D deficiency, severe hypomagnesemia

                              Parathyroid adenoma

                              benign, F:M = 4:1, usually single gland pathology: clonal proliferation of chief cells, encapsulated, displace normal tissue treatment: surgery (check another gland to rule out asymmetrical hyperplasia)

                              Parathyroid carcinoma

                              epidemiology: uncommon, older individuals, severe hypercalcemia, larger, infiltrative (neck dissection harder) pathology: nests of malignant cells separated by fibrous bands; lymphovascular invasion treatment: surgery en bloc resection including ipsilateral thyroid, 50% cure, recurrences within 3 years

                              Hypoparathyroidism

                              Symptoms hypocalcemia – neuromuscular excitability, irritable/depression, calcification of basal ganglia, cardiac conduction problems low PTH and low Ca
                              Causes: usually iatrogenic, occasionally autoimmune, congenital agenesis (DiGeorge syndrome)
