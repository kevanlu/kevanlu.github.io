          Adrenal Focal Cases

          Karim Manji Tuesday, October 21, 2014

          Case #1

          Case Summary

          A 31-year-old woman was referred for treatment of hypertension, first noticed during her last pregnancy two years ago. The hypertension had become progressively worse and required increasing amounts of Propranolol and diuretics. During the work-up for hypertension it was found that she also had glucose intolerance. Initial investigations for the hypertension (done while she was off anti-hypertensive therapy) revealed slightly low potassium levels and elevated blood sugars but no other abnormality.

          On physical examination the patient was obese and had pre-auricular fat pads. The supraclavicular triangle was found to be bulging. She had chemosis and thin skin over the dorsum of her hands. There was minimal proximal muscle weakness in the legs. She had mild hirsutism and acne but no striae.

          Questions

          1. Give at least 2 examples of the effects of each type of hormone involved, and outline the pathophysiology of the symptoms and signs in this lady.
            • What points to Cushing’s Syndrome? (disease = ACTH overproduction, syndrome is any cause)
              • Glucocorticoid excess
                • Elevated blood sugars
                • Hypertension
                • Obesity
                • Muscle weakness
                • Thin skin
                • Chemosis (edema)
                • Bulging fat pads (supraclavicular and pre-auricular)
                  • Also dorsal (“Buffalo hump”) but she doesn’t have this
              • Mineralocorticoid excess (overwhelming MR by glucocorticoid) → not typical in mild Cushing’s
                • Low potassium
              • Adrenal androgens
                • Hirsutism and acne
          2. a) What initial test(s) would be most efficient in this situation? b) List 4 causes of a false positive screening test.
            • 24 hour urinary excretion of free cortisol
            • Overnight 1 mg dexamethasone suppression test
            • Salivary cortisol
            • False positives
              • Obesity
              • Birth control (affects the suppression test)
                • Increases bound cortisol, making it look like your total cortisol is high
                • So must do a test with free cortisol (24 hour urine or salivary)
              • Anticonvulsants that affect metabolism of dexamethasone
              • Pregnancy
              • Alcoholism
              • Depression (increases cortisol endogenously)
          3. How would you confirm the diagnosis?
            • Repeat the screening test
            • If you confirm Cushing’s syndrome, measure ACTH
            • If low, it’s from the adrenals/exogenous steroids
            • If it’s high, it’s from the pituitary/hypothalamus/ectopic
            • Based on that, you do imaging

          Case #2

          Case Summary

          A 42-year-old presents with weight gain of 20 pounds in the past year despite a decrease in her diet. Her husband tells her she doesn’t eat enough for a bird. She has been walking 30 minutes every day. She has otherwise been well. There is no significant family history.

          On examination she was 88 kg with a height of 154 cm.

          Discussion

          • Causes of weight gain in a woman
            • Hypothyroidism
            • Fluid retention (nephrotic syndrome, heart failure)
            • Pregnancy
            • Cushing’s
            • Acromegaly
            • Steroid ingestion
            • Hypoglycemia (if you have low blood sugars, you compensate by eating and some people overcompensate because they are afraid of going too low)
              • E.g. insulinoma causes chronic hypoglycemia

          Questions

          1. What other questions could you ask on history?
            • Constipation
            • Hair falling out
            • Menstrual irregularity
            • Dry skin
            • Cold intolerance
            • Location of weight gain (especially around the trunk)
            • Medications
            • Indication of fluid retention (swelling/edema)
            • Acromegaly: Big jaw, big hands, thick skin, deep voice, change in foot size, forehead getting bigger, big ring size, arthritis
            • Diet history
            • Activity history
            • Stress
          2. What other areas of the physical examination would you be sure to inspect?
            • Blood pressure
            • Skin (purple striae, thin skin, eecchymosis, pitting edema, cool/pale for hypothyroid, thick skin for acromegaly, acanthosis nigricans in insulin resistance)
            • Heart rate (slower for hypothyroidism)
            • Thyroid exam
            • Delayed relaxation phase of deep tendon reflexes (hypothyroidism)
              • Relaxation is ATP-dependent phase and since metabolism is slower in hypothyroidism, it will take longer (contraction is passive phase)
          3. List screening lab tests would you considering ordering and a reason for each.
            • Serum glucose
              • Also prolonged fast to test glucose and insulin levels (traditionally 72 hours)
            • TSH
            • β-HCG (for pregnancy)
            • Cortisol tests
            • IGF-1 levels for acromegaly (more stable than GH)
            • Complications of obesity: hyperlipidemia (cholesterol profile)
            • High blood pressure
              • Electrolytes and creatinine to check for hyperaldosteronism

          Case #3

          Case Summary

          A 41-year-old man is discovered to be hypertensive on a routine physical examination. He had been complaining of some muscle weakness but was otherwise asymptomatic. Physical examination was normal apart from his blood pressure (160/105). BP was repeated on several occasions and always measured above 150/95.

          Laboratory examination reveals:

          Lab Investigations

          • Na 138 mmol/L (135-145) normal
          • K 2.3 mmol/L (3.5-4.5) low
          • Cl 98 mmol/L (95-106) normal
          • HCO3 32 mmol/L (24-31) low

          Questions

          1. What is your differential diagnosis for hypertension?
            • Essential hypertension + diuretic = hypokalemia
            • Primary hyperaldosteronism
            • Cushing’s (severe leads to mineralocorticoid activity)
            • Secondary hyperaldosteronism (renal artery stenosis activates RAAS system)
            • Dutch liquorice
          2. What initial screening test would be most efficient in this situation?
            • Plasma aldosterone:renin ratio (will be elevated)
          3. Assuming the screening test is positive what further tests would you order to confirm the diagnosis?
            • Aldosterone after suppression with saline
              • Aldosterone raises BP, increases Na retention and decreases K
              • Suppression should shut down aldosterone in the body
              • Can do this by administering Florinef as well
            • Can give spironolactone to check if it helps
            • CT adrenals
            • Adrenal venous sampling to check differential aldosterone levels
            • Supine and standing aldosterone
              • Standing drops BP so aldosterone should go up reflexively
                • In primary hyperaldosteronism, it’s already so high that it can’t go any higher
                • From other causes, it will increase
                • Walk for about 2 hours
              • Supine raises BP

          Case #4

          Case Summary

          A 28-year-old man presents with a 6-month history of palpitations, shakiness, and sweating. These symptoms occur episodically starting suddenly and ending in a few minutes to an hour or so. They seem to be occurring more frequently.

          Questions

          • Differential Diagnosis:
            • Pheochromocytoma
            • Hyperthyroidism
            • Anxiety attacks
            • Caffeine withdrawal/excess
            • Alcohol withdrawal
            • Excess T4
            • Cocaine
            • Sympathomimetics (lots of ventolin)
            • Stress
            • Lack of eating (hypoglycaemia)

          Questions

          1. List 5 other questions that will be helpful in determining the cause for these symptoms and indicate how each would be helpful.
            • Drug use
            • Associated weight loss (points towards hyperthyroidism; weight gain points towards hypoglycaemia)
            • Family history of disease (pheochromocytoma can be familial)
            • Triggers? (stress, anxiety)
            • Dizziness on standing? (orthostatic symptoms)
            • Does it improve she you take food? (hypoglycaemia)
            • Sustained hypertension
            • Headaches?
          2. How would you determine whether a pheochromocytoma is causing his symptoms?
            • Plasma metanephrines
            • 24 urine test for metanephrines and catecholamines
            • Significant orthostatic hypotension
              • Usually so vasoconstricted that the body thinks there’s enough fluid and the body normally releases constriction making them volume depleted
                • α-blockers are first line
            • Check blood pressure when having an episode

          Case #5

          Case Summary

          Mary Brown, a 21-year-old law student, is very concerned because she has to shave her chin and upper lip on a daily basis and cannot wear a bikini without shaving her abdomen. The hair growth started when she was 14 years old and has gradually become worse. Her periods started at the age of 12 and have always been very irregular. She has never been on birth control pills. Her father is quite hairy and one of his sisters has facial hair.

          On examination she has significant terminal hair growth on her chin, upper lip, and in front of her ears. She has a male-pattern of hair growth on her abdomen with hair extending above the umbilicus. There is no clitoral hypertrophy and no other signs of virilization.

          Questions

          1. What are the 2 mostly likely diagnoses? Give justifications.
            • Started at puberty, gradual, family history
              • CAH (late onset, non-classic)
              • PCOS
          2. List 5 blood tests you would order.
            • 21-hydroxylase deficiency - 17-hydroxl progesterone will be elevated (precursor)
            • Testosterone - hirsutism
              • In women, it comes from the ovaries
            • Adrenal androgens
              • DHEA
              • Androstenedione
            • Estradiol (to check for regular periods)
            • Cushing’s (endogenous or exogenously?) → more for acute onset
            • Thyroid disease can give extra vellous hair (peach fuzz); also high prolactin
          3. What are 4 therapies you could offer Mary?
            • Cortisol replacement (for CAH adrenal suppression)
            • Mechanical hair removal (waxing, shaving, cream, laser/electrolysis)
            • Anti-androgens (e.g. spironolactone)
            • If she had PCOS and wanted to get pregnant, can use metformin or Clomid
              • Best treatment for PCOS is exercise and weight loss

          Case #6

          Case Summary

          A 37-year-old woman was admitted to hospital with severe weakness. Over the past year she noted increased pigmentation and freckling of the face, back, hands, elbows, and knees. She had a 15 pound weight loss, extreme weakness, abdominal pain, and anorexia. Upon standing she had lightheadedness. She has a past history of goitre and had complaints of cold intolerance, dry skin, somnolence, and constipation. There is no family history of endocrine disorders.

          Examination revealed a thin, pigmented, chronically ill woman. Blood pressure was 100/60 supine, and 0/0 standing (so orthostatically hypotensive that you couldn’t get a blood pressure). The pulse went from 84 to 120 on standing. Increased freckling and vitiligo were apparent around the eyes. No mucosal pigmentations were noted. The thyroid was slightly enlarged and firm. There was no costovertebral angle tenderness. She had dark pigmentation of the nipples. There was no axillary or pubic hair. Neurological examination was normal except for generalized muscle weakness. Deep tendon reflexes were symmetrical with normal relaxation phase. The rest of the exam was normal.

          Lab Values

          • Na 125 mmol/L(135-145) LOW
          • K 6.7 mmol/L(3.5-4.5) HIGH
          • Cl 90 mmol/L(95-106) LOW
          • HCO3 24.8 mmol/L (24-31)
          • Glucose 2.0 mmol/L (3.6-6.3) LOW
          • Creat 110 umol/L (70-120)
          • Urea 7 mmol/L (3.5-7.0)
          • TSH 7.3 mU/L (0.5-5.5) HIGH

          Serum calcium, magnesium and liver function tests were normal. Electrocardiogram showed peaked T waves. Chest X-ray was normal.

          Questions

          1. What is the likely diagnosis? Provide justifications.
            • Primary adrenal insufficiency
            • Hyperpigmentation (low cortisol → extra POMC secretion → raises ACTH (also stimulates MSH - melanocyte stimulating hormone)
            • Chronically ill appearance
            • Decrease in pubic and axillary hair
            • Weight loss
            • Weakness
            • Abdominal pain
            • Anorexia
            • Orthostatic hypotension
            • Vitiligo (autoimmune adrenal insufficiency) → Hashimoto’s thyroiditis
              • Past goitre and cold intolerance, dry skin, constipation
              • Firm thyroid
              • Reflexes are normal with normal relaxation phase, so not clinically hypothyroid
            • Lack of costovertebral angle tenderness (no bilateral adrenal - hemorrhage → Waterhouse-Friedrich syndrome)
            • Bloodwork
              • Low sodium, high potassium
              • Hypoglycemia (seen in either primary or secondary)
          2. What do the peaked T-waves signify?
            • Hyperkalemia (treat immediately!)
          3. How would you confirm the diagnosis?
            • Cortisol and ACTH (she’s very sick so you don’t need to do it at 8 am)
            • Outpatient: 8 am cortisol and ACTH stimulation test
          4. What is the most common cause of the disease in industrialized countries?
            • Autoimmune → Addison’s.
          5. What therapy would you initiate immediately?
            • Calcium gluconate for hyperkalemia
            • Hydrocortisone (high dose - 100 mg IV stat bolus, 100 mg every 6-8 hours)
              • Could give Florinef, but don’t need it with such high doses of steroids
                • Use it when tapered down hydrocortisone to 20-30 mg/day
            • Fluids (normal saline to get BP up)
            • Needs glucose
          6. Why is the TSH elevated?
            • Glucocorticoids inhibit TSH release so lack of glucocorticoids would cause an increase in TSH
          7. What 2 persistent findings would cause you to initiate therapy with the oral mineralocorticoid fludrocortisone (Florinef)?
            • Hyperkalemia
            • Hypotension

          Case #7

          Case Summary

          Oliver is a 2-week-old brought into the ER by ambulance. In the past 24 hours he has been vomiting and dehydrated and his mother found him unconscious in his crib. They are from a small town outside of London and Oliver was born via home birth and didn’t undergo any newborn screening. Oliver is her first child.

          Laboratory results show:

          Lab Investigations

          • Na 120 mmol/L(135-145)
          • K 7.5 mmol/L(3.5-5.0)
          • Glucose 2.7 mmol/L(3.4-7.0)
          • Cortisol 97 nmol/L(119-618)

          Questions

          1. Based on the lab findings, what is currently happening to Oliver?
            • Salt wasting crisis (from CAH)
              • Cortisol deficiency
              • Aldosterone deficiency
          2. Putting the history and lab findings together, what is your suspected diagnosis? List 3 underlying causes.
            • CAH, typically caused by mutations in 3 enzymes:
              • 21-hydroxylase deficiency → >90% of cases.
              • 11-hydroxylase deficiency → 10% of cases.
              • 3ß-hydroxylase steroid deficiency → 1% of cases.
          3. What would be the most common cause? What diagnostic test can you perform to confirm?
            • 21-hydroxylase deficiency (>91% cases of CAH, at least 75% of salt wasting)
            • Usually picked up by newborn screening, but he didn’t have this
              • So use 17-hydroxy-progesterone (should be increased, as the precursor)
          4. How will you manage Oliver: a) Acutely? b) In the long term?
            • ABCs (dehydrated, vomiting, sodium is low)
            • Hydrocortisone IV bolus
            • Fix his potassium
            • Fix his glucose
            • Long term: hydrocortisone + Florinef
              • Glucocorticoid is important for managing steroid levels and managing excess androgens (preventing precocious puberty and early fusion of the epiphyseal growth plate in CAH)
              • Educate on stress dosing, wearing a Medic Alert bracelet
          5. Oliver’s mother wants to know if her future children will have CAH. What do you tell her?
            • Autosomal recessive so there’s a 1/4 chance.
          6. What preventative measures can you suggest to Oliver’s mother?
            • Maternal dexamethasone during pregnancy (prophylactically and then continue if a girl with CAH)
              • Protects baby from seeing excess androgens
            • Newborn screening to prevent salt-wasting crisis in male babies
