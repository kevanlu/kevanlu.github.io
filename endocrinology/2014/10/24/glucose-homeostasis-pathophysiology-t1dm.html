        Physiology of Glucose Homeostasis & Pathophysiology of T1DM

        Kevan Lu Friday, October 24, 2014

        Lecture Objectives

        1. Discuss the importance of the various subtypes of glucose transporters.
        2. Name the endocrine cells of the pancreas and their hormones.
        3. Describe the mechanism of glucose regulation of insulin secretion.
        4. Describe the cellular mechanism of action of insulin.
        5. Compare and contrast the roles of the brain, liver, muscle, fat and pancreas in regulation of blood glucose.
        6. Define incretins and their mechanism of action on insulin secretion.
        7. Describe altered metabolism and fate of glucose in type 1 DM.

        Introduction

        • Glucose is important → it’s the best source of ATP.
        • Hence, the regulation of glucose homeostasis is critical.
        • When you eat, and your plasma glucose increases, this triggers a compensatory release of insulin.
          • This release of insulin is biphasic → there’s an initial sharp increase, followed by a slower increase.
        • We also see a fall in glucagon, as it opposes the effects of insulin.

        Glucose Transporters

        • There are 5 main subtypes of glucose transporters → named GLUT1 through to GLUT5.
        • These are located in different tissues, and have different affinities for glucose.
        • Their differentiating features are described below:

        Glucose Transporters

        Name Tissue Distribution Important Features
        GLUT1 Brain, erythrocytes, placenta, fetal tissue Low Km (~1 mM), constant uptake of glucose
        GLUT2 Liver, kidney, intestine, pancreatic β-cell. High Km (15-20mM), glucose equilibration across membrane.
        GLUT3 Brain Low Km (<1 mM), preferential uptake in hypoglycemia
        GLUT4 Muscle & Adipose Tissue Med Km (2.5-5 mM), insulin sensitive.
        GLUT5 Jejunum Med Km (~ 6 mM), can also mediate fructose uptake
        • Recall from Michaelis-Menten kinetics that is defined as the substrate concentration at which the enzymatic reaction rate is at half of its maximum.
          • Hence, a high means that a low amount of substrate is needed to reach this point (high affinity), while a low means that a lot of substrate is needed (low affinity).
        • The important glucose transporters to remember are:
          1. GLUT1
            • Especially important in the brain.
            • It makes sense that the GLUT1 receptor has a very high affinity for glucose, since the brain almost always relies on glucose as its main source of ATP.
            • This also makes sense for Erythrocytes → they don’t have mitochondria, so they derive all their energy from Glycolysis.
          2. GLUT3:
            • The brain actually has 2 glucose transporters → GLUT1 and GLUT3.
            • GLUT3 has the lowest → it has the highest affinity for glucose.
          3. GLUT4:
            • This is inducible → its function can be induced by insulin.
            • Recall that its function was also inhibited by cortisol (to keep plasma glucose high).

        The Pancreas

        • The pancreas regulates glucose homeostasis (via its endocrine function).
        • Recall that the pancreas has an (1) exocrine function and an (2) endocrine function:
          • Exocrine Function:
            • This primarily involves the Acinar Cells we saw in GI.
            • Recall that acinar cells secrete Amylase, Lipase, and Trypsinogen as zymogens. Duct cells secrete bicarbonate.
          • Endocrine Function:
            • This primarily involves the Islets of Langerhans.
              • α-cells produce glucagon.
              • β-cells produce insulin and amyloid.
              • δ-cells produce somatostatin (aka. SRIF, aka. an inhibitor of pituitary GH secretion).
              • D1 cells produce VIP (Vasoactive Intestinal Peptide).
              • PP cells produce pancreatic polypeptide (allows the pancreas to self-regulate its secretions).
            • Recall from GI that we also had Enteroendocrine cells of the pancreas, that produce Secretin and Cholecystokinin.

        Insulin Biosynthesis

        • Insulin is a polypeptide hormone, that is derived from a pro-peptide.
        • It must undergo post-translational modifications in order to become bioactive.
          1. Upon translation, you end up with a preproinsulin.
          2. Signal peptidases remove an N-terminus signal sequence from the preproinsulin, while it’s still in the ER. This converts it to proinsulin.
          3. As the proinsulin moves into the Golgi apparatus, endopeptidases (proenzyme convertase), PC2, and PC3/PC1 cleave the C-chain component of the insulin (see the diagram below).
          4. Exopeptidases, and carboxypeptidase H, clean up the C-termini of the remaining A and B chains (which are still connected by disulphide bonds). This results in insulin.
          5. Insulin is stored in a secretory granule. Upon activation, these granules can be released.

        • This explains the biphasic nature of the insulin response
          • Phase 1 reflects the release of stored bioactive insulin.
            • It occurs within 1-5 minutes of a glucose load (meal).
            • This is why the response is (1) fast, and (2) strong.
          • Phase 2 reflects an increase in insulin transcription.
            • It occurs 15-20+ minutes after a meal.
            • This is why the response is (1) slower, and (2) more gradual.

        Phase 1 of Insulin Release

        • As mentioned before, this is due to the release of stored insulin.
        • Mechanistically, this release is triggered by an increase in glucose in the β-cells:
          1. Glucose can enter the β-cell through GLUT-2 (which as we saw earlier, was expressed in the β-cell).
          2. Increased glucose metabolism increases ATP levels in the β-cell, which closes ATP-regulated K+ channels.
          3. This increases intracellular K+, increasing the membrane potential, triggering depolarization and the opening of Ca2+ channels.
          4. Ca2+ mediates the release of stored insulin.

        Phase 2 of Insulin Release

        • As mentioned before, this is due to the increased transcription of insulin (rather, preproinsulin).
        • Mechanistically, this is due to the autocrine action of insulin, as well as Ca2+.
          1. The insulin receptor (a Receptor Tyrosine Kinase), is a homodimer, which when bound to insulin, activates a GPCR and a downstream cascade.
          2. One key enzyme in this cascade is IRS-1 (Insulin Receptor Substrate 1). This is a kinase that further phosphorylates the insulin receptor, which leads to a series of biochemical events.
          3. These reactions ultimately lead to an increase in insulin gene transcription.
        • Insulin also functions to increase the surface expression of GLUT-4:
          • In cells that express GLUT-4, activation of the Insulin Receptor leads to the translocation of the GLUT-4 receptor from the cytosol to the membrane.
          • This increases the number of surface GLUT-4 transporters, increasing glucose influx into muscle and adipose tissue (where GLUT-4 is expressed).
          • This is the mechanism by which insulin carries out its main biological function.
        • Insulin receptor activation also opens Calcium Gated Channels:
          • This increase in intracellular calcium leads to increased secretory activity, as discussed before.
          • It also activates CaM kinase, which ultimately also increases insulin gene transcription.

        Major Roles of Insulin

        • Liver:
          • Insulin is high when we’ve eaten a lot of food. This is when we have a lot of energy that must be stored.
          • Hence insulin triggers the production of short- and long-term forms of ATP storage → (1) glycogenesis and (2) fatty acid synthesis.
          • It also increases glucose uptake, which is its obvious function.
        • Muscle:
          • The same idea applies here, with (1) glycogenesis and (2) protein synthesis.
          • Glucose uptake and amino acid uptake also occur.
        • Adipose Tissue:
          • Triglyceride synthesis and lipogenesis.
          • FFA and glucose uptake.
          • Leptin transcription and secretion → Leptin decreases appetite, which is desirable if you’ve just eaten.
        • Brain:
          • Insulin directly suppresses appetite.
        • In summary, insulin has 3 main functions:
          1. Glucose uptake.
          2. Energy storage (synthesis of glycogen, fatty acids, proteins, triglycerides).
          3. Suppression of appetite.

        The Role of Leptin

        • Leptin is secreted by adipose tissue, in response to insulin, and directly inhibits insulin secretion (negative feedback).
        • Leptin also acts on the brain decreases appetite/hunger, and increases energy utilization.
        • Hence, Leptin has (1) direct actions, and (2) central actions:
          • Its direct actions oppose the effect of insulin.
          • Its central actions reinforce the effect of insulin.
        • Finally, the brain itself can regulate insulin secretion:
          • Parasympathetic stimulation leads to increased insulin synthesis → it’s a good idea to synthesize more stored energy when you’re resting and digesting.
          • Sympathetic stimulation leads to decreased insulin synthesis → it’s a bad idea to synthesize more stored energy when you’re fighting or fleeing.

        Glucagon

        • Glucagon is made by α-cells, and opposes the effects of insulin.
          • Glucose release (and gluoconeogenesis).
          • Energy breakdown (glycogenolysis, lipolysis, muscle breakdown).
        • Stimulated by (1) falls in blood glucose, and (2) Cholecystokinin from the GI tract.

        Incretins

        • These are 2 molecules that control our insulin response.
          1. GLP-1 → Glucagon-Like Peptide 1
          2. GIP → Glucose-dependent Insulinotropic Peptide.
        • These are small peptides that are released from the gut cells in response to plasma glucose.
          • They are released in response to gastric stretching.
            • Hence they augment the response to oral glucose only, but not IV glucose, where there’s no gastric stretching.
          • They basically prime the pancreas for increased activity, by stimulating insulin production
          • They function by increasing calcium release from the ER.
            • This gives us another source of intracellular Ca2+, augmenting the insulin response.
        • Incretins are rapidly deactivated by serum enzyme, dipeptidyl-peptidase 4 (DPP-4).
          • This is important later on, when we look at diabetes medications.

        What happens without Insulin?

        • With an inability to transport glucose into cells, we end up with 2 problems:
          1. Too little intracellular glucose.
          2. Too much extracellular glucose.
        1. Too little intracellular glucose:
          • This means what we can’t use the glucose that we have, and an alternative energy source is needed.
          • This alternative energy is provided by protein and fat breakdown.
          • Protein Breakdown:
            • Muscle is broken down into AAs, which are converted to glucose in the liver.
            • This results in muscle wasting and weight loss.
          • Fat Breakdown
            • Fat is broken down into AcetylCoA, which can enter the Kreb’s Cycle.
            • This is not energy efficient, and includes by-products such as ketones.
            • Hence, high ketones are a marker for diabetes.
            • This may result in ketoacidosis, which is dangerous.
        2. Too much extracellular glucose:
          1. Increased glucose filtration
            • This results in an osmotic diuresis, leading to dehydration.
          2. Conversion of glucose to sorbitol (the ‘polyol pathway’)
            • Sorbitol causes microvascular damage, lens damage, and nerve damage.
            • This explains the retinopathies, neuropathies, and microvascular damage that diabetics get.
          3. Increased protein glycosylation
            • This can result in protein mis-folding.
          4. De novo synthesis of diacylglycerol (DAG)
            • Increased DAG leads to increased Protein Kinase C (PKC) activation, which results in nephropathy.
