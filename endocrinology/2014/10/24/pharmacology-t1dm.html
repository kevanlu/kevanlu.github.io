        Pharmacology of T1DM

        Kevan Lu Friday, October 24, 2014

        Lecture Objective

        1. Understand the basic structure of insulin and the role of structure in polymerization.
        2. Understand the actions of insulin on liver, muscle and adipose tissues.
        3. Know the consequences of insulin interaction with its receptor.
        4. Understand the processes which influence and regulate release of insulin.
        5. Be aware of the modifiers used to prepare the insulin preparations used to treat diabetes mellitus.
        6. Be aware of the basic objectives involved in designing therapeutic regimens using insulin.

        Pharmacology for T1DM

        • Treatment is insulin.
          • Synthesized by β-cells of the pancreatic islets of Langerhands.
          • Used sometimes in treating severe T2DM but is essential in T1DM.
        • Types of Diabetes Mellitus:
          • T1DM → Auto-immune, idiopathic.
          • T2DM → also known as non-insulin-dependent DM.
          • Secondary Diabetes (Type 3). Secondary to:
            1. Pancreatic Disease → eg. Pancreatitis.
            2. Endocrine Disease → eg. acromegaly, Cushing’s.
            3. Drugs and Chemicals → eg. glucocorticoids, Cushing’s.
            4. Genetic Abnormalities → eg. MODY, CF, etc.
          • Gestational Diabetes (Type 4)

        Types of Diabetes

          Description
        Type 1 Auto-immune destruction of beta cells, idiopathic
        Type 2 Diabetes due to insulin resistance
        Type 3 Diabetes secondary to other causes (Pancreatic disease, endocrine disease, drugs, genetic abnormalities)
        Type 4 Gestational Diabetes

        Actions of Insulin

        • As mentioned before, this can be broadly seen as:
          1. Increased Glucose Uptake
            • GLUT-4 mediated glucose uptake in muscle and adipose tissue.
          2. Increased Energy Storage
            • Increased glycogenesis and FFA synthesis in the liver, protein synthesis in muscle, and triglyceride synthesis in adipose tissue.
          3. Decreased appetite
        • In summation, insulin conserves fuel.

        Structure of Insulin

        • Insulin forms dimers and hexamers in different conditions, but it interacts with the insulin receptor (IR) as a monomer.
          • Hexameric insulin is shown to the right, as a trimer of insulin dimers. It’s often coordinated around a positively charged ion such as zinc.
          • Hexamers form when the concentration of insulin peptide is high → eg. in β-cell granules or in pharmaceutical preparations.
        • Human insulin has a particular tendency to form dimers.
          • The tendency of insulin to self-associate has effects on its bioavailability and pharmacokinetics, since it can only bind the IR as a monomer.
        • The A and B chains of the insulin molecule contain several highly conserved residues that are involved in creating a binding surface to the insulin receptor.
        • The solubility of insulin is affected by its degree of polymerization:
          • The hexameric form is insoluble.
          • The dimer is usually soluble.
          • The monomer is always soluble.
        • Hence, by varying the degree of polymerization, you can vary a specific insulin’s time until onset, or duration of action.
          • Other factors include the modifiers used in the preparation, buffers, and substitutions in the AA sequence.
        • Insulin is generally classified by its duration of action:
          1. Ultra-short acting Insulin.
          2. Short acting insulin.
          3. Intermediate acting insulin.
          4. Long acting insulin.

        Ultra-Short Acting Insulins

        1. Insulin Lispro (Humalog)
          • Has a reversal of the ProB28-LysB29 sequence at the C-terminal end of the B-chain → hence Lispro.
          • This results in a reduction in its ability to form hexamers.
          • The preparation also has virtually no zinc, so this insulin is designed to remain in its monomeric, active state.
          • Onset → within 10 min.
          • Peak action → 1-2 hours.
          • Total Duration → 4-6 hours.
        2. Insulin Aspart (Novorapid)
          • Similar idea as with Humalog; here we have a substitution of ProB28 by AspB28.
          • Onset → 5-10 min.
          • Peak → ~40 min.
          • Total Duration → 3-5 hours.
        3. Insulin Glulisine (Apidra)
          • Substitution of AsnB3 by Lys and LysB29 + Polysorbate 20 results in a rapidly acting insulin.
          • Onset → 5-15 min.
          • Peak → ~50 min.
          • Total Duration → 3-5 hours.

        Short-Acting Insulin

        1. Insulin Regular (Humulin R)
          • In Ontario, it’s also called Toronto Insulin.
          • Can be administered by IV or by infusion pump.
          • Onset → 0.5 hours.
          • Peak → 2-4 hours.
          • Total Duration → 5-8 hours.

        Intermediate-Acting Insulin

        1. Insulin Lente
          • Not used much today.
          • Don’t worry about it.
        2. Insulin NPH
          • Also called Neutral, Protamine, Hagedorn, aka. Isophane.
          • Uses protamine as a modifier.
          • Onset → 1-2 hours.
          • Peak → 6-12 hours.
          • Total Duration → 18-24 hours.

        Long Acting Insulins

        1. Insulin Glargine (Lantus)
          • Onset → 4-6 hours.
          • Peak → none; steady insulin action.
          • Total Duration → 18-24 hours.
        2. Insulin Detemir (Levemir) - Onset → 4-6 hours.
          • Peak → none; steady insulin action.
          • Total Duration → 5-24 hours.

        Biphasic (Premixed) Insulins

        • These are mixes of different types of insulins.
          1. Insulin Lispro Protamine + Insulin Lispro (Humalog Mix 75/25).
          2. Insulin NPH + Insulin Regular (Humulin) 70/30, 50/50.
          3. Insulin Aspart Protamine + Insulin Aspart (NovoLog Mix) 70/30.
        • There will also be an inhalable form of insulin soon.
          • Will be released in the US next year, and Canada in 2014/2015.
          • Known as Afrezza → will probably become very popular, because it obviates the need for s.c. injections.

        Adverse Effects of Insulin Administration

        • Hypoglycemia is the predominant worry.
        • Lipoatrophy may occur at injection sites, but this is seen more often with less-pure preparations.
        • Lipohypertrophy can occur with human insulins if injected repeatedly at the same site; probably due to local lipogenesis with high concentrations of insulin.
