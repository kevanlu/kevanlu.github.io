          Management of T1DM

          Vince Le Friday, October 24, 2014

          Type 1 Diabetes Mellitus


          Type 1 diabetes is a form of diabetes mellitus that results from the autoimmune destruction of the beta cells in the pancreas that produce insulin.

          The main problem in T1DM is the lack of insulin. Insulin normally stimulates glucose uptake via Glut 4 in all tissues except the liver and the brain. Since there is no insulin, glucose can’t be utilized by the body and the body thinks it’s starving. This triggers a glycogenolysis and gluconeogenesis response which results in hyperglycemia.

          The lack of insulin also results in proteolysis and lipolysis which can lead to weight loss. Lipolysis can also result in keotgenesis, leading to ketosis and acidosis, which cause nausea and hyperventilation.


          Signs and Symptoms of Type 1 Diabetes —

          Early:

          • Polydipsia
          • Polyuria
          • Weight Loss

          Late:

          • Vomiting
          • Dehydration
          • Abdominal Pain
          • Hypovolemic Shock
          • Hyperventilation due to acidosis
          • Drowsiness
          • Coma


          Diagnostic Criteria for Type 1 Diabetes —

          • Polyuria/Polydipsia, Weight Loss
          • Glycosuria and ketonuria
          • Random blood glucose greater than 11.1 mmol/L

          It is usually unnecessary to do a fasting plasma glucose or a glycosylated hemoglobin measurement

          • Symptoms of diabetes plus a random plasma glucose > 11.1 mmol/L or
          • A fasting plasma glucose (FPG) > 7.0 mmol/L or
          • A plasma glucose value in the 2-h sample (2hPG) of the oral glucose tolerance test (OGTT) > 11.1 mmol/L


          Management of Type 1 Diabetes ===

          • Glucose monitoring 4 times daily
          • Start insulin 3 or 4 times daily
            • Humalog/Novorapid and NPH
            • Humalog/Novorapid and Lantus/Levemir
          • No added sugar diet
          • Education


          Blood Glucose Monitoring ===

          Frequency:

          • 4 times daily, below meals and at bedtime
          • occasional nocturnal values
          • every 2-3 hrs during illness
          • symptoms of hypoglycemia

          Monitor with a blood glucose meter


          Target Blood Glucose Range —

          Age <5 yrs, Target: 6-12 mmol/L Age 5-12 yrs, Target: 4-8 mmol/L Age > 12 yrs, Target: 4-7 mmol/L

          A normal fasting BG is 3-6 mmol/l


          Target HbA1C Levels —

          Age <5 yrs, Optimal HbA1C < 8.0%, Adequate 8.0-8.5% Age 5-12 yrs, Optimal HbA1C <7.5%, Adequate 7.5-8.0% Age >12 yrs, Optimal HbA1c <7.0%, Adequate 7.0-8.0%

          A normal, non-diabetic range is 4-6%


          Types of Insulin ===

          Short Acting:

          • Regular
          • Humalog
          • Novorapid

          Intermediate Acting:

          • NPH

          Long Acting:

          • Lantus (Glargine)
          • Levemir


          Insulin Pump ===

          The insulin pump delivers insulin via continuous subcutaneous infusion. It can deliver both basal and bolus insulin. The boluses are given just before meals, adjusted according to intake and the basal rates can be adjusted for the time of day.

          The advantages of an insulin pump include:

          • Better quality of life compared to other methods of administering insulin > Using rapid-acting insulin allows for less structured meals compared to slow acting insulin. People also feel that using an insulin pump is more convenient and discreet than an injection.

          • Improved blood glucose control

          • Precise insulin dosing > Some pumps also have a calculator that determines how much bolus insulin should be given when you input your expected carbohydrate intake, blood sugar levels, and still-active insulin

          • Reduced risk of hypoglycemia

          A disadvantage of the insulin pump is that the insulin pumps, cartridges, and infusion sets may be far more expensive than syringes used for insulin injection. > Although in Canada, the Ministry of Health and Long-Term Care’s Assistive Devices Program covers 100% of the cost of the insulin pumps and pays an additional $2400 to the person per year to cover the cost of supplies for the pump.


          Acute Complication of Diabetes: Hypoglycemia ===

          Symptoms of hypoglycemia include:

          Early (adrenergic):

          • Shakiness
          • Sweating
          • Anxiety
          • Hunger
          • Pallor

          Late (neuroglycopenic):

          • Hunger
          • Drowsiness
          • Confusion
          • Coma
          • Convulsion

          To treat acute hypoglycemia, give a glucose tablet, glucose gel, or juice. In an emergency, give glucagon IM/SC or IV dextrose
