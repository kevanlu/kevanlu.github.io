    Clinical Problems in Pediatric Cardiology

    Kevan Lu Friday, January 24, 2014

    • There are 3 major features that typically cause a referral to a pediatric cardiologist:

      1. Murmur
      2. Chest pain
      3. Palpitations

    Murmurs in Children

    • Murmurs are common in children!

    • 80% of children have a murmur at some point → the majority of these are innocent murmurs.

    • But only 1% of children have a congenital heart disease!

    • When you see a murmur in practice, there are 3 possibilities:

      1. Innocent murmur

        • A sound made by the vibration of blood through a structurally normal heart.

      2. Trivial Heart Diesase

        • Bicuspid Aortic Valve
        • Mitral Valve Prolapse
        • Tiny VSD

      3. Significant Heart Disease

        • ASD
        • Coarctation of the Aorta

    • NOTE: We have already taken most of the severe heart diseases out of the equation, since most of these will have already presented during the newborn period!

    • The two that can masquerade as a trivial heart disease, are an ASD and Coarctation of the Aorta, as listed above.

    • Generally, mitral valve prolapse in children is pretty benign, unless you have a connective tissue disease, like Marfan’s.

    An Approach to the Child with a Murmur

    1. History: → of limited utility. (family history)

    2. Physical Examination

      • Can examine other congenital anomalies & dysmorphic features.
      • Cardiac Exam → pulses, palpation of the precordium, auscultation.

    Dysmorphic Features

    • The child on the right has Down’s syndrome.
    • About 50% of children with Down’s syndrome will have a congenital heart defect.
    We saw DiGeorge Syndrome in I&I! It was also referred to as congenital thymic aplasia, where the thymus fails to develop. This caused frequent infections, due to impaired adaptive immunity.
    • Another disease that raises the risk of CHD (Congenital Heart Disease) is DiGeorge Syndrome.
    • There are also pronounced facies with DiGeorge’s syndrome, and of course the recurrent risk of infection due to impaired maturation of T-cells.
    • Finally, William’s Syndome is another genetic disease that can present with cardiac problems, and also often has obvious facies.

    Pulse Assessment

    Note

    Beware of an absent femoral pulse!

    • You need to assess the femorals, otherwise you might miss coarctation of the aorta!

    Palpation of the Precordium

    • Atrial level shunts load the right ventricle. → RV heave.

    • Ventricular shunts, or more downstream ones, like Patent Ductus Arteriosus load the left ventricle.

      • This is because the LV has to pump blood out multiple routes → increases strain on the heart.
      • Hence we see an active apex.

    • With a VSD, it’s basically as if the heart is ejecting out the pulmonary artery as well!

    • This means that the RV is basically a passive bystander.

    • With a large VSD, it may cause volume overload in the RV, which can cause both the LV and RV to be active, but in most cases, we usually only feel the stronger apex beat.

    Summary of Palpation

    • Atrial Level Shunts load the right ventricle and can be seen as an active RV.
    • Ventricular or downstream level shunts load the left ventricle and can be seen in an active apex.

    Auscultation

    • How do you distinguish an innocent murmur from a pathological murmur?

    • There are 3 major innocent murmurs of childhood:

      1. Still’s Murmur. → vibratory systolic ejection murmur. Very common!
      2. Pulmonary Flow Murmur. → Blowing systolic ejection murmur obviously heard near the pulmonic area.
      3. Venous Hum

    Timing of the Murmur

    • Importantly, systolic murmurs are frequently innocent, but diastolic murmurs are never innocent.
    • There is no such thing as an innocent diastolic murmur → it always suggests some sort of underlying pathology!

    Note

    • Systolic Murmurs are frequently innocent
    • Diastolic Murmurs are NEVER innocent!

    Intensity of the Murmur

    • As we saw before, the classifications of murmur intensity was as follows:

      1. Stage I → Barely audible.
      2. Stage II → Audible.
      3. Stage III → Loud, but not palpable.
      4. Stage IV → Thrill present.
      5. Stage V → Audible when the stethoscope is tilted off the chest wall.
      6. Stage VI → Audible with the stethoscope off the chest wall.

    • Innocent murmurs are always Grade III or less.

    • Once you feel a thrill, it’s not innocent!

    Note

    • If you feel a thrill (Grade IV intensity or higher), the murmur is not innocent!

    Quality of the Murmur

    • A harsh murmur is always organic. (Organic = not functional/innocent)
    • A blowing murmur is often innocent (except maybe an ASD).
    • A musical murmur is a classic innocent murmur (it has a ‘pure tone’)
    • These are obviously difficult to differentiate without experience or practice!

    Note

    • Harsh murmurs suggest an underlying pathology.
    • Blowing murmurs are often innocent (an ASD is an exception).
    • Musical murmurs are always innocent.

    Location of the Murmur

    • In children, we also listen to the heart sounds from the back.

    • This is because coarctation of the aorta may only be heard from the back!.

    • The characteristic locations of the innocent murmurs are:

      1. Venous Hum → Right infraclavicular. → can hear the humming of the jugular vein.
      2. Pulmonary Flow Murmur → pulmonary artery.
      3. Still’s Murmur → mid-left sternal border. → vibratory systolic murmur.

    Note

    The locations where each of the innocent murmurs are heard best are:

    1. Venous Hum → right infraclavicular
    2. Pulmonary Flow Murmur → pulmonary artery
    3. Still’s Murmur → mid left sternal border

    Types of Murmurs

    • Mid-diastolic rumbles are almost never heard in children → they’re too quiet!

    Systolic Ejection Murmur

    • Could be semilunar valve stenosis or high flow across the pulmonary valve (eg. in an ASD)
    • The majority of these are innocent.

    Pansystolic Murmur

    • Could be Mitral Regurgitation or Tricuspid Regurgitation
    • Recall the logic: during systole, the mitral/tricuspid should be closed, so a systolic murmur can be heard when they’re abnormally open at this time.
    • In children, the most likely cause is a VSD.
    • Never innocent!

    Continuous Murmur

    • There’s always sound here → always turbulent flow!

    • There are 24 causes of a continuous murmur in children; you need to know two of them:

      1. Patent Ductus Arteriosus → left infraclavicular. → not innocent
      2. Venous Hum → right infraclavicular. → innocent.

    Early Diastolic Murmur

    • Regurgitation from any of the semilunar valves → Aortic Regurgitation, Pulmonary Insufficiency, as we saw before.
    • Never innocent.

    Summary

    • Systolic Ejection Murmur → mostly innocent, could be due to an ASD, or aortic stenosis.

    • Pansystolic Murmur → most likely to be a VSD in children.

      • Could also be Mitral Regurgitation or Tricuspid Regurgitation.
      • Never Innocent

    • Continuous Murmur

      • Patent Ductus Arteriosus → left infraclavicular (up from pulmonary area) → not innocent
      • Venous Hum → right infraclavicular → innocent

    • Early Diastolic Murmur

      • Aortic Regurgitation or Tricuspid Regurgitation
      • Never innocent.

    Heart Sounds in Congenital Heart Disease

    Ejection Click

    • Follows S1 → stenotic semilunar valve (AS, PS)

    • So, if you take an unimpressive, blowing systolic ejection murmur, and add in a click, it’s probably a bicuspid aortic valve!

    • S2 should split with inspiration!

    • A wide fixed split S2 is suggestive of an ASD → we saw this before!

    • So, if we have a blowing, systolic ejection murmur + a wide, split S2 → ASD

    When do you Worry?

    • Worry about murmurs in infancy that persist beyond a year of age.
    • Worry about murmurs heard best in the back → coarctation!.

    Chronic Recurrent Chest Pain in Children

    • Since it’s chronic, it doesn’t suggest any acute inflammation like pericarditis.
    • In the case of chronic chest pain, it’s rarely cardiac!
    • This makes sense, since children don’t get coronary artery disease.
    • But, as usual, there are some exceptions, listed below:

    Potential Cardiac Causes of Pediatric Chest Pain

    1. Severe aortic stenosis → this usually doesn’t present as chest pain; it presents as a loud murmur!

    2. Hypertrophic cardiomyopathy → again, other symptoms typically present first!

    3. Congenital coronary artery anomaly

      • This is rare, but it exists!
      • Instead of having a left coronary and a right coronary, some people have a single coronary.
      • The anatomical position of the left anterior descending then moves between the two great arteries.
      • During exercise, the great arteries dilate → clamping the LAD → ischemia and chest pain.

    Differential Diagnosis for Pediatric Chest Pain

    1. Muscular Skeletal Problems

      • Costochondritis
      • Precordial catch syndrome → a stitch
      • Sore chest from running.

    2. Gastrointestinal - Sometimes, it could be due to GE reflux.

    • In most of these cases, the pain is just ‘near’ the chest!
    • Oftentimes, a child can’t effectively clarify the location of the pain.

    Differentiating Good Chest Pain from Bad Chest Pain

    Good → Probably not Cardiac

    • At rest or with exercise.
    • Well localized. → angina is usually more diffuse.
    • Stabbing, burning → sounds more somatic, not visceral.
    • Made worse by deep breaths. → probably associated with the chest wall.
    • No associated symptoms.
    • Relieved by position or antacids

    Bad → Could be Cardiac!

    • Only with exercise.
    • Poorly localized → more suggestive of angina.
    • Crushing or pressing pain.
    • Unaffected by respiration.
    • Nausea, syncope.
    • Relieved only by rest.
    • But even so, we could take a child with all the bad features, and 99% of the time, it’s not cardiac!
    • Physical examination is rarely helpful →, maybe you can reproduce pain via compression. This makes it less likely to be cardiac.
    • ECGs are rarely helpful. The more tests we do, the more likely that one will be abnormal by chance!
    • A stress test can be done for reassurance., while on an ECG.

    Palpitations in Children

    • Palpitations can be due to:

      1. An Irregular Heart Beat
      2. A Racing Heart

    Irregular Heart Beat

    Sinus Arrythmia

    • HR changes with inspiration and expiration due to differing sinus enervation.
    • always benign

    Atrial Premature Beats

    • always benign as well.
    • An example os shown on the right → early p-wave stimulation.

    Ventricular Premature Beats

    • This is almost always benign.
    • Check to make sure there isn’t a myocardial tumour.
    • Also check for FHx for cases of people dropping dead, syncope, presyncope, etc.

    Racing Heart

    1. Sinus tachycardia

      • Surprisingly, most of our referrals are for normal tachycardia upon exercise.
      • But children don’t know that this is normal!

    2. Supraventricular tachycardia → this is rare

    3. Ventricular Tachycardia → this is very very rare

    Sinus Tachycardia vs Supraventricular Tachycardia

    Sinus Tachcardia Supraventricular Tachycardia
    Only with vigorous exercise or excitement. At rest or with exercise.
    Comes on gradually and goes away gradually. Can have a sudden onset as well as a sudden offset.
    No syncope or presyncope. Hx of syncope or presyncope.

    Investigations

    • ECG to look for signs of SVT.
    • Symptom diary do determine the severity of symptoms.
    • Holter monitor if frequent events. This is basically a 24 ECG monitor of the child.

    Kawasaki’s Syndrome

    • First described in Japan by Dr. Kawasaki.

    • Self limited, small vessel arteritis.

    • We still don’t know what causes it! We suspect it’s a superantigen.

    • 80% present at < 5 years of age.

    • Male preponderance.

    • Asian > Black > Caucasian.

    • It is a clinical syndrome → we have no lab test for it yet.

    • 5 days of fever plus 4 of:

      1. Cervical Lympadenopathy
      2. Conjunctivitis
      3. Mucous membrane changes (eg. the strawberry tongue)
      4. Rash
      5. Extremity Changes

    • This kind of sounds like a viral infection! It’s very difficult to diagnose.

    • Why is this pediatric cardiology problem?

      • Because it affects the small arteries, and some of the most important small arteries are the coronary arteries.
      • This can cause aneurysmal dilation of the coronary arteries.
      • Aneurysms occur in 18% of untreated.
      • This decreases to 8% if treated with high dose Ig’s and ASA → important to recognize!
      • Mortality ~0.1%.

    Criteria for Kawasaki’s Disease

    5 days of fever as well as 4 of:

    1. Cervical Lympadenopathy
    2. Conjunctivitis
    3. Mucous membrane changes (eg. the strawberry tongue)
    4. Rash
    5. Extremity Changes
