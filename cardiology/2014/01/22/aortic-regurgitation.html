    Aortic Regurgitation

    Kevan Lu Wednesday, January 22, 2014

    • Aortic Regurgitation (AR): Regurgitation from the aortic valve.

    • The etiology of AR could be two things:

      1. A valve leaflet problem.
      2. Dilation of the aortic root. → when the aortic root enlarges, it pulls the leaflets apart.

    • Since the aortic valve closes during diastole, you shouldn’t have any backflow. In AS, you do have backflow, so you hear a diastolic murmur. This is just as we saw in the Clinical Assessment of Heart Sounds lecture.

    • The most common cause of a valve leaflet problem is calcific degeneration of the aortic valve leaflets, or a congenital bicuspid aortic valve.

    • Root enlargement of the aorta is the leading cause of AR in the western world that ends up leading to AVR (aortic valve repair). This is typically because of age-related degenerative enlargement.

    Recall that Marfan's disease is also a risk factor for Myxomatous Mitral Valve Disease (MVP), because it weakens connective tissue. The same applies here!
    • Marfan’s disease is also common → it raises the risk of all valvular regurgitation problems!

    The Etiology of AR

    AR can be caused by two things:

    1. A Leaflet Problem

    • Calcific Degeneration
    • Bicuspid Aortic Valve
    • Endocarditis
    • Lupus (it’s always Lupus!)

    Calcific Degeneration and a Congenital Bicuspid Aortic Valve are the most likely causes.

    2. Aortic Root Enlargement

    These are more common than a leaflet problem in the western world!

    • Age-related degeneration
    • Hypertension → chronic hypertension can cause the aorta to dilate.
    • Marfan’s Disease → can cause dilatation of the aortic root!
    • Bicuspid aortic valve aortopathy

    Age-related Degeneration is the most likely cause of aortic root enlargement.

    Chronic Aortic Regurgitation

    • Here, the aortic root is dilated, causing the three leaflets to spread apart.
    • Note that the 3 cusps can’t completely appose each other, so the seal is leaky.
    • The primary cause of this is ectasia of the aortic root → spreading of the valve annulus
    • Ectasia = dilation of a tubular structure.
    • Usually this is idiopathic, but it can sometimes be caused by chronic hypertension!
    • Since blood flows back from the aorta, the LV dilates → systolic heart failure over time!

    The hollow area in the center is actually a giant dilated aortic root!

    This is a doppler ultrasound that cuts the aortic valve transversely. Here we see that there's turbulent flow in the center of the valve, suggesting that the dilated aortic root has prevented it from closing properly.

    Functional Findings in Aortic Regurgitation

    • Volume overload into the LV causes eccentric hypertrophy.

    • Dilation of the LV increases the radius. Since wall-tension = (P × R)/(2 × Wall-thickness), as the radius increases, the wall stress does as well!

    • Early on, systolic function is maintained because contractility can be increased with the increase in volume. But in late stages, we reach a breaking point, systolic heart failure begins to occur! (Recall the Frank-Starling Law of the Heart).

    • The pulse pressure widens!

      • The LV dilates → stroke volume ↑. Because of this, the heart pumps out a lot of blood during systole, causing an elevated systolic BP.
      • But during diastole, because blood flows back into the LV through the insufficient aortic valve, the diastolic BP falls
      • As a result, the PP = systolic - diastolic, increases!

    Look at how big that left ventricle is! Eccentric hypertrophy is obvious here. The echo showed that the systolic function was still maintained pretty well.

    Key Findings in Aortic Regurgitation

    1. Diastolic Murmur → high pitched, best heard at the base. (the aortic region)
    2. Widened pulse-pressure → increased contractility in response to increased volume causes the systolic to rise, and regurgitant flow from the aorta causes the diastolic to fall.

    Clinical Findings in Aortic Regurgitation

    • Diastolic murmur: soft, high-pitched, and heart best along the 3rd ICS.

    • It’s heard best at the end of expiration when leaning forward.

    • The location where the murmur is heard best can help determine whether it’s a leaflet problem or a root abnormality

      1. Leaflet abnormalities are heard best left of the sternum (near the pulmonic area).
      2. Root abnormalities are heard best right of the sternum (near the aortic arch).

    • The length of the murmur correlates with severity → the longer it is, the more persistent the regurgitation! Assess murmur length by assessing whether it’s longer or shorter than half of diastole

    • The loudness of the murmur does not correlate with severity, just as we had before.

    • Because the LV dilates, sometimes we can feel a Left Ventricular Heave.

    Summary of Clinical Findings

    • Diastolic Murmur that is soft and high pitched, near the base.

      • If heard best left of the sternum, it’s a leaflet abnormality
      • If heart best right of the sternum, it’s a root abnormality
      • The length of the murmur correlates with severity, but not the intensity

    • The Apex is displaced because of LV hypertrophy.

    • An S4 can be heard because of LV hypertrophy.

    • An S3 can be heard if there’s CHF causing a baggy ventricle

    Signs of a Widened Pulse Pressure

    • There’s a whole gamut of interesting clinical signs with funny names that are a consequence of a widened pulse pressure.

      1. Quincke’s Sign: You can see the capillaries flush and blanch with each cardiac cycle!

      2. Muller’s Sign: Systolic pulsations of uvula.

      3. Duroziez’s Sign: The sign that is seen the most frequently!. There’s a bounding pulse in the femoral artery.

        • When you compress it proximally, you hear a systolic murmur.
        • When you compress it distally, you hear a diastolic murmur.

      4. Traube’s Sign: When you listen over the femoral artery, you hear something that sounds like a pistol shot.

      5. Corrigan’s Sign: A collapsing pulse. Can be felt at the carotid!

    • Note, that as the LV begins to fail, the contractility will fall, and the systolic will fall

    • CHF also causes an increase in sympathetic tone → systemic vasoconstriction → diastolic will increase

    • As a result, the pulse pressure will fall!

    • This means that once AR has progressed to cause CHF, these exam findings will start to disappear!

    • The length of the murmur will remain a good clue to the severity of the AR.

    Pulse Pressure Findings

    1. Quincke’s Sign: Alternating flushing and blanching of the capillaries in the nails.
    2. Muller’s Sign: Pulsation of the uvula with systole.
    3. Duroziez’s Sign: Most common. Bounding femoral pulse, where you hear a systolic murmur if compressed proximally, and a diastolic murmur if compressed distally.
    4. Traube’s Sign: ‘Pistol shots’ when you listen over the femoral artery.
    5. Corrigan’s Sign: A collapsing pulse.

    When a patient progresses to CHF, the pulse pressure narrows again, so these findings disappear.

    Symptoms of Aortic Regurgitation

    • Long asymptomatic period, because the heart can compensate for increased preload by jacking up contractility!
    • Exertional dyspnea and fatigue are early signs, suggesting the LV has begun to fail.
    • Once it gets worse, it can back up to the lungs → orthopnea, PND.
    • Sometimes Angina can occur, because the aorta perfuses the coronary arteries during diastole.
    • So if the diastolic is low, flow into the coronaries will fall, affecting perfusion.
    • This, coupled with increased myocardial oxygen consumption because of LV hypertrophy, can result in angina.

    Diagnosing Aortic Regurgitation

    • Same as with all the other valvular lesions.
    • ECG → large R-wave in V5-V6 & large S-wave in V1-V2 may suggest LV hypertrophy. Not too useful.
    • CXR → large LV. Not too useful.
    • Echocardiogram = diagnostic. Gets you lots of info!

    Treating Aortic Regurgitation

    • Same as with all the other valvular lesions mostly.
    • If asymptomatic → good prognosis, monitor.
    • If symptomatic → endocarditis prophylaxis. No medications have been shown to improve survival. Surgery is indicated.

    This is a survival curve for patients with Aortic Regurgitation as well as CHF. CHF is grouped according to the New York Heart Association classifications (I, II, III-IV). Obviously, the more severe the heart failure, the worse the survival. But even if you have mild-moderate symptoms, the survival is bad enough that we should intervene!

    • Hence, surgery is always done in symptomatic patients.

    • We also sometimes operate on asymptomatic patients!

      • This is because the patients do poorly once the LV already begins to fail!
      • It’s better to have the surgery before their condition has progressed that far!
      • Hence, we operate in asymptomatic patients if their LV starts failing or stretches significantly → LVEF < 50% or LVESD > 55 mm
      • If not, we monitor them frequently so we catch progression early!

    Indications for Surgery for AR

    1. Symptomatic
    2. Asymptomatic and LVEF < 50% or LVESD > 55 mm

    Compare this to Mitral Regurgitation, where the cutoffs are LVEF < 60% or LVESD > 40 mm.

    Surgical Therapy for Aortic Regurgitation

    • Depends on the etiology:

      • If it’s a leaflet problem → aortic valve replacement.
      • If it’s a root problem → aortic valve sparing operation, where you fix the aortic root, but spare the valve itself. No need for a replacement!

    • These surgical approaches will be covered on Friday.

    Acute Aortic Regurgitation

    • The most common causes of acute AR are:

      1. Endocarditis causes disruption of the valve leaflets.

      2. Aortic Dissection, where the dissection flap falls into the leaflets.

        • Aortic Dissection = a tear in the aorta. There are 2 types.
        • Type A = ascending aorta, surgical emergency.
        • Type B = descending aorta, usually dealt with medically first. If things don’t stabilize, then surgeons are involved.
        • Usually caused by hypertension.

    • Because of the sudden onset, the heart doesn’t have time to compensate! Hence we have:

      1. A sudden drop in stroke volume → hypotension
      2. A marked increase in LV pressure → LA → lungs → pulmonary edema
    • There is a Short murmur, that may be inaudible, because of a rapid equalization of pressure due to severe valve damage.
    • Here is a case of acute AR because of infective endocarditis.
    • There’s actually a hole in one of the leaflets!
    • Consequently, emergency surgery is usually indicated.
