    Physiology of Hypertension

    Nicole Arseneau Tuesday, January 28, 2014

    Physiology of Hypertension

    How Does Hypertension Affect Us?

    • A leading cause of disease and death worldwide.

    • Risk factor with the most attributable burden of disease.

    • One of the most common reasons for a primary care visit.

    • The leading diagnosis resulting in a visit to a physician’s office in Canada.

    • One of the most important risk factors for stroke.

      Cardiac Output

      Blood is contained within a closed system.

      Remember

      Blood Pressure (BP) = Cardiac Output (CO) x Peripheral Resistance (TPR) Cardiac Output (CO) = Heart Rate (HR) x Stroke Volume (SV)

    Stroke Volume (SV) is determined by preload, afterload, and contractility.

    • Preload is primarily determined by the patient’s blood volume.

      • Generally, stroke volume rises as preload rises
      • However, if the preload rises above a certain threshold, cardiac muscle fibres can overstretch, and stroke volume will decrease

    • Afterload is primarily determined by the total peripheral resistance.

      • Generally, stroke volume rises as resistance increases.

      • Total peripheral resistance can be increased by:

        • SNS activation via α1 adrenergic receptors
        • Angiotensin II via AT1 receptors
        • ↑ blood volume in patients lacking compliant vessels

    • Contractility is the intrinsic ability of the heart muscle to contract, but can be modified through hormonal stimulation: - SNS activation and adrenal hormones cause production of catecholamines, which stimulate β1 adrenergic receptors, resulting in increased influx of Ca2+

    Regulation of Blood Pressure

    • Carotid baroreceptors influence sympathetic and parasympathetic activity based on changes in transmural pressure. This is a fast response that can have an effect in sedonds. - Remember, the SNS modifies vasoconstriction while the PSNS modifies heart rate and contractility
    • The Renin-Angiotensin-Aldosterone System can affect vasoconstriction, contractility, and water retention. This is a slow response that take 10 min – 10 h to take effect.

    Measuring Blood Pressure

    • Some variations in blood pressure are normal.

      • ↓ when sleeping (”nocturnal dip”)
      • May vary depending on the person doing the exam or between machines.
      • ↑ with age, including a widened pulse pressure on average

    • Blood pressure may vary based on the method used to measure it. - Office measurement should be done electronically (oscillometrically) rather than by auscultation according to the CHEP 2015 recommendations. Office BP readings may be affected by device, practitioner, and patient factors: - Conversation during readings - Single BP rocording - No antecedant period of rest - Rapid cuff deflation - Rounding of readings to -0 or -5 - Patient anxiety

      • Ambulatory blood pressure measurements are usually taken every half hour by devices worn by the patient throughout the day. This is useful for identifying problems such as white coat hypertension, and gives more detailed information than office measurement.
      • Home blood pressure measurements are used similarly to ambulatory devices, except cannot usually be used for monitoring during sleep.

    • Clinical BP readings are, on average, 6-9 mm Hg higher than standardized BP taken in a research setting.

    Hypertension and Cardiovascular Risk

    Elevated Blood Pressure is defined as a level of blood pressure associated with an increase in long term risk.

    Hypertension is defined as a level of blood presure where treatment is associated with reduced long term risk.

    • Ambulatory and home BP measurements are better at predicting long term risk than office measurements.

    • Nocturnal elevations in BP are associated with more risk than daytime elevations or the averaged 24 h elevations. Smaller elevations in nocturnal pressures are still associated with increased risk and reduction in this risk with adequate treatment.

    • Hypertension is a risk factor for cerebrovascular disease, dementia, CHF, atrial fibrillation, renal failure, coronary artery disease, peripheral vascular disease, and erectile dyfunction.

    • Risk can be calculated using online resources, including the Framingham Risk Score and SCORE Canada. These take into account:

      • Age (older = more risk)
      • Sex (male = more risk)
      • Smoking status
      • Systolic BP
      • Total cholesterol/HDL ratio

    Diagnosis of Hypertension

    • The WHO Blood Pressure classification is as follows:

      • Optimal BP → <120 / <80
      • Normal BP → <130 / <85
      • High Normal → 130-139 / 85-89
      • Grade 1 Mild HTN → 140-159 / 90-99
      • Grade 2 Moderate HTN → 160-179 / 100-109
      • Grade 3 Severe HTN → >180 / >110

    • Need at least 3 elevated BP measurements on 2 separate visits to diagnose hypertension, unless BP > 180/110, when you only need 2

      Hypertension is a BP > 140/90 mmHg.

    • If 140/90 mmHg < BP < 179/109 mmHg 3 times on two separate visits, refer to Canadian Hypertension Education Program guidelines for recommendations - Perform ambulatory or home measurement if available

    • If onset is before 20 or after 50, BP > 180/110 mmHg, there is organ damage, or features of a secondary cause, it is considered ”innappropriate hypertension”.

    If possible, try to identify the etiology of the hypertension. However, 70-90% of hypertension is considered idiopathic, also known as primary hypertension or essential hypertension. Preliminary investigations include:

    • Urinalysis
    • Blood chemistry
    • Fasting glucose and/or glycosylated hemoglobin (A1c)
    • Fasting LDL, HDL, total cholesterol, and triglycerides
    • Standard 12-lead EKG

    Secondary Causes of Hypertension

    • Patient Factors include salt consumption, drug use (NSAIDs, stimulants, alcohol, etc.), withdrawal from medications or other drugs, and pain. These factors may also worsen existing hypertension

    • Doctor Induced Factors include wearing a white coat, incorrect measurement of BP, and prescribing medications such as glucocorticoids, stimulants, NSADIs, hromones, cyclosporine, and decongestants.

    • Medical Conditions include:

      • Obstructive sleep apnea (this is the most etiology seen in Dr Dresser’s clinic, after essential hypertension)
      • Hyperaldosteronism, especially in those with low potassium
      • Kidney disase (can be detected by urinalysis for blood and protein)
      • RAS, especially in those with atherosclerosis
      • Thyroid/parathyroid disase (can be detected by measuring serum TSH and calcium)
      • Other endocrine causes (pheochromocytoma, Cushing’s, acromegaly, etc.)

    Treatment

    • Lifestyle management for all patients
    • More aggressive management for those at higher risk of adverse events, such as diabetes. Pharmacologic therapy may be used earler, and BP targets may be lower.
    • Medications are chosen based on patient factors, disease factors, and drug factors.

    Summary

    • Hypertension can be caused by a disruption in any of several blood pressure control systems, including RAAS and the baroreflex.
    • Hypertension is a risk factor for many cardiovascular sequelae, including MI, stroke, and renal disease.
    • A diagnosis of hypertension should only be made after 3 BP measurements.
    • Most hypertension is considered essential, however secondary causes should be investigated.
