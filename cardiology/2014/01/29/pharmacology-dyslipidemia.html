    Pharmacology for Dyslipidemia

    Jordan Lafranier Wednesday, January 29, 2014

    Pharmacology of Dyslipidemia

    Review

    Triglycerides

    • are used as a source of energy and for insulation

      Cholesterol is…

    • used in steroid synthesis

    • incorporated in to cell membranes

    • converted in to bile acids by the liver

    Chylomicrons - very rich in TG, used to transport TG and cholesterol from the intestines to the peripheral tissues.

    VLDL - Very low density lipoprotein, synthesized and secreted by the liver and are very rich in TG. Function to bring TG to peripheral tissues.

    LDL - Low Density Lipoprotein. “Bad Cholesterol”. Function is to transport cholesterol from the liver to the peripheral tissues.

    HDL - High Density Lipoprotein. “Good Cholesterol” Function is to transport cholesterol from the periphery to the liver.

    Phenotypic Classification of Dyslipidemias

    Type IIa - High LDL and Total Cholesterol Type IIb - High VLDL, LDL, Total Cholesterol and TG Type V - High VLDL and TG

    Knowledge of a patients phenotypic classification can aid in the selection of treatment options

    Systemic Approach to Treatment of Primary Dyslipidemias

    1. Make an accurate diagnosis
    2. Identify and control risk factors - Lifestyle changes
    3. Add a drug if response to diet is inadequate - Use the Framinham Risk Score ( 10 year risk of cardiovascular events) to determine when to initiate treatment and what target LDL levels are appropriate (see diagram below)
    4. Substitute drug or use combination therapy if necessary

    Drugs Used in the Treatment of Dyslipidemia

    Statins

    Mechanism of Action:

    selective inhibitors of HMG CoA Reductase, a rate limiting enzyme in the production of cholesterol by the liver. A decrease in liver LDL upregulates the amount of LDL receptors on the liver → increased LDL absorption → decreased systemic LDL.

    Indications:

    • hypercholesterolemia
    • mixed hyperlipidemias in which LDL cholesterol is the primary abnormality

    Adverse Effects

    • G.I upsets, rash
    • Liver enzyme elevation
    • Myopathy, due to increase creatinine phosphate
    • Rhabdomyolysis, very rare
    • caution in use with fibrates and drugs that undergo metabolism via CYP450-3A4 enzymes

    Fibric Acid Derivitives (Fibrates)

    Mechanism of Action:

    activators of the transcription factor PPARα. Has multiple sites of action but fibrates predominately lower lipid rich lipoproteins (VLDL) and TG due to increased lipoprotein lipase. Fibrates also increases levels of HDL cholesterol.

    Indications

    • hypertriglyceridemia
    • mixed hyperlipidemia in which TG’s are elevated (type IIb). Used in combination with statins (use caution)

    Adverse Effects

    • G.I upsets, rash
    • Liver enzyme elevation
    • contraindicated in those with hepatic or renal dysfunction
    • potentiates action of anticoagulants

    Niaspan

    extended release nicotinic acid with no benefit on CHD events

    Ezetimibe

    Mechanism of Action:

    specific inhibitor of cholesterol absorption

    Indication:

    • Typically used as an add on therapy with statins to further reduce cardiovascular disease endpoints.

    Adverse Effects:

    • Well tolerated and the adverse effects of ezetimibe and statin in combination are similar to statin used alone.

    Review of Dyslipidemia Drugs

    • Statins → HMG CoA Reductase inhbitors, decrease cholesterol synthesis
    • Fibrates → Activate PPARα, increase HDL and decrease VLDL and TG
    • Niaspan → extended release nicotinic acid (no benefit)
    • Ezetimibe → inhibits cholesterol absorption
