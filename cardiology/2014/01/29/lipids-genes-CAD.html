    Lipids, Genes, and CAD

    Charles Yin Wednesday, January 29, 2014
    • Cholesterol to liver.
    • Triglycerides to peripheral tissue.

    • The most common class of dyslipidemia is IIa.

      • IIa → Normal VLDLs, high LDLs, high cholesterols, normal triglygerides
      • IIb → High VLDLs, high IDLs, high LDLs, high cholesterol, high triglycerides.

    • Another common one is Type IV → high VLDL, normal/low LDL, normal cholesterol, high triglycerides.

      • Common in obese, with insulin resistance.
      • ie. Type II Diabetes.

    • Drug treatment will focus on the most common forms of lipid disorders.

      • Increased LDL-cholesterol.
      • Increased VLDL-triglyceride.
      • Increased LDL-cholesterol and VLDL triglyceride.

    • LDL-C < 2.0 or 50% decrease in LDL-C is the goal.

    Lipids, Genes & Cardiovascular Disease

    Part I - Pharmacology for Dyslipidemia

    Lipid Transport

    • Cholesterol to liver.
    • Triglycerides to peripheral tissue.

    Abbreviations: CM = chylomicron – transports dietary lipids from intestines TG = triglyceride CE = cholesteryl ester – cholesterol-fatty acid, hydrolyzed by cholesterol esterase to cholesterol Rem = chylomicron remnant LRP = LDL receptor-related protein – removes chylomicron remnants and IDL LDLr = LDL receptor HTGL = hepatic lipase – converts ILD to LDL VLDL = very low-density lipoprotein LDL = low-density lipoprotein LCAT = lecithin-cholesterol acyltransferase – converts cholesterol to cholesteryl ester CETP = cholesterylester transfer protein – mediates exchange of CE from HDL with triglycerides from VLDL, IDL and LDL LPL = lipoprotein lipase – hydrolyzes triglycerides and mediates lipoprotein uptake

    Apoproteins: A   A1 – promotes cholesterol efflux from tissues and acts as a cofactor of LCAT   A2 – function unclear, involved in HDL metabolism B48 – targets chylomicrons, VLDL, IDL and LDL to tissues, synthesized in the intestines B100 – targets VLDL, IDL and LDL to tissues, synthesized in the liver C –   C1 – inhibits hepatic lipoprotein receptors   C2 – activates LPL   C3 – inhibits LPL E – targets chylomicrons, VLDL, IDL and LDL to liver

    Phenotypic classification of dyslipidemias

    Risk categories and targets

    • FRS → Framingham Risk Score

    Drugs for hyperlipidemia

    Statins

    • Biochemistry: - Competitively inhibits HMG CoA reductase - HMG CoA reductase = rate limiting step in endogenous cholesterol synthesis

      • ↓ intracellular cholesterol → ↑ LDLr → ↓ LDL/VLDL/Rem levels in serum + ↑ cholesterol clearance
      • Bottom line: lower cholesterol

    • Indications: - Major - Elevated FRS - Cardiovascular event - Minor - Heterozygous familial hypercholesterolemia - Other forms of hypercholesterolemia - Mixed hyperlipidemias w/ elevated LDL cholesterol

    • Side-effects:

      • Elevated liver enzymes
      • Myalgia

    Fibrates

    • Biochemistry: - Activates peroxisome proliferator-activated receptor α (PPARα) - PPARα = transcription factor that activates ketogenesis in the liver under conditions of energy deprivation - Anti-hyperlipidemia effects - ↑ LPL activity + ↑ fatty acid β-oxidation → ↓ VLDL - ↓ TG + Rem - ↑ HDL + ↑ biliary cholesterol secretion - Anti-clotting effects - ↓ fibrinogen - ↓ PAI-1 → ↑ TPA activity → ↓ thrombosis

    • Bottom line: lowers triglycerides

    • Indications:

      • Hypertriglyceridemia if LDL cholesterol is normal
      • Secondary prevention in combination w/ statins in hyperlipidemia IIb or type 2 DM

    • Contraindications:

      • Hepatic or renal dysfunction
      • Pre-existing gall stone disease
      • Oral anticoagulants (?)

    • Side effects: - Elevated liver + heart enzymes

    Other drugs

    • Niaspan:

      • Extended release nicotinic acid
      • ? evidence of benefits in CVD events
      • Usage rate has recently dropped

    • Ezetimibe: - Inhibitor of cholesterol absorption - Exact mechanism unknown, thought to block NPC1L1, a mediator of cholesterol absorption in the intestines - Localizes to intestinal epithelial cells and circulates enterohepatically - Rapid onset + long half-life

      • Indicated in px who cannot tolerate statins or in combination with statins
      • Side effects → slightly elevated liver enzymes

    Part II - Genetic Basis of Atherosclerosis

    Loci associated with increased risk of atherosclerosis

    Risk allele frequency → % of all alleles at a given locus in a population gene pool represented by the risk allele Genetic effect → relative risk of developing disease when the risk allele is present

    Familial hyperlipidemia

    • Epidemiology:

      • Most common genetic disorder that increases risk of CVD

      • Prevalence:

        • 1 in 500 heterozygous
        • Increased prevalence in certain populations (Quebecois, Christian Lebanese, Dutch Afrikaner)

    • Etiology:

      • Autosomal dominant genetic disorder

      • Possible mutations

        1. Functional mutation in LDLr (most common)
        2. Functional mutation in apoB100
        3. Functional mutation in AHR (LDLr adapter protein), recessive
        4. Gain of function mutation in PCSK9 (targets LDLr for degradation)

      • Classifications

        1. No receptor synthesized, promoter defect
        2. Receptor not expressed on cell surface, post-translational modification defect
        3. Mutant receptor fails to localize to lipid rafts
        4. Mutant receptor fails to bind ligand

    • Pathogenesis:

      • Cells cannot synthesize LDLr or cannot internalize LDLr/LDL complex
      • ↑↑ circulating LDL

    • Presentation: - History - CVD in 1st degree relatives - Physical - Xanthelasma - Tendon xanthomas (esp on Achille’s tendon) - Corneal arcus

    • Diagnosis - Plasma cholesterol > 6.6mmol/L

    • Management - Lifestyle modification - Reduce total fat to < 30% of caloric intake - Reduce dietary cholesterol to < 300mg/day - Increase polyunsaturated fat to 10% of caloric intake - Reduce total caloric intake - Medical - Statins - Add ezetimibe if therapeutic goals not met w/ statins alone

    • No effective treatments for homozygotes

      • Plasmapheresis of LDL
      • Liver transplant
      • Gene therapy for LDLr mutations
      • Experimental drugs: antisense apoB100, MTP (microsomal triglyceride transfer protein) inhibitors
