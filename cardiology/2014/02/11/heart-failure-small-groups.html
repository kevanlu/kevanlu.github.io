    Heart Failure Small Groups

    Kevan Lu Tuesday, February 11, 2014

    Case #1:

    Mr. HV, age 48, presents with the complaint of progressive shortness of breath and fatigue. In retrospect, he has not been feeling well for 4 to 6 weeks. In the last 2 days, he has been short of breath with minimal exertion, has developed a nocturnal cough and has been unable to lie flat without the feeling of suffocation.

    Notes

    • Nocturnal cough → asthma and congestive heart failure in the DDx.
    • Can’t lie flat → orthopnea
    • Check for paroxysmal nocturnal dyspnea → where the redistribution of fluid upon sleep jacks up the preload and triggers congestive heart failure. The patient often wakes up, and feels like they’re suffocating.

    What is the differential diagnosis for this man’s presentation?

    • Shortness of breath could be cardiac or pulmonary

    • Cardiac Causes:

      1. Pulmonary Edema
      2. Valvular Disease
      3. Palpitations
      4. Ischemia → can cause stiffening of the ventricles, or can directly cause shortness of breath!
      5. Alcoholic cardiomyopathies

    • Pulmonary Causes:

      1. Asthma, COPD
      2. Infection, eg pneumonia
      3. Pulmonary Embolism
      4. Pulmonary effusion
      5. Connective Tissue Disease, or Autoimmune Diseases

    • Other:

      1. Severe Metabolic Acidosis → causes increase in respiratory rate as you try to let out excess CO2
      2. Anemia
      3. Pain/Anxiety

    What additional history may be helpful?

    • Examples of useful additional history include:

      • HPC: SOB/fatigue, 4-6/52, 2/7 on min exertion, nocturnal cough, orthopnea.
      • PMH: eg. hypertension, high cholesterol, diet controlled DM.
      • Meds/Allergies: Associated meds (eg. statins)
      • FHx: eg. FHx of ischemic heart disease.
      • SHx: Social history, eg. smoking, EtOH, occupation, recreational drugs.
      • ROS: Fill in the gaps by asking questions from head → toe.

    What physical findings are associated with heart failure?

    • Vitals:

      • BP: Could be ↑, ↓, —
      • HR: ↑
      • O2 Saturation: ↓ if you have pulmonary edema, as well as reduced CO.
      • RR: ↑
      • Rhythm: Sinus rhythm.

    • Lung Assessment:

      • Could hear crackles upon auscultation because of pulmonary edema.
      • Could also hear wheezes, or no breath sounds.

    • Cardiac Assessment:

      • Could hear murmurs suggesting Mitral Stenosis, Mitral Regurgitation, Aortic Stenosis, Aortic Insufficiency.
      • Tricuspid Regurgitation is less critical, because it’s less key to forward flow in the heart!
      • MS & AI → Diastolic murmur
      • MR & AS → Systolic murmur

    • Other findings: Could see Hepatomegaly because of backflow from the right side of the heart, or ascites

    What investigations will be helpful to clarify this patient’s problem?

    • Chest X-Ray
    • Bloodwork: Check for anemia, kidney function, electrolytes, liver function tests, albumin, bilirubin, INR/PTT.
    • ECG → Check for ischemia! Can see bundle branch blocks, Q-waves, etc.
    • Test heart function with an Echo.
    • Electrocardiogram ≠ Echocardiogram!
    • The gold standard test for ischemic disease is a coronary angiogram!

    What therapy would you initiate in this man’s case?

    • Sit-up, Oxygen, Diuretic, Morphine, Nitrates

    • Most commonly used loop diuretic is furosemide (Lasix)

    • Can also use non-invasive ventilation to assist their breathing and try to get rid of edema.

    • Non medical interventions are important too, although patients are generally less willing to adhere to them.

    • MONA mnemonic for Ischemia:

      1. Morphine
      2. Oxygen
      3. Nitrates
      4. ASA

    • The treatment path once the patient has stabilized follows the flowchart seen in the previous lecture!

      1. ACE inhibitors

      2. β-blockers

      3. If still symptomatic:

        • Class II-III → digoxin & nitrates
        • Class IIIb-IV → spironolactone & digoxin

    Case #2:

    Mrs. SL, aged 73, with a history of hypertension (25 years) and diabetes (10 years), presents with sudden onset of dyspnea. She was sitting, watching TV, when she began to have a “tickling cough” and then rapidly worsening shortness of breath and cough productive of frothy sputum. In the ER she was noted to be struggling for breath with a respiratory rate of 30/min.

    What is your initial approach to this patient?

    • Look for Vitals, ABCs
    • Frequently follow up to ensure that you catch any changes in her health status.
    • Rapid assessment because this is an acute situation.
    • Try to stabilize the patient, eg. treat with a loop diuretic
    • Once the patient has stabilized, proceed with investigation.
    • Sometimes, if we have no idea what’s causing it, we just throw everything at the patient! For instance, we would give her a loop diuretic like Lasix, a broad spectrum antibiotic, eg. a fluoroquinolone like levofloxacin, and a bronchodilator like ventolin

    How can a cardiac cause be differentiated from other causes of shortness of breath?

    • H/PE is critical!
    • Acute SOB with crackles is probably a cardiac problem.
    • Atriopeptin is a cardiospecific peptide similar to a troponin (recall ANP)
    • Recall that the D-Dimer is highly sensitive, but not very specific, so it’s a good test to rule out a thrombo-embolic condition.
    • Recall from blood that the pulmonary angiogram was the gold standard to Dx a PE, but most people just do a CTPA.

    What are the possible precipitants of acute heart failure?

    • Acute mitral regurgitation
    • Rapid Atrial Fibrillation → prevents atrial systole.
    • Ischemia → eg. due to hypertension reducing LV perfusion.
    • Medications (poor adherence, poor drug regimen, etc.)
    • Infection → vasodilation → increased HR to compensate → can overwhelm a weak heart!
    • Hypertension
