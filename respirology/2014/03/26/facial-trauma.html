    Facial Trauma

    Kevan Lu Wednesday, March 26, 2014

    Initial Care

    1. Establish airway and ventilation.
    2. Arrest hemorrhage.
    3. Restore blood pressure.
    4. Splint fractures temporarily to arrest bleeding or assist airway.
    • Is the airway open? If not, make it open.
    • Breathing → are they breathing normal?
    • Circulation → do they have a blood pressure?
    • Keep cycling between primary survey and resuscitation until stabilized.
    • Balance function and aesthetic demands.
    • When you fracture something, it involves a bone.
    • There are ligaments on top of the bone, that come off the bone, and insert into the cutaneous margins, and keep the soft tissue on the face.
    • When you’re young, these ligaments are tight → tight faces.
    • As you age, they start to relax, and your face looks older.
    • Muscles → depressors, elevators, sphincters.
    • Make sure you record the extent of damage beforehand, so you won’t be accused of causing further damage later on.
    • Poor fracture reduction → not placed properly, won’t reattach properly, can cause appearance of premature aging.
    • INCISIONS → there are 4 incisions with which you can expose the whole craniofacial skeleton. 1. Coronal Incision (the old scalping incision). - Exposure to cranium, down to zygomatic arch, orbit, naso-orbital ethmoid area. 2. Subciliary Incision (eyelid incision). - Right below eyelashes. - Exposure to orbital rim, anterior zygoma, maxilla. 3. Transconjunctival Incision - On the other side, just inside the eyelid. - Can get the same exposure with this incision too. - Helps hide the scar a little bit. 4. Upper Gingivobuccal Incision. - Incision inside the mouth, between teeth and cheek. - Can get exposure to the maxilla through this. 5. Lower Gingivobuccal Incision. - Same, except on the lower side of the mouth, can get exposure to the mandible.

    Mandible Fractures

    Movement of the Mandible

    1. Excursion (opening), is due to the internal medial pterygoid, masseter, temporalis.
    2. Lateral movement → side to side.
    3. Translation → front to back.

    • Often with a jaw fracture, translation is the last axis of movement that returns.

    • Structure of the Mandible.

      • Areas of bone thickening of the skull → horizontal buttresses.

      • These are areas where force is transferred through the face due to mastication.

      • When you reconstruct someone, you want to put the horizontal buttresses back in their original place for optimal recovery.

      • Since you can’t put on a face cast, you have to wire your jaws together → Maxillomandibular Fixation (MMF).

      • The most important thing in repairing a jaw fracture is fixing the occlusion. You have to make sure that the teeth can meet together!

      • How do you know if the teeth meet? You can’t talk to the patient if they’re intubated/sleep.

      • What we use as hints instead are the vertical grooves in the teeth → called mamelons. These are worn away where your teeth occlude!

      • So you can use these to map the occlusion.

      • You can also map the occlusion with signs of wear.

      • If you’re a couple of millimetres off, it’ll feel odd → it’s as if you had a tiny piece of popcorn stuck between your upper and lower teeth, offsetting them by just a bit.

      • Feels weird!

      • So you don’t want to place someone in the wrong occlusion!

      • How do you know if your fix was good?

        • Can they open their mouth with minimal chin drift? Does the chin move down straight, and not to the side?
        • Is the inter-incisal opening normal?
        • Do your teeth meet together properly?

      • Preserve the mental nerve → otherwise they won’t be able to feel their teeth/lips.

    Naso-orbital Ethmoid Fractures

    • One of the most commonly missed diagnoses in facial trauma.

    • These need a lot more care than just a nasal fracture.

    • TAKE HOME POINT → if you have a nasal fracture, could it be a nasoorbital ethmoid fracture?

    • What’s the difference?

      • Naso-orbital ethmoid fractures involve parts of the ascending maxilla that are high.
      • Medial canthal tendon of the eye inserts into the bone.
      • When this is fractured, you get telecanthus → distance between nose and medial canthus of the eye increases.
      • MEDIAL CANTHUS = inside corder of the eye.
      • LATERAL CANTHUS = outside corner of the eye.
      • TELECANTHUS = wide distance between eyes.
      • So if you see this, could this be a peri-naso-orbital ethmoid fracture!

    • SIGNS:

      • Bilateral periorbital ecchymosis. (nasal fracture, naso-orbital too).

      • Medial canthal displacement → ONLY GET THIS WITH A NASO-ORBITAL ETHMOID FRACTURE. IMPORTANT.

      • Telecanthus → increased distance between eyes. Canthus to midline is normally 16 mm. Intercanthal distance is 30-34 mm.

        • Of course, this should be a new telecanthus. If they always had this, isn’t worrisome.

    • CAUSES:

      • Low velocity → punch, kick, or high velocity → MVAs.
      • Typ. at nasal root.

    • CLINICAL TESTS:

      • Bimanual test → get a little instrument to fly it up nose, put finger into medial canthus, and move it. If the bone moves too, then they have a naso-orbital ethmoid fracture.
      • Definitive Dx is by CT.

    • Classification (NOT IMPORTANT):

      • Type I: Bone fragment attaching medial canthal tendon is nondisplaced.
      • Type II: Bone fragment attaching medial canthal tendon is displaced but canthal tendon is intact and still attached to a central fragment of bone.
      • Type III: Fragmentation and communition of the bones on which the medial canthal ligament is attached. In this type, the tendon cannot be reattached to the usual structures, as they have been communited as well.

    Zygomatic Fractures

    • Cheekbone fractures are probably the 3rd most common type of facial fracture.

    • Usually due to fist-fighting.

    • Zygoma is very thick → it usually actually fractures at its attachment points with other bones (eg. the zygomatic process of the temporal bone, the frontal bone, maxilla).

    • Vertical buttresses (of which the zygoma is part of) → zygomatico-maxillary buttresses???!

    • These fractures are important to repair → failure to do so can affect force transmission through the face.

    • Two types of zygoma fracture:

      • Type 1 → where the arch is okay.
      • Type 2 → where the arch is fractured.

    • You need a coronal approach to fix a type 2, but you don’t for a type 1 (could use a buccalgingival for instance).

    • Type 2 fractures → fracture the extension onto the temporal bone. The only way to get a full exposure is via a coronal or endoscopically. Doing it endoscopically is a little challenging.

    • Zygoma repair is ideally done within 5-7 days, otherwise the masseter starts to spasm and it’s difficult to pull the bone back out.

    • Always get an optho consult, because the zygoma meets with the sphenoid bone within the eye.

    • Once you release ligaments from the bone, you gotta re-attach them! If you don’t, then you’ll have premature aging of the face.

    Orbital Fractures

    • Form and function.
    • If they can’t look down → entrapment.
    • For instance, it could be caused by a fracture of the zygoma impinging on the inferior rectus of the eye.

    Maxillary (Lefort) Fractures

    • Upper jaw fractures.

    • Lefort fractures → after Henri Lefort.

    • Categorized into 3 groups:

      • Lefort I → upper jaw separated from craniofacial skeleton.
      • Lefort II → nasal bone + upper jaw separated from craniofacial skeleton.
      • Lefort III → orbits are separated too!

    • Because of the buttresses in the face, the maxilla generally fractures in one of these 3 ways.

    • Testing → grab Pt’s forehead, grab their jaw, check to see if there’s any movement. If there is → Lefort fracture.

    • Fix it the same way as an upper mandible fracture.

    • When ppl try to close their mouth, since the upper jaw separated from the craniofacial skeleton, the back teeth meet before the front teeth do! They get an open bite deformity.

    • As before, try to get the occlusion right first! Fixation and then reduction.

    Frontal Bone Fractures

    • Paranasal sinuses, frontal sinuses.

    • Obviously a coronal exposure.

    • Donald Classification of Frontal Sinus Fractures: 1. Anterior wall a. Linear b. Depressed c. Compound d. Extensively comminuted 2. Posterior wall a. Linear b. Depressed c. Depressed with CSF leak d. Extensively comminuted

      1. Frontonasal duct
      2. Through-and-through

    • We usually just see a plain frontal wall fracture.
