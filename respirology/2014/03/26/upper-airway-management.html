    Management of the Upper Airway

    Kevan Lu Wednesday, March 26, 2014

    Airway Obstruction

    1. Definition

    • Obstruction → blockage of airflow. This can either be complete or incomplete.
    • Don’t forget that there are two passages for airflow → the nose and the mouth! You can intubate someone through either.
    • If airway obstruction isn’t corrected quickly, it can cause brain damage and even death.

    2. Etiology

    • (1) Tumours, (2) trauma, (3) infections, and (4) allergic reactions make up the bulk of the causes of airway obstruction.
    • In children, foreign body aspiration is also a significant cause.
    • For example, obstruction can be caused by a tonsilar abcess → infection (seen on the right).

    Remember

    • Causes of airway obstruction include:

      1. Tumours.
      2. Trauma.
      3. Infections.
      4. Allergic Reactions.
      5. Foreign Body Aspiration → especially in children.

    3. Risk Factors

    1. Decreased Level of Consciousness → eg. due to narcotics.
    2. Infection, Tumours
    3. Obesity → especially since it’s associated with sleep apnea.
    4. Trauma, Burns → because burns can cause rapid swelling.
    5. Systemic Disease → for instance, rheumatoid arthritis can cause arthritic changes to the TMJ, and the arytenoids, affecting opening of the jaw and the vocal cords respectively.
    6. Sleep Apnea → obstruction of the airway during sleep.

    4. Signs

    • Choking.
    • Nasal Flaring → an especially important sign in children, who may not be able to convey distress as easily.
    • Accessory Muscle Use → for instance the scalene muscles of the neck may be strained.
    • Snoring → extremely loud snoring is suggestive of sleep apnea.
    • Stridor → Stridor is a high pitched crowing sound.
    • Abdominal Paradox → instead of the abdomen expanding during inhalation, it contracts! This is suggestive of accessory muscle use (such as the intercostals), one cause of which is respiratory distress.
    • Cyanosis.

    5. Symptoms

    • Air Hunger → they start gasping for air.
    • Agitation, Disorientation
    • Dyspnea
    • Hoarseness → especially if the obstruction is at the level of the glottis, and is interfering with the approximation of the vocal folds.
    • Dysphagia → difficulty swallowing.
    • Odynophagia → pain when swallowing.

    Non-Surgical Management of Airway Obstruction

    • Look, listen, & feel.

      • Can you see the chest rise or fall?
      • Can you hear breath sounds? Can you feel breath sounds?

    • Head tilt and chin lift → lifting the chin helps move the tongue off the posterior pharyngeal wall.

    • Jaw Thrust → used in patients with a potential C-spine injury.

      • Obviously, if the patient has a neck injury, you want to keep the neck as static as possible. So a head tilt and chin lift may not be that great an idea.
      • Instead you can do a Jaw Thrust → you pull up the jaw by the mandible, and open the mouth slightly simultaneously.
      • This lets you open the airway without moving the neck.

    • Suction Pharynx → you can use suction to remove and secretions and possible foreign bodies that are lodged in the pharynx.

    • Reposition the Patient → if you don't suspect a C-spine injury, then placing a pillow under the patient’s head will help open their airway.

    • Oral/Nasal Airways → consider nasal/oral intubation.

      • Never nasally intubate a patient if you suspect head trauma.
      • This is because there could be damage to the cribriform plate separating the nasal cavity from the base of the skull, so sticking a tube in there might be a bad idea.
      • If the patient has a clotting disorder, it’s also contraindicated → there’s significant nasal blood flow!

    • Supplemental O2.

    • Assisted Ventilation → Bag & Mask.

    Remember

    • Nasal intubation is contraindicated in patients with:

      1. Head Trauma.
      2. Coagulopathies.

    Bag Mask Ventilation

    • The C-Grip → maintain the face seal with your index finger and thumb positioned as in the picture.

    • The remaining 3 fingers are used to lift the jaw into the mask.

      • These 3 fingers should be on the bony prominences of the jaw → if you keep them on the soft tissue, you might actually worsen the obstruction.

    • The other hand pumps oxygen via the O2 bag.

    Laryngeal Mask Airway

    • Laryngeal Mask Airway (LMA) → a simple, effective, and cheap airway rescue device. They’re ubiquitous!
    • It’s placed into the mouth, into the throat, and sits above the glottis → it’s a supraglottic device.
    • This differentiates it from an endotracheal tube, which goes into the trachea.
    Recall that the right main stem bronchi was particularly susceptible because of its steeper angle.
    • Because it sits above the vocal cords, it does not protect against aspiration, where foreign bodies can get stuck in the bronchi.

    Remember

    • The Laryngeal Mask Airway (LMA) is a supraglottic device → it sits above the glottis!
    • The Endotracheal Tube is a subglottic device → it goes below the glottis, into the trachea!
    • Corollary → a LMA would not protect against foreign body aspiration, because the obstruction would be distal to the device.

    7. Intubation

    • Intubation is not only important for maintaining oxygenation and ventilation, but also for clearing secretions → this is known as tracheal toilet.

    • How do you intubate someone?

      • Evaluate the airway by Hx and P/E, to determine how difficult it will be.
      • For instance, some patients will have a larger tongue, or a recessed jaw, which will make intubation more challenging.

    • Physical Exam Findings → ideally, the mouth should look like what’s seen to the right.

    • P/E findings that may complicate intubation include:

      • Decreased mouth opening.
      • The inability to see the entire uvula upon mouth opening.
      • A small chin.
      • Prominent teeth.
      • Morbid obesity.
      • Poor cervical spine mobility.

    The Mallampati Score

    • The Mallampati score is an assessment of how easy it will be to visualize the glottis. It’s based on how much of the uvula we can see.

      1. Class I → Soft palate, uvula, fauces, pillars visible.
      2. Class II → Soft palate, uvula, fauces visible.
      3. Class III → Soft palate, base of uvula visible.
      4. Class IV → Only hard palate visible.

    • The space between the base of the tongue and the epiglottis is called the vallecula. It’s what you stick the blade of your laryngoscope into when you pull the tongue out of the way.

    The Mallampati Score

    • The Mallampati Score tells us how easy it will be to get a good view of the glottis:

      1. Class I → Soft palate, uvula, fauces, pillars visible.
      2. Class II → Soft palate, uvula, fauces visible.
      3. Class III → Soft palate, base of uvula visible.
      4. Class IV → Only hard palate visible.

    Equipment for Intubation

    The equipment you need for intubation can be remembered with the STATICS acronym:

    • S → Scope (laryngoscope).

    • T → Tube (7.0 mm & 8.0 mm).

      • 7.0 mm for women.
      • 8.0 mm for men.

    • A → Airways (oral & nasal).

    • T → Tape. To keep your endotracheal tube in place.

    • I → Introducer & Intravenous.

      • Introducer → the firm stylette you use to shape the endotracheal tube.
      • Intravenous → used because intubating someone can cause significant hemodynamic changes. So you want to be able to rapidly administer medications.

    • C → Circuit → the circuit we use to oxygenate and ventilate the patient.

    • S → Suction & Syringe to inflate the endotracheal tube cup.

    Pharmacological Agents

    • Intubating someone will usually cause their sympathetic tone to increase, if they’re still conscious.

    • So medications are often used to assist with intubation.

      • Topical anesthetics and muscle relaxants.
      • Atropine is also used. Recall that atropine is an anti-muscarinic, so it’ll block salivation, which is useful. (Don’t forget that SLUDGE acronym).

    Performing an Intubation

    1. Monitor vitals (Sats, RR, BP, HR, Temp).

    2. Put the patient in “sniffing position” → with the neck flexed back, and the head pulled out.

    3. Hold the laryngoscope in (L) hand & insert it into the patient’s mouth.

    4. Sweep the tongue away with the blade of the laryngoscope.

    5. Advace the blade to the midline until the epiglottis is seen.

    6. Lift the tongue to expose the glottis.

    7. Hold the endotracheal tube in (R) hand, and advance it through the vocal cords.

      • Make sure you actually see it going through the vocal cords, and that you didn’t accidentally put it down the esophagus.

    8. Place the the end past the vocal cords!

    9. Inflate the cuff to obtain the seal.

    10. Check the tube placement:

      • To ensure that it was inserted properly, see if the chest is rising or falling, or if there’s condensation in the tube.
      • Listen over the lung fields.

    What if you can’t perform the Intubation?

    • Call for Help!
    • Continue to ventilate by Bag & mask, or LMA.
    • Reposition the head & Neck.
    • Change the ETT size.
    • Change the blade size or type.
    • Get someone to apply pressure on the larynx → helps you see the glottis.

    Complications of Intubation

    • There are a lot of complications, but they can be easily figured out by looking at the steps involved with intubation:

      1. Positioning the Head → C-spine trauma.
      2. Inserting the Scope → soft tissue trauma, dental trauma, sore throat/jaw, etc.
      3. Placing the Tube → hoarseness, vocal cord paralysis, bronchospasm, laryngospasm.
      4. Physiological Response → to the stress of intubation (high sympathetic tone → HR↑, hypertension), or because of the decreased airflow (hypoxia, hypercarbia).
      5. Making a mistake → bronchial (went too far), or esophageal (went into the wrong tube) intubation.
    • In patients that have to be chronically intubated there may be an inflammatory response.
    • This can result in the formation of vocal cord granulomas, as seen to the right.

    Surgical Management of Airway Obstruction

    • As previously mentioned, there are two openings to the airway → the nose and the mouth.
    • You can make a third opening through the neck! That’s what a surgical airway aims to do!
    • Note: Even if you can’t intubate a patient, if you can still ventilate them (bag-mask, LMA), then it’s okay!
    • If you can neither intubate nor ventilate someone, that’s when you go for the neck.
