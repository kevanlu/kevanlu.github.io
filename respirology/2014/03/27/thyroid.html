    Introduction

    Thyroid Anatomy

    • The thyroid gland sits on top of the trachea, right below the cricoid cartilage.

    • It’s butterfly shaped, with an isthmus crossing over the trachea.

    • The vascular supply comes in laterally into the thyroid.

      • Arteries → superior and inferior thyroid artery.
      • Veins → superior, middle, and inferior thyroid veins.

    • The thyroid sits adjacent to the carotid sheath, which is why thyroid cancer can often involve the structures in it.

    • The parathyroid glands sit on the back of the thyroid gland.

    • The recurrent laryngeal nerve travels behind the thyroid gland.

      • The left RLN loops around the arch of the aorta.
      • The right RLN loops around the right subclavian artery.

    Thyroid Function

    • The thyroid is an endocrine gland.

    • It only produces two things:

      1. Thyroid Hormone.
      2. Calcitonin.

    • The majority of the cells in the thyroid are follicular cells, which secrete thyroglobulin.

    • This thyroglobulin is converted to thyroid hormone in the colloid.

    • Parafollicular cells (clear cells) which are adjacent to the follicular cells, are of a different embryological origin from the follicular cells, and secrete calcitonin.

    Remember

    1. Follicular Cells → secrete thyroglobulin.
    2. Colloid → converts thyroglobulin to thyroid hormone.
    3. Parafollicular Cells (clear cells) → secrete calcitonin.

    Thyroid Hormone Physiology

    • Like most endocrine organs, there’s a negative feedback loop involving the hypothalamus:

      1. The hypothalamus produces TRH → Thyrotropin Releasing Hormone.
      2. TRH stimulates the pituitary to produce TSH.
      3. TSH stimulates the thyroid to produce thyroid hormones → namely T3 & T4.
      4. T3 & T4 inhibit TRH and TSH release from the hypothalamus and pituitary.

    • T3 is the most active form of thyroid hormone.

    • However, the thyroid produces T4 >> T3.

    • T4 is converted to T3 in the periphery.

      • T3 has a short half life → 1 day.
      • T4 has a longer half life → 7-10 days.

    • It makes sense that the more active component would have the shorter half-life!

    • Thyroxine = T4.

    • Cytomel = T3.

    • T4 is usually what’s given in patients with hypothyroidism, or post-surgery, simply because its systemic absorption is more predictable.

    Remember

    • T4 = Thyroxine. Longer half life, converted to T3 in the periphery.
    • T3 = Cytomel. Shorter half life.
    • The thyroid produces T4 >> T3.

    Recent Trends and Demographics

    • Thyroid nodules are extremely common.

      • 30-60% population will have nodules on thyroid ultrasound.
      • 4% of the general population have clinically palpable nodules.

    • More common in females.

    • However, thyroid malignancy is not common.

      • Only 10-15% of clinically palpable nodules will harbour malignancy.
      • The vast majority of subclinical nodules will never become clinically significant.

    Etiology of Thyroid Cancer

    • The vast majority of thyroid nodules are sporadic, with no preceding risk factors.

    • Risk Factors include:

      1. Radiation Exposure → following irradiation, up to 40% of patients develop nodules. Of these, 30% are malignant.

      2. Pre-Existing Thyroid Disease.

      3. Genetic Factors → examples include:

        • Gardner’s Syndrome → germline APC mutation which causes congenital adenomas of the colon.
        • Cowden’s Syndrome → germline mutation in the P10 tumour supressor gene causing diffuse hamartomas.
        • Family Hx of thyroid cancer → 1st degree relative.

    Epidemiology of Thyroid Cancer

    • Pretty rare → 0.5-1.0% of all Cancers.

    • Incidence → 25/million/year.

      • 1100 deaths/year in the USA.
      • 100 deaths/year in Canada.

    • Deaths are almost all due to the more aggressive thyroid cancers like anaplastic subtype. The well differentiated ones have a fairly good prognosis.

    • The incidence of thyroid cancer has increased in recent years → is this due to better screening or increased exposures?

      • Judging from epidemiological studies, this is probably due to increases in screening.
      • Note that the majority of the increase is ascribed to papillary cancer, which is the most indolent, and often wouldn’t present clinical signs.
      • The cancers with more obvious clinical presentations (such as the poorly differentiated thyroid cancers), have stayed stable in incidence.
      • This is reflected in the fact that the mortality rate has not changed even with the increase in mortality.
      • This suggests an improvement in detecting the subtle nodules, which we would have otherwise missed in the past.

    • This suggests that we should avoid signing patients up for unnecessary tests → the risk will often exceed the benefit if it’s a node that doesn’t present clinical signs.

    Remember

    • The increased incidence of thyroid cancer is probably due to earlier detection (perhaps unnecessarily) rather than a systemic cause.

      • Epithelial Thyroid Cancer.
      • Non-epithelial Thyroid Cancer.

    • Of the epithelial thyroid cancers, papillary/follicular cancers are relatively indolent. They often take years/decades to present problems.
    • In contrast, anaplastic thyroid cancer is probably the most deadly cancer there is → with a median survival time of around 2 months!

    Frequency of Thyroid Cancers:

    • In order from most common to least common:

      1. Papillary Carcinoma (55-65%)
      2. Follicular Carcinoma (20-30%)
      3. Medullary Carcinoma (3-8%)
      4. Anaplastic Carcinoma (<5%)

    • Papillary carcinomas and follicular carcinomas are the well differentiated thyroid cancers → WDTC.

    • Papillary carcinomas have some high risk variants that are more aggressive → eg. tall cell, columnar, diffuse, sclerosing.

    Thyroid Surgery

    • There are 3 indications for thyroid surgery:

      1. Rule out/treat malignancy.
      2. Compressive symptoms → this usually only occurs with an extremely large thyroid. One example is seen to the right → you can actually see compression of the trachea due to a large thyroid mass!
      3. Hyperfunctional nodule/gland → which is refractory to medical management.

    • Surgical Options include:

      1. Open Biopsy.
      2. Lobectomy, ‘hemi-thyroidectomy’ → taking out half the thyroid.
      3. Subtotal Thyroidectomy → rarely done. We usually either take out half (hemi-thyroidectomy), or take it all out (total thyroidectomy).
      4. Total Thyroidectomy → taking out the entire thyroid.

    • If the patient has thyroid cancer, depending on if/where it has spread, you may also need to do a:

      • Central Neck Dissection.
      • Lateral Neck Dissection.
      • Sternal Split.

    Indications for Thyroid Surgery

    1. Rule out/treat malignancy.
    2. Compressive symptoms.
    3. Hyperfunctional nodule/gland refractory to medical Tx.

    Work-up of the Thyroid Nodule

    History

    You’re considered a high risk patient if you have had:

    1. Head and neck irradiation.
    2. Chernobyl under 14 years of age.
    3. First degree relative w/ thyroid malignancy.
    4. Rapid growth.

    Remember

    Pertinent signs on Hx for high risk include:

    1. Head and neck irradiation.
    2. Chernobyl under 14 years of age.
    3. First degree relative w/ thyroid malignancy.
    4. Rapid growth.

    Physical Examination

    1. Breathy voice → possible involvement of the RLN.
    2. Cervical lymphadenopathy.
    3. Fixed nodule.

    Work-up Algorithm

    • The algorithm is complicated, but focus on a few salient points:

    Bloodwork

    What bloodwork should you do with a thyroid nodule work-up?

    1. TSH → yes!

    2. Thyroglobulin → no!

      • Recall that thyroglobulin is produced by the follicular cells of the thyroid, and it’s converted into thyroid hormone in the colloid.
      • Studies have shown that thyroglobulin levels have no correlation with the actual risk of malignancy.

    3. Calcitonin → maybe! - This is ultraspecific for medullary cancers, but it’s controversial whether this should be regularly measured, since medullary cancers are so rare.

    Remember

    • TSH should be measured when working up a thyroid nodule.
    • Calcitonin is only useful for medullary thyroid cancers.

    Low TSH

    • A low TSH is important because a small subset of thyroid nodules will be hyperactive.
    • A hyperactive thyroid will produce excess thyroid hormone which inhibits TSH production.
    • In this case, what’s recommended is a thyroid scan (radiation uptake scan → an iodine scan or a technetium scan).
    • Low TSH is the only indication for a radiation uptake scan.
    • If you do find a hyperfunctioning nodule (also known as a hot nodule), then the chances of it being cancer is low.
    • In these cases, medical treatment should be done first, and surgical treatment should only be considered if it’s refractory to treatment.

    The Radiation Uptake Scan

    • Depending on the pattern of radiation, you can determine whether you have a hyper-functioning nodule, or the entire gland is over-active.

      1. Hyper-functioning Nodule → you’ll see a localized high uptake. This would be a hot nodule.
      2. Entire Gland → if the entire gland is over-active, then you’ll see diffuse high-uptake. One possible cause is Grave’s Disease.

    • Again, recall that a hot nodule is less likely to be malignant, and would be treated medically rather than surgically.

    • Caveats:

      1. Recent CT Scan → CT scan dye is heavy in iodine, so it can cause a false positive.
      2. Jod-Basedow Effect → Hyperthyroidism following excess iodine intake (eg. via dye, as described above, or as a dietary supplement).

    On the left would be diffuse hyperactivity, suggestive of Grave's disease. On the right would be localized hyperactivity, suggestive of a hot nodule.

    Radiation Uptake Scan

    • Can be used to differentiate between a hot nodule (localized uptake), or Grave’s Disease (diffuse uptake).
    • Beware of recent CT scans, because the contrast is high in iodine, and can cause a false positive.

    Grave’s Disease

    • Recall from I&I that Grave’s Disease was a Type II Hypersenstivity reaction.
    • It’s an auto-immune disease that attacks the TSH receptors on the thyroid gland, and stimulates them, triggering excess T3 & T4 production.
    • This gives you symptoms of hyperthyroidism → tachycardia, fine tremor, etc.
    • Recall that exopthalamos also occurs because of an attack on the periocular tissues.

    Diagnostic Ultrasound

    • You should perform an U/S if the TSH isn’t low, and you also have suggestive signs on Hx & PE.
    • Ultrasound is actually the most sensitive & specific (over an MRI, and CT).
    • If no nodule is found → you don’t need to biopsy it.
    • If the patient had a high TSH, evaluate them for hypothyroidism.

    FNAB

    • If a nodule is found on U/S, then you should perform an FNAB if it’s significant.

    • It used to be significant if the U/S has detected a nodule that’s:

      1. > 1cm in size or palpable.
      2. In a high risk patient (FHx, head & neck radiation, etc).

    • The guidelines have changed however, and have become much more complicated (see below).

    • In general, you can summarize it as this however:

      1. Any nodule should get a TSH and U/S.

      2. If the nodule is just a cyst, then you don’t need to perform a FNAB.

      3. If it’s a true nodule, the consider referral for FNAB if:

        1. Solid > 1cm.
        2. Complex > 1.5cm.
        3. High risk patient > 5mm.

    Remember

    A nodule should be biopsied if you see:

    1. Solid > 1cm.
    2. Complex > 1.5cm.
    3. High risk patient > 5mm.

    Diagnosis & Management of Thyroid Cancer

    Diagnosis of Thyroid Cancer

    • Diagnosis is made from the FNAB.

    • The interpretation of the FNAB results are as follows:

      • Low Risk → < 5% risk of malignancy.

        1. Hashimoto’s thyroiditis.
        2. Follicular hyperplasia.
        3. Benign Nodule.
        • With these, you perform a repeat U/S at 6 mo, to see if it’s grown. After the 6 mo repeat U/S, you then have annual U/S.

      • Intermediate Risk → 15% risk of malignancy.

        1. Follicular lesion.
        2. Follicular neoplasm.
        3. Hurthle cell lesion.
        • Usually managed with hemi-thyroidectomy for diagnosis or serial ultrasound.

      • High Risk → >80% of malignancy.

        1. Papillary Thyroid Cancer.
        2. Other malignancy.
        • If the FNAB comes back saying it’s ‘diagnostic of papillary thyroid cancer’, then it’s an even higher risk of malignancy (> 99%).
        • Managed with total thyroidectomy, with neck dissection if the cancer has spread.

      • Indeterminate Risk → 15-50% malignant.

        • Where we see tumour, but we’re not sure if it’s benign or malignant.
        • Often due to cystic contents → we miss the tumour itself, or where the biopsy is insufficient.
        • Usually requires re-biopsy → not a low-risk lesion!

    • If the U/S also showed any of the following characteristics, then it increases the risk of malignancy.

      • Micro-calcifications.
      • Hypoechogenicity.
      • Intra-nodal Hypervascularity.

    • If you see suspicious features, pick the most suspicious nodule.

    • If you don’t see suspicious features, pick the largest nodule.

    FNAB Interpretation

    • Low Risk → serial ultrasound → 6 month repeat U/S, then every year.
    • Intermediate Risk → hemi-thyroidectomy for Dx, or serial ultrasound.
    • High Risk → total thyroidectomy, with possible neck dissection if suspicious of spread.
    • Indeterminate Risk → often due to cystic contents, a repeat biopsy is suggested.

    Management of Thyroid Cancer

    1. Surgery:

      • As mentioned before, if you have a high risk FNAB result, then you have to undergo a total thyroidectome +/- central neck dissection.
      • You should not perform a hemi-thyroidectomy in PTC > 1cm.

    2. Post-op Radioactive Iodine:

      • Used as an adjuvant in high risk disease.
      • Also can be therapeutic, to treat distant metastases (since iodine uptake is very specific for thyroid tissue).

    3. Follow-up

      • Try to keep the TSH < 0.1, to suppress thyroid growth.

    Thyroid Cases

    Case #1

    • 63 year old female.

    • Otherwise healthy, no history of radiation exposure.

    • Asymptomatic.

    • Grandmother had thyroid cancer.

    • Undergoing carotid doppler.

      • Multiple nodules throughout gland largest measuring 0.7cm.

    • How would you manage this finding?

      1. Reassure patient and repeat U/S in 6 months.
      2. Request an U/S FNA of largest nodule.
      3. Refer to surgeon for total thyroidectomy.
      4. Disregard the finding.
      5. RAI uptake scan to determine if it is a cold nodule.

    • Explanation:

      • The patient doesn’t have a low TSH, which is the only instance where you’d use a radioactive iodine (RAI) uptake scan.
      • You only use a FNAB if there’s a solid tumour > 1cm, or a complex nodule > 1.5 cm, or if she’s a high risk patient. In her case, it’s only 0.7 cm, and she has no risk factors!
      • So in this case, the answer is serial U/S → repeat after 6 months.

    Case #2

    • 63 year old female.

    • Otherwise healthy, no history of radiation exposure.

    • Asymptomatic.

    • Grandmother had thyroid cancer.

    • Undergoing carotid doppler.

      • Multiple nodules throughout gland largest measuring 1.7cm.
      • (Same as Case 1, except 1.7 cm vs 0.7 cm)

    • How would you manage this finding?

      1. Reassure patient and repeat U/S in 6 months.
      2. Request an U/S FNA of largest nodule.
      3. Refer to surgeon for total thyroidectomy.
      4. Request U/S guided FNA of smaller nodule with calcification and hypervascularity.
      5. RAI uptake scan to determine if it is a cold nodule.

    • Explanation:

      • Her grandmother has thyroid cancer → this is a second degree relative, not a first degree relative, so it’s not a risk factor.

      • However, the largest nodule is 1.7 cm, so this would be something you should biopsy (> 1cm).

        • If there’s a nodule with suspicious features, remember that you always biopsy the most suspicious one!
        • Otherwise, biopsy the largest nodule.

    Case #3

    • 63 year old female.

    • Otherwise healthy, no history of radiation exposure.

    • Long history of goiter.

    • Grandmother had thyroid cancer.

    • Progressive dysphagia, sensation of choking, dyspnea with elevation of arms.

    • U/S Thyroid:

      • Diffusely, significantly, enlarged gland with multiple nodules throughout gland largest measuring 0.7cm.

    • How would you manage this finding?

      1. Reassure patient and repeat U/S in 6 months.
      2. Request an U/S FNA of largest nodule.
      3. Refer to surgeon for total thyroidectomy.
      4. Disregard the finding.
      5. RAI uptake scan to determine if it is a cold nodule.

    • Explanation:

      • In this case, the patient has structural problems associated with the thyroid nodules → airway obstruction, dysphagia, etc.

      • When you elevate your arms with a large goiter, you can displace the thyroid below the thoracic inlet, and compress the jugular vein and trachea.

      • As before, the second-degree relative isn’t relevant.

      • This patient doesn’t have indications for malignancy, but does have indications for a total thyroidectomy due to obstruction.

      • Recall that there are 3 indications for thyroid surgery:

        1. Rule out/treat malignancy.
        2. Compressive symptoms.
        3. Hyperfunctional nodule/gland refractory to medical Tx.

    Case #4

    • 33 year old female.
    • Otherwise healthy, no history of radiation exposure.
    • Progressive heat intolerance, sweating, tremor.
    • No palpable thyroid nodules.
    • TSH < 0.01.

    • How would you manage this finding?

      1. Reassure patient.
      2. RAI uptake scan to determine if it is a hot nodule.
      3. Refer to surgeon for total thyroidectomy.
      4. Request U/S guided FNA.

    • Explanation:

      • In this case, the patient has functional problems associated with the thyroid nodules → signs of hyperthyroidism.
      • Recall that the only indication for an RAI uptake scan is a low TSH, to Dx a hot nodule! This is what you see here.

    Case #5

    • 63 year old female.
    • Otherwise healthy, no history of radiation exposure.
    • Asymptomatic.
    • Grandmother had thyroid cancer.
    • Routine physical exam note new solitary thyroid nodule 2cm.

    • How would you manage this finding?

      1. Reassure patient and repeat U/S in 6 months.
      2. FNAB then request a TSH and U/S.
      3. Refer to surgeon for total thyroidectomy.
      4. Disregard the finding.
      5. RAI uptake scan to determine if it is a cold nodule.

    • Explanation:

      • Note that we can basically never disregard a found nodule → the best case is a serial U/S. This is a shame, because is many cases, it may be preferential to disregard subclinical nodules.
      • Here, the nodule > 1cm, which was one of our indications for a FNAB.
      • And as mentioned before, we should always get a TSH + U/S with any thyroid nodule.

    Case #6

    • 43 year old female from Ukraine.
    • Otherwise healthy.
    • Asymptomatic.
    • Brother had thyroid cancer.
    • Routine physical exam note new solitary thyroid nodule 3.0cm.

    • How would you manage this finding?

      1. Reassure patient and repeat exam in 6 months.
      2. FNAB then request a TSH and U/S.
      3. Refer to surgeon for total thyroidectomy.
      4. Disregard the finding.
      5. RAI uptake scan to determine if it is a cold nodule.

    • Explanation:

      • Her history suggests she may have gotten radiation exposure from the Chernobyl incident, since she’s from Ukraine!
      • Also has a first degree relative with thyroid cancer.
      • So she would be considered a high risk patient.
      • So here you’d definitely do a FNAB (> 0.5 mm + high risk).
      • You’d also do a TSH + U/S, which is done with all thyroid nodules.

    Additional Information

    • TSH normal.
    • U/S confirms 3cm thyroid solitary nodule.
    • Also notes ipsi-lateral 2.5cm Level II lymph node.

    • Now how would you manage this finding?

      1. Reassure patient and repeat exam in 6 months.
      2. Refer the patient to a surgeon to manage.
      3. Refer to surgeon for total thyroidectomy.
      4. Disregard the finding.
      5. RAI uptake scan to determine if it is a cold nodule.

    • Explanation:

      • TSH normal → not a hot nodule.
      • The ipsi-lateral 2.4cm lymph nodes suggests possible spread.
      • You haven’t bone the biopsy yet, so you’re not sure if it’s malignant yet → you wouldn’t want to remove the thyroid yet.
      • But you are more suspicious, so you should refer to a surgeon.

    • The surgeon should:

      1. Reassure patient and repeat exam in 6 months.
      2. Perform an FNA of the neck mass.
      3. Perform a total thyroidectomy.
      4. Perform a hemi-thyroidectomy for diagnosis.
      5. Perform an FNA of thyroid nodule.

    • Explanation:

      • Not only should you FNA the thyroid nodule, but you definitely should FNA the neck mass.
      • Because if that comes back positive for thyroid cancer, then it’ll clinch the diagnosis.

    • FNA Neck reveals Papillary thyroid cancer, the surgeon should?

      1. Reassure patient and repeat exam in 6 months.
      2. Perform a total thyroidectomy, central and lateral neck dissection.
      3. Perform a total thyroidectomy.
      4. Perform a hemi-thyroidectomy for diagnosis.

    • Explanation:

      • Since the lymph node was positive, there’s spread! So you need to do a neck dissection as well as a total thyroidectomy.

    Case #7

    • 35 year old female.
    • Rapidly enlarging palpable right thyroid mass.
    • Grandmother had thyroid cancer.
    • Progressive hoarseness (could be due to RLN damage).
    • Radiation for childhood lymphoma.

    • What symptom is NOT concerning for malignancy

      1. Rapidly enlarging.
      2. Grandmother had thyroid cancer.
      3. Progressive hoarseness.
      4. Radiation for childhood lymphoma.

    • Explanation:

      • First degree relative is a risk factor, but not a second degree relative.
      • Remember that the risk factors were (1) radiation, (2) first-degree relative, (3) rapid-growth, and (4) chernobyl before 14.
      • Progressive hoarseness would be a sign of rapid growth (compression of the recurrent laryngeal).

    • What would be your next investigation?

      1. TSH, U/S.
      2. Serum Thyroglobulin to follow possible thyroid cancer.
      3. Serum Calcitonin to rule out medullary thyroid cancer.
      4. RAI uptake scan to determine if it is a cold nodule.

    • Explanation:

      • TSH and U/S are standard with all nodules.
      • If the vocal cords were paralyzed, then would be an indication for surgery (obstruction).
