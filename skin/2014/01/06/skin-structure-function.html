Skin Structure & Function

Kevan Lu Monday, January 06, 2014

  • The skin is the largest organ in the body.

  • It has a number of functions:

    1. Protection → protection against UV light, mechanical, chemical damage.
    2. Thermal Regulation → heat conservation or loss.
    3. Sensation → pain, temperature, fine touch, etc.
    4. Metabolic Functions → skin has fat underneath it.

Layers of the Skin

  • The skin can be considered as three layers:

    1. Epidermis
    2. Dermis
    3. Subcutaneous Tissue

  • The epidermis and the dermis are connected by Rete-ridges on the epidermis, and dermal papillae on the dermis.

  • The junction between the epidermis and the dermis is the basement membrane.

Remember

  • The layers of the skin are:

    1. Epidermis.
    2. Dermis.
    3. Subcutaneous Tissue.

  • The junction between the epidermis and the dermis is the basement membrane.

Glands of the Skin

  1. Sebaceous Gland → attached to hair follicles.
  2. Apocrine Gland → produces odorous sweat, and is connected to the sebaceous gland.
  3. Eccrine Gland → produces sweat.
  • The odour from the apocrine gland arises from bacterial decomposition.
  • Remember that the A-poo-crine gland produces smelly sweat, while the Eccrine gland produces non-odorous sweat!

The Epidermis

  • The epidermis is composed of keratinized stratified squamous epithelium

    • Keratinized = there’s a keratin layer on top. This is what you exfoliate when you undergo exfoliation.
    • Stratified = has layers.
    • Squamous = the type of cells. Squame = flat.

  • The epidermis itself is comprised of 4 layers (bottom to top):

    1. Stratum Basale (Base)
    2. Stratum Spinosum
    3. Stratum Granulosum
    4. Stratum Corneum

  • 95% of the epidermis is composed of keratinocytes (aka. squamous cells).

  • There are also 3 specialized cells:

    1. Melanocytes → these produce melanin (pigment).
    2. Langerhans Cells → these have immune functions (these are differentiated macrophages).
    3. Merkel Cells → these are responsible for our sense of touch.

  • Tumours can arise from all 3 of these specialized cell types, as well as squamous cells

    • Squamous Cell Carcinoma arises from squamous cells
    • Merkel Cell Carcinoma arises form Merkel cells
    • Melanoma arises from Melanocytes.
    • Langerhans Cell Sarcoma arises from Langerhans Cells.

  • The basement membrane attaches the epidermis to the dermis. It’s literally the basement of the epidermis!

Remember

The layers of the epidermis are:

  1. Stratum Basale (Base)
  2. Stratum Spinosum
  3. Stratum Granulosum
  4. Stratum Corneum

Skin Types

  • Our Palms/Soles are covered with a thick, compact stratum corneum, which increases protection.

    • This compact stratum corneum is also why the palms and soles look whiter.

  • Elsewhere, we have a thinner, less dense stratum corneum.

  • Our Mucosa has no stratum corneum → the first layer is then the stratum granulosum!

    • So our esophagus, or the lining or our mouth, would not have a stratum corneum.
    • Instead, the only layers would be the stratum granulosum, stratum spinosum, and stratum basale.

Summary of Skin Types

  • Palms/Soles → thick stratum corneum
  • Mucosa → no stratum corneum

The Melanocyte

  • Melanocytes have a main body in the stratum basale, and dendritic appendages that spread through the entire epidermis.

    • These dendritic appendages are used to transfer melanin, which is produced by the melanocyte, to the keratinocytes on the surface of the skin.
    • Melanin is produced from tyrosine, and is transported via melanosomes.
    • These melanosomes coat the keratinocyte, protecting the DNA from UV injury.

What Determines Skin Colour?

  • The melanocyte number does NOT determine skin colour.

    • Instead, it’s the difference in the number of melanosomes/melanocyte.
    • Darker skinned people will have more melanosomes per melanocyte.
    • Fairer skinned people will have fewer melanosomes per melanocyte.

The Dermis

  • The dermis is split into two components:

    1. The upper papillary dermis.
    2. The lower reticular dermis.

  • Both of these components have lots of collagen, elastic fibers, and ground substance, which confers elasticity to the skin.

  • Blood vessels and nerves are also found in the dermis.

    • There’s a superficial plexus in the upper papillary dermis.
    • There’s a deep plexus in the lower reticular dermis.

  • These plexuses play a critical role in regulating temperature:

    • When you have a fever, and need to cool your body, there will be vasodilation of the superficial plexus, increasing blood flow, and increasing heat loss.
    • Similarly, when you want to conserve head, there will be vasoconstriction of the superficial plexus.

Remember

  • The vasculature in the dermis is critical in controlling temperature!

Skin Appendages

  • Skin appendages are also referred to as adnexal structures. They include:

    1. Hair follicles.
    2. Sebaceous Glands.
    3. Nails, hair.

  • The following are some disease states involving these appendages:

1. Skin Acne

  • Acne involves the pilo-sebaceous unit → the hair follicle and the attached sebaceous glands.
  • Sebaceous glands produce sebum, which is a natural skin moisturizer.

  • Normally, this production does not cause any problems, and the pilo-sebaceous unit looks like what’s seen on the right.

  • In acne, there’s excess sebum production by the sebaceous glands, causing obstruction:

    • This can be an open comedome, where the obstruction meets with the surace.
    • This can be a closed comedome, where the obstruction is deep to the pore opening.

  • Grossly, these will cause different types of acne:

    • Open Comedomes will cause blackheads.
    • Closed Comedomes will cause whiteheads → that’s why you get the urge to squeeze out the crap with a whitehead!
    • When you rupture a closed comedome, you can cause inflammatory acne.

  • The bacteria most commonly associated with acne is Propionibacterium acnes.

Remember

  • An open comedome has a dilated follicle opening, and is associated with blackheads.
  • A closed comedome has an closed follicle opening, and is associated with whiteheads.

2. Alopecia

  • Alopecia → excessive hair loss.

  • The normal hair growth cycle is:

    • Anagen → growing phase.
    • Catogen → regressing phase.
    • Telogen → resting phase.

  • More will be seen in the PCCIA small group.

Epidermal Changes

  • The Epidermal Desmosomes join keratinocytes together.

    • Pemphigus vulgaris arises from an auto-immune reaction to Desmolgein 3 in the desmosome complex.

  • The Epidermal Basement Membrane separates the epidermis from the dermis.

    • Bullous Pemphigoid arises from an auto-immune reaction to BPAg1 and BPAg2 in the basement membrane complex.

1. Spongiosis

  • Spongiosis → acute intercellular edema.
  • You see edema fluid creating microvesicles in the epidermis.
  • This can occur in acute dermatitis.
  • Clinically, this will present as juicy papules.

2. Hyperkeratosis

  • Hyperkeratosis → chronic thickening of the stratum corneum.

  • This can present in two forms:

    1. Orthokeratosis → no nuclei in the cells.
    2. Parakeratosis → nuclei in the cells.

  • Clinically, both of these will present with scale.

3. Acanthosis

  • Acanthosis → chronic thickening of the stratum spinosum.

    • So this differs from hyperkeratosis in that it’s the straum spinosum that’s thickening now, and not the stratum corneum.
    • Often, both hyperkeratosis and acanthosis will present together.

  • Clinically, there are many skin conditions where we see both hyperkeratosis and acanthosis, including:

    1. Psoriasis, → a chronic plaque with a silvery scale.
    2. Lichenification → scaly skin.

Psoriasis is seen above. Note the silvery scale, and the nucleated thickened stratum corneum (parakeratosis).

Above is lichenification, which also presents with both hyperkeratosis and acanthosis.

4. Acantholysis

  • Acantholysis → loss of the desmosomal connection between keratinocytes, causing them to round up and separate.

    • Clinically, this can be seen with pemphigus vulgaris
    • Recall that pemphigus vulgaris is an auto-immune disease that attacts a component of the desmosome → this is why we lose the desmosomal connection!

Summary

  1. Spongiosis → acute intercellular edema in the epidermis.

    • Will present with juicy papules.
    • Often seen with acute dermatitis.

  2. Hyperkeratosis → chronic thickening of the stratum corneum.

    • Will present as scale.

  3. Acanthosis → chronic thickening of the stratum spinosum.

    • Will also present as scale.

    • When you have both hyperkeratosis and acanthosis, you can see:

      • Psoriasis.
      • Lichenification.

  4. Acantholysis → loss of desmosomal connections between keratinocytes.

    • Will present with flaccid bulla with focal erosions.
    • Seen with pemphigus vulgaris → auto-immune attack on a component of the desmosome.

Review of Lecture Material

    • The melanocyte.

  2. Name the gland involved in acne?

    • The sebaceous gland → excess production of sebum.

  3. Name the odour producing gland?

    • The apocrine gland.

  4. What’s the function of the Langerhan’s cell?

    • The immune response → it’s a differentiated macrophage.

  5. Where is Vitamin D synthesized?

    • In the epidermis or the dermis?
    • In the epidermis → more precisely in the stratum basale and the stratum spinosum.
