Pediatric Dermatoses

Kevan Lu Monday, January 06, 2014

Lecture Objectives

  • Develop an approach to the diagnosis and treatment of children with atopic dermatitis.
  • Contrast the presentation of atopic dermatitis in infants, children and adults.
  • Be aware of childhood dermatologic emergencies such as eczema herpeticum.
  • Develop an approach to the diagnosis and treatment of children presenting with a viral exanthem.
  • Recognize dermatologic manifestations of childhood disease such as meningococcemia and scarlet fever.

Atopic Dermatitis

  • Atopic Dermatitis → aka. atopic eczema.

  • Chronic, inflammatory skin disease characterized by intense pruritis (itchiness).

  • One of the most common skin diseases worldwide → affects up to 25% of children.

    • Usually arises before the age of 5 y/o.

  • Atopic dermatitis is often the first manifestation of the atopic triad → three allergic diseases that tend to occur in a sequence:

    1. Atopic Dermatitis.
    2. Asthma.
    3. Rhinitis.

  • Progression from Atopic Dermatitis → asthma → allergic rhinitis is often referred to as the atopic march.

  • What do you see with Atopic Dermatitis?

    • Dermatitis → duh! Inflammation of the skin.
    • Note that atopic dermatitis is a subset of all dermatitis/eczematous conditions.
    • You’ll often see red scaly patches, with possible vesicles, exudates, erosions, or crusting.

  • What’s other dermatosis/eczematous conditions are there?

    • Atopic Dermatitis → most common.
    • Nummular dermatitis.
    • Seborrheic dermatitis.
    • Irritant contact dermatitis → eg. diaper dermatitis due to stool/urine.
    • Allergic contact dermatitis → eg. with poison ivy. Recall that this was actually due to a Type IV delayed hypersensitivity reaction.
    • Tinea.
    • Scabies.
    • Immunodeficiency.

  • What is Atopic Dermatitis?

    • Inherited, chronic, relapsing pruritic skin, with dry skin (xerosis), and acute/chronic inflammation.
    • Patients will have a tendency to overproduce IgE → makes sense, since this is an atopic condition.

  • What’s the prevalence of AD?

    • Atopic dermatitis is seen in 10-25% of children, and 1-2% of adults.
    • This is because most children grow out of atopic dermatitis.
    • The incidence of dermatitis has been increasing recently, for unclear reasons (one candidate is the hygiene hypothesis).

  • What psychosocial consequences of Atopic Dermatitis are there?

    • AD is the leading cause of sleep loss in children.
    • This affects growth and development, school attendance, grades, etc.
    • Treatment also has a significant financial burden.

  • What is the presentation of Atopic Dermatitis?

    • 90% of patients present before the age of 5.

    • If you see a patient older than 5 with new-onset dermatitis, you should think of alternative diagnoses.

    • As you age, the presentation of atopic dermatitis changes:

      • As you get older, you have more chronic dermatitis.
      • Lesions become less wet.
      • The distribution moves from the extensor to flexural surfaces.

    • Acute Dermatitis:

      • Very wet, very red looking.
      • Often, these will look very inflamed, with lots of crusting.

    • Chronic Dermatitis:

      • More lichenified and leathery.
      • Will often see exaggerated skin markings.
      • There’s almost no other disease that causes this type of lichenification → chronic dermatitis is almost pathognomonic for atopic dermatitis.

    • Extensor Surfaces

      • Where AD is distributed in infants.
      • One good way to remember this is to think of the locations where a child learning to crawl would touch the ground.
      • The elbows, the knees, etc.
      • You also see AD on the face in infants.

    • Flexural Surfaces

      • Where AD is distributed in older children and adults.
      • You see patches in the antecubital fossa, popliteal fossa, neck, wrists, ankles, buttocks.
      • One good way to remember these locations is to think of them as the areas prone to sweating.

Remember

  • Infantile Atopic Dermatitis:

    • Acute Dermatitis.
    • Wet Lesions.
    • Extensor Surfaces.

  • Adult Atopic Dermatitis:

    • Chronic Dermatitis.
    • Dry Lesions.
    • Flexural surfaces.

  • What associated finding are there with atopic dermatitis?

    • Pruritis → all patients with AD are itchy!

      • If the patient doesn’t have itching, then they don’t have atopic dermatitis.

    • This is often worse in the evening/night.

    • Patients will also almost always have xerosis → dry skin.

    • Icthyosis vulgaris is seen in half of patients with atopic dermatitis. This is ‘fish-scale’-like scaling of the skin, often on the wrists.
    • Hyperlinearity of the Palms.
    • Dennie Morgan Lines → recall that these were associated with atopic diseases. Eczema being one of them!
    • Pityriasis Alba → white macules/patches on the cheek.
    • Keratosis Pilaris → pink little follicular bumps.

  • How do you diagnose Atopic Dermatitis?

    • This is a clinical diagnosis based on the Hx and P/E.

    • No other tests are usually required.

    • To make this diagnosis, you have to have all 4 of the essential features:

      1. Pruritis → they gotta be itchy!
      2. Dermatitis → can be acute, subacute, chronic.
      3. Proper Distribution → extensor regions in infants, flexural regions in adults. Also, the groin and armpits (axilla) will be spared with atopic dermatitis.
      4. Chronic or Relapsing History → you should see exacerbations that come and go.

    • As before, you should rule out mimickers of atopic dermatitis → recall that not all dermatitis is atopic dermatitis!

    • You can use other characteristics such as the age of onset, and the morphological features described previously (icthyosis vulgaris, hyperlinearity, etc).
