            Pediatric Orthopedics II

            Kevan Lu Tuesday, February 10, 2015

            Lecture Overview

            • The previous lecture focused on acute trauma.
            • This lecture will focus on pediatric orthopedic conditions.
              • Emphasis on congenital/developmental abnormalities.
              • Overuse syndromes will be discussed in the small group.

            Clinical Approach to Pediatric Ortho Conditions

            • There are a number of different presenting complaints:
              • Gait → limp, legs turns in/out, tripping.
              • Appearance → angulation, rotation, abnormal shape.
              • Function → clumsy, weak, uncoordinated.
              • Instability/Stiffness → gives out, doesn’t trust it, wobbly, doesn’t move.
              • Pain.
            • Taking a history and physical exam with children can be difficult, but they key thing is to recognize atypical findings.
            • Key Points on History:
              1. Age of Patient
              2. Onset & Duration of Symptoms → Acute? Chronic?
                • Acute onset suggest trauma/infection.
                • Chronic/gradual onset suggests overuse, congenital/developmental abnormalities, or inflammatory conditions.
              3. Symptoms → Pain, weakness, stiffness, etc.

            Normal Growth in a Child

            • Frequent pediatric complaints are as follows:
              • Knock-knees/bowlegs (angular variation).
              • Intoeing/out-toeing (rotational variation).
              • Flat feet.
            • In many cases, these can actually be normal variants, so it’s important to avoid overtreating them.

            1. Angular Variation

            • Bow-legs/Knock-knees:
              • Genu varum → bow legs.
              • Genu valgum → knock knees.
            • Both are normal at different phases of development:
              • Genu varum is normal from birth - 2 y/o.
              • Genu valgum is normal from 3-7 y/o.
            • So if you see either of these within these age ranges, and the patient is asymptomatic, you probably don’t need to treat it.

            Normal Angular Variation

            • It’s normal to see genu varum from birth - 2 y/o.
            • It’s normal to see genu valgum from 3-7 y/o.

            2. Rotational Variation

            • In-toeing/Out-toeing:
              • In-toeing → internal rotation of the feet.
              • Out-toeing → external rotation of the feet.
            • Extremely common reason for referral.
              • However, a certain degree of either is normal; it should only be looked at when the in-toeing or out-toeing is excessive.

            Causes of an Abnormal Gait

            • When is a gait CONCERNING?
              1. Falls outside of the normal age range
                • >2 y/o with genu varum.
                • >7 y/o with genu valgum.
              2. Excessive angulation or rotation.
              3. Symptoms
              4. Asymmetric Gait
            • Causes of an Abnormal Gait:
              1. Developmental Dysplasia of the Hip (DDH).
              2. Clubbed Foot.
              3. Slipped Capital Femoral Epiphysis (SCFE).
              4. Legg-Calve-Perthes Disease
            • The latter two will be discussed in the small group session.

            Developmental Dysplasia of the Hip (DDH)

            • DDH → a spectrum of dysplastic hip disorders ranging from a shallow acetabulum to a perinatal hip subluxation or dislocation.
              • Typically presents with painless abnormal gait in a toddler.
              • However, the presentation may vary significantly depending on age and severity of the hip dysplasia.

            Risk Factors

            • Firstborn, Female.
            • Breech presentation.
            • Family History
            • Oligohydramnios.
            • Multiparous birth.
            • Other MSK problems.

            Physical Exam Findings

            • LIMITED HIP ABDUCTION.
            • Leg length discrepancy.
            • Hip instability → assessed with the Barlow test and the Ortolani test.

            • Waddling gait; painless limp.

            • Barlow Test:
              • Sign of Exit
              • Dislocation test for reduced but dislocatable hip.
              • Child supine with hips and knees flexed at right angles
              • Thighs adducted and posterior telescoping force applied
              • (+) if the head of femur is felt to slip out of the acetabulum.
              • Important to reduce it back if (+).
            • Ortolani Test:
              • Sign of Entry; the opposite of the Barlow test.
              • Reduction test for dislocated hip.
              • Child supin with hips and knees flexed at right angles.
              • Long finger on greater trochanter.
              • Thigh lifted and abducted.
              • (+) if head of femur felt to slip back into acetabulum.
              • This is done to reduce the hip if the Barlow test is (+).
            • Both the Barlow test and the Ortolani test are unreliable > 3-6 m/o.
              • So this is only a good screening test in infants.

            Management

            • Diagnose early:
              • Barlow and Ortolani to screen
              • X-rays to confirm the diagnosis.
              • This prevents long-term disability and early onset OA.
            • Treatment depends on age.

            Clubbed Foot

            • Clubbed Foot → Congenital Talipes Equinovarus.
              • Ankle in plantar flexion.
              • Heel and forefoot in varus alignment.
            • Typically diagnosed at birth (when you see something as shown to the right).
            • Causes of Clubfoot:
              • Genetic component, but many are sporadic.
              • Associated with spina bifida, and other syndromes.

            Natural History

            • Progressive deformity.
            • Increasing stiffness.
            • Significant reduction in ambulation and activities.
            • Shoe wear is problematic.

            Treatment of Clubfoot

            • Ponsetti Treatment Protocol → the gold standard for treatment around the world for clubfoot. It comprises of:
              1. Weekly manipulations and casting for 4-6 weeks.
              2. Percutaneous Achilles tendon lengthening.
              3. Post-operative casting for 3 weeks.
              4. Abduction brace.
            • Post-op, bracing will need to be worn:
              • Boots and bars (as shown to the right).
              • Must be worn for 3 months full time.
              • After that, 2-3 more years at night.
            • What happens if Ponsetti fails or the clubfoot recurs?
              • Posteromedial/comprehensive release is done.
              • Surgical release and lengthening of the contracted structures, and realignment.
              • This involves extensive joint, tendon, and ligament dissection.
              • Used to be the mainstay of treatment, but outcomes are much poorer, and there’s tremendous scarring.
