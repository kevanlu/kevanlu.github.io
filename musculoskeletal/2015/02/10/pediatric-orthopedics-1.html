                            Anatomical Differences in Children

                            • Children are NOT little adults; there are key musculoskeletal differences.
                            • Key musculoskeletal components that differ include:
                              • Bone is more porous and pliable.
                              • Periosteum → soft (osteogenic) tissue that surrounds the bone.
                                • In children, this is thicker and more active than in adults.
                                • It also contributes to healing; this is why children generally heal faster and better with a fracture.
                                • However, this large periosteum can sometimes get in the way when attempting to reduce a fracture.
                              • Physis → aka. Growth Plate.
                                • Seen as cartilage at ends of long bones.
                                • These are a site of relative weakness.
                                • Damage to the growth plate can result in growth and growth arrest issues.

                            Anatomical Terminology

                            • Epiphysis → above the physis (growth plate).
                            • Physis → at the growth plate.
                            • Metaphysis → recall that this was the box around the widest part of the bone.
                            • Diaphysis → the shaft of the bone.
                            • Apophysis → tendon insertion sites (ie. tubercles).

                            Pediatric MSK Trauma

                            • Most single extremity, generally minor.
                            • Common mechanisms include falls from low heights, sport-related injuries, pedestrians struck by vehicles, NAI (non-accidental injury, aka. child abuse) infants/toddlers, vehicle operators (kids on ATVs, snowmobiles, etc).
                            • Differences with Pediatric Trauma:
                              • Because children’s bone is less dense and more porous, there is less comminution due to lower energy requirements for fracture.
                              • Physeal (growth plate) fractures are a worry in children, but not adults.
                              • Children are less likely to get ligament damage or dislocation.
                            • Pediatric Fracture Types:
                              1. Buckle Fracture → incomplete fracture of the shaft of a long bone, with ‘bulging’ of the cortex on one side only.
                              2. Greenstick Fracture → similar to a buckle fracture (where the fracture is incomplete), but here the bone actually cracks, and you see bending on the other side of the bone.
                              3. Plastic deformation → no fracture line is seen, but the bone has been curved due to continuous microtrauma. These require treatment, even though there’s no fracture!
                              4. Physeal injury → will be discussed later.

                            Physeal Injuries

                            Salter-Harris Classification

                            • The Salter-Harris Classification system is used to describe damage to the physis (growth plate).
                              • It’s not necessary to remember this, but note that higher values correspond more severe damage to the growth plate.
                            • A useful mnemonic to help remember the classification is SALTER itself!
                              • S → Slip (separated/straight across): fracture of the physis only.
                              • A → Above: fracture starts above the physis (metaphysis), and travels into it.
                              • L → Lower: fracture starts below the physis (epiphysis), and travels into it.
                              • TE → Through Everything: fracture goes through the epiphysis, physis, and metaphysis.
                              • R → Rammed: the growth plate is crushed.

                            Salter Mnemonic for Salter-Harris Classification

                            • S → Slip (separated/straight across): fracture of the physis only.
                            • A → Above: fracture starts above the physis (metaphysis), and travels into it.
                            • L → Lower: fracture starts below the physis (epiphysis), and travels into it.
                            • TE → Through Everything: fracture goes through the epiphysis, physis, and metaphysis.
                            • R → Rammed: the growth plate is crushed.

                            Prognosis

                            About 10% develop growth disturbances • Factors – Age most significant – Severity – Type of injury • Type IV and V – Method of reduction – Vascularity of growth plate – Closed versus open injury

                            Growth Arrest

                            Growing kids • Asymmetrical growth main problem • Angulation of bone to compensate? • Length discrepancy • Treatment dependent on amount of growth remaining

                            How do you treat growth arrest? Very ortho dependent. If close to end of growth, try arrest the other limb to prevent leg length discrepancy. If very young, will resect piece of bone in growth plate, and hope growth remains. If that fails, reconstructive surgeries will be undergone.

                            Summary of Physeal Injuries First Message Any growth plate injury is SERIOUS no matter the TYPE OF INJURY or SITE OF INJURY

                            Second Message All growth plate injuries should be followed at least 1 year (preferably 2 years).

                            Third Message After any growth plate injury, warn the family about possible complications. The importance of follow‐up can not be over‐emphasized.

                            CASE - 14 y/o male. - Acute onset of right hip pain while participating in 100 m dash at school track meed. - Antalgic limp, complains of anterior hip pain. - AP XR of the R hip shows a little flake of bone there. - Look at it 2 weeks later, more bone there. - This is an avulsion fracture. - Muscle contracts and pulls of a piece of bone with a muscle tear. - This can occur at ANY APOPHYSIS. - In this case, this is where the rectus femoris attaches (recall that this was one of the quadriceps -> vastus lateralis, vastus medialis, vastus intermedialis, rectus femoris). - Commonly seen in pelvis, 5th MT, medial epicondyle. - Heal with conservative Tx.

                            Summary of Pediatric Fracture Treatment - Healing is rarely a problem -> as long as the two bone ends are in the same room, they’ll heal together. - Recall that periosteum very thick and active, so bone heals easily. - Problem is getting it right before they heal. - SPecific alignment criteria depend on Pt. age.

                            Remodelling: Ability of immature skeleton to reconstitute pre‐injury alignment by responding to biomechanical stimuli  ACCEPT that it will occur, don’t EXPECT it to occur (treatment easiest initially)

                            Will help: – Children with at least 2 years of growth remaining – Metaphyseal »>diaphyseal location of fracture - CLOSER TO GROWTH PLATE = EASIER REGROWTH. – Defect in the plane of the joint (i.e. dorsal/volar angulation in wrist fracture) - Eg. pronation and supination of forearm more common than radial or ulnar deviation, so displacements in the transverse plane will heal better. – Roughly 1° / month of remaining growth (<10yrs)
