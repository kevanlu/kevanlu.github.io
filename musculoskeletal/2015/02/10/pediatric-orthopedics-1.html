          Kevan Lu Tuesday, February 10, 2015

          Overview

          • The first lecture of the series will be podcasted.
          • The second lecture has some videos of patient faces, which will be edited out out of the podcast.
            • These videos aren’t crucial for exam purposes.
          • The first hour will focus on pediatric trauma.
          • The second hour will focus on general pediatric orthopedics.

          Anatomical Differences in Children

          • Children are NOT little adults; there are key musculoskeletal differences.
          • Key musculoskeletal components that differ include:
            • Bone is more porous and pliable.
            • Periosteum → soft (osteogenic) tissue that surrounds the bone.
              • In children, this is thicker and more active than in adults.
              • It also contributes to healing; this is why children generally heal faster and better with a fracture.
              • However, this large periosteum can sometimes get in the way when attempting to reduce a fracture.
            • Physis → aka. Growth Plate.
              • Seen as cartilage at ends of long bones.
              • These are a site of relative weakness.
              • Damage to the growth plate can result in growth and growth arrest issues.

          Anatomical Terminology

          • Epiphysis → above the physis (growth plate).
          • Physis → at the growth plate.
          • Metaphysis → recall that this was the box around the widest part of the bone.
          • Diaphysis → the shaft of the bone.
          • Apophysis → bony tubercles, where tendons insert.
            • Enthesis → the connective tissue that attaches a tendon or ligament onto an apophysis.
            • Enthesitis → inflammation at the point of attachment (will be seen in future lectures).

          Pediatric MSK Trauma

          • Most single extremity, generally minor.
          • Common mechanisms include falls from low heights, sport-related injuries, pedestrians struck by vehicles, NAI (non-accidental injury, aka. child abuse) infants/toddlers, vehicle operators (kids on ATVs, snowmobiles, etc).
          • Differences with Pediatric Trauma:
            • Because children’s bone is less dense and more porous, there is less comminution due to lower energy requirements for fracture.
            • Physeal (growth plate) fractures are a worry in children, but not adults.
            • Children are less likely to get ligament damage or dislocation.
          • Pediatric Fracture Types:
            1. Buckle Fracture → incomplete fracture of the shaft of a long bone, with ‘bulging’ of the cortex on one side only.
            2. Greenstick Fracture → similar to a buckle fracture, but here you bending of the entire bone, along with a crack.
            3. Plastic deformation → no fracture line is seen, but the bone has been curved due to continuous microtrauma. These require treatment, even though there’s no fracture!
            4. Physeal injury → will be discussed later.

          Physeal Injuries

          Salter-Harris Classification

          • The Salter-Harris Classification system is used to describe damage to the physis (growth plate).
            • It’s not necessary to remember this, but note that higher values correspond more severe damage to the growth plate.
            • The ultimate worry with this is growth arrest; this can lead to limb length discrepancies if the whole growth plate stops working, or angular deformities if only part stops working.
          • A useful mnemonic to help remember the classification is SALTER itself!
            • S → Slip (separated/straight across): fracture of the physis only.
            • A → Above: fracture starts above the physis (metaphysis), and travels into it.
            • L → Lower: fracture starts below the physis (epiphysis), and travels into it.
            • TE → Through Everything: fracture goes through the epiphysis, physis, and metaphysis.
            • R → Rammed: the growth plate is crushed.

          Salter Mnemonic for Salter-Harris Classification

          • S → Slip (separated/straight across): fracture of the physis only.
          • A → Above: fracture starts above the physis (metaphysis), and travels into it.
          • L → Lower: fracture starts below the physis (epiphysis), and travels into it.
          • TE → Through Everything: fracture goes through the epiphysis, physis, and metaphysis.
          • R → Rammed: the growth plate is crushed.
          • Salter-Harris I:
            • Fracture straight through the physis.
            • Prognosis is very good, however follow-up is still needed.
          • Salter-Harris II:
            • Fracture above the physis, going into the physis.
            • Most common injury to the growth plate.
            • Most commonly affects the distal radius.
            • Treated with a closed reduction and fixation (casts).
            • Prognosis is usually good, but as with all growth plate injuries, follow-up is needed.
          • Salter-Harris III:
            • Fracture in the epiphysis, going into the physis.
            • These are worse than Salter-Harris II fractures, because the joint will be involved.
            • Since it’s an intra-articular fracture, it needs an anatomic reduction (surgery).
            • The image on the right shows a specific type of Salter-Harris III fracture called a Tillaux Fracture:
              • This is a fracture of the distal tibia that occurs because the medial side of the distal physis closes earlier than the lateral side.
              • As a result, with inversion or external-rotation, the anterior talofibular ligament (ATFL) on the lateral side of joint can actually avulse the lateral epiphysis.
          • Salter-Harris IV:
            • Fracture both above and below the physis.
            • Danger of a ‘bony bridge’ forming between the metaphysis and epiphysis, through the physis.
              • This increases the risk of growth arrest.
            • As with a Salter-Harris III, this needs an anatomic reduction.
          • Salter-Harris V:
            • Crushing of the growth plate.
            • Often difficult to see radiographically, but will have the most significant affects on growth.

          Prognosis of Physeal Injuries

          • About 10% develop growth disturbances; this is more likely with increasing Salter-Harris grade.
          • Factors that influence prognosis:
            • AGE → this is MOST SIGNIFICANT.
              • The younger the patient is, the more problematic a growth plate injury will be in the future.
              • The younger you are, the greater the amount of missed growth.
            • Type of injury → Type IV or V are more worrisome.
            • Method of reduction.
            • Vascularity of the growth plate.
            • Closed versus open injury.
          • Growth Arrest:
            • Worrisome in young children who still have a lot of growth left.
            • The biggest worry is asymmetrical growth; angulation defects or length discrepancies can result in pain and impairment in the future.
            • Treatment of growth arrest depends on the amount of growth remaining:
              • If close to end of growth, you can try to arrest the other limb to prevent leg length discrepancy.
              • If very young, you can try to resect the piece of bone in the growth plate, and hope that growth remains.
              • If that fails, reconstructive surgeries will be undergone.

          Key Points

          1. ANY growth plate injury is SERIOUS no matter the TYPE OF INJURY or the SITE OF INJURY.
          2. All growth plate injuries should be FOLLOWED FOR AT LEAST 1 YEAR (preferably 2 years).
          3. After any growth plate injury, warn the family about possible complications. It’s critically important to follow them up!

          Pediatric Elbow Injuries

          • The elbow is a very confusing area of pediatric trauma.
            • In very young children, the elbow joint is comprised entirely of cartilage, so on an XR, you won’t be able to see the joint.
            • As they age, more and more of the elbow joint becomes ossified.
            • Those weird fragments of bone floating in the elbow joint over there? They’re just different parts of the joint that have begun to ossify.

          The CRITOE Mnemonic

          • CRITOE → a mnemonic to help remember the order of expected ossification in the elbow.
          • Ossification should be seen by these times:
            • C → Capitellum (3 years).
            • R → Radial head (5 years).
            • I → Internal (medial) Epicondyle (7 years).
            • T → Trochlea (9 years).
            • O → Olecranon (11 years).
            • E → External (lateral) Epicondyle (13 years).

          CRITOE

          CRITOE Anatomic Location Expected Time of Ossification
          C Capitellum 3 years
          R Radial Head 5 years
          I Internal (medial) Epicondyle 7 years
          T Trochlea 9 years
          O Olecranon 11 years
          E External (lateral) Epicondyle 13 years

          Assessing Elbow Injuries on XR

          • Quantitative features to assess on XR:
            • Anterior Humeral Line → if you draw a line along the anterior border of the humerus, it should bisect the capitellum.
              • If it doesn’t align as such, it suggests that there’s been a displacement, as seen on the right.
              • Will often be accompanied by swelling (on the right, you can see joint effusion as a darker region around the joint).
            • Radiocapitellar Line → if you draw a line through the neck of the radius, it should also bisect the capitellum.
              • If it fails to do so, this is suggestive of a dislocation of the radial head.
              • It’s important to draw this along the neck, and not the shaft, in order to make an accurate diagnosis.

          Example Elbow Fracture

          • Monteggia Fracture → fracture of the proximal ulna, resulting in the dislocation of the radial head.
          • Here, the apex of the fractured ulna pushes the radial head off the capitellum; this is the clinically important component of the fracture.
          • The image on the right shows an example of a Monteggia fracture. Note how the radiocapitellar line fails to bisect the capitellum. This is suggestive of a radial displacement.
          • Here, it’s clear that the cause of the displacement was the fractured ulna.

          Management of Pediatric Elbow Injuries

          • Use non osseous injury indicators such as swelling to keep high index of suspicion of more serious injury.
          • Refer if:
            • Any significantly swollen elbow, especially in non ossified elbows. This may be a hemarthrosis of the joint.
            • Concern of instability.
            • Any displaced bony fracture.
            • Any incongruously reduced dislocation.

          Treatment of Pediatric Fractures

          • Healing is seldom a problem; pediatric bones heal very easily:
            • “As long as the two bone ends are in the same room, they’ll heal together!”
          • The problem is getting the alignment right before the bones heal.
          • In some scenarios, the bone can actually re-align itself; this is called bone remodelling, and it means that we often need less significant treatment to obtain a good result.
          • Otherwise, trauma treatment is very similar (reduction, fixation, follow-up).

          Bone Remodelling

          • Bone remodelling → the ability of an immature skeleton to reconstitute its pre-injury alignment by responding to biomechanical stimuli.
            • On the right is a (crazy) example of what can happen!
            • In children, the periosteum is so active that this is possible.
          • You should ACCEPT that it will occur, but don’t EXPECT it to occur.
            • Treatment is easiest initially, so if you forgo treatment expecting the bone to remodel, you might be screwed later.
          • Remodelling occurs at roughly 1° per month of remaining growth (<10 years).
          • When will remodelling occur?
            • More likely in children with at least 2 years of growth remaining.
            • Metaphyseal >>> diaphyseal location of fracture; in other words, the closer to the growth plate, the better the remodelling will be.
            • Defects in the plane of the joint will remodel better:
              • For instance, since pronation and supination of the forearm are more common than radial or ulnar deviation, a transverse fracture of the radius is more likely to remodel than one with radial or ulnar angulation.
          • When will remodelling NOT help?
            • Displaced intra-articular fractures → will still need surgery.
              • IMPORTANT → INTRA-ARTICULAR FRACTURES WON’T REMODEL.
            • Mid-shaft fractures if there’s significant angulation or rotation.
              • As mentioned before, since this is farther from the growth plate, it’s less likely to remodel.
            • Fractures out of plane of the joint movement:
              • For instance, radial or ulnar angulation of a wrist fracture.
