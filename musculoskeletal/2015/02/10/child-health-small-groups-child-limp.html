            Child Health Small Groups – Limp

            Kevan Lu Tuesday, February 10, 2015

            Lecture Objectives

            • By the end of the session students should be able to:
              1. Develop a systematic method to approaching acute limb pain.
              2. List at least 4 important factors for the diagnosis of acute limb pain.
              3. Define Septic Arthritis and Transient Synovitis.
              4. Define Slipped Capital Femoral Epiphysis (SCFE).
              5. List at least 3 common clinical manifestations of SCFE.
              6. Describe characteristics of limb pain which would suggest child abuse.

            Case #1

            Case Summary

            John, a 13-year-old boy, presents with a 6 day history of right sided limp. He complains of pain involving the right thigh and knee. He denies fever, a preceding illness or trauma and is otherwise in good health.

            • John presents with the clinical problem of new onset change in gait and pain. When taking the history from John, what additional information would be helpful in determining the cause of his limp?
              • OPQRST.
              • Associated symptoms (rashes, fever, weight loss, recent growth).
              • History of trauma.
              • Past medical history.
              • Family history.
              • Travel history.
            • What factors would be important in the physical examination?
              • Look, feel, move, special tests, neuromuscular exam, including the joint above and below.
              • Height and weight.
              • Gait assessment.
              • Hepatosplenomegaly.
              • Height, weight.

            Additional Information

            On examination, John looks well. Height is 158 cm and weight is 80 kg. Movement of the right hip in flexion, extension, and rotation is painful. There is no swelling or warmth. The rest of the general physical examination is normal. There is no rash or organomegaly.

            • Examine John’s growth chart. What do you conclude?
              • Height is normal (near the 50th %’ile), but his weight is significantly elevated (around the 99.9th %’ile).
              • In other words, he has a higher weight than expected given his height.
            • Body mass index (BMI) is calculated as kg/meter squared. What is John’s BMI? What do you conclude?
              • .
              • Hence, he’s obese (BMI greater than 95th %’ile)
            • John complains of thigh and knee pain, but the physical findings point to the right hip as the painful joint. How can you explain this?
              • Impingement of the obturator nerve can result in radiation to the thigh and knee.

            • Review the table on the next two pages. When assessing a child with a limp, what do you think would be the most important factors from the history and physical examination, to help in determining a differential diagnosis?
              1. Acute vs. chronic.
              2. Age of patient.
              3. Systemic symptoms.
              4. Painful vs. non-painful limp.
            • Which diagnoses do you think are likely possibilities for John? (List at least 4). Why? Which is MOST likely?
              • Slipped Capital Femoral Epiphysis (SCFE).
              • Septic arthritis and osteomyelitis.
              • Trauma.
              • Rheumatologic disorder.
            • What is the most common cause of hip pain in this age group? Do you think that is the case here? Why/why not?
              • Trauma, but this unlikely because there’s no history of trauma.
              • The next most common cause is SCFE.
            • Why might trauma still be in the differential diagnosis even if there is no history of injury?
              • The kid might be lying about his history.
              • There might also be the risk of non-accidental injury (child abuse).
            • What would you do next? How can the diagnosis be confirmed?
              • For SCFE, diagnosis is confirmed by bilateral hip radiography, which needs to include anteroposterior and frog-leg lateral views in patients with stable slipped capital femoral epiphysis, and anteroposterior and cross-table lateral views in patients with the unstable form.

            On this anteroposterior pelvic view, note the increased opacity of his left metaphysis and the subtle widening of the physis. Also note the malalignment of the proximal femur, i.e. the change in position between the femoral head and neck.

            • Define Slipped Capital Femoral Epiphysis
              • Fracture of the growth plate due to slippage of the epiphysis.
              • Non-inflammatory, results is displacement of the femoral head relative to the neck.
              • Occurs in the growth plate during periods of rapid growth in adolescents (age 10-14).
              • Risk factors include:
                • Obesity.
                • Male gender.
                • Hypothyroidism.
                • GH deficiency.
              • SCFE is classified as follows:
                • Acute = < 3 weeks.
                • Chronic = > 3 weeks.
                • Acute/unstable = non-weight bearing.
                • Chronic/stable = Antalgic gait only.
            • What is the etiology of this disorder?
              • Etiology is thought to be multifactorial; it’s more commonly seen in the obese with delayed skeletal maturation, or taller patients who recently underwent a rapid growth spurt.
            • Why is an x-ray taken of both hips when only one is symptomatic?
              • Some may have bilateral involvement already; others may soon progress to bilateral involvement.
              • Even if the other hip is normal, it’s useful to have a normal reference.
            • What are the treatment options for SCFE?
              • Stop weight-bearing in the interim.
              • Surgical fixation.
              • Possible need for late osteotomies.
            • What is the long-term prognosis?
              • Generally speaking, the prognosis is good.
              • However, there’s a risk of subsequent avascular necrosis and/or DJD.
            • If a similar history were obtained regarding a 6 year old boy (James) would another diagnosis be more likely?
              • Yes; it would more likely be Legg-Calvé-Perthes disease.
              • This is where there’s interruption of the blood supply to the femoral head, leading to AVN.
              • More common in boys between the ages of 3-10.

            Case #2

            Case Summary

            Mai, a 3 year old girl, presents to the emergency department with acute onset limp and refusal to walk. Mai was well until 8 days ago when she had an upper respiratory tract infection. She had a clear nasal discharge, low-grade fever and decreased appetite for two days. Three days ago she seemed well and back to her usual self. On the day prior to this visit she awoke after her afternoon nap and refused to walk. Today she will walk if encouraged but limps and complains that it hurts.

            • What other information would be helpful?
              • OPQRST.
              • Associated symptoms (rashes, fever, weight loss, recent growth).
              • History of trauma.
              • Past medical history.
              • Family history.
              • Travel history.

            Additional Information

            Mai has previously been well. Her parents are South Korean. Mai was born in Canada. Mai’s maternal grandmother has osteoarthritis. Mai is the only child born to her parents. There is no history of trauma. She currently has no rash, has a good appetite and feels well.

            • What would you be looking for on physical examination?
              • Look, feel, move, special tests, neuromuscular exam, including the joint above and below.
              • Height and weight.
              • Gait assessment.
              • Hepatosplenomegaly.
              • Height, weight.

            Additional Information

            Mai is playing with her doll on the examining bed when you come to see her. She looks well and is chatty and friendly. She is sitting with her left leg flexed and externally rotated. She cooperates until you try to examine her legs at which point she cries. You examine her in her mother’s lap and determine that there is no tenderness in the ankle and knee but there is decreased range of motion in the left hip and pain with internal rotation. There is no joint swelling or redness. Mai walks a couple of steps with encouragement from her mother. Temperature is 37.4 C.

            • What are the most important (discriminating) features of this history and physical?
              1. Patient age.
              2. Patient looks well.
              3. Patient is improving.
              4. Previous URTI.
            • What diagnoses would you consider? Are they likely? Why and/or why not? What is the most likely diagnosis
              • Transient monarticular synovitis/tenosynovitis.
              • Occult trauma.
              • Septic arthritis and osteomyelitis → unlikely w/o constitutional symptoms.
              • Juvenile rheumatoid arthritis → unlikely w/o redness or swelling.
              • Malignancy.
              • SCFE isn’t on the DDx; she’s too young.
            • What diagnoses would it be important to rule out?
              • Septic arthritis and osteomyelitis.
              • Malignancy.
              • Child abuse.
            • What tests would help you out?
              • CBC-D (and white count), ESR, CRP for inflammatory/infectious causes.
              • X-rays.
            • Tests are all normal (leukocytes and ESR are only mildly elevated), X-ray is normal. What do you conclude?
              • Transient synovitis.
            • What is transient synovitis?
              • Self-resolving joint inflammation, with an unknown etiology.
              • Often preceded by a viral infection.
              • Common; accounts for 30% of non-traumatic limps in children.
            • What is the natural history of this disorder?
              • Resolves in 1-2 weeks with no long-term complications.
            • What will be the treatment plan for this girl?
              • Conservative treatment.
              • The patient should return if there are:
                • Persistent symptoms past 10 days.
                • Worsening symptoms or systemic symptoms.
            • Why must septic arthritis and osteomyelitis be considered? What factors in the history and physical would make these diagnoses more likely?
              • They must be considered because they’re serious, can result in long-term complications, and can spread.
                • Septic arthritis → infection of the joint.
                • Osteomyelitis → infection of the bone.
              • Factors that make these more likely include:
                • High fever, markedly elevated WBC or ESR.
                • Severe pain and tenderness.
            • Would your approach and differential diagnosis be different if Mai was 6 months old?
              • Transient synovitis is less likely in younger iBnfants.
              • Infection/trauma are more likely.
            • Why does trauma need to be considered? What factors in the history and physical would make this diagnosis more likely?
              • Risk of child abuse (NAI, non-accidental injury).
              • Factors that make this more likely include:
                • History of trauma or atypical trauma.
                • Bruises of different ages.
                • Social/financial stressors.

            Case #3

            Case Summary

            Tom is a 10-month-old male infant is brought to the emergency department (ED) following a fall which occurred yesterday morning. He will no longer stand up. The parents explain that Tom was trying to run away during a game and fell down. The infant seems in obvious discomfort and is crying.

            • What seems inconsistent in the brief description given of the traumatic event?
              • 10 month-olds don’t run.
              • It happened yesterday morning (delay in seeking medical attention).

            History

            Tom is the product of a full term delivery born to a 17 year old mother who is unmarried. She and the baby live with her grandmother. The 18 year old father is involved in the care of the baby. The infant’s birth weight was 3.89 kg (90%ile). The mother says that the infant is doing well but he has not yet received any immunizations. Tom does not have a family doctor. Tom was seen in the ER 2 months ago with an accidental ingestion of acetaminophen. Tom’s father was recently fired from his job.

            • Discuss the risk factors for child abuse and neglect that are evident from this history.
              • Young mother.
              • Child doesn’t have regular medical care.
              • Father has prior history of drug overdose.
              • Financial and psychosocial stressors.

            Physical Exam

            Tom is clearly in pain. Temperature is 36.8 C. There are bruises on the infant’s back, left buttock and the right thigh. There is tenderness over the right leg from the hip to the knee. The right leg looks slightly swollen. Tom will sit but will not bear weight or crawl. There is no rash or organomegaly. The diaper is soaked through.

            • What are the important findings in the physical exam?
              • Bruises in multiple locations.
              • Swelling and tenderness over leg.
              • The diaper is soaked through (suggestive of neglect).
              • No evidence of other causes (constitutional symptoms, etc).
            • An X-Ray shows a diaphyseal femoral fracture. Does this fit with the history given?
              • No, femur fractures in this age group are very rare, particularly since infants of this age are not ambulating.
            • Are there circumstances where a fall as described by the parents could result in a femoral fracture?
              • Probably not, unless they have some sort of bone pathology (Osteogenesis imperfecta, chronic steroid use, etc).
            • Are there any fractures which occur with simple falls in toddlers?
              • Yes; Toddler’s Fracture, spiral fracture of the distal tibia.
              • Metatarsal fractures are also common with simple falls.
            • What is the role of a physician when child abuse is suspected?
              • Mandatory reporting to CAS (Children’s Aid Society).
            • State some of the forms of abuse that a child may be subjected to.
              • Physical, emotional, psychological, neglect.
            • What factors may influence the under-reporting of child abuse by parents or other people involved with the child’s care?
              • Denial, fear of the perpetrator, fear of retribution.
              • Not recognizing signs of abuse.
              • Not knowing how to report the abuse.
              • Avoiding the demands of becoming involved.
              • Having personal views which condone the use of physical punishment.
              • Not knowing that reports can be made anonymously, with no legal consequence.

            Case #4

            Case Summary

            Yvette is an 11-year-old girl who comes to her family doctor’s office complaining of right knee pain. She limps into the office. The problem began approximately 3 weeks ago. The pain comes and goes, is worse with activity and if she bangs her knee. Yvette plays competitive soccer and is on the school basketball team. There is no history of trauma, joint swelling, rash, other affected joints, fever or malaise. Yvette has never been in hospital and has had no surgery. She is on no medication.

            • What diagnoses would you consider?
              • Osgood-Schlatter Disease
              • Septic arthritis and osteomyelitis.
              • Trauma.
              • Rheumatologic disorder.
              • Slipped capital femoral epiphysis.

            Physical Exam

            On examination there is tenderness over the right tibial tuberosity with mild swelling. The other joints are normal. There is no rash or organomegaly. Yvette looks well. Temperature is 36.9°C

            • What do you think is the most likely diagnosis? Why?
              • Osgood-Schlatter Disease.
              • Most often seen in patients who actively participate in sports.
            • What test(s) would you order? Why?
              • XR, CBC-D, ESR, CRP.
              • We need to rule out more dangerous causes.
            • What is the cause of the most likely diagnosis?
              • Osgood–Schlatter disease (also known as apophysitis of the tibial tubercle or OSD) is an inflammation of the patellar ligament at the tibial tuberosity.
              • It is characterized by a painful lump just below the knee and is most often seen in young adolescents.
              • Risk factors include overuse (especially in sports involving running, jumping and quick changes of direction) and adolescent growth spurts.
            • What is the treatment of the most likely diagnosis? What is the prognosis?
              • Generally conservative with rest and ice.
              • specific exercises can be done to stretch the quads and hamstrings.
              • NSAIDs can be used for pain.
              • The prognosis is good.
