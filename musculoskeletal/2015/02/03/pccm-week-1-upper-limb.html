                    PCCM Week 1 – Upper Limb

                    Kevan Lu Tuesday, February 03, 2015

                    Approach to the MSK Exam

                    • The MSK exam is organized as follows:
                      1. Look
                      2. Feel
                      3. Move
                      4. Special Tests
                      5. Neuromuscular Screen
                    • To remember the things you look for, use the SEADS mnemonic:
                      • S → Symmetry (or swelling).
                      • E → Erythema.
                      • A → Atrophy.
                      • D → Deformities.
                      • S → Scars.
                    • When you’re feeling the area of interest, describe the surface anatomy, and any joints you feel.
                      • Name everything you touch.
                      • Work proximally to distally.
                    • The neuromuscular screen involves looking at muscle bulk, tone, power, and symptom provocation:
                      • Bulk → is the muscle bulk symmetric or asymmetric?
                      • Tone → resistance to passive movement (spasticity, rigidity, flaccidity).
                        • Increased tone is seen with upper motor neuron problems.
                        • Decreased tone is seen with lower motor neuron problems.
                      • Power → resistance with active movement.
                      • Symptom provocation → is there pain with muscle contraction?
                    • MRC (Medical Research Council) Grades for Power:
                      • 0 = No contractions.
                      • 1 = Muscle flickers.
                      • 2 = Muscle movement without gravity.
                      • 3 = Muscle movement against gravity only.
                      • 4 = Reduced power.
                      • 5 = Full power.

                    The Shoulder Exam

                    Look

                    • SEADS.
                    • In particular, look at the shoulder from the posterior, and look for wasting of the supraspinatus or infraspinatus (hollowing out above or below the spine of the scapula).

                    Feel

                    • Start from the sternum, move along the clavicle, and feel both anterior and posterior landmarks.
                    • Again, look for muscle wasting of the rotator cuff muscles (Supraspinatus, infraspinatus, etc).

                    Move

                    • Start with active movement, and ONLY do passive movement if there’s a problem with the active movement.
                      • Flexion → have the patient bring their arms forward and above their head.
                      • Extension → have the patient bring their arms backward.
                      • Abduction/adduction → have the patient bring their arms out and back in.
                      • Internal/external rotation → have the patient bring their elbows to their side, with their hands up. Have them move their arms out and back in.

                    Special Tests

                    • Special tests for the shoulder assess 3 things:
                      1. Ligament Impingement
                      2. Ligament Irritation
                      3. Ligament Tears.
                    • Tests for Ligament Impingement
                      1. Neer’s Test
                        • The examiner moves the arm through the full range of forward flexion or until reports of pain.
                        • The sign is positive if there is pain with flexion.
                        • See https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=U8-yLHQ_JaM.
                      2. Hawkins Kennedy Test
                        • The examiner places the patient’s arm shoulder in 90 degrees of shoulder flexion with the elbow flexed to 90 degrees and then internally rotates the arm.
                        • The test is considered to be positive if the patient experiences pain with internal rotation
                        • See https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=3LU1xsUrKV4
                      3. Painful Arc Test
                        • Have the patient abduct their arms in the plane of the scapula. The test is positive when there’s pain between 60 and 120 degrees of elevation (where the space between the humerus and the acromion is minimal, exacerbating impingement).
                        • Note that unlike the Neer’s Test and the Hawkin’s Kennedy test, this is active, and not passive.
                    • Tests for Ligament Irritation
                      1. Supraspinatus Tendon
                        • This is assessed via the Empty Can Test.
                        • See https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=qjHOqydDhxo.
                        • The test is positive when there’s pain when pressure is applied.
                      2. Infraspinatus Tendon
                        • Bring the patient’s elbows to their sides, and have their hands facing outwards.
                        • Push their arms in and have them resist.
                        • This strains the infraspinatus since it’s an external rotator of the shoulder.
                        • The test is positive if this elicits pain.
                      3. Teres Minor
                        • This is the same test as with the infraspinatus, except in this case, have the patient’s arms up.
                        • Again, bring the patient’s arms inwards, and have them resist.
                        • The test is positive if this elicits pain.
                      4. Subscapularis
                        • This ligament is somewhat more difficult to assess because it’s a medial rotator of the humerus.
                        • To assess it, have the patient put their hands on their belly, and try to pull their arms away from their stomach.
                        • Again, the test is positive if this elicits pain.
                    • Tests for Ligament Tears
                      1. Supraspinatus Tendon
                        • Since the supraspinatus is a shoulder abductor, passively abduct the patient’s arms for them, and then let go.
                        • If there’s a supraspinatus tear, their arm will drop.
                        • Hence, this is known as the Dropped arm test.
                      2. Infraspinatus Tendon
                        • Here, passively externally rotate the patient’s arms with their elbows to their side.
                        • When you release their arms, since the infraspinatus (an external rotator) is no longer there, the patient’s arms will slowly move medially.
                        • This is known as a Lag Sign.
                      3. Teres Minor
                        • This is another lag sign similar to the one with the infraspinatus tendon.
                        • Here, passively move the patient’s arm to a position similar to the one where we assessed for ligament irritation.
                        • When you release their arms, the patient’s arms will internally rotate such that their hands are in front of their mouth.
                        • Hence this is known as the Hornblower’s Sign.

                    Summary of the Shoulder Exam

                    1. Look → SEADS.
                    2. Feel
                    3. Move
                      • Flexion/Extension.
                      • Abduction/Adduction.
                      • Internal/External Rotation.
                    4. Special Tests
                      • Ligament Impingement:
                        1. Neer’s Test
                        2. Hawkins Kennedy Test
                        3. Painful Arc Test
                      • Ligament Irritation:
                        • Supraspinatus (Empty Can Test)
                        • Infraspinatus (arms down)
                        • Teres Minor (arms up)
                        • Subscapularis (hands on belly)
                      • Ligament Tears:
                        • Supraspinatus (Dropped Arm sign)
                        • Infraspinatus (arms down + lag sign)
                        • Teres Minor (arms up + lag sign; aka. the Hornblower’s sign)

                    The Elbow Exam

                    Look

                    • SEADS.
                    • Also look at the carrying angle: the angle at which the forearm attaches to the arm.
                      • Normally, we all have a degree of valgus (the forearm normally attaches at an outward angle to the arm), and this is more prominent in women than in men.
                      • It’s pathological when the angle is greater than 15°, or less than 5°.
                      • Cubitus Valgus → where the joint angle exceeds 15°.
                      • Cubitus Varus → where the joint angle is less than 5°. Also referred to as a Gunstock Deformity.

                    • You can also look for the Popeye Sign (Vince!), which is suggestive of a tear of the biceps tendon
                      • With the Popeye sign, because the biceps tendon is torn, the biceps muscle will clump up and look exaggerated (just like Popeye’s guns).

                    Feel

                    • Feel the following structures:
                      1. Lateral epicondyle.
                      2. Medial epicondyle.
                      3. Olecranon.
                      4. Triangular Space.
                      5. Musculotendinous origins (the tendons of the muscles as they attach to the epicondyles).

                    Move

                    • Start with active movement, and ONLY do passive movement if there’s a problem with the active movement.
                      • Flexion (N = 150°)
                      • Extension (N = 0°, ie. the arm is completely flat)
                      • Supination (palms up).
                      • Pronation (palms down).
                    • To remember which direction is supination, imagine a poor boy holding his hands up for soup (soup = supination).

                    Special Tests

                    1. Tests for Epicondylitis
                      • Lateral Epidondylitis
                        • For these 2 tests, palpate the LATERAL EPICONDYLE, and have the patient’s palms FACING DOWN.
                        • Perform the following:
                          1. Resisted Wrist Extension (Cozen’s Test).
                          2. Passive Wrist Flexion (Mill’s Test).
                        • Either of the above signs is positive if there’s associated pain.
                        • These tests rely on the fact that the forearm extensor muscles originate at the lateral epicondyle.
                      • Medial Epicondylitis
                        • For these 2 tests, palpate the MEDIAL EPICONDYLE, and have the patient’s palms FACING UP.
                        • Perform the following:
                          1. Passive Wrist Extension (no fancy name).
                          2. Resisted Wrist Flexion (Reverse Mill’s Test).
                        • These tests rely on the fact that the forearm flexor muscles originate at the medial epicondyle.
                    2. Varus/Valgus Stress Tests
                      • Have the patient supinated for the following tests.
                      • Valgus Test
                        • Put one hand on the lateral side of the elbow, and use the other to push the forearm laterally.
                        • This strains the ulnar (medial) collateral ligament.
                      • Varus Test
                        • Put one hand on the medial side of the elbow, and use the other to push the forearm medially.
                        • This strains the radial (lateral) collateral ligament.
                    3. Neurologic Tests
                      • Tinel’s Sign → tap over the ulnar nerve just distal to the medial epicondyle to see if it elicits pain.
                      • Note that this is different from Tinel’s sign in the wrist (tapping on the median nerve at the carpal tunnel). Tinel’s sign refers to the general approach of tapping a nerve to elicit paresthesiae.

                    Neuromuscular Tests

                    • Assess muscle bulk, tone, power, and symptom provocation, as mentioned before.
                    • Assess reflexes (triceps C6,7,8, biceps C5,6, and brachioradialis C5,6).

                    Summary of the Elbow Exam

                    1. Look → SEADS, carrying angle (varus/valgus).
                    2. Feel:
                      • Lateral and medial epicondyle.
                      • Olecranon.
                      • Triangular space.
                      • Musculotendinous origins.
                    3. Move
                      • Flexion (N = 150°)
                      • Extension (N = 0°)
                      • Supination/Pronation.
                    4. Special Tests
                      • Epicondylitis:
                        • Lateral Epicondylitis (feel lateral epicondyle, palms down):
                          • Resisted wrist extension (Cozen’s Test)
                          • Passive wrist flexion (Mill’s Test)
                        • Medial Epicondylitis (feel medial epicondyle, palms up):
                          • Passive wrist extension
                          • Resisted wrist flexion (Reverse Mill’s Test)
                      • Varus/Valgus Stress Tests:
                        • Varus test → strain lateral ligament.
                        • Valgus test → strain medial ligament.
                      • Neurologic Tests:
                        • Tinel’s Sign.
                    5. Neuromuscular Tests
                      • Muscle bulk, tone, power, and symptom provocation.
                      • Assess reflexes (biceps, triceps, brachioradialis).

                    The Wrist Exam

                    Look

                    • SEADS
                    • Nail changes, changes in hair distribution (may be suggestive of a vascular issue).

                    Feel

                    • Feel for all the bony landmarks (Scaphoid, Lunate, Triquetrum, Pisiform, Trapezium, Trapezoid, Capitate, Hamate).
                      • Recall the mnemonic ‘Some Lovers Try Positions That They Can’t Handle’.
                    • Feel for all the joints.

                    Move

                    • Wrist movements → flexion, extension, adduction, abduction, circumduction, supination, pronation.
                    • Finger movements (excluding little finger) → flexion, extension, adduction, abduction.
                    • Thumb movements → flexion, extension, abduction, adduction, opposition.
                    • Little finger movements → flexion, extension, abduction, adduction, opposition.

                    Special Tests

                    1. Finkelstein’s Test
                      • The examinar ulnar deviates the wrist (straining the radial side of the wrist).
                      • If sharp pain occurs at the distal radius, then this is suggestive of De Quervain’s Tenosynovitis.
                    2. Tinel’s Sign
                      • The examiner taps on the Carpal Tunnel to try to elicit median nerve irritation.
                      • Pain is suggestive of Carpal Tunnel Syndrome.
                    3. Phalen’s Test
                      • Have the patient bring their wrists together in forced flexion (as shown to the right).
                      • This increases the pressure in the carpal tunnel, and can also exacerbate irritation to the median nerve.
                      • The test is positive if this elicits characteristic signs of Carpal Tunnel Syndrome (such as burning, tingling or numb sensation over the thumb, index, and middle fingers).

                    Summary of the Wrist Exam

                    • Look → SEADS, nail changes, changes in hair distribution.
                    • Feel → bones, joints.
                    • Move → (1) wrist, (2) fingers, (3) thumb, (4) little finger.
                      • Make sure you include opposition for the thumb and little finger.
                    • Special Tests
                      • Finkelstein’s Test
                      • Tinel’s Sign
                      • Phalen’s Test
