            Approach to the Hip

            Kevan Lu Tuesday, February 03, 2015

            Lecture Objectives

            • Develop approach to MSK problems in the upper leg
            • What is the origin of the problem; pelvis, hip, thigh(soft tissue), back or intra-abdominal.
            • Discuss investigation, treatment to the most common problems.

            Review of Hip Anatomy

            • The medial femoral circumflex artery is what feeds the neck of the femur.
            • If you devascularize this, you put the patient at a high risk of avascular necrosis.
            • Innervation to the thigh:
              • The sciatic nerve innervates the posterior compartment.
              • The obturator nerve innervates the medial compartment.
              • The femoral nerve innervates the anterior compartment.
            • Recall that the femoral and obturator nerves only function above the knee, while the sciatic goes below the knee.
            • Recall that the sciatic nerve branches into the (1) tibial nerve, and (2) common fibular nerve (aka. peroneal nerve).
              • Branching of the sciatic nerve is highly variable.
              • It can divide above/at/below the piriformis muscle.

            Differential Diagnosis for Hip Pain

            1. Referred Pain from another System:
              • From Lumbar Spine → lumbar spinal stenosis, radiculopathy, myofascial/mechanical irritation, fractures.
              • From the Knee → internal derangement, musculature.
              • Viscera/Other → hernia, abdominal, GU.
            2. Local Causes:
              • Problems relating to the hip, sacro-iliac joint, muscles of hip, or bursa.

            History for Hip Pain

            • Pain History:
              • OPQRST.
              • Tingling, numbness, weakness.
                • Help differentiate between nerve vs joint causes.
              • Red flags for fracture, cauda equina, or infection:
                • Cauda Equina: Bowel problems, bladder problems, erectile dysfunction, saddle anesthesia
                • Cancer/Infection: Systemic symptoms, age >50 or <20, past history of cancer, immunosuppression, IVDU, night pain.
                • Fracture: Hx of Trauma, steroid use, age > 70.
            • Differentiating Features (IMPORTANT):
              • Buttock pain → from back/SI joint.
              • Distal leg pain → from sciatic nerve.
              • Lateral pain → from greater trochanter.
                • Muscles that attach to the greater trochanter: gluteus medius and minimus.
                • Could also be trochanteric bursitis.
              • Groin/thigh/knee pain → HIP
                • Why? because the major nerve to the hip is the femoral nerve; which innervates the anterior thigh and knee.
                • Knee pain is often referred pain from the hip!

            Pain Patterns

            • Buttock Pain → Back/SI joint.
            • Distal leg pain → Sciatic nerve.
            • Lateral Pain → Greater trochanter (muscles, bursa).
            • Groin/thigh/knee pain → Hip.
            • Physical Exam:
              • Look, Feel, Move, Special Tests, Neuromuscular exam.
              • Examine the joint above (spine) and below (knee).

            Osteoarthritis of the Hip

            • aka. Hip DJD → degenerative joint disease.
            • Symptoms:
              • GROIN PAIN: aching, occasionally sharp.
              • Worse with activity, better with rest.
              • Occasionally radiates to the thigh.
              • Weakness → hip feels weaker, as if it’s about to give out.
            • Signs:
              • Reduced ROM (both active and passive), as with all OA.
              • Antalgic weakness (limping).
              • Tredelenberg gait (patient will lean on bad hip).
            • Examination:
              • Compare leg lengths (many methods):
                1. True leg length → ASIS to medial malleolus.
                2. Apparent leg length → umbilicus to medial malleolus.
                3. Standing block method → place blocks under the short leg while the patient is standing, until the hip is no longer tilted. The difference in leg length is the thickness of the blocks.
                4. Orthoentgenogram → radiographic measure of leg lengths using radiographs.
            • Imaging (X-ray) → 4 cardinal XR features of OA:
              1. Asymmetric joint space narrowing.
              2. Osteophytes.
              3. Subchondral sclerosis.
              4. Subchondral cysts.
            • Treatment
              • Patient education → weight loss, diet, activity modification to reduce hip strain.
              • Physiotherapy → ROM and strengthening of the surrounding muscles.
              • Pharmacotherapy:
                • Oral → NSAIDs, Acetaminophen, opiates.
                • Injectable → Local analgesia and/or steroids.
              • Surgery:
                • Debridement → remove things that ‘stick’ in the joint.
                • Osteotomy → bone cutting to fix alignment.
                • Hip Arthroplasty → standard of care; joint replacement.
                • Fusion → if very young (replacement won’t last), and has a severe hip problem (eg. infected).

            Sacroiliac Joint Pathology

            • L-shaped joint, this is the connection between the sacrum and ilium.
              • Synovial joint anteriorly.
              • Syndesmosis posteriorly.
            • Syndesmosis → fibrous joint; there’s reduced mobility and no synovial fluid, when compared to a synovial joint.
              • Another example of a syndesmosis is between the distal tibia and fibula (the distal tibiofibular joint), as well as the radioulnar joint.
            • Causes of Sacroiliac Joint Pathology:
              • Inflammation → the classic cause is ANKYLOSING SPONDYLITIS, which causes eventual spinal and SI joint fusion. We’ll be seeing this more in rheumatology week.
              • Strain → injury/trauma, repetitive overload (eg. in pregnancy).
            • Symptoms:
              • As mentioned above, will see buttock pain.
              • Can also see back, groin, or leg pain.
              • Often described as vague aching or sharp pain.
              • Transitional → hurts more getting up, climbing stairs, etc. Will often collapse onto a chair instead of comfortably sitting down.
              • Pain with walking, climbing, squatting.
              • Pain at PSIS.
            • Special Tests
              • FABER Test (aka. Patrick Test) → Flexion, Abduction, External rotation. Will exacerbate SI joint pain.
              • Leg Length comparison → just as before.
            • Investigations:
              • Bone scan or CT.
              • But typically a clinical diagnosis → does it hurt when you push on the PSIS?
            • Treatment
              • SURGERY DOES NOT WORK.
              • Patient → Activity modification, weight loss, SI joint belt.
              • Physiotherapy → ROM, strengthening of supportive musculature, orthotics.
              • Pharmacology → oral or injections, as before.

            Bursitis/Tendonitis

            • Lateral pain can be caused by anything nearby the greater trochanter of the femur:
              • Hamstring strain.
              • Trochanteric bursitis.
              • Iliopsoas tendonitis.
            • With respect to the greater trochanter, bursitis and tendonitis can occur as follows:
              • Bursitis → inflammation at the trochanteric bursa.
                • The trochanteric bursa lies between the shaft of the femur, and the attachments of the gluteus medius and minimus.
                • It’s often irritated by the IT band, which also runs over it.
              • Tendonitis → inflammation of the tendons of the muscles that attach nearby (gluteus medius, IT band, tensor fascia lata).
            • Symptoms of Bursitis/Tendonitis:
              • Aching pain.
              • Will occur with initial activity, get better with continued activity, and recur when activity is stopped.
              • Patients will often report BEING UNABLE TO SLEEP ON THAT SIDE.
              • No associated neurologic symptoms.
            • Signs of Bursitis/Tendonitis:
              • Pain with passive stretch or resisted activation.
              • Pain on palpation.
              • Normal neurologic exam.
              • Gait is often normal.
            • Investigations:
              • Typically normal; U/S may show inflammation/bursitis.
              • YOU DO NOT NEED AN MRI.
              • Diagnosis should be based on CLINICAL judgement and response to treatment.
            • Treatment:
              • As before, education, activity modication, weight loss.
              • Appropriate exercise.
              • Physiotherapy.
              • Oral/injectable medications.
