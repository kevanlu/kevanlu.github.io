          Rheumatology Review Jeopardy

          Kevan Lu Tuesday, February 24, 2015

          Monoarthritis

          1. What’s the most important test to do in a patient presenting with a hot, red, inflamed joint?
            • Aspirate the joint.
          2. What do you send the fluid for?
            • 3 C’s and a G:
              • Cell Count.
              • Culture & Sensitivity.
              • Crystals.
              • Gram Strain. 3.
          3. Gout.
            • Negatively birefringent in polarized light.
          4. Chondrocalcinosis
            • Sign of CPPD, same with pseudogout.
            • But psuedogout =/= chondrocalcinosis!

          Polyarthritis

          1. Mrs Johnson has a 3 week history of swelling, pain, and stiffness of the small joints of the hands and feet. She has fatigue and morning stiffness for 3 hours along with an ESR of 82. These are 3 possble causes.
            1. Infectious (Parvovirus, EBV, Rubella)
            2. Rheumatoid Arthritis.
            3. Seronegative Arthritis.
            4. Connective Tissue Disorder.
            5. Vasculitis.
          2. Three of seven possible tests you should order with an inflammatory arthritis are:
            • List below.
          3. These are 3 causes of a hot red acute monoarthritis.
            • Infection.
            • Gout.
            • Pseudogout.
          4. This percentage of pi… have a positive RF?
            • 70-85%.
            • 30-50% have a positive RF with new onset RA.
            • 70-85% have a positive RF with established RA.
            • Hence, it’s not useful for the diagnosis of RA.
          5. This is the current treatment philosophy (paradigm) for early Rheumatoid Arthritis.
            • Early aggressive treatment.
            • Commonly triple therapy → methotrexate, sulfasalazine, hydroxychloroquine.
          • What are the 5 signs of OA on a radiograph?
            • Asymmetric joint space narrowing.
            • Osteophyte formation.
            • Subchondral sclerosis.
            • Subchondral cysts.
            • Malalignment.
          • Non joint problems with rheumatoid arthritis.

          Connective Tissue Diseases

          1. These are 4 of the 11 criteria for Systemic Lupus Erythematosus.
            • MD SOAP BRAIN.
            • Although Raynaud’s and alopecia are common, they’re not part of the diagnostic criteria.
          2. What is the percentage of people with SLE who have a positive ANA?
            • *99% → basically everyone.
            • Note that a ds-DNA is a more specific test than an ANA; it’s a type of ANA (double stranded DNA is a nuclear component).
          3. There’s a 44-fold increased risk of lymphoma with this disease.
            • Sjogren’s Disease.
            • Underlying disorder is an expansion of B-cells (B = grows in Bone marrow, T = grows in Thymus).
            • As a result, you also see a polyclonal IgG expansion; contrast this to a monoclonal expansion (what you’d see in cancer).
            • Characterized by dry eyes and mouth (sicca complex).
          4. Disease in this picture.
            • Scleroderma, aka. systemic sclerosis.
          5. Most common cause of death in patients with inflammatory myopathy (polymyositis or dermatomyositis)?
            • Aspiration pneumonia.

          Vasculitis

          • Leukocytoplastic Vasculitis.
          • Dependent purpura on the lower extremities.
          • Most common rash that we see with vasculitis.
          • Begin as non-palpable petechiae or purpura.
          • Get larger, coalesce, become palpable ove time.

          3 BROAD CATEGORIES THAT VASCULITIS IS GENERALLY CATEGORIZED INTO: LARGE MEDIUM AND SMALL. Newer nomenclature:

          Seronegative Arthritis

          1. What seronegative arthritis is more common in men?
            • Ankylosing spondylitis.
            • Ultimately results in fusion of the lumbar spine and sacroiliac joints.
          2. Inflammation at insertion to bone.
            • Enthesitis.
            • Most common at heel, plantar fascia, patella.
