          Synovial Fluid

          • Produced by the synovial lining cells, and helps the surfaces of the cartilage slide together.
          • Normally there’s only a few mL of fluid in the joint, but production increases with inflammation of the synovium.
          • A joint should always be aspirated for fluid when you see an acute, hot, red joint.

          Objective

          • What routine tests should synovial fluid be sent for?
          • 3 C’s and a G.
            1. Cell Count & Differential.
            2. Crystals.
            3. Culture & Sensitivity.
            4. Gram Stain.
          • This is ALL you ever test synovial fluid for; if you’re ever asked to test protein, or RF, or anything like that, it’s useless!

          Objective

          • What is the difference between normal, non-inflammatory, and inflammatory synovial fluid?

          • Normal Fluid → clear and straw coloured fluid.
            • Viscous like “egg-white”.
          • Non-Inflammatory Fluid → remains clear.
            • Viscous like “egg white”.
          • Inflammatory/Infectious Fluid → becomes cloudy to opaque, depending on the cell count.
            • Becomes more watery; inflammation increases the water content of the fluid, making it less viscous.
            • In an obvious infection, it may look like pus.
            • Can be caused by infection, gout/pseudogout, or an autoimmune reaction.
          • Findings on Cell Count:
            • Normal Fluid → cells/Litre.
            • Non-Inflammatory Fluid → cells/Litre.
            • Inflammatory Fluid → cells/Litre.
            • Infectious Fluid → with an infection, the cell count can rise as high as cells/Litre!
          • Findings on Cell Differential:
            • Normal Fluid → < 25% PMNs (Polymorphonuclear Neutrophils).
            • Non-inflammatory Fluid → < 25% PMNs.
            • Inflammatory/Infectious Fluids → >75% PMNs.
          • Findings with Crystals:
            • If inflammation caused by gout → monosodium urate.
            • If inflammation caused by CPPD → calcium pyrophosphate.
              • CPPD → Calcium pyrophosphate deposition disorder.
              • Leads to both pseudogout and chondrocalcinosis.

          Summary of Synovial Fluid Findings

            Normal Non-Inflammatory Inflammatory
          Colour Clear Clear Cloudy-Opaque
          Viscosity High High Low
          Cell Count (cells/L)
          Gram Stain Negative Negative Positive if infectious
          Culture Negative Negative Positive if infectious
          Crystals Negative Negative Positive if gout or CPPD

          Inflammatory Markers

          • What blood tests give us a clue that something ‘inflammatory’ may be at play?
            • In other words, how do we differentiate an inflammatory vs degenerative cause?
            • We can look for serologic markers of systemic inflammation!
          • Serologic Markers of Inflammation:
            1. CBC → Hb, WBC, Plt.
            2. Acute Phase Reactants (ARPs) → ESR, CRP, Ferritin, complement, immunoglobulins.

          Objective

          • What are the common serologic abnormalities which reveal an “inflammatory picture”?

          Findings on the CBC

          • Hb → may see normocytic anemia with chronic inflammation. Recall, this was Anemia of Chronic Inflammation, which we saw in Blood!
          • WBC → infectious/crystalline causes of inflammation may result in increased WBC levels.
            • However, in inflammatory diseases like RA, WBCs will often be normal.
            • In SLE, the WBC can actually be low, because there are antibodies against the WBCs themselves!
          • Plt → will increase in inflammatory diseases, ie. reactive thrombocytosis.
            • In overwhelming infection (sepsis) however, one can see a significant drop in platelets, due to diffuse coagulation.

          Remember

          • WBC only rises in infectious/crystalline causes of inflammation.
          • WBC should be normal with RA, and may even fall in diseases like lupus.

          Findings on CBC

          Non-Inflammatory Inflammatory
          Hemoglobin Normal ↓ (Normocytic Anemia)
          WBCs Normal
          • Will ↑ if infectious cause
          • Will be NORMAL if RA.
          • Can ↓ if SLE
          Platelets Normal
          • ↑ with reactive Thrombocytosis
          • ↓ with sepsis

          Findings with Acute Phase Reactants

          • Acute Phase Reactant → a protein that either rises or falls acutely, in response to a stimulation.
            • For instance, albumin and transferrin will fall in response to inflammation, while ESR and CRP will rise.
            • The 2 ARPs that we’ll be focusing on are ESR and CRP.

          Objective

          • What is an Erythrocyte Sedimentation Rate (ESR)?

          1. Erythrocyte Sedimentation Rate (ESR)

          • ESR → a measure of how quickly our blood ‘sediments’.
          • Initially developed as a serologic test for pregnancy, but it was later found to be a useful non-specific marker of inflammation.
          • The ESR isn’t a fancy test; you could do it at home!
            • Put a 200 mm long x 2.5 mm diameter tube of anticoagulated blood in a room.
            • Set a timer for an hour.
            • Look at how far the column of blood has fallen in an hour (mm/hr).
            • Normal ESR
              • Men → Age/2.
              • Women → (Age + 10)/2.
          • Why is this an inflammatory marker?
            • Forces affecting the sedimentation of RBCs:
              • The size of the RBCs.
              • Viscosity of the plasma.
              • Repellant forces between negatively charged RBC membranes.
            • The presence of asymmetric positively charged proteins (eg. fibrinogen), which is produced with inflammation, affects the repellant force between these RBCs.
            • These proteins allow for clumping of RBCs, allowing them to form rouleaux.
            • This allows for the RBCs to settle more rapidly.
              • Hence, with inflammation, your blood sediments more rapidly!
          • We saw a condition which resulted in rouleaux formation in Blood as well → Multiple Myeloma
            • With multiple myeloma, which was a cancer of the plasma cells, the sharp increase in IgG antibodies also mediates rouleaux formation.
            • Hence, the ESR is very non-specific; it can rise with hypergammaglobulinemia, hypoalbuminemia (recall that albumin is negatively charged), pregnancy, or with tissue necrosis.

          Objective

          • What is a C-Reactive Protein (CRP)?

          2. C-Reactive Protein (CRP)

          • CRP → an inflammatory protein produced by the liver under the stimulus of interleukin-6
            • Function → able to bind to the surface of microbes and improve their removal from the body.
            • Hence, during infection or inflammation, CRP increases in order to remove foreign microbes.
            • Note that ESR is an indirect marker of inflammation, while CRP is a more direct marker; hence they’re not equivalent.
            • Different things can affect CRP versus ESR.
          • CRP Reference Ranges (not important):
            • Most normal people → ().
            • Normal range → ().
            • Inflammation → ().

          Rheumatoid Factor

          Objective

          • Simplistically, what is the basic immunologic structure of a rheumatoid factor?

          • Rheumatoid Factors → Auto-antibodies directed against antigenic determinants on the Fc fragment of immunoglobulin (Ig) G molecules
            • Can be IgM, IgG, IgA, IgE (IgM only routinely tested for).
            • See previous lecture → Rheumatoid Arthritis.
          • How do we measure Rheumatoid Factor
            1. Latex Agglutination → HISTORICAL METHOD.
              • This involved the use of latex particles coated with human IgG, which would agglutinate when patient serum was added.
              • If there were antibodies against the IgG coated particles, then they would cause the latex particules to clump.
            2. Nephelometry → CURRENT METHOD.
              • More sensitive than latex agglutination.
              • Measures the amount of scattered light when you shine a light source on the tube with the antibody-antigen complexes.
              • Clumping would result in increased light scattering; in other words .

          Objective

          • Is a rheumatoid factor is necessary to make a diagnosis of rheumatoid arthritis?
          • How many patients with early RA and with established RA will have a rheumatoid factor?
          • RF is NOT USEFUL in diagnosing Rheumatoid Arthritis:
            • Recall that only 30-50% of patients with early RA have a positive RF.
            • Only 70-85% of patients with established RA have a postiive RF.
            • So even if the patient has a negative RF, if they fit the clinical picture, then it’s still possible that they have it!
          • RF does not precisely predict who will get RA:
            • Asymptomatic people who happen to be RF positive are unlikely to develop RA.
            • However, if you have a very high RF, you are at increased risk.

          Objective

          • What does the presence of a rheumatoid factor in rheumatoid arthritis actually mean?
          • RF does predict disease severity:
            • Between 2 patients with RA, the one with the higher RF will be more likely to have an aggressive joint disease and extra-articular manifestations.
            • Recall that extra-articular manifestations included Sicca features, Raynaud’s, CTS, rheumatoid nodules, rheumatoid vasculitis, and pleural effusions.
          • Following titres in someone with RA is not important:
            • Levels of RF do not predict a flare.
            • Levels of RF do not predict improvement.
            • So repeat RFs are useless!

          Objective

          • In what rheumatic and non-rheumatic diseases is a rheumatoid factor present?
          • RF is very non-specific; it’s seen in a number of other conditions:
            • Rheumatic → Sjogren’s, SLE, MCTD, etc.
            • Non-rheumatic → Infection, Sacroidosis, IPF.
            • Note that increases in RF are NOT SEEN IN NORMAL AGING; this is commonly seen in texts, but it’s not true!

          Anti-Nuclear Antibodies

          Objective

          • Why is an anti-nuclear antibody a screening test for antibodies directed at “something” in the nucleus?
          • Anti-Nuclear Antibodies are antibodies directed against “something” in the nucleus
            • However, it’s not particularly specific because there are thousands of potential antigens in the nucleus.
            • An ANA test is looking to tell you is there an antibody directed at any one of these potential antigens.
            • It is a screening test; it’s positive in a lot of the normal population!
          • So when are ANAs important?
            • Since ANAs are so non-specific (you see them in so many people), why is it still a useful test?
            • High levels of ANA are important → high titres are more significant.
            • Strongly bound ANAs are important → unfortunately, there’s no way of measuring this.
            • “ANAs are like boyfriends or girlfriends. You only get in trouble when you have (1) lots of them, or (2) when they’re really clingy”.

          Objective

          • What are ds-DNA antibodies and the antibodies in the ENA panel anti-nuclear antibodies?

          • Some ANAs are more important than others:
            • Recall that ANAs → any antibody directed at anything in the nucleus.
            • We also have tests that can look for some specific, important ANAs:
              • ds-DNA → antibodies directed against your double-stranded DNA.
              • ENAs (Extractable Nuclear Antigens) → a panel of antibodies that are also more significant ANAs (ENAs are assessed with suspected lupus).
                • There are 6 antibodies included in the ENA panel: Ro, La, Smith, RNP, Scl 70, Jo-1.
          • If the ANA is negative, you shouldn’t be ordering these tests!
            • Obviously, the ds-DNA and the ENA are SUBTYPES of the ANA, so if the ANA is negative, these are negative by definition!
            • It’s the equivalent of scouring a lake for fish, and upon finding none, trying to look for bass → very stupid.
          • How do you measure the ANA?
            • Not too important; won’t be tested.
            • Basically involves using a slide covered with cells, and exposing them to the patient serum.
            • If ANAs bind to the nuclei of these cells, these can then be detected with fluorescently tagged antibodies against these antibodies.
            • Since ANAs are measured by looking for fluorescence with a microscope, it’s operator dependent (why don’t use software to do this? beats me).
            • You repeat this test with progressively more dilute patient serum samples, until fluorescence cannot be detected. This gives you a measure of how high the ANA levels were.
            • Any dilution > 1:320 is important to assess.
          • Fluorescence patterns with ANA:
            • Because ANA is assessed fluorescently, you can also see the pattern of antibody binding.
            • This results in 6 archetypical fluorescence patterns:
              1. Homogenous → most benign pattern, also most common.
              2. Rim Enhanced
              3. Speckled → more worrying pattern.
              4. Nucleolar
              5. Centromere
              6. Cytoplasmic

          Objective

          • What rheumatic and non-rheumatic diseases can the ANA be present in?

          Diseases associated with +ve ANA

          Disease Percentage with a (+) ANA
          Drug-Induced Lupus 100%
          Chronic Active Hepatitis 100%
          Systemic Lupus Erythematosus 99%
          Systemic Sclerosis 95%
          Mixed Connective Tissue Disease 95-99%
          Polymyositis/Dermatomyositis 90%
          Sjogren’s Syndrome 80%
          Rheumatoid Arthritis 60%
          Myasthenia Gravis 50%
          Diabetes 25%

          Objective

          • Are patients with a negative ANA unlikely to have systemic lupus erythematosus?
          • A NEGATIVE ANA is very effective at ruling out Lupus:
            • Since basically 100% of patients with lupus have a positive ANA, if you get a negative ANA with a suspected patient, they don’t have lupus.
            • Hence, since ANA is so specific, it’s very good at ruling out diseases!

          Other Antibodies

          1. Anti-Double Stranded DNA

          • Anti-ds-DNA (often referred to as ds-DNA), is a specific ANA that’s very specific for Lupus.
            • So unlike ANA, which is sensitive but not specific, ds-DNA is very specific!
            • As mentioned before, if the ANA is negative, the ds-DNA will be negative.
            • Always do the ANA first, and if that’s positive, then do the ds-DNA!

          Objective

          • Do all patients with SLE have to have a positive ds-DNA?
          • If a patient has a high-titer positive ds-DNA then they are likely to have SLE (>90%)
            • However, a negative ds-DNA does not rule out SLE.
            • In London only about 50% with definite SLE have a positive ds-DNA.
              • In literature, this ranges from 60-83%.
            • In some patients with SLE, rising titers of ds-DNA may be associated with a flare.

          2. Extractable Nuclear Antigens

          • ENAs were another subset of ANAs, as mentioned previously.
            • These are 6 antibodies which correlate to a higher degree with certain disease states.
            • So, as mentioned before, if ANA is negative, ENA is negative.
          • The 6 antibodies in the ENA panel are:
            1. Ro (SSA).
            2. La (SSB).
            3. Smith (Sm).
            4. RNP
            5. Scl 70 (Topoisomerase-I).
            6. Jo-1 (Histidyl tRNA-syntetase).
          • When you do an ENA test, they’ll first look for the presence/absence of any of the ENAs, and if any are present, they will then do the levels.
          • The disease associations for each antibody are listed below (YOU DO NOT NEED TO KNOW THIS FOR THE EXAM).

          ENA Disease Associations

          Antibody Disease Association
          SSA/Ro SS, SCLE, NLE, SLE
          SSB/La SS, SCLE, NLE, SLE
          Smith (Sm) SLE
          RNP MCTD, SLE
          Scl 70 (Topoisomerase-I) Diffuse SSc
          Jo-1 (Histidyl-tRNA-synthetase) PM/DM
          • SS → Sjogren’s Syndrome.
          • SCLE → Subacute Cutaneous Lupus.
          • NLE → Neonatal Lupus.
          • MCTD → Mixed Connective Tissue Disease.
          • SSc → Systemic Sclerosis.
          • PM → Polymyositis.
          • DM → Dermatomyositis.

          3. Anti-Neutrophil Cytoplasmic Antibody

          Objective

          • What is an ANCA, what are the two types, and what diseases is it associated with?
          • ANCAs → Anti-Neutrophil Cytoplasmic Antibodies.
            • These are a collection of antibodies directed against components of neutrophil granules.
            • These are related to vasculitis; a strong ANCA is a good surrogate marker of small vessel vasculitis.
            • However, the diagnosis of this must be confirmed by histopathologic findings on biopsy.
          • There are 2 types of ANCAs
            1. c-ANCA.
            2. p-ANCA.
          • As we saw a very long time ago, in GU, these were associated with the Classical Systemic Vasculitis Diseases, which could cause nephritic syndrome:
            1. Wegener’s Granulomatosis → c-ANCA
              • Recall that this was renamed to GPA: Granulomatosis with Polyangiitis; one of the reasons was because Dr. Friedrich Wegener was a nazi, and may have experimented on concentration camp inmates.
            2. Microscopic Polyangiitis (MPA) → p-ANCA.
            3. Churg-Strauss Syndrome (CSS) → c-ANCA or p-ANCA.
              • As with Wegener’s this was renamed to EGPA: Eosinophilic Granulomatosis with Polyangiitis, although neither of these physicians were as controversial.

          Remember

          • Wegener’s Granulomatosis → c-ANCA.
          • Microscopic Polyangiitis → p-ANCA.
          • Churg-Strauss → c-ANCA or p-ANCA.
          • Of these, the scariest (ie. most predictive), is c-ANCAs for Wegener’s.
          • However, these disease associations aren’t completely sensitive or specific.
            • c-ANCA is 98% specific for Wegener’s, but is only 40-95% sensitive depending on disedase activity.
            • This is similar with the other vasculitides.
            • Hence, although strong ANCA activity is a good surrogate, it must fit within a good clinical context.
            • As mentioned before, the only definitive diagnosis is by biopsy.

          Objective

          • What antibodies are c-ANCA and p-ANCA a result of?
          • c-ANCA → caused by anti-proteinase 3 (PR3).
          • p-ANCA → caused by anti-myeloperoxidase (MPO).
          • In London hospitals now, c-ANCA and p-ANCA are reported as PR3 and MPO respectively.

          4. Complement

          • It is named “complement system” because it was first identified as a heat-labile component of serum that “complemented” antibodies in the killing of bacteria.
            • It is now known that it consists of over 30 proteins and contributes 3 g/L to overall serum protein quantities.
          • There are 2 pathways through which it’s activated:
            • Classical Pathway → activated through antibody binding to foreign material.
            • Alternative Pathway → activated through binding of one specific complement protein (C3b) to foreign material.
              • The alternative pathway is actually downstream of the classical pathway; activation of the classical pathway involves the proteins of the alternative pathway.
            • The ultimate result of the complement system is the activation of the Membrane Attack Complex (MAC).
          • The complement system has a number of functions:
            • Opsonization → enhances phagocytosis.
            • Chemotaxis → attracts other inflammatory cells.
            • Increases vasodilation & permeability → allows other inflammatory cells to enter a region more easily.
            • Cellular lysis → activation of the MAC helps destroy offending cells.

          Objective

          • Why do some patients with connective tissue disorders and vasculitis have a low complement level?
          • Complement is important to measure because it can be INAPPROPRIATELY expended in some diseases:
            • For instance, in SLE (lupus, which is a connective tissue disorder), the body’s immune system becomes confused, and actually attacks healthy tissue.
            • Since auto-antibodies are formed, when these antibodies bind to our tissue, it can activate the complement system via the classical pathway.
            • This results in the consumption of complement proteins, as they’re being used to attack our own body. This is reflected in a low complement.
            • As a result, we always measure complement levels in diseases like SLE.
