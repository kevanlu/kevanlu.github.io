          Rheumatoid Arthritis

          Kevan Lu Wednesday, February 18, 2015

          Lecture Objectives

          1. How does rheumatoid arthritis usually start in the majority of patients? How does it start in the minority?
          2. What joints does rheumatoid arthritis have a predilection for?
          3. What age range of people does rheumatoid arthritis affect?
          4. Describe a prototypical patient with inflammatory arthritis, such as rheumatoid arthritis, including what type of people usually develop it, how the disease typically starts, what joints it affects, how it progresses, and what constitutional features are present?
          5. Why should suspected rheumatoid arthritis be referred early?
          6. What blood-tests should be ordered when suspecting a diagnosis of rheumatoid arthritis and why?
          7. How does the philosophy of DMARD/biologic use differ from NSAID/steroid use in rheumatoid arthritis? Why early therapy is important?

          Objective

          • What age range of people does rheumatoid arthritis affect?
          • Anyone can get RA.
            • It can be seen in babies to the very old.
          • Common age to start → 20’s to 50’s.
          • Sex → F>M 3:1.

          Objective

          • How does rheumatoid arthritis usually start in the majority of patients? How does it start in the minority?
          • How does RA start?
            • 70% of patients → RA starts off slowly (insidious) over weeks to months, and progresses in an additive fashion.
            • 20% of patients → comes on over a few weeks (subacute).
            • 10% of patients (rare) → comes on overnight (acute).
            • Can sometimes have a palindromic presentation; this is a pattern where a joint will swell and then go back to normal, and after a variable number of days, a different joint will swell.
              • This is different from an additive presentation, where the swollen joints add on top of one another.
              • Here, different joints swell, but then go back to normal.

          Objective

          • What joints does rheumatoid arthritis have a predilection for?

          • RA usually begins as an oligoarticular (≤4 joints), and then progresses to polyarticular involvement.
          • It has a predilection for the small joints of the hands and feet.
          • In specific, as we saw before, it has the following patterns:
            • Sparing of the DIPs, involvement of the MCPs and PIPs.
            • Can have involvement of all of the joints of the limbs → wrists, elbows, shoulders, hips, knees, ankles.
            • Joints are usually swollen and warm, but they will not be red (although they may be a bit purple).

          Objective

          • Describe a prototypical patient with inflammatory arthritis, such as rheumatoid arthritis, including what type of people usually develop it, how the disease typically starts, what joints it affects, how it progresses, and what constitutional features are present?
          • Features seen in general inflammatory arthritis were seen in the previous lecture.
            • Features specific to Rheumatoid arthritis are discussed below.
          • Features of Rheumatoid Arthritis:
            • Morning stiffness → prominent feature, >1 hour (recall previous lecture).
            • Oftentimes this can last all day, so the best way to obtain the duration is “how long does it take until you are the best you are going to be?”.
            • Symptoms improve with activity → this is because the fluid surrounding the joints gets absorbed as the patients move.
            • Fatigue → VERY COMMON.
            • Sleep Disturbance → VERY COMMON.
            • Other constitutional symptoms like fever or systemic weight loss are rare though.
          • Extra-Articular Features of Rheumatoid Arthritis:
            1. Xerophthalmia → Dry eyes.
            2. Xerostomia → Dry mouth.
            3. Raynaud’s Phenomenon → characteristic colour changes in the distal digits when exposed to the cold.
              • This is due to impaired distal circulation.
              • After being re-exposed to warmth, these joints can then turn red, due to subsequent hyperperfusion.
            4. Carpal Tunnel Syndrome
            5. Pleural Effusion → typically unilateral.
            6. Rheumatoid Nodules → can occur anywhere, including over the DIP joints; don’t mistake this for actual DIP joint involvement!
            7. Rheumatoid Vasculitis → bad complication; inflammation of small blood vessels in long-standing RA.
          • Xerostomia and Xeropthalmia are known as Sicca features (sicca- means dry).
            • These are classically found in Sjogren’s syndrome, but RA can lead to a “Secondary Sjogren’s.”

          Extra-articular Manifestations of RA

          1. Sicca Features → Xerostomia, Xerophthalmia.
          2. Raynaud’s Phenomenon.
          3. Carpal Tunnel Syndrome.
          4. Rheumatoid Nodules.
          5. Pleural Effusions.
          6. Rheumatoid Vasculitis.

          Objective

          • What blood-tests should be ordered when suspecting a diagnosis of rheumatoid arthritis and why?
          • CBC, ESR, CRP → look for signs of inflammation.
          • Urinalysis, Creatinine → assess KIDNEY FUNCTION.
          • AST, ALT, ALP, Albumin → assess LIVER FUNCTION.
            • We need to assess both kidney and liver function because our drugs (DMARDs) can cause liver inflammation and renal damage.
          • Hepatitis B&C Testing
            • To rule out viral arthritis caused by Hep B&C.
            • We also do it because liver inflammation caused by hepatitis + liver inflammation caused by DMARDs = really bad.
          • Anti-Nuclear Antibody → often seen in autoimmune diseases, but is very non-specific.
            • In women particularly, it’s very common to have a positive ANA and be completely normal.
          • Rheumatoid Factor
            • Recall from I&I that our IgG antibodies have 1 Fc (constant) portion, and 2 Fab (antigen binding) portions.
            • Rheumatoid factors are IgM autoantibodies (shown in blue) that are directed against the Fc portion of our IgG antibodies (shown in orange).
            • In other words, rheumatoid factors are IgM antibodies against IgG antibodies.
            • Their role in RA is not understood, but we know that they’re there.
            • RF is very non-specific; it’s seen in a number of other conditions:
              • Rheumatic → Sjogren’s, SLE, MCTD, etc.
              • Non-rheumatic → Infection, Sacroidosis, IPF.

          Blood Tests with Suspected RA

          • CBC, ESR, CRP → look for signs of inflammation.
          • Urinalysis, Creatinine → assess kidney function.
          • AST, ALT, ALP, Albumin → assess liver function.
          • Hepatitis B&C Testing.
          • ANA.
          • Rheumatoid Factor.

          Notes on Rheumatoid Factor

          • What percentage of new onset RA will have a positive RA?
            • 30-50%.
          • What percentage of established RA will have a positive RA?
            • 70-85%.
          • Hence, RF IS NOT USEFUL FOR THE DIAGNOSIS OF RA.
          • In addition, asymptomatic people with positive RF are unlikely to go on to develop RA.
          • However, the higher the RF value, the greater the likelihood of rheumatic disease.
            • Hence, RF IS USEFUL FOR PROGNOSIS; patients who are RF+ are more likely to have aggressive disease.
            • It’s NOT USEFUL TO FOLLOW TITRES though; an increase in RF isn’t predictive of a flare, and a decrease in RF isn’t predictive of improvement.

          Key Points on Rheumatoid Factor

          • RF → IgM autoantibodies against the Fc component of IgG antibodies.
          • Not useful for diagnosis → often seen in autoimmune diseases, however it’s very non-specific, and is also not very sensitive for RA.
          • Useful for prognosis → higher levels of RF are predictive for increased disease severity.
          • Not useful to follow titres → once you have a positive RF, you have a positive RF; changes in it aren’t predictive of changes in your disease.

          Radiographic Findings in RA

          1. Periarticular Osteopenia → loss of bone density near the joints, seen as increased lucency on XR.
          2. Joint Space Narrowing
          3. Erosions → this can look as if ‘bites’ are taken out of the bones.
          4. Mal-alignment → ulnar deviation of the PIP joints are shown to the right.

          Objective

          • Why should suspected rheumatoid arthritis be referred early?
          • 90% of the joints involved in RA are affected within the first year
            • So joint damage is actually an early feature of RA.
            • You have to treat RA quickly, before permanent damage occurs.

          Objective

          • How does the philosophy of DMARD/biologic use differ from NSAID/steroid use in rheumatoid arthritis? Why is early therapy important?
          • See DMARDs and Biologics lecture.
          • In general, DMARDs are great at slowing down the progression of disease, but have side effects. However, the benefit of preventing permanent damage outweighs the cost of side effects.
            • Clinically, the most important side effect is infection.
          • Classically, we use combination therapy with 2 or 3 DMARDs; the most common combination is triple therapy with:
            1. Methotrexate.
            2. Sulfasalazine.
            3. Hydroxychloroquine (Plaquenil).
          • If there’s a failure to control symptoms with this combination, we often add a 4th DMARD (leflunomide), or add Biologics.
            • Biologics are very effective, but very expensive.
            • These are generally classified into TNF antagonists and non-TNF antagonists.
            • Recall that TNF is a potent inflammatory cytokine, which has a number of downstream effects.
            • TNF is a major contributor to the inflammatory & destructive changes seen with RA, which is why blocking TNF is so clinically effective.
