          Acute Polyarthritis

          Kevan Lu Wednesday, February 18, 2015

          Lecture Objectives

          1. What are the historical features that discriminate inflammatory from non-inflammatory arthritis?
          2. Understand the differences between inflammatory, degenerative, and chronic pain conditions.
          3. Describe a prototypical patient with inflammatory arthritis, such as rheumatoid arthritis, including what type of people usually develop it, how the disease typically starts, what joints it affects, how it progresses, and what constitutional features are present?
          4. Describe a prototypical patient with degenerative arthritis, such as osteoarthritis, including what type of people usually develop it, how the disease typically starts, what joints it affects, how it progresses, and what constitutional features are present?
          5. Describe a prototypical patient with fibromyalgia including what type of people usually develop it, how the disease typically starts, what joints it affects, how it progresses, and what constitutional features are present?
          6. What joints (identified on a homunculus) are affected by inflammatory conditions versus those affected by degenerative conditions?
          7. If you think it is an inflammatory arthritis what blood work should you send and why is it useful?
          8. Gain a basic understanding of an approach to polyarthritis.

          ####Objective - Understand the differences between inflammatory, degenerative, and chronic pain conditions.

          • When a patient presents with pain and stiffness in multiple areas of the body, you can think of 3 broad categories of causes:
            1. Inflammatory Conditions.
            2. Degenerative Conditions.
            3. Chronic Pain Conditions.
          • Inflammatory Conditions:
            • Caused by inflammation of the synovial membrane, due to immune attack.
            • Cardinal signs → rubor, tumor, calor, dolor, functio leasa. (recall ITM).
            • Inflammation of the synovium causes increased fluid production → swelling.
          • Degenerative Conditions:
            • Caused by the breakdown of cartilage, eg. OA.
          • Chronic Pain:
            • Diffuse soreness and stiffness in the joints and surrounding muscles
            • No joint swelling, redness, or warmth
            • Causes pain and loss of function, eg. fibromyalgia.
          • What gender is more predisposed to these conditions?
            • All 3 are more common in women.

          ####Objective - What joints (identified on a homunculus) are affected by inflammatory conditions versus those affected by degenerative conditions?

          • What joints are involved? CMC = carpometacarpal joint, MCP = metacarpophalangeal joint, PIP = proximal interphalangeal joint, DIP = distal interphalangeal joint.
            • Inflammatory → MCP, PIP involvement, DIP spared.
              • Wrist also often very swollen.
              • Ankle swelling is also specific for inflammatory arthritis; very rare to get ankle swelling with OA.
              • Knee, hip involvement can be seen in both inflammatory and degenerative conditions.
            • Degenerative → DIP, PIP involvement, MCP spared.
              • 1st CMC joint (for thumb) also swollen in majority of cases.
              • Same goes for the 1st MTP joint.
            • Chronic Pain → no truly swollen joints on examination, although patients may complain of a subjective experience of swelling.

          ####Objective - What are the historical features that discriminate inflammatory from non-inflammatory arthritis?

          1. Age of onset:
            • Inflammatory → bimodal; seen in younger patients, but can also be seen in older ages.
            • Degenerative → seen in the elderly.
            • Chronic Pain → seen in younger patients (around 40).
          2. Pattern of onset:
            • Inflammatory → additive (adds joints), insidious (over weeks to months). Can also sometimes be acute.
            • Degenerative → slow progression, (over months to years).
            • Chronic pain → may be triggered; insidious onset of diffuse aching pain.

          ####Objective - Describe a prototypical patient with inflammatory arthritis, such as rheumatoid arthritis, including what type of people usually develop it, how the disease typically starts, what joints it affects, how it progresses, and what constitutional features are present? - Describe a prototypical patient with degenerative arthritis, such as osteoarthritis, including what type of people usually develop it, how the disease typically starts, what joints it affects, how it progresses, and what constitutional features are present? - Describe a prototypical patient with fibromyalgia including what type of people usually develop it, how the disease typically starts, what joints it affects, how it progresses, and what constitutional features are present?

          • The pertinent features are described below.
          • Joint involvement was described previously as well:
            • Inflammatory → MCPs, PIPs, spares the DIPs, often involves the wrists, elbows, shoulders, and ankles.
            • Degenerative → PIPs, DIPs, spares the MCPs, often involves the 1st CMC joint, acromion, and 1st MTP joint of foot.
              • Both inflammatory and degenerative arthritis affect the knees and hips.
            • Chronic Pain → the joints themselves do not have specific pain; rather, the pain is diffuse over the joints and muscles.

          ####Inflammatory, Degenerative, & Chronic Pain Disorders

            Inflammatory Degenerative Chronic Pain
          Description: Joint inflammation due to immune attack Degenerative breakdown of the joint cartilage Diffuse (not joint-specific) pain
          Gender most often seen in: Women Women Women
          Age of onset: Bimodal, both younger and more elderly. Elderly Younger (~40)
          Pattern of onset: Additive, insidious (weeks-months) Slow (months-years) Often can be triggered, insidious
          Joint pain: YES YES NO
          Swelling: YES YES NO
          Joint Redness: YES NO NO
          Morning Stiffness: >1 hour 15-20 minutes >1 hour
          Fatigue: New and severe Mild Severe
          Loss of function: Rapid Slow Rapid
          Fever: Possibly Never Never
          Weight Loss: Possibly Unusual Unusual

          ####Objective - If you think it is an inflammatory arthritis what are some possible causes?

          • Note that we can usually narrow it down to one of (1) inflammatory, (2) degenerative, or (3) chronic pain just from history and physical exam.
            • This determination is a clinical diagnosis; no lab tests or imaging is needed.
            • If the history is suggestive of osteoarthritis (degenerative), or chronic pain, then no blood work is needed.
            • If the history is suggestive of inflammatory arthritis, then some lab tests can help differentiate the cause.
          • Most common causes of inflammatory arthritis:
            1. Infection
            2. Rheumatoid Arthritis.
            3. Seronegative Arthritis.
            4. Connective Tissue Disease.
            5. Associated with another Systemic Disease.
          • Infection:
            • Acute inflammatory polyarthritis is usually associated with a viral infection, such as:
              • Parvovirus B19, EBV, Rubella, Hep B/C, HIV.
            • Note that this is different from acute inflammatory monoarthritis, which was usually bacterial (S. aureus was most common).
            • Typically self-limited over 2-6 weeks.
          • Rheumatoid Arthritis:
            • Chronic, destructive inflammatory arthritis.
            • Prominent morning stiffness + fatigue.
            • Rheumatoid nodules and bone erosions on XR can be seen (although these are rare early in disease).
          • Seronegative Arthritis → 4 types.
            1. Psoriatic arthritis → associated with Hx (personal of FHx) or psoriasis.
            2. Ankylosing Spondylitis → inflammatory lower back disease.
            3. Reactive Arthritis → arthritis, conjunctivitis, urethritis reaction after a urinary or GI tract infection.
              • Affects the joints of the lower extremity predominantly.
            4. Enteropathic Arthritis → associated with IBD (UC or Crohn’s).
          • Connective Tissue Disorders → inflammatory CTDs include:
            1. Systemic Lupus Erythematosus (SLE, lupus).
            2. Sjogren’s Syndrome.
            3. Polymyositis/Dermatomyositis.
            4. Systemic Sclerosis.
            5. Mixed Connective Tissue Disorder.

          ####Objective - If you think it is an inflammatory arthritis what blood work should you send and why is it useful?

          1. CBC → signs of infection (WBC ↑) or inflammation (reactive thrombocytosis, Plt ↑)
          2. ESR, CRP → markers for inflammation.
          3. Albumin, AST, ALT, ALP → liver function tests.
            • This is important because some drugs we use (eg. some DMARDs) can cause liver inflammation.
          4. Creatinine, Urinalysis → kidney function tests.
            • This is important because some drugs we use (eg. some DMARDs) can cause renal damage.
          5. Hep B&C Testing → rule out viral arthritis caused by Hep B or C.
          6. Rheumatoid Factor → will be negative in seronegative arthritis.
            • However, may also be negative with rheumatoid arthritis, especially early on in disease.
          7. ANA, ENA, ds-DNA, Complement (C3/C4) → often associated with connective tissue diseases (will see more in later lectures).
          8. IgG → polyclonal increase is suggestive of acute inflammation.
