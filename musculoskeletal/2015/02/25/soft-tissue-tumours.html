              General Considerations

              What is Soft Tissue?

              • Soft Tissue → any tissue that connects, supports, or surround other structures and organs of the body, but isn’t bone.
                • Basically, everything that’s not in the
                • All soft tissues are derived from the mesoderm.
                • Hence, soft tissue tumours are often referred to as ‘mesenchymal’.
                • Tumours of the soft tissue can be both benign and malignant.

              Incidence & Demographics of Soft Tissue Tumours

              • Benign:malignant → 100:1
                • Most benign tumours will stay benign; they won’t transform to malignant tumours.
                • There are however, a few exceptions; one example is neurofibromatosis, where diffuse tumours form.
              • Sarcoma = malignant soft tissue tumour.
                • So ‘malignant sarcoma’ is redundant.
                • The incidence of sarcomas increase with age, much like most other cancers.
                • Most generally arise in areas where there’s lots of soft tissue, such as the large muscles of the retroperitoneum.
                • Soft tissue tumours are relatively rare, compared to the ‘big cancers’ (breast, lung, colon, etc).

              Estimated New Cases of Cancer by Site (USA, 2000).

              • How do sarcomas differ from carcinomas?
                • Recall from ITM that these have different embryological origins.
                • Sarcoma → malignant smooth muscle tumour.
                  • Arises from cells of mesodermal origin.
                • Carcinoma → malignant epithelial cell tumour.
                  • Arises from cells of endodermal or ectodermal origin.

              Causes of Soft Tissue Tumours

              • Environmental causes
                • Asbestos (mesothelioma), vinyl chloride, herbicides, dioxin, radiation (eg. radiotherapy), foreign materials.
                • The most common of these is probably radiotherapy; since patients survive much longer after the use of radiotherapy,
              • Oncogenic Viruses:
                • HHV8 (Human Herpes Virus 8) → Kaposi’s sarcoma, in immunocompromised patients.
                • EBV → less common, but also sometimes associated with smooth muscle tumours.
              • Immunodeficiency States:
                • Stewart Treves → often seen post-axillary dissection in women who had a mastectomy, chronic lymphedema.
                  • This is an angiosarcoma (vascular tumour) related to the chronic lymphedema seen post axillary dissection.
                  • Now occurs less commonly because of an increase in breast sparing operations, and more precise and judicious axillary LN dissection.
              • Genetic Disposition
                • Li-Fraumeni Syndrome, aka. Sarcoma, breast, leukemia and adrenal gland (SBLA) syndrome.
                • Rb → Retinablastoma.
                • NR → Neurofibromatosis.
              • The majority of sarcomas have an UNKNOWN etiology.

              Clinical Evaluation of Soft Tissue Tumours

              History & Physical

              • Typically presents as a lump.
              • History
                • Mass +/- pain, increasing in size
                • Age, duration, change in size over time
                • Predispositions/ risk factors
              • Physical Exam
                • Location, size, multifocality, involvement of adjacent structures.
                • Try to determine whether the lesion is superficial or deep:
                  • Superficial lesions (skin, cutaneous tissue) are more likely to be benign.
                  • Deep lesions (fascia or below) are more likely to be malignant.
                • Determine regional lymph node status:
                  • Carcinomas prefer lymphangitic spread.
                  • Sarcomas prefer hematogenous spread.
                  • So if you see regional lymph node involvement, you would suspect a carcinoma over a sarcoma.

              Investigations

              • Imaging
                • XR and U/S don’t give you much information with a suspected soft tissue tumour; XR is only really good for bony lesions.
                • When you suspect a soft tissue tumour, a CT and/or an MRI are better.
              • Tissue Sampling
                • Aim to be as minimally invasive as possible, in order to avoid seeding the tumour.
                • However, although FNABs are minimally invasive, FNABs are a poor choice, because the diagnostic yield is poor.
                  • Often it’s difficult to classify the tumour and perform ancillary tests (such as histochemical staining).
                • Incisional/excisional biopsies give you more tissue (and more diagnostic power), but are much more invasive, and carry the risk of seeding.
                  • Incisional biopsy → partial removal of lesion.
                  • Excisional biopsy → compelete removal of lesion with minimal surrounding tissue.
                • A core biopsy is the best choice.
                  • Offers a compromise between the non-invasiveness of a FNAB and the diagnostic power of an incisional/excisional biopsy.

              Remember

              • When dealing with a suspected soft tissue tumour, the best biopsy method is a core biopsy.
              • Histopathological Examination
                1. Routine histology (looking at it).
                2. Histochemistry (staining it).
                3. Immunohistochemistry (looking at protein expression).
                4. Cytogenetics, molecular techniques, electron microscopy.
              • For many of these modalities, sufficient tissue is needed, which is why a core biopsy is superior to a FNAB.

              Classification of Soft Tissue Tumours

              • Soft tissue tumours are classfied on a ‘histogenetic’ basis, according to the adult tissue that they most resemble.
                • In other words, they are classified according to ‘what the tumour is trying to be’.
                • ‘Histogenetic’ is a bit of a misnomer; they’re classified more by phenotype as opposed to genotype.
              • As with all tumours, benign tumours are better differentiated:
                • Benign soft tissue tumours resemble their mature soft tissue counterparts.
                • Malignant soft tissue tumours have less of a resemblance.

              Soft Tissue Tumour Nomenclature

              • The classification of soft tissue tumours is extremely complicated.
              • However, the basics of soft tissue nomenclature is as follows.
              • Recall from ITM that:
                • ‘-oma’ → tumour.
                • ‘-sarcoma’ → soft-tissue tumour.
              • The prefix is assigned according to the soft tissue resemblance.
                • Lipo- → fat.
                • Rhabdomyo- → skeletal muscle.
                • Leiomyo- → smooth muscle.
                • Fibro- → fibrous tissue.
                • Neurofibro- → peripheral nerve.
              • Tumours with mixed phenotypes can have multiple prefixes.

              Management of Sarcomas

              • Treatment of Sarcomas:
                • SURGICAL RESECTION.
                • (+/-) Radiotherapy
                • (+/-) Chemotherapy.
              • In all sarcomas, surgical resection is done, but radiotherapy and chemotherapy may or may not be added depending on the context:
                • Radiotherapy:
                  • Almost always done, except for cases where the tumour is intra-abdominal, where radiotherapy is controversial due to fear of affecting the surrounding organs.
                  • It’s important to do the radiotherapy before the surgical resection, but not too early or it’ll affect wound healing.
                • Chemotherapy:
                  • Doesn’t have a role in most soft tissue tumours.
                  • However, it does have a role in select subtypes; one example, which we’ll see later, is rhabdomyosarcoma.
                  • In most cases, it’s only used for unresectable, high-stage disease, as a last ditch effort.

              Remember

              • Surgery and radiotherapy are the mainstay of treatment for sarcomas.

              Common Soft Tissue Tumours

              • We’ll be talking about 3 specific soft tissue tumours:
                1. Malignant Fibrous Histiocytoma (MFH) → most common adult sarcoma of the limbs.
                2. Liposarcoma → most common adult sarcoma of the retroperitoneum.
                3. Rhabdomyosarcoma → most common pediatric sarcoma.

              Common Soft Tissue Tumours

              1. Malignant Fibrous Histiocytoma (MFH) is the most common adult sarcoma of the limbs.
              2. Liposarcoma is the most common adult sarcoma of the retroperitoneum.
              3. Rhabdomyosarcoma is the most common pediatric sarcoma.

              1. Malignant Fibrous Histiocytoma

              • The most common adult sarcoma of the limbs.
              • The name is a bit of a misnomer; the ‘fibrous histiocyte’ doesn’t exist, but the name persists for historical reasons.
              • Thought to be the end-stage form of other types of soft tissue tumours; highly undifferentiated.
                • They do however resemble fibrous cells most, hence the name.

              • Histological features
                • Will see poorly differentiated tissue.
                • Cells will be pleomorphic, will look ‘bizarre’.
              • Treatment
                • Wide resection → 1-2 cm margin of normal tissue surrounding the lesion.
                • Radiotherapy.
                • CT lungs to r/o lung mets.
                • F/U with clinical exam and imaging.
              • Prognosis
                • MFH has a very high metastatic potential; 40% of patients who present with this already have lung mets.
                • Hence the 5-year survival is only 50-60%.

              2. Liposarcoma

              • The most common adult sarcoma of the retroperitoneum.
                • However, it is VERY rare in pediatrics, it’s pretty much exclusive to adults.
              • This, as the name suggests, is a malignant sarcoma that shows fatty differentiation.
                • Unlike MFH, which we saw just previously, the cells within a liposarcoma are very well-differentiated.
              • Can grow to be very large (Guinness World Records large), since they’re often in the abdomen, and have lots of room to grow.
              • Unlike MFH, liposarcomas very rarely metastasize, even though they are malignant.
                • The risk instead is actually recurrence.
                • Liposarcomas have a habit of recurring over and over and over again.

              • Histological Features
                • Will resemble adipose tissue.
                • With immunohistochemical staining, will see MDM2 protein overexpression.
              • Treatment
                • Wide resection → 1-2 cm margin of normal tissue surrounding the lesion.
                • Radiotherapy not done (if retroperitoneal) → this differentiates liposarcomas from MFH; since they’re more likely to be retroperitoneal, radiotherapy is done less frequently.
                • CT lungs.
                • F/U with clinical exam and imaging.
              • Prognosis:
                • Low risk of metastasis, but frequent local recurrence.
                • 5-year survival quite good.

              3. Rhabdomyosarcoma

              • The most common pediatric sarcoma.
              • A malignant tumour that resembles skeletal muscle cells.
              • Incidence wise, this is the opposite of MFH or liposarcoma; these are much more common in children.
              • Another exception is that, unlike most sarcomas, these can (sometimes) metastasize via local regional lymph nodes.
                • Recall that most sarcomas spread hematogenously; rhabdomyosarcomas are an exception.

              • Histological Features
                • Small round cells that stain blue on routine H&E (hence this is classified as a small-blue-round-cell tumour).
                • When looking at the tissue organization, you can often see cross-striations, where the tumour cells try to mimic skeletal muscle.
                • Will often express Desmin, and MyoD1.
              • Treatment
                • Wide resection (as before).
                • Chemotherapy → this is an exception to the rule where most sarcomas do not receive chemotherapy! With rhabdomyosarcoma, we actually use chemo up front.
                • (+/-) Radiotherapy → may or may not be used depending on tumour size.
              • Prognosis:
                • Multimodal therapy (ie. with chemo, with possible radiotherapy) improves survival from 20% to 70%.
                • The alveolar subtyle has a worse prognosis.
