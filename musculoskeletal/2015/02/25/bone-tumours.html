          Bone Tumours

          Kevan Lu Wednesday, February 25, 2015

          ####Bone Tumours Notes - See Bone Tumours Notes.

          Lecture Objectives

          • General classification of bone tumours.
          • Clinical presentation of bone tumours.
          • Radiographic analysis of bone tumours.
          • Work-up of bone tumours.

          #Classification of Bone Tumours

          • Primary → tumours arising within bone.
            • Non-neoplastic tumours → reactive/infectious tumours.
            • Neoplastic → further subclassified based on morphology (what it’s producing), and whether it’s benign or malignant.
          • Secondary → tumours invading into, or metastasizing to bone.
            • Most common malignant bone tumour is metastasis!
            • The most common cancers that metastasize to bone, are (1) Prostate, (2) Thyroid, (3) Breast, (4) Lung, (5) Kidney.
            • Mnemonic: “P.T. Barnum Loves Kids”.
            • More common in adult patients.
            • Will typically presents with slowly progressive pain, and will culminate in a pathologic fracture due to bone damage.

          ####Cancers that Metastasize to Bone - The most common cancers that metastasize to bone are: 1. Prostate. 2. Thyroid. 3. Breast. 4. Lung. 5. Kidney. - Mnemonic: “P.T. Barnum Loves Kids”.

          #Radiographic Analysis of Bone Tumours

          • Lytic Lesion → loss of bone density (increased blackness, less whiteness).
            • Radio-lucent, bone destroying.
          • Blastic Lesion → induction of bone formation (increased whiteness).
            • Radio-opaque, bone forming.
            • Bone formation will be diffuse and poorly controlled, and will often look like a ‘cloud of whiteness’.
          • Multifocal lesions → lesions affecting many regions of bone.
            • Secondary bone tumours are often multifocal; this makes sense, if the tumour is due to metastases, it’s probably seeded to other areas.
            • In contrast, primary bone tumours are typically unifocal.

          ##Radiology: Questions to Ask

          • Where is lesion? What bone? What part?
          • How large is lesion?
          • What is lesion doing to bone?
          • What is bone doing in response?
          • Is the lesion producing matrix? What kind?
          • Is the cortex eroded?
          • Is soft tissue extension present?

          ##Examples

          1. Example 1
            • This is an AP radiograph of the distal tibia and femur. The patient is a child (you can still see the growth plates).
            • There is a well-circumscribed metaphyseal lesion with a sclerotic margin.
            • The cortex has a normal contour (there is no ‘bulging’).
            • As it’s well circumscribed and fairly-non destructive, this seems benign.
            • These are the typical radiographic features of a non-ossifying fibroma (name isn’t important).
          2. Example 2
            • Here we see a diaphyseal lesion of some bone (not clearly indicated).
            • Although there is no patently obvious lesion, there are some important features to note.
            • Note that there is a peri-osteal reaction on one side of the bone.
              • There are many layers of new bone deposition; this is called ‘onion-skinning’
              • This is a worrisome finding, because it suggests that the periosteum is being irritated by something (often a tumour), and is producing new bone in response..
            • In addition, the medullary cavity is more radio-opaque, suggesting that there’s a solid tumour inside.
            • This is an Ewing’s sarcoma.
          3. Example 3
            • This is a radiograph of an (adult) knee, showing ballooning of the proximal fibular head.
            • The cortex is still intact, but is thin, and has swollen.
            • Bubbles/loculations are visible within the bone.
            • These are classic findings with a benign bone lesion called an aneurysmal bone cyst.

          #Clinical presentation of bone tumours

          • Patients may present for any of the following reasons:
            • Bone mass.
            • Soft tissue mass.
            • Pain → although often patients will have no pain.
            • Fractures → patients are at risk for pathologic fractures due to bone damage.
            • Incidental → a bone lesion may be picked up during an XR for something completely different.
          • The presentation will also differ depending on whether it’s a primary or secondary tumour:
            • Primary Tumours → rare, can be seen in both young or old patients, will often involve a solitary site, and can involve any bone.
            • Secondary Tumours → common, usually in elderly, often affects multiple sites, and will usually affect proximal bones and vertebrae.

          #Work-up of bone tumours

          ##History & Physical

          • History
            • Age of patient → older, suspect secondary; younger, suspect primary.
            • Bone involved/part of bone involved
            • Duration of symptoms/growth rate → A more rapid progression of symptoms would suggest a more aggressive tumour.
            • Presence or absence of pain.
            • Mechanical effects of mass.
            • Hereditary conditions.
          • Physical Exam
            • Ensure that the mass originates in the bone (it’s fixed).
            • Identify the size of the lesion, and how many there are.
            • Extent of local symptoms during joint motion.
            • Assess neural function (touch, proprioception).
            • Assess vascular patency (pulses, skin warmth).
            • Look for possible signs of an underlying hereditary condition.
              • Examples: Cafe au Lait spots or Lisch nodules (seen in NF Type 1).
            • Look for signs of primary cancer elsewhere:
              • Since the most common cancers that metastasize to bone are Prostate, Thyroid, Breast, Lung, Kidney, assess each.
              • Prostate → DRE if male.
              • Thyroid → TSH, thyroid exam.
              • Breast → Mammogram, U/S.
              • Lung → CXR, respiratory exam.
              • Kidney → BUN, Cr.
            • Lymph Node Examination → bone tumours, like sarcomas, tend not to metastasize to lymph nodes.
              • So if you find positive lymph nodes, it’s less likely to be a bone tumour.

          ##Investigations - X-ray → unlike with sarcomas, XR is super useful with bone tumours. - Not only are they cheap and quick, they also allow you to assess the biologic potential of the tumour (is it indolent? is it aggressive?). - If the lesion looks borderline, you can even repeat the XR in a few weeks and look for a change. - CT and MRI are less often used, although MRI can be used to look for soft tissue involvement. - CBC, ESR, CRP → infection, inflammation. - Bone Scans → these should be done if you suspect metastases, in order to look for multifocal lesions. - Signs of Primary Tumour: - Prostate → PSA. - Lung → CXR, chest CT. - Breast → U/S, mammogram. - Biopsy - If you suspect an active, aggressive, or malignant lesion, A biopsy MUST be done in order to establish a diagnosis and plan management. - Complications of a biopsy include fracture (especially since the tumour will weaken the bone), infection, and hemorrhage. - Be as atraumatic as possible when performing the biopsy to avoid seeding the tumour.

          ####Summary of Investigations 1. X-ray 2. Bone scan if you suspect metastases (eg. older patient). 3. Look for signs of primary tumour → investigations correspond to the most common cancers that metastasize to bone (Prostate, Breast, Lung, etc). 4. Biopsy in order to make a diagnosis and plan management.
