            Case #1

            Case Summary

            A 65-year-old male presents to you with a history of left knee pain. The pain is dull, has been present for several months, and recently increasing in severity. There is no history of trauma. He is otherwise well. Examination of his knee is normal. You order plain film radiographs (‘X-rays’) of his left knee to rule out any obvious bone abnormality.

            • Review the radiograph provided and describe the findings.
              • Radiograph of the distal femur, proximal tibia & fibula.
              • Lytic, radiolucent, well circumscribed metaphyseal lesion.
              • The rim is slightly sclerotic.
              • No growth outside the normal margins of the bone.
            • What is your differential diagnosis?
              • Older patient, pain has been increasing in severity, no obvious predisposing factors, so must rule out metastases.
              • Most common sources of metastases (Prostate, Thyroid, Breast, Lung, Kidney).

            Additional Information

            You order a bone scan to determine whether this is a solitary lesion versus a multifocal process.

            • Describe what you see:
              • Multifocal lesions, makes this more likely to be metastasis (primary tumours will more likely present as a unifocal lesion).
              • Lymphoma, multiple myeloma.
            • What is a bone scan?
              • Radiolabeled technetium is used to look for regions of high bone turnover (where bone is rapidly being destroyed or formed).
              • Useful when looking for infection or cancer of the bone.
            • What is a hot spot?
              • A region of high bone turnover, where more of the radiolabeled agent is taken up.
            • Given the presence of multiple abnormal foci on the bone scan, what is your differential diagnosis now?
              1. Metastatic carcinoma.
              2. Lymphoma.
              3. Multiple myeloma.
            • What other investigations might be useful to determine the nature of the lesions?
              1. Biopsy → you need a tissue sample in order to determine the nature of the lesion.
              2. Look for signs of primary cancer:
                • Prostate → DRE, PSA.
                • Thyroid → TSH.
                • Breast → breast examination, U/S, mammogram.
                • Lung → CXR.
                • Kidney → BUN, Cr.
            • Describe what you see in the biopsy
              • Abnormal cells are forming tubular/glandular structures.
              • Hence, this is suggestive of a glandular cancer; an adenocarcinoma
            • What is your diagnosis?
              • Adenocarcinoma.
            • What cancers, in adults, most commonly metastasize to bone?
              • PT Barnum Likes Kids.
              • Prostate, Thyroid, Breast, Lung, Kidney.
            • By what pathways do these tumours spread to bone?
              • Hematogenous/lymphangitic spread.
              • Direct invasion.
              • Seeding via nerves (especially with prostate cancer, which can invade via the rectal plexus).
            • Which bones are most commonly affected by metastatic cancer?
              • Axial skeleton → vertebrae, pelvis, ribs, skull, sternum.
              • Proximal appendicular skeleton → femur and humerus.
              • It’s rare to see metastases to the distal appendicular skeleton (hands, feet).

            Additional Information

            A complete physical examination, including a digital rectal exam, reveals a slightly enlarged, irregular prostate gland with some questionable firm nodularity.

            • What do you suspect now?
              • Metastatic prostate adenocarcinoma.
            • What can you do to confirm your suspicions?
              • Serum PSA.
              • Possibly could to a biopsy of the prostate.
            • What are treatment options?
              • Palliative care; the patient already has diffuse metastases, so surgical resection probably won’t help.

            Case #2

            Case Summary

            • A 16-year-old male presents to you with a history of left knee pain.
            • The pain is dull, has been present for several months, and recently increasing in severity. He is otherwise well.
            • Examination of his left knee reveals a hard, irregular mass.
            • You order plain film radiographs of his knee.

            • Describe the findings
              • Radio-opaque tumour; blastic tumour.
              • The mass is uneven; some regions are more radio-opaque than others.
            • What is your differential diagnosis?
              • The patient is younger, so it’s probably a primary bone tumour (either benign or malignant).
              • Patient doesn’t have systemic symptoms, so it isn’t osteomyelitis.
              • The most likely cause is Osteosarcoma.
            • What do you see in the biopsy?
              • Really ugly cells here are intertwined with the bone.
              • The idea here is that the ‘ugly cells’ are trying to make bone.
              • Hence this is suggestive of an osteosarcoma.
            • What is the diagnosis?
              • Osteosarcoma.
            • What is the treatment?
              • Generally poor; most patients will have clinically detectable metastases at the time of diagnosis.
              • Some patients will have to undergo amputation +/- chemotherapy, although we aim to spare the limb when possible.
            • Who gets this disease? Why?
              • Most are younger patients (2nd and 3rd decades of life).
              • Males are more commonly affected.
              • There’s another peak seen in patients >50; these are often secondary to other causes (Paget’s disease, post-radiation, implants, chronic osteomyelitis).
                • These forms of osteosarcomas (secondary osteosarcomas) usually behave more aggressively and do not respond as well to therapy as do osteosarcomas in younger individuals.
              • So these are seen in both young and old patients.
                • Young patients → primary osteosarcoma.
                • Older patients → secondary to other causes.
            • How is this disease classified?
              • Not going to be tested on exams.
              • By location (medullary, surface, cortical).
              • By association with underlying disease (secondary osteosarcoma).
              • By histological features, or if it’s a rare inherited form.
            • What is the prognosis?
              • Not too great.
              • Long-term survivals of 60% to 70%.

            Case #3

            Case Summary

            • A 15-year-old female injured her knee while rollerblading.
            • A plain film radiograph was taken in Emergency.

            • Describe what you see
              • Lateral radiograph of the knee (distal femur, tibia, fibula, patella).
              • Radio-opaque mass extending from the metaphyseal surface of the distal humerus (posterior surface).
              • Seems to be continuous with the bone, well circumscribed.
            • What is an osteochondroma (exostosis)?
              • Osteo- → bone.
              • Chondro- → cartilage.
              • Oma → benign.
              • So this is a benign tumour that resembles bone and cartilage.
              • Most common primary bone tumour.
            • Who gets osteochondromas? Where do they occur?
              • Occur in the long bones.
              • Usually presents in late adolescence and early adulthood. M>F 3:1.
              • Hereditary multiple osteochondromatosis → genetic disease, will present with osteochondromas in childhood.
                • This arises due to a mutation in the EXT gene, which is involved in the synthesis of heparan sulphate, which plays a role in bone formation.
            • What is the difference between sessile and pedunculated osteochondromas?
              • Pedunculated → has a stalk.
              • Sessile → it’s a bump, no stalk (like speedbumps).
            • How might patients present with osteochondromas?
              • Many patients with osteochondromas will present incidentally.
              • Osteochondromas generally grow slowly, but can present with pain if there’s nerve impingement.
              • Rarely, they may transform into a malignant tumour, such as a chondrosarcoma. This is more likely with a hereditary syndrome.
            • What is the treatment?
              • If asymptomatic, nothing needs to be done.
              • If symptomatic, surgical resection.

            Case #4

            Case Summary

            • A 75-year-old female is referred to you because of a shoulder mass.
            • The mass is painless and has been gradually increasing in size over the last year and is associated with decreased mobility.
            • She is otherwise healthy.
            • A plain film radiograph is obtained.

            • Describe what you see
              • Large, destructive, fairly well defined tumour of the proximal humerus.
              • Destruction of the cortex, extension into the soft tissue.
            • What is your differential diagnosis?
              • Chondrosarcoma.
              • Osteosarcoma.
              • Metastasis from a primary tumour.
            • How do you confirm the diagnosis?
              • Biopsy.
            • Using clinical-radiographic-pathological correlation, what is the diagnosis?
              • Given that it’s a central lesion (ie. the hip girdle, shoulder, and pelvis vs. the limbs), large, destructive, and seen in a female, this makes it very likely to be a chondrosarcoma.
              • Histologically, when we look at the biopsy, we see the formation of bluish nests surrounding pink osteoid.
              • This is suggestive of cartilage-like cells (which stain bluer).
              • So this is a Chondrosarcoma.
            • What is the treatment?
              • Surgical resection; since most tumours are central, this often involves amputation.
              • Radiotherapy and chemo are only used for some types of chondrosarcoma, or high grade (poorly differentiated) ones.
            • Who gets this disease?
              • Chondrosarcoma is about half as common as osteosarcoma.
              • Patients who get it are usually 40 or older, M:F = 2:1.
            • In what bones does this disease occur?
              • As mentioned before, chondrosarcomas commonly arise in the central portions of the skeleton, such as the pelvis, shoulder, and ribs.
            • What is the prognosis?
              • Most chondrosarcomas are low grade, and they have a higher long-term survival than osteosarcomas.
              • However, they are locally aggressive and can metastasize.
              • Prognosis varies based on the lesion characteristics, and the treatment approach.
