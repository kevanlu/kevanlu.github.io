              What is Connective Tissue?

              • A connective tissue can be defined as a tissue which supports, connects or separate tissues or organs in the body.
              • Comprised of cells scattered in an extra-cellular matrix.
              • Matrix is composed of mainly collagen, elastin, and reticular fibers.
              • Since almost every organ in the body has supporting connective tissues, a connective tissue disease can affect many different organs.

              What is Connective Tissue Disease?

              • We’ve seen some examples of genetic connective tissue disorders, such as Marfan’s Syndrome, or Ehlers-Danlos Syndrome.
                • However, today we’ll be focusing on autoimmune connective tissue disorders.
              • Connective Tissue Disease (autoimmune) → a heterogeneous group of diseases characterized by autoimmune-mediated inflammation of connective tissues.
                • End result of inflammation can be scarring (fibrosis) and damage to the tissue.
                • This autoimmune attack also results in accelerated atherosclerosis of the blood vessels; hence CTDs can result in cardiac problems.
              • Examples of CTDs include:
                • Systemic Lupus Erythematosus (SLE) → affects 1 in 1000.
                • Systemic Sclerosis (SSc) → affects 1 in 10,000.
                • Sjogren’s Syndrome (SS) → affects 2 in 10,000.
                • Polymyositis/Dermatomyositis → affects 1 in 100,000.

              Criteria for SLE

              • These are ‘classification’ criteria for SLE, developed by:
                • The American College of Rheumatology (ACR)
                • The SLICC (SLE International Co-operating Clinics)
              • These criteria were intended as a means for standardizing patient populations for the purpose of clinical research rather than a diagnostic tool.
                • In other words, it’s aimed to be very specific, but may not be completely sensitive.
                • Hence, not all patients with SLE will satisfy this criteria.
              • To be classified as SLE, you need to satisfy the following:
                • Symptom duration of > 6 weeks.
                • Presence of at least 4/11 of the following criteria.
              • Criteria for SLE:
                1. Malar Rash
                  • Photosensitive erythema, flat or raised, over the malar eminences.
                  • Presenting feature in less than 1/3 of patients with SLE.
                  • Spares the nasolabial folds → ie. the smile lines under your cheeks.
                  • Involvement of the nasolabial folds (and rhinophyma) will make a rash more suggestive of rosacea over SLE.
                2. Discoid Rash
                  • This is a circular rash, with central scarring.
                  • This can be differentiated from a ringworm rash in that the centre will be clear with a ringworm, but will be scarred with SLE.
              • Mnemonic → ‘MD SOAP BRAIN’:
                • M → Malar Rash.
                • D → Discoid Scars.
                • S → Serositis.
                • O → Oral Ulcers
                • A → Arthritis
                • P → Photosensitivity
                • B → Blood Disorders
                • R → Renal involvement
                • A → ANA (Anti-nuclear Antibodies)
                • I → Immunologic phenomena (dsDNA; anti-Smith antibodies).
                • N → Neurologic phenomena

              Serositis - Pleurisy, pericarditis on examination or diagnostic electrocardiogram (ECG) or imaging Oral ulcers - Oral or nasopharyngeal, usually painless; palate is most specific Arthritis - Nonerosive, 2 or more peripheral joints with tenderness or swelling Photosensitivity - Unusual skin reaction to light exposure Blood disorders - Leukopenia (< 4 × 103 cells/µL on >1 occasion), lymphopenia (< 1500 cells/µL on >1 occasion), thrombocytopenia (< 100 × 103 cells/µL in the absence of offending medications), hemolytic anemia Renal involvement – Based on presence of proteinuria (>0.5 g/day or 3+ positive on dipstick testing) or cellular casts (including red blood cells [RBCs], hemoglobin, granular, tubular, or mixed)[90] or based on the opinion of a rheumatologist or nephrologist[90] Antinuclear antibodies (ANAs) - Higher titers generally more specific (>1:160); must be in the absence of medications associated with drug-induced lupus Immunologic phenomena - dsDNA; anti-Smith (Sm) antibodies; antiphospholipid antibodies (anticardiolipin immunoglobulin G [IgG] or immunoglobulin M [IgM] or lupus anticoagulant); biologic false-positive serologic test results for syphilis, lupus erythematosus (LE) cells (omitted in 1997 revised criteria) Neurologic disorder - Seizures or psychosis in the absence of other causes Malar rash - Fixed erythema over the cheeks and nasal bridge, flat or raised Discoid rash - Erythematous raised-rimmed lesions with keratotic scaling and follicular plugging, often scarring

              Discoid rash = circular rash, with clearness (scarring) in the centre. Differentiate with ringworm in that centre is clear in ringworm, and scarred in the centre.

              Photosensitivity =/= low Fitz. It’s an almost instantaneous blistering rash when exposed to sun.

              Recurrent ulcers in mouth or nose. Not just a singular cold sore. Classically hear that these lesions aren’t painful with SLE, versus a cold sore. But this isn’t really that useful diagnostically, as opposed to how recurrent the incidence is.

              VS RHEUMATOID ARTHRITIS. Inflammatory arthritis that is NON-EROSIVE. No damage to bone, so you can put their joints back to their original position. Compared to RA, where you can’t reduce their joints back. This is referred to as ‘reducibility’; RA isn’t reducible, SLA is. Jaccoud’s arthropathy = very suggestive of Lupus. This is reducible arthritis.

              Jaccoud’s arthropathy (JA) is a condition characterised clinically by ‘reversible’ joint deformities such as swan neck, thumb subluxation, ulnar deviation, ‘boutonniere’ and hallux valgus, along with an absence of articular erosions on a plain radiograph. JA was initially described in patients with rheumatic fever (RF), but as this disorder has become rare the main clinical entity associated to JA at present is systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE).

              Inflammation of serosal surfaces (pleural cavity, lining of heart) - NO INFLAMMATION OF PERITONEUM, so that doesn’t count. - Pleuritis -> sharp pain with inspiration. - Pericarditis -> sharp pain, often position dependent, can result in SOB.

              LAB TESTS. Renal: Glomerulonephritis or nephrotic syndrome. Deposition of immune complex into kidneys. Blood in urine, protein in urine, altered f(x) Hence urinalysis necessary when investigating possible lupus.

              Neurologic disorder Not headache/drowsy. More like new onset seizures, psychoses, profound delirium, etc.

              CBC: Leukopenia, hemolytic anemia, thrombocytopenia.

              Positive ANA

              Positive dsDNA, Anti-Smith (Sm), or Anti-phospholipid Ab. SM = smith, NOT ANTI-SMOOTH MUSCLE ANTIBODY (which we saw for Celiac’s Disease)!!!!!!!
