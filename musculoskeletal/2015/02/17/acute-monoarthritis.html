          Introduction to Arthritis

          • Arthritis can be divided into 2 broad categories:
            • Non-inflammatory Arthritis
            • Inflammatory Arthritis
          • Non-inflammatory arthritis is essentially only osteoarthritis.
          • Inflammatory arthritis can be further divided into:
            • Infectious arthritis → bacterial, viral, parasitic, etc.
            • Non-infectious arthritis → rheumatoid arthritis, seronegative arthropathies, crystal induced arthritis.
          • See the below chart (IMPORTANT).
            • Today we’ll be dealing with a subset of these conditions which can result in an acute arthritis in a single joint.

          • Arthritis can also be described by the number of joints involved:
            • Monoarthritis → 1 involved joint.
            • Oligoarthritis → 2-4 involved joints.
            • Polyarthritis → >4 involved joints.

          Introduction to Acute Monoarthritis

          • Acute → < 6 weeks.
          • Mono → 1.
          • Arthritis → inflamed joint.
          • Acute Monoarthritis → a single inflamed joint for less than 6 weeks.

          Objective

          • What are the common causes of non-traumatic intense acute mono-arthritis (warm, hot, red, and swollen joint)?
          • Causes of an acute, hot, red, monoarthritis include:
            • INFECTION!
            • Gout.
            • Pseudogout.

          Objective

          • What important diagnosis should be ruled out in ALL cases of non-traumatic intense acute mono-arthritis (warm, hot, red, and swollen joint)?
          • Whenever you see an acute monoarthritis, suspect infection.
            • Septic arthritis is what’s dangerous, so it’s what you HAVE to rule out.
            • Gout and pseudogout can mimic infection, but are less serious.

          Septic Arthritis

          • Septic Arthritis → infection of a joint leading to arthritis.
          • Why is it so important to treat septic arthritis so quickly?
            • Acute Bacterial Arthritis is a medical emergency.
            • If it’s not treated quickly, it can lead to permanent, irreversible joint damage.
          • Risk Factors for Septic Arthritis:
            • Immunosuppressed individuals.
            • The very old, or the very young (extremes of age).
            • IVDU, alcohol.
            • Recent infection.
            • Prior joint operations (prosthetic joint, recent surgery).
            • Underlying joint abnormality → eg. arthritis.
            • Direct penetrating trauma.
            • Skin infection.
          • How does an infection get to the joint?
            • Most commonly this is via hematogenous spread from a remote site.
            • Less common are direct inoculation (eg. trauma/surgery), or from spread from a contiguous site (eg. from adjacent osteomyelitis).

          Objective

          • Describe the initial management & investigative strategy for suspected septic arthritis.
          • If you see an acute, hot, red joint, you always aspirate the joint.
            • IF THE JOINT IS RED, THE TUBES GET FED.
          • What do you send the joint aspirate for?
            • The 3 C’s
              1. Cell count → takes a few hours.
              2. Cuture & Sensitivity → takes 1-2 days.
              3. Crystals → takes a few hours.
            • You should also do a gram stain.
          • Initial Management
            • Urgent I&D (Incision & Drainage).
            • Antibiotics.
            • Analgesia.
            • Rest.
          • Continued management
            • Repead I&D until the surgeon is confident the joint is clean.
            • IV antibiotics for 4-6 weeks.

          Objective

          • Suggest an initial empiric antibiotic regimen for septic arthritis and explain your rationale based on the common organisms along with your suggested duration of treatment?
          • Most common organisms with septic arthritis:
            1. Gram positive cocci (80%)
              • Staphylococcus (60%) → most commonly S. aureus.
              • Streptococcus (20%).
            2. Gram negative (15%).
              • Seen in the immunocompromised, elderly, neodates, and IVDUs.
            3. Anaerobic organisms.
          • Depending on the gram stain results, some empiric Abx choices are as follows:
            • Gram positive → 1st generation Cephalosporin, or Vancomycin if you’re worried about MRSA.
            • Gram negative → 3rd generation Cephalosporin (Ceftriaxone, Cefotaxime, Ceftazidime).

          Objective

          • Describe why you would consult orthopedic surgery in a case of septic arthritis?
          • Because the infection is localized to the joint, systemic antibiotics are unlikely to clear it unless the joint has been drained and cleaned.
            • Surgical drainage is necessary.

          Gout

          • Gout → Acute inflammatory arthritis caued by uric acid crystal deposition in the joint.
            • Often referred to as “The Disease of Kings”, for reasons that will be discussed shortly.
          • Pathophysiology of gout:
            • Normally, the body’s production of uric acid is matched by its excretion, leaving the body in a steady state.
            • With underexcretion, uric acid can accumulate in the body, leading to hyperuricemia.
            • With excessive hyperuricemia, the uric acid may oversaturate the blood, resulting in crystal deposition.

          Objective

          • What is the description of a classic attack of acute gout including the types of people it affects and the characteristics of the attack including the onset, progression, pain intensity, joints affected and duration?

          • Who gets gout?
            • The age of onset of gout differs between men and women.
            • In men, the first attack is between the ages of 35 and 50.
            • In women, the first attack is often after menopause, as estrogen has a protective effect on uric acid secretion.
          • Clinical Features of the attack:
            • Starts very quickly and very intensely – progressing over a few hours.
              • Untreated attacks will typically take about 7-10 days to resolve.
            • Very painful (can’t stand having the bed sheets touch it).
            • Swollen, warm, and red.
            • May feel unwell and have an associated fever.
            • In other words, this is very similar to an infected joint.
              • Hence, you always have to rule out a septic arthritis, even if he has no risk factors for infection, and a history more suggestive for gout.
          • What joints does it affect?
            • Usually a single joint in the lower extremity.
            • Most commonly (50% of cases), it’s the First Metatarsophalyngeal (MTP) joint; ie. the MTP of the big toe.
            • Other common joints include the ankle and knee.
          • What characteristics do people with gout typically have?:
            • Obesity.
            • Hypertension.
            • Diabetes.
            • Hyperlipidemia.
          • These are pretty similar to the vascular risk factors (minus smoking).
            • This is also why it’s called ‘a disease of plenty’, or ‘The disease of Kings’; historically it was seen in fat, coddled royalty.

          Objective

          • What are the common risk factors for the development of hyperuricemia and acute gout?
          • Common risk factors:
            1. Impaired renal function.
              • Impaired renal function results in impaired uric acid excretion.
            2. Diuretics → Lasix, hydrochlorothiazide.
              • These not only affect uric acid excretion, but also result in uric acid concentration due to the peeing out lots of water.
            3. Excessive alcohol intake.
            4. Family history.
            5. Male sex.

          Objective

          • What are the common precipitating factors for the development of acute gout?
          • Patients will be predisposed to having gout by having a high uric acid concentration in their blood (either due to fluid loss or impaired uric acid secretion).
            • However, this does not necessarily result in gout itself; usually, a trigger is required.
          • What ‘triggers’ an attack of gout?
            • Remember this with the SMARTS acronym:
              • S → Surgery.
              • M → Mechanical Injury.
              • A → Alcohol.
              • R → Recent Illness.
              • T → Travel/Dehydration.
              • S → Start/stop Allopurinol.

          Triggers for Gout

          • Remember this with the SMARTS acronym:
            • S → Surgery.
            • M → Mechanical Injury.
            • A → Alcohol.
            • R → Recent Illness.
            • T → Travel/Dehydration.
            • S → Start/stop Allopurinol.
          • Notes on Triggers:
            • It’s often thought that the big toe is so commonly involved because microtrauma to the toe occurs when you walk.
            • Travel/dehydration can trigger an attack of gout by increasing the concentration of blood uric acid.
            • Although allopurinol is a treatment for hyperuricemia, it can actually exacerbate an acute episode of gout.

          Objective

          • What investigations should be ordered in a patient presenting with suspected gout?
          • The only way to confirm a diagnosis of gout is to aspirate the joint.
            • This is something you should be doing ANYWAY in order to rule out septic arthritis.
              • As before, IF THE JOINT IS RED, THE TUBES GET FED.
            • In the case of gout however, the key diagnostic feature is the presence of a specific type of crystal:
              • Needle shaped urate crystals, which are negatively birefringent under polarized light.

          Diagnosis of Gout

          • Needle shaped crystals that are negatively birefringent under polarized light are pathognomonic for gout.
          • What other blood tests should be ordered?
            • CBC-D.
            • Creatinine → assess kidney function (uric acid excretion).
            • Uric acid → you’d think this would always be high, but it may actually be normal during an acute attack.
              • So during an acute attack of gout, uric acid is unreliable; this should be taken once the attack subsides.
            • Fasting glucose → diabetes is a risk factor.
            • Fasting lipids (triglycerides, cholesterol) → obesity and hyperlipidemia are risk factors.

          Objective

          • What is the utility of uric acid levels in the diagnosis and management of gout?
          • As mentioned above, the uric acid levels may actually be normal when measured during an acute attack of gout.
            • Uric acid should hence be measured after an acute attack has subsided.
          • What should be done if a patient has hyperuricemia?
            • Many people have hyperuricemia and are completely asymptomatic.
            • Most of these patients will never get gout.
            • Hence, hyperuricemia CANNOT be used to make a diagnosis of gout on its own.
            • Do NOT treat isolated hyperuricemia.

          Objective

          • What is the only true way to confirm a diagnosis of gout?

          • Urate crystals on joint aspirate.
            • As mentioned before, this is the only way to confirm the diagnosis.
            • These crystals should be negatively birefringent under polarized light.

          Objective

          • What is the management strategy for acute gout including both non-pharmacologic and pharmacologic therapy?
          • Non-pharmacologic (fairly obvious, given risk factors):
            • Rest, ice, elevation.
            • Dietary modification:
              • Red meat and seafood are bad.
              • Vegetables and low fat dairy are good.
            • Reduce alcohol intake.
            • Good hydration.
          • Pharmacologic (excluding urate lowering therapy):
            • Intra-articular corticosteroids → inject the affected joint.
            • Oral NSAIDs or COXIBs.
            • Oral Colchicine.
            • Oral Prednisone → if the joint is hard to inject (eg. the big toe).
          • Recurrence Rates
            • 1 year → 60%.
            • 2 years → 80%.
            • 5 years → 90%.
            • In other words, if you have 1 attack, you’re very likely to get another one!

          Objective

          • List the indications for allopurinol in patients with gout and discuss risks and benefits of this medication?

          • Allopurinol → urate lowering medication, that reduces hyperuricemia.
            • Usually only used in more extreme cases, because once you’re on a urate lowering therapy, you have to stay on it for life.
            • Another medication that can be used is Febuxostat.
            • Both of these inhibit xanthine oxidase, which converts xanthine to uric acid.
          • When should urate lowering therapy be considered?
            1. Recurrent acute episodes of gout that are affecting lifestyle.
            2. Patient at risk from complications of treatments required for acute attacks.
            3. Patient accepts the need to stay on these medications for life.
            4. Uric acid tophaceous deposits → nodular, often deforming masses of uric acid that deposit in soft tissue (skin, internal organs)
              • An example is shown to the right.
              • This is considered an absolute indication for uric acid lowering treatment, even if they’re not having many acute attacks.
          • What are the risks associated with urate lowering medications?
            • Whenever you start or stop one of these medications (allopurinol, febuxostat), it can actually precipitate an attack of gout (the last S in SMARTS was ‘start/stop allopurinol’).
            • So if you have a non-compliant patient that only remembers to take it a few days, this can actually increase the number of flares a patient will experience!
            • When you start a patient on this medication, you have to warn them of the risk of an immediate flare!
              • Oftentimes, prophylaxis with an NSAID/Colchicine/Prednisone is given early on, in order to reduce the risk of this occurring.
          • What are the benefits of urate lowering medications?
            • Will help to reduce uric acid to the lower limit of laboratory reference range.
            • Will prevent tophi from forming.
            • Will reduce flares long-term if taken continuously.

          Pseudogout

          • Acute Pseudogout → acute inflammatory arthritis caused by calcium pyrophosphate crystals.
            • Here, the pathognomonic finding will be rhomboid shaped, intracellular positively bi-refringent crystals.
            • Pseudogout is also generally less painful than gout.

          Gout vs Pseudogout

            Gout Pseudogout
          Crystal composition: Uric acid crystals Calcium pyrophosphate crystals
          Crystal shape: Needle shaped Rhomboid shaped
          Under Polarized Light: Negatively bi-refringent Positively bi-refringent
          Crystal appearance:
          • Useful mnemonics for comparing gout to pseudogout:
            • Pseudogout is Positively birefringent.
            • Gout has needle shaped crystals, and is hence more painful.

          Objective

          • What is the description of a classic attack of pseudogout including the types of people it affects and the characteristics of the attack including onset, progression, pain intensity, joints affected, and duration?
          • Who gets pseudogout?
            • Typically older individuals.
            • Most will have a concomitant osteoarthritis.
          • Clinical features of the attack
            • These are similar to with gout or an infected joint.
            • Starts quickly and very intensely, but tends to be less intense than gout, and also has a less rapid onset.
              • Untreated attacks can last several days to a few weeks.
            • Very painful, swollen, red, warm joint.
            • May feel unwell and have an associated fever.
          • What joints does it affect?
            • Usually a single joint in the lower extremity.
            • Knee is the most common.

          Objective

          • What is the difference between chondrocalcinosis and pseudogout?

          • Pseudogout → an acute inflammatory arthritis caused by calcium pyrophosphate crystal deposition in the joint
          • Chondrocalcinosis → a radiographic finding of calcium pyrophosphate deposition in the cartilage.
            • In other words, chondrocalcinosis is a sign that calcium pyrophosphate had been deposited into the joint at some point in the past.
            • It is not indicative of a current episode of pseudogout; it just suggests that there has been past deposition.

          Objective

          • What is the management strategy for acute pseudogout including both non- pharmacologic and pharmacologic therapy?
          • Non-pharmacologic:
            • Rest, Ice, and Elevate the Joint.
            • Good Hydration.
          • Pharmacologic → fairly similar to with gout, except without colchicine.
            • Intra-articular corticosteroids → inject the affected joint.
            • Oral NSAIDs or COXIBs.
            • Oral Prednisone → if the joint is hard to inject (eg. the big toe).
          • There’s no equivalent to allopurinol or febuxostat in pseudogout
            • In other words, there’s no preventative treatment we can give that will lower calcium pyrophosphate levels.
