            Osteoarthritis

            Objective

            • What is the most common type of chronic monoarthritis?
            • Osteoarthritis.
              • Also known as degenerative joint disease; results in the wearing down of the cartilage and surrounding bone of the joint.
              • Extremely common; currently affects 1 in every 10 Canadians.

            Objective

            • What is the difference between primary and secondary osteoarthritis.
            • Primary Osteoarthritis → degeneration of the joints due to ‘wear and tear’; no obvious cartilage injury.
            • Secondary Osteoarthritis → the degeneration is secondary to something, ie. an injury to the cartilage.
              • Eg. athletes who overuse certain joints throughout their career.

            Objective

            • What is the prototypical presentation for primary osteoarthritis including the common age of affected individuals, typical joints affected (identify on a homunculus), modifiable risk factors, diurnal variation and effects of activity, and
            • Onset of Osteoarthritis
              • Seen in the elderly, with women > men.
              • Often exacerbated by higher BMIs (increases load on joints).
              • Progression is insidious, over the span of months to years.
            • Symptoms of Osteoarthritis
              • Pain that worsens with weight bearing and activity, and improves with rest.
              • Worse in the morning; often presents with morning stiffness (15-30 min).
              • The involved joint(s) will often be stiff after periods of inactivity.
              • Tenderness on palpation, bony enlargement, crepitus on motion, limitation of joint motion.
              • Sparing of the MCP Joints.
                • This is an important differentiator from rheumatoid arthritis, which does affect the MCP joints.
                • Rheumatoid arthritis → MCP + PIP, spares the DIPs.
                • Osteoarthritis → DIPs and PIPs, spares the MCPs.

            Finger Joint Involvement with Arthritis

            • Rheumatoid arthritis:
              • Affects the MCPs and PIPs.
              • Spares the DIPs (farthest).
              • Also affects the wrist.
            • Osteoarthritis:
              • Affects the PIPs and DIPs.
              • Spares the MCPs (closest).

            • What other joints are affected with osteoarthritis?
              • In addition to the DIP and PIP joints, osteoarthritis commonly affects other joints in the body.
              • Hands → DIP, PIP, 1st CMC joint.
              • Hip
              • Knee
              • Feet → first MTP.
              • Spine → cervical, lumbar.

            Objective

            • What are the five classic radiographic findings of osteoarthritis?

            1. Non-uniform Joint Space Narrowing → typically the side that bears more weight will be narrower.
            2. Subchondral Sclerosis:
              • This is increased density of the bone right underneath the cartilage (hence sub-chondral).
              • Seen on XR as opacification of the bone at the joint.
            3. Osteophytes → small bone projections form, particularly at the margins.
            4. Subchondral Cysts → sometimes fluid will be pushed underneath the cartilage, forming cysts in the bone.
            5. Malalignment.

            Objective

            • What laboratory abnormalities are seen in osteoarthritis?
            • All laboratory investigations should be normal in OA
              • Recall that OA is a ‘non-inflammatory’ arthritis, so we wouldn’t expect to see anything like an elevated CRP or ESR, etc.

            Objective

            • What common strategies are utilized for the management of osteoarthritis?
            • NON-PHARMACOLOGIC:
              • Exercise & weight loss → very effective for reducing joint burden.
              • Strength training → strengthen the surrounding muscles.
              • Proper footwear.
              • Assistive devices.
              • Education.
            • PHARMACOLOGIC:
              1. Topical Medications
                • Capsaicin creams → hot pepper creams.
                  • Depletes nerve endings of chemicals needed for pain transmission.
                  • Results in ‘analgesia’; touch and temp sensation are not affected.
                  • Somewhat annoying to use; DON’T TOUCH YOUR EYE after applying it!
                • Topical NSAIDs
                  • Little systemic absorption, so less risk of side effects like PUD.
                  • But only has shown small effects in clinical trials, and is messy.
              2. Intra-articular Medications
                • Corticosteroids
                  • Cheap, relatively safe.
                  • Has a short term, 4-8 week benefit, but no evidence for benefit at 12 and 24 weeks.
                  • Predictors of response are unclear (it’s not obvious who will feel better and who won’t).
                • Viscosupplementation → synthetic/biologically derived Hyaluronic acid.
                  • Supplementation of the synovial fluid of the joint with hyaluronic acid (a normal component of the fluid).
                  • Only approved for OA of the knee (no other joints).
                  • Variable effect; some people have a great response, others have no response at all.
                  • Very expensive; $300 per course.
              3. Systemic Medications
                • Simple Analgesics → Acetaminophen.
                • NSAIDs → or COXIBs.
                  • NSAIDs (eg. Ibuprofen) block the actions of both COX-1 and COX-2.
                  • COXIBs (eg. Celebrex) only block the actions of COX-2.
                  • Both perform better than Acetaminophen, but carry additional risks (GI, renal, cardiovascular).
                    • Recall the role that prostaglandins play on the afferent arterioles of the kidney.
                    • Recall that NSAIDs can cause PUD, particular gastric ulcers.
                    • Also now thought that all NSAIDs may increase the risk of MIs, particularly the COXIBs (recall the Vioxx recall).
                • Narcotics (Opioids)
                  • Exceedingly rare for patients with OA to show addictive behaviour.
                  • Most patients will voluntarily stop their opioids after joint replacement.
                  • The primary worry with OA is actually with the side effects: sedation, confusion, constipation.
                  • Sedation and confusion in particular can increase the risk for falls, especially in the typical demographic for OA (old).

            Objective

            • When should a patient with osteoarthritis be referred to a surgeon?
            • Most people with arthritis, including older individuals, should be referred for surgical treatment when other treatment is ineffective and function is impaired.
              • Surgery should not be used as a last resort; don’t wait until there is significant deformity, muscle weakness, or loss of function. If you do, the replacement will do little to help.
              • There is no “magic age” for surgery.
            • The decision for whether to undergo surgery depends both on patient factors (their function, their goals), and physician factors (XR changes, medical risk factors).
