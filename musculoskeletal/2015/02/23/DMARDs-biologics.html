          Review Questions

          • How long do you need to wait before you start DMARD treatment?
            • If it’s been present for more than 6 weeks and hasn’t been getting better.
          • If your RF and ANA are negative, is it still rheumatoid arthritis?
            • Yes, it could still be RA.
            • RF is negative in 50-70% of early cases, she will still need DMARD treatment.

          Introduction to DMARDs

          • DMARDs → Disease Modifying Anti-Rheumatic Drugs.
            • It’s best to think of these medications as forms of mild chemotherapy.
          • Typically given to patients as triple therapy; a combination of multiple DMARDs.
            • Historically, this hasn’t been the case. Because of worries of side-effects with ‘mild chemotherapy’, we usually waited until the symptoms became quite severe.
            • However, now we aim to treat much earlier, with multiple agents in order to increase effectiveness.
            • Triple therapy is classically described as:
              1. Methotrexate
              2. Sulfasalazine.
              3. Hydroxychloroquine (Plaquenil)
            • Sometimes Leflunomide is added (quadruple therapy).
            • More recently, there’s a push to use methotrexate with biologics instead (depending on patient drug coverage, since biologics are $$$).
          • Why do we treat earlier now?
            • This has been a paradigm shift in the treatment of inflammatory arthritis.
            • This is mainly because joint damage is an early phenomenon of rheumatoid arthritis; if you wait until the inflammation gets worse, the joint damage may already be done!
              • Joint erosions occur in 93% of patients with less than 2 years of disease activity.
              • The rate of radiographic progression is greatest in the first 2 yeras.
            • There’s also a ‘window of opportunity’ where, if we treat heavily enough, we can actually get the disease in remission.
              • This has made the tradeoff with side-effects more bearable.

          Remember

          • Joint damage occurs early on with RA, so DMARD therapy is given earlier.
          • It’s thought that strong treatment early on can put the disease into remission.

          What are some types of DMARDs?

          • The main DMARDs are:
            1. Methotrexate
            2. Sulfasalazine (Salazopyrin©).
            3. Hydroxychloroquine (Plaquenil©)
            4. Leflunomide (Arava©)
          • Other DMARDs, which are used less frequently, are:
            • Gold (Myochrisine©)
            • Cyclosporine, Azathioprine, Cyclophosphamide.
          • Common Drug Therapies:
            1. Triple Therapy → Methotrexate + Sulfasalazine + Hydroxychloroquine.
            2. Double Therapy:
              • Methotrexate & Leflunomide.
              • Methotrexate & Sulfasalazine.
              • Methotrexate & Hydroxychloroquine .
              • Methotrexate & Gold.
              • Sulfasalazine & Plaquenil.
            3. Monotherapy
          • Of these, triple therapy is used (by far) the most.

          Methotrexate

          Objective

          • How does methotrexate work?
          • Mechanism of action:
            • Similarity in structure between folate and methotrexate.
            • Competitive inhibitor of THF, interferes with purine synthesis, and thus rapidly dividing cells.
            • Side effects relate to this; affects hair and serosa.
            • Doesn’t work right away; takes 6-8 weeks to start working.

          Dose and Administration  Dose ranges from 7.5 to 25 mg  ONLY GIVEN ONCE A WEEK  2.5 mg Tablets or Subcutaneous Injection 25 mg/mL  Onset of Action  6-8 weeks  Avoid  Pregnancy – Teratogenic  Alcohol

           Sulfa Antibiotics (Sulfasalazine is ok)

          Why is alcohol contraindicated?

          Objective

          • What are the common and important side effects of methotrexate?
          • Common Side Effects:
            • Malaise, nausea.
            • Rise in liver enzymes (due to liver inflammation).
          • Rare (important) Side Effects:
            • Anemia, leukopenia.
            • Pulmonary → fever, cough, SOB.
            • Infection, since it is an immunosupressant.
            • Teratogenic in pregnancy.
            • Lymphoma with long-term use.

          Objective

          • What routine investigations are recommended for patients taking methotrexate?
          • CBC & Inflammatory markers → CBC, ESR, CRP.
          • Liver function tests → AST, ALT, ALP, Albumin.
          • Kidney function tests → Creatinine.

          Objective

          • How much alcohol is ok when taking methotrexate?

          Methotrexate causes inflammation of liver as well. CAN’T DRINK ALCOHOL WHILE ON METHOTREXATE 0 -> less than 1 beverage/week. Septra is contraindicated.

          Objective

          • Is methotrexate safe in pregnancy?

          NO, teratogenic.

          Hydroxychloroquine

          • Trade name → Plaquenil.
          • Use to be anti-malarial.
          • No one really knows how it works.
          • Increases pH of cells.
          • Thought that this affects ability of cells to degrade proteins. (lysozymes acidic?)

          Dose & Administration  200 mg tablets  Dose ranges from 200 – 400 mg per day  Onset  6-12 weeks  Avoid  May increase sensitivity to the sun

          Objective

          • What are the common and important side effects of hydroxychloroquine?
          • Common Side Effects:
            • Rash, nausea, diarrhea.
            • Affects accomodation (how quickly your eye can change focus); can cause difficulty with reading.
          • Rare (important) Side Effects:
            • Ocular, skeletal muscle, heart problems.
            • Long term use can cause extra pigment to deposit in the back of the retina.

          Objective

          • What routine investigations are recommended for patients taking hydroxychloroquine?

          No lab monitoring. Yearly eye exams. Plaquenil can cause extra pigment to deposit in back of retina. Over time, can cause visual disturbances.

          Sulfasalazine

          Developed in the late 1930s by Dr. Nana Svartz (Swedish Rheumatologist) for the treatment of “infective polyarthritis”  Anti-inflammatory: Salicylic Acid  Antibiotic: Sulfapyridine  Sulfasalazine consists of salicylic acid and sulfapyridine joined by an azo bond.  Sulfinpyradone is thought to be the active ingredient although, in RA, the mechanism of action is not understood

          ills taken twice daily (CPS and some pharmacists – QID!)  Start at a low dose 1 per day and gradually work up to 4 per day  Takes 6-8 weeks to work  Common S/E: Malaise, Nausea, Abd pain, Rash, headaches, dizziness  Rare S/E:  Hypersensitivity reaction  Liver: Follow enzymes – not usually a problem  Bone Marrow: Follow bloodwork – dose/sensitivity related  Renal: Very Rare

          Objective

          • What are the common and important side effects of sulfasalazine?
          • Common Side Effects:
            • Malaise, nausea, abdominal pain, rash, headaches, dizziness.
          • Rare (important) Side Effects:
            • Hypersensitivity reaction (Sulfa allergy).
            • Liver damage → follow enzymes, usually not a problem.
            • Bone marrow damage → follow bloodwork, dose/sensitivity related.
            • Renal damage → very rare.

          Objective

          • What routine investigations are recommended for patients taking sulfasalazine?

          Exactly the same as methotrexate. Follow liver/kidney/bone marrow. f(x).  Lab Monitoring (week 2, 4 and then monthly)  CBC, ESR, CRP  AST, ALT, ALP, Albumin Cr

          Leflunomide

          • Trade name → Arava. Inactive and converted to A177-1726 in the GI tract and liver A77-1726 inhibits the activity of dihydro-orotate dehydrogenase, an important enzyme in the de novo synthesis of pyrimidines. Opposite of methotrexate, which works on purines.

           Dose and Administration  10 & 20 mg Tablets  Given daily – ranges from 10-20 mg per day  Onset of Action  6-8 weeks  Avoid  Pregnancy – Teratogenic  Alcohol

          NOT ONLY VERY TERATOGENIC, BUT STAYS IN BODY FOR A LONG TIME. Need to do a complicated washout procedure before conceiving. Similar to methotrexate.

          Objective

          • What are the common and important side effects of leflunomide?

          Common S/E  Diarrhea, nausea, malaise, hypertension, alopecia, rash  Rare S/E  Liver: Follow enzymes – not usually a problem  Bone Marrow: Follow bloodwork – dose/sensitivity related  Infection  Pregnancy  Malignancy

          As with methotrexate and sulfasalazine, follow liver, kidney, and BM f(x) Both the ones that affect purine/pyrimidine f(x) highly teratogenic.

          Objective

          • What routine investigations are recommended for patients taking leflunomide?

           CBC, ESR, CRP  AST, ALT, ALP, Albumin Cr

          Same as sulfasalazine and methotrexate.

          COMMON APPROACH

           Triple therapy for 3-6 months  Optimize dose and route of medications  Incomplete Response  Changing to sc methotrexate  Adding Leflunomide  Adding a Biologic Agent sc methotrexate increases absorption, reduces side-effects. Big push in canada to start with sc before oral. Add leflunomide -> quadruple. Add biologic.

          Biologics

          • Roughly broken down into TNF-Alpha inhibitors and non-TNF-alpha. Specially designed to treat inflammatory types of arthritis such as rheumatoid and psoriatic arthritis and ankylosing spondylitis.  Six biologics currently available (and lots more coming)  Work by different mechanisms.  Like DMARDs, biologics are used to suppress inflammation and help prevent damage to the joint.

          TNF Inhibitors  Adalimumab (Humira)  Etanercept (Enbrel)  Infliximab (Remicade)  Golimumab (Simponi)  Certolizumab (Cimzia)  IL-1 Inhibitors  Anakinra (Kineret)  IL6 Inhibitors  Tocilizumab (Actemra)  T-Cell Co-Stimulatory Blockade  Abatacept (Orencia)  B-Cell Depletion  Rituximab (Rituxan)

          In some people with arthritis, a protein called Tumour Necrosis Factor (TNF) is present in the blood and joints in excessive amounts where it increases inflammation (pain & swelling).

          How to TNF-inhibitors work? Block inflammatory cascade.  Mechanism of Action  Infliximab and Adalimumab are antibodies directed against TNF  Etanercept is a soluble receptor decoy!!  Administration  Infliximab is given intravenously  Etanercept, Adalimumab, Golimumab, & Certolizumab are all given by subcutaneous injection

          MAB = monoclonal AB. Cept = receptor blocer.

          Objective

          • What are the common and important side effects of the biologic agents?

          TNF INHIBITION. Infusion Reactions with Infliximab, Injection Site Reactions with Adalimumab and Etanercept  Infection  Tuberculosis  Serious resulting in death  Malignancy  Increased risk of melanoma ?lymphoma - Might be due to by indication (?) people with RA already at increased risk of lymphoma.  Neurologic  Multiple Sclerosis, seizures, inflammation of the ocular nerve  Autoimmune  Antibody formation – SLE like illness  Worsening of Congestive Heart Failure

          How to T-Cell Co-Stimulatory Blockades work? T cell needs 2 signals: 1 from APC, and 1 from MHC. Orencia blocks the costimulatory signal and prevents it from activating. Anti CD-80, 86 receptor.

          Given on week 0, 2, and 4 then every 4 weeks  Quick infusions over 30 minutes  Well tolerated  Home infusion program

          Side Effects  INFEC TION  COPD Exacerbation  MALIGNANC Y – Lung Cancer  Infusion Reactions – Rare

          Objective

          • When should biologic agents be stopped?

          STOP if develop a fever, have an infection, or have been prescribed antibiotics  Tell your doctor about any upcoming surgeries

          LUPUS + RA = RUPUS!?! :P Started as a joke, now the actual name.

          B-Cell Depletion (Rituximab) (I GIVE UP HERE) RA was thought to be T-Cell mediated  Most widely accepted hypothesis:  Professional Antigen Presenting Cell encounters some “unknown” antigen  It presents this “unknown” antigen to a CD4 T-helper Cell  In a genetically predisposed individual, this starts an immune chain reaction  Immune system is confused and targets healthy tissue
