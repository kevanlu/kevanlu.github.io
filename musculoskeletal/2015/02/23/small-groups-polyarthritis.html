                    Case #1

                    Patient History

                    • Identifying Data → John is a 26 year old recently married man with no children.
                    • Presenting Problem → Pain and swelling in many joints.
                    • Past Medical History → John is otherwise healthy.
                    • Current Medications → Ibuprofen 200 mg q4d.
                    • Allergies → none.
                    • History of Presenting Illness:
                      • Over the last three months, John has developed swelling and pain in a number of joints. It began in his right index MCP and PIP and shortly afterwards the left ring DIP, right wrist, and left ankle became involved. He describes two hours of morning stiffness in the swollen joints with a mild reduction in his energy. He is still able to perform his basic activities of daily living and is able to continue working at his job in the stock-room of a local auto-parts dealership.
                      • When asked about previous joint problems he recounts a right 4th toe becoming very painful, red, and swollen one year previously, to the point where he was referred to a local surgeon for treatment of what was felt to be either a cellulitis or an osteomyelitis. The toe had been amputated. He also recalls an episode of left Achilles’ tendonitis several years ago, and recurring problems with what had been diagnosed as plantar fasciitis.
                    • Social History → John does not smoke and drinks occasionally at social gatherings.
                    • Review of Systems → John has itching and scaling in the skin folds behind his ears. He also gets patches of “dry skin” in his scalp. About six months ago John had been under the care of an ophthalmologist for an episode of intense redness and pain in his right eye which cleared up with steroid drops. John denies any problems with his low back.
                    • Family History → John’s father has psoriasis as does his brother. His sister and mom are both healthy.
                    • Physical Examination → John appears well in no obvious distress. Cutaneous examination confirms extensive scaling on an erythematous base behind the ears along with scaly erythematous plaques on the scalp. Multiple nails had pits in them and some of the nails appeared to be lifting off the nail-bed (onycholysis). The involved digits and joints were diffusely swollen, warm to touch, and tender to palpation. The MCP and IP joints have a reddish-purple hue.
                    1. List your differential diagnosis for John’s problem
                      • Seronegative Arthritis → Psoriatic Arthritis.
                        • Recall that there were 4 types of seronegative arthritis:
                          1. Psoriatic Arthritis.
                          2. Reactive Arthritis.
                          3. Enteropathic Arthritis (history of IBD).
                          4. Ankylosing Spondylitis.
                      • Rheumatoid Arthritis.
                      • Arthritis associated with viral hepatitis.
                      • SLE is possible.
                      • Viral hepatitis → unlikely due to 3-month history of disease.
                    2. Cite those features which strongly support one of those diagnoses.
                      • Most likely to be psoriatic arthritis.
                      • Patient has family history of psoriasis, and seems to have psoriasis himself.
                      • Psoriatic arthritis usually affects younger patients, so John fits the bill.
                      • DIP involvement makes it less likely to be rheumatoid arthritis.
                      • Signs of dactylitis and enthesitis (inflammation of attachments of ligaments and tendons).
                      • Past eye problems are suggestive of iritis.
                      • Nail findings; recall that nail findings with psoriatic arthritis includes:
                        • Onycholysis → lifting off the nail beds.
                        • Oil-drop sign → yellow discoloration of the nail.
                        • Hyperkeratosis → can be so bad that it can resemble a fungal infection.
                    3. What investigations should be done?
                      • Blood tests & Urinalysis.
                        • CBC → look for anemia and platelets.
                        • ESR, CRP → look for inflammation.
                        • Creatinine → kidney function (DMARDs can cause renal problems).
                        • AST, ALT, ALP, albumin → liver function (DMARDs can cause liver inflammation).
                        • Hepatitis B&C serology → r/o arthritis associated with hepatitis.
                        • Rheumatoid Factor → by definition, should be negative in psoriatic arthritis (seronegative), but small percentage can have a low grade RF.
                        • ANA → also should be negative since seronegative.
                      • Radiographs
                        • Radiograph the involved joints to follow joint damage over time.
                    4. Outline an immediate management plan for the most likely diagnosis.
                      • Refer to rheumatologist.
                      • Non-pharmacologic → education, PT, OT.
                      • Pharmacologic → NSAIDs, steroids, DMARDs, biologics.
                        • NSAIDs → pain relief, but doesn’t alter course of disease.
                        • Corticosteroids → often used to improve function, and act as a bridge to DMARDs.
                        • DMARDs → need to be used. Monotherapy/double-therapy/triple-therapy can be used depending on disease severity.
                      • Biologics → TNA-α inhibitors can be used if there’s a failure to respond to DMARD therapy.

                    Case #2

                    Patient History

                    • Identifying Data → Pauline is a 42 year old mother of 4. She is married and is working part-time as a medical secretary.
                    • Presenting Problem → Pain and swelling in many joints.
                    • Past Medical History → Pauline is otherwise healthy.
                    • Current Medications → enteric coated aspirin.
                    • Allergies → none.
                    • History of Presenting Illness → Pauline has experienced a gradual progressive pattern of pain, swelling, and stiffness involving the small joints of her hands (MCPs and PIPs), wrists, shoulders, knees, and in the balls of her feet over the last 6 months. She is significantly fatigued requiring a nap each afternoon; she sleeps poorly because of shoulder pain, and each morning requires 30 minutes just to get dressed for work. Her morning stiffness eases around noon. Pauline is finding it increasingly difficult to cope at home. She has taken the last week off work and the house is a mess as she hasn’t been able to clean.
                    • Social History → Pauline does not smoke and drinks occasionally at social gatherings.
                    • Review of Systems → Pauline has had no fevers or chills. Her appetite has been slightly lower and she thinks she might have lost a few pounds. She has noticed “itchiness” and irritation of her eyes and has taken to buying “artificial tears” at the drug store. She keeps a glass of water at her bedside, and quite unusual for her, has had to have four dental caries attended to in the last year. The remaining review of systems was non-contributory.
                    • Family History → Pauline’s father has hypertension and gout and her mother is healthy after recently recovering from breast cancer. Her 3 sisters are healthy. Her children are all healthy.
                    • Physical Examination → Pauline looks tired and uncomfortable. Her blood pressure is 120/80 and she is afebrile. Examination of the eyes appears to show a reduced tear film. Her mouth appears dry with no salivary pool and she has significant halitosis. Her respiratory, cardiac, and abdominal examination is normal. All of the MCPs, PIPs, and both wrists are warm, tender, and swollen with capsular distension. A tight finger tuck is impossible and her grip strength is reduced. Her shoulders and knees exhibit stress pain, and she has large bilateral knee effusions. Her MTPs are thickened and tender when squeezed.
                    1. List your differential diagnosis for Pauline’s problem
                      • Rheumatoid Arthritis
                      • Seronegative Arthritis
                        • Psoriatic Arthritis
                      • Connective Tissue Disease
                        • Sjogren’s Syndrome
                        • Systemic Lupus Erythematosus
                        • Mixed Connective Tissue Disease
                        • Scleroderma
                        • Polymyositis.
                      • Infectious Arthritis → eg. associated with Hep B & C.
                    2. Cite those features which strongly support one of those diagnoses.
                      • The most likely diagnosis is Rheumatoid Arthritis for the following reasons:
                        • Rheumatoid arthritis is more common in women (2.5:1) and tends to occur between the ages of 30 to 50.
                        • The arthritis began as an inflammatory oligoarthritis and has progressed in an additive fashion to a polyarthritis which is a typical onset for rheumatoid arthritis. Prolonged morning stiffness, swollen joints, loss of energy, and a decline in day to day functioning are key features of this inflammatory polyarthritis.
                        • The pattern of joint involvement is typical for rheumatoid arthritis being symmetric involving MCPs, PIPs, wrists, shoulders, and knees.
                        • The associated dry eyes (xerophthalmia) and dry mouth (xerostomia) are typical extra-articular features of RA occurring in up to 25% of patients.
                    3. Propose and defend further investigations
                      • Bloodwork & Urinalysis
                        • As with the previous case:
                          • CBC, ESR, CRP.
                          • Creatinine.
                          • AST, ALT, ALP, albumin.
                          • Hepatitis B & C serology.
                        • TSH → rule out other causes of fatigue.
                        • RF → should be positive.
                        • ANA → fairly non-specific, but can help rule out causes.
                        • ENA → Extractable Nuclear Antigen Antibody Panel; look for Abs associated with Sjogren’s.
                      • Radiographs:
                        • As before, image the involved joints to get a baseline and to follow joint damage.
                    4. Outline an immediate management plan for the most likely diagnosis.
                      • Refer to a rheumatologist; as mentioned today, will likely have aggressive early treatment with DMARDs.
                      • Non-pharmacologic → treatment, PT, OT.
                      • Pharmacologic:
                        • NSAIDs → for symptomatic relief, doesn’t change disease progression.
                        • Corticosteroids → to reduce inflammation and increase function.
                        • DMARDs → aggressive therapy early on to try and alter disease course and minimize joint damage.
                          • Triple Therapy → Methotrexate, Sulfasalazine, Hydroxychloroquine.
                        • Biologics
                          • TNF-α antagonists are used first (Etanercept, Infliximab, Adalimumab, etc).
                          • If those fails, non TNF-α antagonists are used (Rituximab, Tocilizumab, Abatacept, etc).

                    Patient #3

                    Patient History

                    • Identifying Data → Ana is a 17-year-old high school student living at home with her parents.
                    • Presenting Problem → Multiple complaints including joint pain, chest pain, and a rash.
                    • Past Medical History → Ana has otherwise been healthy.
                    • Current Medications → Occasional Ibuprofen (Advil®, Motrin®) for joint pain and minocycline for acne.
                    • Allergies → None.
                    • History of Presenting Illness → Over the past two weeks Ana has had almost constant severe right-sided chest pain, aggravated by movement and deep breathing. For somewhat longer – three months – she has complained of painful stiff fingers, wrists, and knees. Acetaminophen and Ibuprofen haven’t been of much help. She has noted profound fatigue. Early this past summer she broke out in blisters on her arms and cheeks after a trip to the beach, and has avoided direct sun exposure since.
                    • Social History → Ana does not smoke and drinks occasionally with her friends.
                    • Review of Systems → Ana has experienced occasional chills but has not documented a fever. Her appetite and weight have been stable. She admits to noting an increase in hair loss (strands of hair on the pillow each morning) and more frequent mouth sores.
                    • Family History → Ana’s parents are both healthy. Her brother is healthy. There is no history of arthritis in the family.
                    • Physical Examination → Ana appears well. Her blood pressure is 110/70 and she is afebrile. She does exhibit a diffuse erythema over her malar regions, but no areas of discrete alopecia are noted. There are several raw superficial ulcerations in her mouth and on the nasal septum. Chest and cardiovascular examinations are unremarkable. Her fingers are diffusely puffy, with fusiform swelling about the PIP joints of both hands. She cannot make a tight fist. Wrists are swollen, and a “bulge” effusion is detectable in both knees.

                    Malar rash = acute rash, sun exposed areas Discoid rash = chronic rash, less sun exposed areas.

                    LUPUS NEPHRITIS. RA vs Lupus, lupus is non-eroding.

                    AT LEAST 4/11 AND AT LEAST 1 OF BOTH CLINICAL AND BIOCHEMICAL.

                    Methotrexate is very effective for inflammatory arthritis But lupus can cause pancytopenia and affect kidneys, so could be exacerbated with methotrexate. So not first line.

                    Best drug is PACQUINEL. WE DON’T KNOW WHY, BUT BEST!! If fails, use stronger drug, chloroquine?

                    For sake of exam. Pacquinel. Avoid sun. With exception of lupus nephritis by itself, by biopsy, is diagnostic of lupus.

                    MINOCYCLINE CAN CAUSE LUPUS! Get her off of them. Hydralazine can too.

                    Dx by exclusion, so do blood tests to r/o other causes. ANA will likely come back positive, neither RF and ANA are specific.
