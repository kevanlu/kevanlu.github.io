                    Anatomy of the Hand

                    Andrew Zaleski Tuesday, January 27, 2015

                    A. Bones of the Hand

                    • Carpal-Metacarpal Joints (CMC) → joints between the carpal and metacarpals.
                    • Metacarpal-Phalangeal Joint (MP) → joints between metacarpals and phalanges (knuckles)
                    • Interphalangeal Joints (IP) → joints between phalanges (proximal, middle, distal)
                      • The joints between the proximal and middle phalanges are the proximal interphalangeal joints (PIPs).
                      • The joints between the middle and distal phalanges are the distal interphalangeal joints (DIPs).
                    • Trapezium-1st Metacarpal Joint → different type of joint from the other MP joints, forming a saddle joint allowing for 2 planes of rotation. This is why the thumb can move in multiple planes.
                    • Carpal Bones:
                      • There are 8 carpal bones, arranged in 2 rows.
                      • Mnemonic = Some Lovers Try Positions That They Can’t Handle.
                        1. Scaphoid
                        2. Lunate
                        3. Triquetrum
                        4. Pisiform
                        5. Trapezium
                        6. Trapezoid
                        7. Capitate
                        8. Hamate
                      • S → P = row one from lateral to medial (proximal row).
                      • T → H = row two from lateral to medial (distal row).
                    • Hamate has a hook that projects onto the palmar surface to which the flexor retinaculum attaches and on the other side, the flexor retinaculum attaches to the scaphoid bone.
                    • Scaphoid only receives blood supply from one side (distally). So if there’s a fracture of the scaphoid, it’s at high risk for avascular necrosis, and poor healing.
                    • Therefore the real depth of the carpal tunnel is comprised of pisiform, triquetral, hamate, and scaphoid bones
                    • Metacarpals are numbered lateral to medial (aka Thumb = 1)
                    • Sesamoid Bones → well circumscribed round bones which variably form along the flexor pollicis longus or along other tendons within the hand.
                      • These are bones that are embedded within the tendon of a muscle. A classic example is the patella.
                      • They function as pulleys; they provide a smooth surface with which the tendon can slide on, improving force transmission.

                    Intrinsic Muscles of the Hand

                    • The extrinsic muscles of the hand (ie. the ones that go from the forearm to the hand) mediate powerful hand motions.
                    • The intrinsic muscles of the hand (ie. the ones that both originate and attach within the hand) mediate precise hand motions.
                    • The intrinsic muscles of the hand are classified into 3 groups:
                      • Palmar → the muscles in the palm itself.
                      • Thenar → the muscles in the lateral pad of the palm, that control the thumb.
                      • Hypothenar → the muscles in the medial pad of the palm, that control the pinky.
                    • All of the intrinsic muscles of the hand are innervated by the ulnar nerve (deep branch), with 4 exceptions, which are innervated by the median nerve:
                      • Lumbricals 1 and 2.
                      • Opponens Pollicis
                      • Abductor Pollicis Brevis.
                      • Flexor Pollicis Brevis.
                    • Collectively, these form the ‘LOAF’’ muscles.

                    The ‘LOAF’ Muscles

                    • The LOAF muscles are:
                      1. L → Lumbricals 1 and 2
                      2. O → Opponens Pollicis
                      3. A → Abductor Pollicis Brevis
                      4. F → Flexor Pollicis Brevis
                    • These are the only intrinsic muscles of the hand that are innervated by the median nerve
                    • Everything else is innervated by branches of the ulnar nerve.

                    B. Intrinsic Palmar Muscles of the Hand

                    • Palmar Muscles → the muscles of the palm itself. Grouped into 3 sets:
                      1. Dorsal Interossei (x4)
                      2. Palmar Interossei (x3)
                      3. Lumbricals (x4)

                    1. Dorsal Interossei (DAB) x4

                    • O → sides of metacarpals of all 4 intermetacarpal spaces
                    • I → ipsilateral proximal phalanges
                    • A → Abduction of digits 2, 3, and 4 (the abductor digitis minimi does the pinky).
                    • N → Deep branch of ulnar nerve (C8, T1)
                    • Clinical → bilateral on middle finger, all bipennate muscles
                    • Note: The middle finger has a dorsal interossei on both sides; hence the ring and index fingers abduct away from the middle finger.

                    2. Palmar Interossei (PAD) x 3

                    • O → single metacarpals in medial three intermetacarpal spaces
                    • I → proximal phalanx and extensor expansion
                    • A → Adduction of fingers (digits 2, 4, 5) towards middle finger.
                    • N → Deep branch of ulnar nerve (C8, T1)
                    • Clinical → all unipennate muscles.
                    • Note: The middle finger has no palmar interossei; instead (as mentioned before), it has 2 dorsal interossei.
                      • Also, the pinky has a palmar interossei, but not a dorsal interossei.

                    3. Lumbricals*

                    • O → Flexor digitorum profundus
                    • I → Extensor expansion
                    • A → Flexion at MCP and Extension at IP
                    • N → 1/2 median nerve, 3/4 ulnar nerve
                      • Recall that Lumbricals 1 & 2 were part of the LOAF muscles.
                    • Clinical → digits 2 and 3 are unipennate, digits 4 and 5 are bipennate
                    • Note → there are 4 lumbricals, lumbricals 1 is attached to the 2nd digit, lumbricals 2 is attached to the 3rd digit, etc.
                      • Since the muscles originate on the palmar surface, and go past the metacarpophalygeal joint, they cause flexion at the MCP joint.
                      • Since the muscles attach to the dorsal surface, on the extensor expansion, they actually cause extension of the IP joints.
                      • Because the muscles wrap around from the palmar to dorsal side, they mediate hand motion as shown to the right.

                    DAB vs PAD

                    • DAB → Dorsal ABduct (orange on diagram).
                    • PAD → Palmar ADduct (pink on diagram).
                    • The middle finger has 2 dorsal interossei and no palmar interossei.

                    C. Intrinsic Thenar Muscles of the Hand

                    • Thenar Muscles → a set of 4 muscles that form the lateral pad on your palm; these all control the motion of the thumb.
                      1. Opponens Pollicis
                      2. Abductor Pollicis Brevis
                      3. Flexor Pollicis Brevis
                      4. Adductor Pollicis.
                    • The first 3 were LOAF muscles (innervated by the median nerve).
                      • More specifically, it’s the recurrent branch of the median nerve.

                    1. Abductor Pollicis Brevis*

                    • O → Flexor retinaculum and tubercles of scaphoid and trapezium
                    • I → Lateral base of proximal phalanx of thumb
                    • A → ABDUCTS thumb and helps oppose it
                    • N → Recurrent branch of median nerve

                    2. Flexor Pollicis Brevis*

                    • O → Flexor retinaculum and tubercles of scaphoid and trapezium
                    • I → lateral base of proximal phalanx of thumb
                    • A → Flexes thumb
                    • N → Recurrent branch of median nerve

                    3. Opponens Pollicis*

                    • O → Flexor retinaculum and tubercles of scaphoid and trapezium
                    • I → Lateral side of 1st metacarpal
                    • A → Brings thumb laterally to oppose
                    • N → Recurrent branch of median nerve

                    4. Adductor Pollicis

                    • O → Differs depending on the head.
                      • Oblique Head: base of 2nd and 3rd metacarpals, capitate, and adjacent carpals
                      • Transverse Head: anterior of 3rd metacarpals
                    • I → medial side of proximal phalanx of thumb
                    • A → draws 1st metacarpal laterally to oppose and adduct thumb
                    • N → Deep branch of Ulnar Nerve (C8, T1)
                    • Note: Often considered as the 4th palmar interosseous (since it ADducts the thumb; PAD).

                    Note

                    • *: LOAF Muscles
                    • Opponens pollicis → deep to, and in the middle of both APB and FPB.
                    • Abductor pollics brevis → more lateral.
                    • Flexor pollicis brevis → more medial.

                    D. Intrinsic Hypothenar Muscles of the Hand

                    • Hypothenar Muscles → a set of 3 muscles that form the medial pad on your palm; these all control the motion of the pinky finger.
                      1. Abductor Digiti Minimi
                      2. Opponens Digiti Minimi
                      3. Flexor Digiti Minimi Brevis

                    1. Abductor Digiti Minimi

                    • O → Pisiform
                    • I → medial side of proximal phalanx of 5th digit
                    • A → Abduction of little finger
                    • N → Deep branch of ulnar nerve

                    2. Opponens Digiti Minimi

                    • O → Hook of hamate and flexor retinaculum
                    • I → medial border of 5th metacarpal
                    • A → opposition; draws 5th metacarpal anteriorly and rotates it, opposes pinky and thumb
                    • N → Deep branch of Ulnar Nerve (C8, T1)

                    Note that brevis is usually included to differentiate a muscle from a longus muscle of the same name. The flexor digiti minimi longus, however, is not found in the typical human, but instead is a rare anatomical variation.

                    3. Flexor Digiti Minimi Brevis

                    • O → Hook of hamate and flexor retinaculum
                    • I → medial base of proximal phalanx of 5th digit
                    • A → flexion of proximal phalanx of 5th digit
                    • N → Deep branch of Ulnar Nerve (C8, T1)
                    • Notes: Opposes the action of the extensor digiti minimi (superficial muscle of the posterior forearm).

                    E. Fascial Layers of the Hand

                    • Flexor Retinaculum → facial band between the hamate and scaphoid carpal bones.
                    • Flexor Synovial Sheaths → covers the long flexor tendons of the hand.
                      • Digits 2-4 have separate distinct sheaths
                      • Digit 1 has separate distinct sheath that goes through the carpal tunnel into the forearm
                      • Digit 5 has sheath which connects to the sheath of the lower half of the long tendons and continues into the forearm via the carpal tunnel
                      • Clinical → Tenosynovitis infections in either the 1st and 5th digit can track back up into the forearm since they’re continuous with spaces in the forearm (see right). However, this is not the case for digits 2-4, because the sheaths are separate.
                    • Fibrous Flexor Sheath → surrounds IP joints for digits 2-5 (shown in green).
                    • Extensor Expansion (Extensor Hood) → covers the extensor surfaces of the MP and IP joints; but has openings that allow for insertion of intrinsic and extrinsic extensor muscles.

                    F. Fascial Compartments of the Hand

                    • Palmar Aponeurosis (central compartment) → connected to the palmaris longus tendon located in the middle of the palm,
                      • Clinical → Dupuytren’s Contracture: palmar aponeurosis undergoes thickening leading to contracture, starting with nodules and cords in the skin.
                      • Flexure of 4th and 5th digits is most common, accompanied by dimpling of the skin near palmar crease of affected digits. Often impairs function of hand.
                    • Thenar Compartment → formed by lateral septum (thumb)
                    • Hypothenar Compartment → formed by medial septum (5th digit)

                    G. Arterial Supply of the Hand

                    • Hand receives arterial supply from ulnar (medial) and radial (lateral) arteries.
                    • Branches of Radial Artery:
                      1. Princeps Pollicis Artery → pierces adductor pollicis to supply thenar muscles.
                      2. Radialis Indicis Artery → supplies 2nd digit (index finger) with blood
                      3. Deep Palmar Arch → mainly supplied by radial artery but does join deep branch of ulnar artery to form a combined atch.
                    • Ulnar artery comprises the majority of the superficial palmar arch
                    • Common Digital Arteries branch off the superficial palmar arch and anastomose with Palmar Metacarpal Arteries off the deep palmar arch to form Proper Digital Arteries.
                      • Hence, each of the arteries to the fingers (the ‘proper’ digital arteries) have a dual supply from both the radial and ulnar artery, via the deep and superficial palmar arches respectively.
                    • Proper Digital Arteries travel up each side of the digits 2-5, meeting at the tip to supply the fingers with blood

                    H. Venous Drainage of the Hand

                    • Highly variable from person to person.
                    • Medially blood drains into the Basillic Vein.
                    • Laterally blood drains into the Cephalic Vein.
                    • Both the basillic and cephalic veins drain into the axillary vein.
                    • Median Cubital Vein → communication vein between cephalic and basillic veins.
                    • Median Antebrachial Vein → drains the forearm.
                    • Brachial Vein → drains upper arm.

                    I. Innervation of the Hand

                    • Motor Innervation
                      • Ulnar Nerve → all intrinsic muscles of the hand except for LOAF muscles
                      • Median Nerve → LOAF muscles
                      • Extrinsic Muscle innervation see forearm lecture:
                        • Recall that flexors are generally innervated by the median nerve (except for the flexor carpi ulnaris and half of the flexor digitorum profundus).
                        • Recall that extensors are all innervated by the radial nerve (most via the posterior interosseous nerve, except for the extensor carpi radialis longus, which is via the radial directly.
                    • Sensory Innervation
                      • Nerve Testing: best spots on hand for testing different nerve function.
                        • In spite of the overlap and variation in sensory innervation, there are certain areas in the hand where cutaneous innervation is fairly consistent; these are known as the autonomous zones.
                      • These consist of the following:
                        • Median Nerve → index finger (2nd digit)
                        • Ulnar Nerve → pinky finger (5th digit)
                        • Radial Nerve → web of thumb (between 1st and 2nd digit)

                    J. Movements of the Hand

                    • Flexion → bending of the thumb to the medial palm
                    • Extension → movement of the thumb laterally in the plane of the palm
                    • Adduction → movement of thumb towards side of palm
                    • Abduction → movement of thumb perpendicular to the plane of the palm
                    • Opposition → touch pulp of thumb to pulp of other digits (pulp = pad of fingers)
