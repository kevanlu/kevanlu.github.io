            Workplace Injuries and WSIB

            Kevan Lu Tuesday, January 27, 2015

            Lecture Outline

            • Overview of the WSIB
            • Concepts and strategies
              • For workplace injuries and WSIB claims
                • Assessment → Is a condition work-related?
                • Treatment → Is a treatment covered?
                • Functional abilities → Is return to work possible?
            • Case example

            What is the WSIB?

            • WSIB → Workplace Safety & Insurance Board.
              • Founded in 1914 by the Ontario government.
              • Crown corporation; owned by the government.
              • Functions like an insurance company; business pay money to cover their employees for work-related injuries.
            • Provides disability insurance for workplace injuries and illnesses
              • Used by businesses and their employees.
              • Benefits that the WSIB offers include income replacement, health care, and retraining.
              • Funded entirely by employer premiums.
                • The premiums that an employer has to pay is determined by its claims statistics; much like with personal insurance, if you have a lot of costly injuries, your premiums will go up.
              • The WSIB has a no fault system, and was built on an ‘historic compromise’ between workers and employees:
                • Workers compromise by giving up the right to sue, even if the accident was clearly the fault of the employer. Payment is instead be determined by the WSIB.
                • Employers compromise by giving up the right to opt out, so all employers must pay money to the WSIB.
                • There are a few exceptions where employers are allowed to opt out: banks, doctors offices, hairstylists (?), taxidermy (??), etc.

            The Claims Process

            • With every claim, there are 2 key parties: the worker and the employer.
              • Both the worker and the employer have a duty to report a workplace injury to the WSIB.
            • Where to clinicians play a role then?
              • Clinicians have a duty to provide the clinical information that’s necessary to make a decision on the claim that’s being made.
              • The WSIB case manager makes decisions based on the information provided.
            • Both employees and employers have a right to appeal
              • There’s a 3-stage appeals process that’s available to them.

            WSIB Forms

            • 3 of the common forms that are used by the WSIB are:
              • Form 8 (F8) → this is the one that health professionals fill out.
              • Functional Abilities Form (FAF) → this is the one that workers/employers fill out.
              • Progress Report (F26) → this is the one that the WSIB fills out.
            • Since we’re discussing the WSIB from the perspective of a health professional, we’ll be focusing only on the Form 8.
            • With respect to the worker/employer initiated FAF form:
              • The employer must report a work-related accident within 3 days.
              • The worker must report a work-related accident within 3 months.
                • This deadline is more flexible because it may take longer for the injury to manifest itself.
            • As healthcare professionals, we cannot report a workplace accident without the permission of the worker.
              • There’s a spot on the Form 8 where the patient must sign to indicate consent to report.

            The Form 8

            What is the Form 8?

            • The Form 8 provides clinical information in 5 general areas:
              1. Hx → History.
              2. Px → Physical findings.
              3. Dx → Diagnosis.
              4. Tx → Treatment plan.
              5. Fx → Functional abilities.
            • For today’s presentation, we’ll be focusing on the Hx and Fx components of the Form 8.

            History

            • Looks at the question ‘is a condition work related?’.
            • With the history, you should include these 2 components:
              • Mechanism of injury
              • Risk factors
            • Mechanism of Injury
              • What was happening to the body at the time of the injury?
              • With respect to MSK injuries (the bulk of workplace injuries), there are 2 basic mechanisms: a single event, or prolonged exposure.
                • Single event → position, angle, force, weight, timing, speed, distance, constraints, etc.
                • Prolonged exposure → additional temporal factors such as duration, frequency, repetition, etc.
              • You should also note the presence of vulnerable movements; examples include:
                • Neck → acceleration/deceleration.
                • Shoulder → overhead work.
                • Wrist → prolonged flexion/gripping.
                • Low back → heavy lifting with flexion.
                • Knee → twisting movements.
            • Risk Factors:
              • Stratify these risk factors into occupational and non-occupational ones.
                • Non-occupational → smoking, alcohol, diabetes, family history, gender, infection, menopause, obesity, etc.
                • Occupational → prolonged flexion, repetitive movement, vibration, etc.

            Functional Abilities

            • Looks at the question ‘is return to work possible?’.
              • Can we match the functional abilities of the worker with the demands with a safe and appropriate work option?
              • With impaired functional abilities, matching may involve modifying the type of work, or retraining the worker for a new job.
            • With the assessment of functional abilities, you should include these 2 components:
              • Specifying abilities.
              • Discussing recovery.
            • Specifying Abilities
              • What can the patient do? → what activities are safe and appropriate for the worker to attempt, on a trial basis?
              • What can’t the patient do? → what activities are unsafe or contraindicated from a medical point of view? An example of how to specify the abilities of the patient. Note that we note things that the patient can do, as well as what they can't do. Things are also described in a very general fashion.
            • Discussing Recovery
              • Foster a recovery mindset; highlight what the patient can do, not what they can’t.
                • Discuss with them the benefits of staying active.
                • Discuss the benefits of staying connected with work.
                • Set expectations and timeframes for improvement.
              • Help the patient find a way to stay at work:
                • Patients that stay at work generally have faster, more complete recoveries; there’s an important psychosocial impact in ‘having a purpose’.
              • Going back to work early also makes it less likely that you’ll drop out of the workforce:
                • Patients that are away from work for over 6 months only have a 50% chance of ever working again.
                • Patients that are away from work for over 12 months only have a 25% chance of ever working again.
                • Patients that are away from work for over 2 years have an almost 0% chance of ever working again.
              • Help the patient stay positive and avoid the vicious cycle.
                1. Patient has symptoms
                2. This leads to specific mindset
                3. This drives behaviour that exacerbates the symptoms.
              • Avoid magnifying problems; this can often change an impairment to a disability.
                • Impairment → a change or problem with the body’s structure or function (physical or mental).
                • Disability → an inability to perform important activities and roles in one’s life, eg. a loss of earning capacity.
