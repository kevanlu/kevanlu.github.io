            Kevan Lu Thursday, January 29, 2015

            Fractures and Joint Injuries Notes

            • See Fractures and Joint Injuries Notes.

            Lecture Objectives

            • Understand basic principles of fracture management.
            • Be able to describe a fracture.
            • Understand the two types of fracture healing.
            • Know a bit about complications of fractures and fracture management.

            Introduction to Fractures

            • Fracture → Where so much pressure is applied to the bone that it undergoes deformation.
            • Healed fracture → Bone that can again withstand physiological force without deformation.
            • Describing fractures is important, as it gives you important information on the nature of the injury. It helps:
              • Classify the fracture.
              • Guide treatment.
              • Provide a prognosis for the injury.

            Objective

            • Be able to describe a fracture.

            What do you Include in a Fracture Description?

            • One common (and simple) classification of fractures is the ABC Classification:
              • A → Almost broken.
              • B → Broken.
              • C → Comin’ through the skin.
            • When describing a fracture more formally, characteristics that should be included are:
              1. Location
              2. Joint Surface Involvement
              3. Displacement
              4. Complexity
              5. Fracture Pattern/Mechanism
              6. Soft tissue Injury
              7. Bone Quality
              8. Unique Pediatric Fractures
            1. Location:
              • Where is the fracture on the bone?
                • Diaphysis → shaft of the bone.
                • Metaphysis → widened area of the bone. It can be circumscribed by drawing a square that just covers the widest part of the bone (see right).
                • Physis → in children only, this is the growth plate.
                • Epiphysis → end of the bone.
            2. Joint Surface Involvement
              • Does the fracture involve the surface of the joint or not?
              • If it does involve the joint surface, then a reduction must be more precise to preserve function.
            3. Displacement
              • Has the bone moved or not?
              • If there is more than 1 fragment, by convention, we describe displacement of the distal fragment.
              • For instance, an anterior displacement of the fracture means that the distal fragment has displaced anteriorly to the proximal one.
            4. Complexity
              • How many fragments are there?
                • Single/non-comminuted → 1 fracture point, 2 pieces.
                • Comminuted → >2 pieces.
              • The higher the energy of the fracture, the more likely that it will be comminuted.
            5. Fracture Pattern/Mechanism
              • Spiral Fractures → produced by torsional loads.
              • Transverse Fractures → produced by bending loads.
              • Wedge Fractures → produced by bending loads.
              • Comminuted Fractures → produced by axial loads.
              • Avulsion Fractures → Produced by traction loads.
            6. Soft Tissue Injury
              • Closed fractures → the soft tissue envelope is intact with no environmental contamination.
              • Open fractures → the soft tissue envelope is disrupted and the internal zone of injury has been contaminated by the external environment.
                • Open fractures have higher risks of complications (infections, nonunion) and poorer functional outcomes.
                • They require urgent surgical management to decontaminate the open wounds
            7. Bone Quality
              • A bone fracture can be pathologic or non-pathologic:
                • Non-pathologic → fracture of a healthy bone.
                • Pathologic → fracture of an abnormal bone.
              • Pathologic bone problems include osteoporosis, bone cysts, osteomalacia, Paget’s, etc.
            8. Pediatric Features
              • Pediatric bone is softer and less brittle than adult bone.
              • As a result, instead of cracking, pediatric bone will often bend.
              • This results in a buckle or greenstick fracture; this is analogous to trying to break a ‘green branch’ (it bends rather than breaks).
              • Furthermore, if there’s physeal involvement (growth plate), then growth arrest may occur.

            Objective

            • Understand the two types of fracture healing.

            Fracture Healing

            • One important factor that guides your approach to treatment is whether you’re aiming for primary or secondary bone healing:
              1. Primary → bone remodelling occurs as if it was never broken.
                • Here, the normal bone biology is uninterrupted, and remodelling occurs right over the fracture.
                • This is never seen without surgical intervention.
              2. Secondary → bone will grow around the fracture, via a multistage inflammatory mediated healing process.
                • This is what happens with the majority of fracture healing.
                • Surgery is not necessary for secondary healing.
            • Secondary healing is faster, but primary healing gives you better functional results.
              • Hence, for joint injuries, primary healing is the goal.
              • With bone fractures, we often use secondary healing instead.
              • You can always tell if a repair is aiming for primary vs secondary healing:
                • If you see screws to maintain the stability of the reduction, it’s primary.
                • If you see nails, it’s secondary.

            Types of Fracture Healing

            Primary Bone Healing Secondary Bone Healing
            Mechanism Simple osteon remodelling An inflammatory process
            Used in Fractures with joint involvement Less complex bone fractures
            Advantages
            • Better results (no bone scar)
            • No fracture callus.
            • Does not require surgery; can be done with casting
            • Can still have a little bit of movement across the fracture site
            Disadvantages
            • Needs surgery
            • Requires very precise anatomic reduction
            • Requires absolute stability
            • Will result in fracture callus in the short term
            • Even long term, will still have a fracture line

            Objective

            • Understand basic principles of fracture management.

            Fracture Treatment

            1. PHYSICAL EXAM AND INVESTIGATIONS
              • Physical Examination
                • Soft tissue injuries.
                • Neurologic examination → it’s particularly important to do this before and after a reduction.
                • Vascular examination → pulses and perfusion.
              • Investigations
                • Start with plain radiographs (XRs)
                  • Image the entire bone.
                  • Include the joint above, and the below.
                  • Have multiple, perpendicular views.
                • CT Scan → usually done after XRs, especially if the joint is involved.
                • MRI → usually not done with fractures due to cost/availability.
                  • The exception is with suspected spinal fractures, or when you want to look for soft tissue injury.
            2. TREATMENT
              • Stabilization
                • Pain medications.
                • Tetanus prophylaxis if open fracture or if there are significant lacerations.
              • Reduction
                • Closed → without surgery.
                • Open → with surgery.
                • An open approach is only done if the fracture involves the joint, or if it’s in a difficult to manipulate location (eg. the hip).
              • Fixation
                • Internal → External fixators, intramedullary devices, plates and screws.
                • External → Casts, splints, braces, (traction).
              • Dealing with open fractures
                • Open fractures are graded as:
                  1. Grade 1
                  2. Grade 2
                  3. Grade 3 → 3A (no flap), 3B (flap), 3C (vascular injury).
                • When dealing with an open fracture, the goal is to sterilize the area of injury and prevent infection:
                  • Tetanus prophylaxis.
                  • Antibiotics.
                  • Debridement and irrigation.
                  • Early closure of the soft tissue envelope.
            3. REHABILITATION
              • Early motion → reduces DVT risk and improves functional outcomes.
              • Protected weightbearing.
              • Physiotherapy.
              • Occupational therapy.

            Objective

            • Know a bit about complications of fractures and fracture management.

            1. Thromboembolic Disease

            • Risk factors → Virchow’s Triad:
              • Hypercoagulability
              • Vascular Injury
              • Stasis.
            • In other words, the typical orthopedic trauma patient.
            • Indications for DVT Prophylaxis:
              • Multiple injury.
              • Spinal cord injury.
              • Head injury.
              • Lower extremity or pelvic fx.
              • Older patient.
              • Prolonged surgery or wait for surgery.
            • DVT Prophylaxis:
              • Anticoagulation → Aspirin, LMWH, warfarin.

            2. Compartment Syndrome

            • Compartment Syndrome → Excessive compartment pressure begins to cut off blood flow in compartment.
            • Causes damage, myonecrosis, and nerve injury.
            • Most commonly caused by internal pressure (eg. due to blood leaking into a fascial comparment); less commonly caused by external pressure.
            • The tissues most at risk are the metabolically active ones:
              • Nerve.
              • Muscle.
            • Easily treatable, but the consequences are very difficult to treat.
              • Transient muscle and nerve injury can occur with less than 2 hours of ischemia.
              • Permanent nerve and muscle injury occurs after 6 hours!
            • Clinical Signs:
              • Early → pain out of proportion to the injury, or with passive stretching of the muscles in the compartment.
                • This can obviously be difficult to distinguish between simple swelling sometimes.
                • You can sometimes palpate for compartment ‘firmness’.
                • As soon as you see nerve issues then this is compartment syndrome until proven otherwise.
              • Middle → paresthesia.
              • Late → paralysis.
              • Very late → loss of pulses.
                • Once you lose your pulses, the tissue is probably dead.
            • What do you do if you’re not sure if it’s compartment syndrome?
              • Compartment pressure measurements with an arterial line.
              • Indications:
                • For patients who are uncooperative.
                • Equivocal clinical findings.
              • Positive if: ICP → Intra-compartment pressure. So if the difference between the diastolic blood pressure and the pressure in the compartment is less than 30, then that’s a positive finding.
                • or
                • .
            • Treatment of Compartment Syndrome:
              • Elevate limb.
              • Remove compressive dressings/casts.
              • Cool the limb and monitor closely.
                • Urgent fasciotomy.

            Remember

            • As soon as you see nerve problems, it’s compartment syndrome until proven otherwise.
            • Permanent nerve and muscle damage will occur after 6 hours of ischemia.

            3. Nonunion

            • Nonunion → failure of a fracture to heal in a period of time twice the time expected for that fracture to heal.
            • Classification:
              1. Atrophic → there’s no healing. This is a biologic problem.
              2. Oligotrophic → there’s little healing.
              3. Hypertrophic → there’s enough healing, but the fracture just can’t finish healing. This is a mechanical problem; it needs stabilization (see right).
              4. Infected → an infection is preventing the fracture from healing.
            • Treatment
              • Eradication infection, if any.
              • Stabilize the fracture if it’s a mechanical problem.
              • Biologic activation if it’s a biological problem.

            4. Malunion

            • Malunion → improper healing of the fracture.
              • Seen in patients that didn’t seek medical attention, or if ORIF failed.
            • Evalulating Malunion (LART)
              • L → Length
              • A → Angulation
              • R → Rotation
              • T → Translation
            • These are the 4 planes in which malunion can occur.

            5. Osteonecrosis

            • Osteonecrosis → where the bone dies due to lack of blood supply.
              • Complication that’s unique to orthopedics.
            • Most common in bones with an intrinsic poor blood supply, that are prone to dislocation:
              • Femoral head.
              • Talus.
              • Scaphoid.
