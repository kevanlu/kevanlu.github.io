              MSK Infections

              Kevan Lu Wednesday, January 28, 2015

              MSK Infections Notes

              • See MSK Infections Notes.

              Lecture Objectives

              1. Know the differential diagnosis of monoarthritis.
              2. Understand the presentations of septic arthritis, cellulitis and crystal arthropathy.
              3. Outline the initial management of septic arthritis.
              4. Understand the three main variants of osteomyelitis.
              5. Know the diagnostic features and management of osteomyelitis.
              6. Outline the presentations and management of pyogenic spondylodiscitis.

              Important Points

              • All of the material is in the lecture notes linked above.
                • However, the important points are listed below.

              1. Septic Arthritis

              1. When a patient presents with monoarthritis, you HAVE to rule out septic arthritis.
                • If it’s not treated rapidly, the joint will be destroyed!
                • It’s more likely to be septic arthritis is the patient has persistent bacteremia, or they have an anatomic abnormality (eg. damaged/prosthetic joint).
              2. The classical presentation of septic arthritis is a sudden onset of monoarthritis, typically in a weight bearing joint.
                • Migratory polyarthralgia (where multiple joints hurt, and the aching joints change over time) make it less likely to be septic arthritis.
              3. DDx of monoarthritis + erythema:
                1. Cellulitis → particularly if ROM near normal.
                  • This makes sense, since the infection is superficial.
                2. Septic Arthritis → ROM is never normal.
                  • This also makes sense, since the infection involves the joints.
                  • If pain at rest, the Dx is septic arthritis until proven otherwise!!
                3. Crystal Arthropathy (eg. gout) → 90% male, usually >30.
                  • Can be confusing; the only way to r/o gout is to aspirate the joint fluid and look for crystals.
              4. Septic arthritis has 2 classic patterns, depending on if its gonoococcal or nongonococcal:
                • Pattern 1 → sudden onset in older patient, no skin eruption → nongonococcal septic arthritis (or crystals).
                • Pattern 2 → prodrome, generalized skin eruption in younger patient (especially if pregnant or menstruating) → gonococcal septic arthritis.
                • However, there are many presentations that don’t fit into either pattern, so don’t box yourself in.
              5. It’s important to separate gonococcal from nongonococcal causes because Neisseria gonorrhoeae often moves into the joints.
                • Neisseria gonorrhoeae used to be, by far, the most common cause of septic arthritis.
                • However, when HIV emerged in the late 20th century, the amount of casual sex dropped significantly, leading to a drop in the number of Neisseria infections.
                  • Now, the most common cause is probably Staph. aureus (ie. a non-gonococcal cause).
                • Nonetheless, they can still be easily identified by falling into the pattern 2 type of behaviour.
              6. Joint aspiration with WBC > 50,000/µL is suggestive of septic arthritis
                • Also do a CBC, blood cultures.
                • Also assess the aspirate with a gram stain, culture, and look for crystals.
                • If pattern 2 (gonococcal), screen for STIs; urethral, cervical, rectal swabs.

              2. Osteomyelitis

              1. Osteomyelitis = infection in a bone.
                • As opposed to a joint infection (septic arthritis) as discussed before.
                • Osteomyelitis has 3 main etiologies:
                  1. Hematogenous Osteomyelitis → spread of bacteria through the bloodstream.
                  2. From a contiguous focus → local spread.
                  3. Secondary to vascular insufficiency → bacterial growth due to blood stasis and no influx of immunologic factors.
                • Hematogenous osteomyelitis is most comonly seen in the elderly, IDVUs, or with sickle cell.
                  • Here, systemic signs (high fever, chills) are common.
                • From a contiguous focus is most commonly seen with penetrating wounds, compound fractures, and post ORIF.
                  • Here, systemic signs are uncommon, because the open wound helps drain the abscess.
                • Secondary to vascular insufficiency is most commonly seen with diabetes.
              2. Bone scans, MRI, and CT are more sensitive that XR for Osteomyelitis
                • XR early on are typically negative.
                • Bone scans are quite good for osteomyelitis, especially for early infections where XR is negative. The bone will ‘light-up’.
              3. Bone biopsies should be done for C&S unless there’s a positive blood culture
                • If you have a blood culture result, you don’t need the bone biopsy to determine the treatment.
                • However, you cannot substitute a bone biopsy with a wound culture because there are so many other obfuscating organisms there.
                • Guessing the antibiotic to use is dangerous; oftentimes the patient will improve, but it won’t completely resolve. This then becomes much harder to treat.
              4. Treatment is as follows:
                1. Surgical debridement of necrotic bone, with removal of all sequestra (eg. hardware, cement).
                2. 4-6 weeks of antimicrobial therapy, based on bone biopsy C&S or blood C&S.
                3. Follow ESR until resolution.
              5. ESR can be used to follow response to treatment
                • As the ESR falls, you know that your Abx treatment is working.
                • If the patient is immunocompetent, and doesn’t have any vascular insufficiency, prognosis is very good.

              3. Pyogenic Spondylodiscitis

              1. Pyogenic Spondylodiscitis → basically infection of the vertebral discs themselves, so this is a form of vertebral osteomyelitis.
                • In adults, this is either hematogenous, or iatrogenic (eg. after spine surgery).
                • In children, spontaneous infection sometimes occurs.
              2. Pyogenic Spondylodiscitis is difficult to catch early on
                • Symptoms are non-specific: pain is most common, with fever and radicular symptoms in only 50% of patients.
                • Can often be mistaken for DDD, cancer, herniation, or inflammatory diseases.
              3. Treatment is similar to general osteomyelitis
                • Culture + 6 weeks of antimicrobial therapy + spinal immobilization.
                • With immobilization, you need DVT prophylaxis to prevent a possible PE.
                • Surgery is done if the patient fails to respond to conservative Tx, if there’s progressive neurological deficits, or significant vertebral damage.
