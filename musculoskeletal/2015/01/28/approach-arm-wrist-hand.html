            Approach to the Arm, Wrist & Hand

            Kevan Lu Wednesday, January 28, 2015

            Arm, Wrist & Hand Notes

            • See Arm, Wrist & Hand Notes.

            Lecture Objectives

            • To provide a rational approach to hand and wrist disorders.
            • To discuss common hand and wrist problems including:
              • Distal radius fractures.
              • Scaphoid fractures.
              • Infections.

            General Approach

            • Depending on the timeframe of the injury, different etiologies for the injury are suspected.
            • Acute Problems:
              • Fractures
              • Dislocations
              • Infections
            • Chronic Problems:
              • Tendonosis
              • Arthrosis
              • Nerve compression

            Distal Radius Fractures

            • Very common injury.
            • Typically arises from FOOSH → Falling on an Outstretched Hand.
            • General findings → localized pain, with possible associated injuries:
              • Ligament tear.
              • Scaphoid fracture.
            • The forearm will have poor function if the anatomy is not restored.

            Imaging of Distal Radius Fractures

            • What X-Rays would you order for a suspected radius fracture?
              1. PA view.
              2. Lateral view.
            • There are 3 parameters you need to assess:
              1. Radial length → how long is the radius versus the ulna?
              2. Articular angle → with lateral view.
              3. Articular surface

            1. Radial Length

            • The radius should be a little longer than the ulna normally.
            • If this isn’t the case, then the radius is probably fractured, and has collapsed.
            • This can be assessed with the ulnar variance, which assesses the relative positions of the surfaces of the radius and ulna where they articulate.
            • This can either be neutral, positive, or negative:
              • Neutral → both the ulnar and radial articular surfaces are at the same level.
              • Positive → ulna projects more distally.
              • Negative → ulna projects more proximally.
            • If there’s a significant difference in the ulnar variance in one wrist versus the contralateral one, it’s more suspicious for a fracture.
            • If the radius collapses enough, over time there may be an ulnar impaction syndrome:
              • This is where the relative length of the ulna allows it to impact against the bones and ligaments of the carpus.

              Note the lines on the XR on the left. These reflect the relative positions of the ulna and radius where they articulate; the ulnar variance. On the right, notice the presence of an ulnar impaction syndrome.

            2. Articular Angle

            • The angle at which the radius meets the carpus should not be crooked.
              • Normally, the articular surface should be angled towards the palm (see the black lines in the image to the right).
              • More specifically, this is considered 12° of palmar flexion on a lateral XR.
              • 0° of palmar flexion is considered to be ‘neutral’.
              • An abnormally finding is anything past 0° of dorsiflexion.

            • What happens if the articular surface is at an angle?
              • The motion of the distal radio-ulnar joint is altered.
              • Because an abnormal angle increases the gap between the two bones it makes it harder to rotate the radius with respect to the ulna.
              • This restricts forearm rotation.

            Remember

            • The normal articular angle with a lateral XR is 12° of palmar flexion.
            • Any degree of dorsiflexion is considered abnormal.

            3. Articular Surface

            • The articular surface of the radius should be smooth.
              • >1mm step deformity is associated with post traumatic arthrosis, because it results in irregular joint wear.
                • Note that arthrosis = osteoarthritis.
                • Post traumatic osteoarthritis is not the same as degenerative osteoarthritis; as the name suggests, post-trumatic OA is caused by an injury.
              • If the joint surface is too irregular, it can cause radiocarpal arthritis, which will result in pain and stiffness, and may require surgery.

            Treatment of Distal Radius Fractures

            • Treatment proceeds as follows:
              1. Adequate Analgesia
              2. Reduction
              3. Fixation
            • Adequate Analgesia → options include:
              • Hematoma block → any time you have a bone fracture, there’s a hematoma. With a hematoma block, you inject anesthetic (typically lidocaine) into the hematoma.
              • Regional anesthesia → numbing the entire arm.
              • Conscious sedation → put the patient to sleep. Often not used in adults unless larger joints are involved. For the wrist, really only used in children.
            • Reduction → restoring the normal anatomy of the fractured bones.
              1. Traction.
              2. Increase the deformity → seems counterintuitive, but the purpose of this is to help dislodge fragments.
              3. Correct the deformity
              4. Cast
              5. Xray
            • Fixation → keep the reduction in place.
              1. Internal Fixation → use implants such as screws or pins to keep the bones together from the inside.
              2. External Fixation → use casts or other external structures to keep the bones together from the outside.

            Scaphoid Fractures

            • Most common carpal bone fracture.
            • Also often seen with FOOSH.
            • Usually heals uneventfully.
            • Sometimes can be difficult to diagnose.
              • Delayed diagnosis is associated with complications.
            • General findings → swelling, localized tenderness.

            Imaging of Scaphoid Fractures

            • XR is usually sufficient, but not completely sensitive.
              • Sometimes the patient may have snuff box pain (where the waist of the scaphoid is located), but the XR is negative.
              • In these cases, you’re often treated as if it was fractured, because the risk of missing the diagnosis exceeds the risks of immobilization.
            • So if clinical suspicion is high and XRs are normal:
              • Treat as if fractured.
              • Get additional imaging → CT, MRI, bone scan.

            Treatment of Scaphoid Fractures

            • Treatment depends on if the fracture is displaced or not:
              • Undisplaced → cast until union.
                • Unlike with distal radius fractures, casting involves immobilizing the thumb as well.
              • Displaced → surgery to bring things back to their normal position.
                • Surgery is done via a volar (palmar) approach, and typically involves placing a single screw inside the bone.

            • The scaphoid bone is fairly unique in that is has a retrograde blood flow:
              • What this means is that the blood supply to the scaphoid comes distally, as opposed to proximally.
              • As a result, proximal scaphoid fractures heal more poorly; if they do heal, it takes much longer.
              • Proximal fractures also carry a risk of nonunion and osteonecrosis (ie. AVN).
            • Hence, the most common complications of scaphoid fractures are related to this retrograde blood flow:
              1. Nonunion → particularly with proximal fractures.
              2. Osteonecrosis → AVN due to lack of blood supply proximally.
              3. Late arthrosis → if the proximal region dies, then it can lead to OA.

            Hand Infections

            • The hand is anatomically predisposed to abscess formation:
              • It has many joints, all of which are subcutaneous.
              • It has a lot of discrete anatomical compartments:
                • Tendon sheaths.
                • Thenar eminence.
                • Pulp space.
                • Within the nails.
              • The key is to differentiate cellulitis from an abcess, because their treatment is different:
                • Cellulitis → superficial skin infection.
                • Abscess → deep tissue infection.
                • An abscess needs to be drained; a cellulitis does not.

            Cellulitis vs Abscess

            Cellulitis Abscess
            Superficial skin infection Deep tissue infection
            Wrist to MCP Joints Finger tips
            Dorsal surface Around the joints
            Margins are irregular Palmar surface involvement

            Examples of Joint Infections

            1. Cat Bites
              • Cat bites are more dangerous than dog bites because they more often puncture the skin, and inoculate bacteria deeper in.
              • Typically at the PIP and DIP joints.
              • Often a mixed infection.
              • Tx must cover Pasteurella multocida.
              • Broad spectrum Abx.
            2. Human ‘Bites’
              • Often seen with young men who get into fights, and teeth are punched.
              • Typically at the MCP joint.
              • Mixed flora, as with cat bites.
              • Tx must cover Eikenella corrodens.
              • Broad spectrum Abx.
            3. Paronychia
              • aka. a nail fold infection.
              • F > M, probably due to manicures being more common in women.
              • Most common organisms is S. aureus.
                • If immunocompromised, may have fungi.
            4. Felon
              • Felon → pulp space infection (pronounced fee-lon, not fell-on, like the other felon).
              • These are little abscesses of the pulps of the finger, and, like other abscesses, these require drainage.
            5. Suppurative Tenosynovitis
              • Recall that tendonitis is inflammation of the tendon, while tenosynovitis is inflammation of the tendon sheath.
              • A suppurative tenosynovitis is thus an infectious inflammation of the tendon sheaths.
                • This is almost always flexor tenosynovitis; the flexor compartment has more defined synovial sheaths compared to the extensor compartment.
                • Suppurative tenosynovitis is a surgical emergency.
              • There are 4 cardinal signs of Suppurative Tenosynovitis (Kanavel’s 4 signs): The patient’s hand remains in flexion because it increases the space within the tendon sheath, and reduces pain. Pain with extension thus occurs because it reduces this space.
                1. Swelling of the entire finger.
                2. Flexion posture.
                3. Tenderness along the tendon sheath.
                4. Intense pain during extension.
              • An example of a patient with suppurative tenosynovitis is shown below.
              • As with other abscesses, treatment involves surgical drainage (in the OR), wound care, and Abx. Note the flexion of the hand, and the swelling of the entire finger. This patient would also have tenderness along the sheaths, and pain with extension.

            Kanavel’s Four Cardinal Signs

            • Kanavel’s 4 Cardinal Signs relate to the presence of suppurative tenosynovitis.
              1. Swelling of the entire finger.
              2. Flexion posture.
              3. Tenderness along the tendon sheath.
              4. Intense pain during extension.

            Treatment of Joint Infections

            • Catch these early.
            • With all abscesses, the treatment is as follows:
              • Surgical drainage.
              • Wound care (debridement, irrigation).
              • Antibiotics (broad spectrum).
            • With a cellulitis, antibiotics are sufficient on their own.

            Applied Example

            • What is seen in the above XR?
            • A general approach to reading XRs is as follows:
              1. State what you’re looking at:
                • What views?
                • What structure is being looked at?
                • Example → this is an AP and lateral view of the right distal radius.
              2. State what’s wrong
                • Describe what you see for each film.
                • For example:
                  • “On the AP film, I see a comminuted fracture of the distal radius, and the articular surface seems to be irregular.”
                  • “On the lateral film, I see dorsal angulation of the joint surface, as well as translation.”
                • In this case, the distal radius fracture causes the wrist surface to curve like the back of a dinner fork; this is known as a dinner fork deformity.
                  • The fracture that causes a dinner fork deformity (fracture of the distal radius with dorsal displacement of the radius) is known as Colle’s fracture.
            • The patient had tingling in the thumb, index, and long fingers. What did he have?
              • Acute CTS due to a compression of the median nerve.
              • Importantly, you always have to do a neuromuscular exam before and after reduction.
              • This lets you know whether you caused an injury or not.

            Remember

            • Always do a neuromuscular exam both before and after a reduction.
