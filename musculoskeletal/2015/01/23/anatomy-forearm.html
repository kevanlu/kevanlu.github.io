                    Anatomy of the Forearm

                    Andrew Zaleski Friday, January 23, 2015

                    Introduction

                    • Arm → shoulder to elbow.
                    • Forearm → elbow to wrist.
                    • The forearm is split into 2 compartments, just like the arm.
                      1. Anterior Compartment → involved in flexion of the wrist, and (some) pronation.
                        • Almost all of the muscles of the anterior compartment are innervated by the median nerve (C5,6,7,8 T1)
                      2. Posterior Compartment → involved in extension of the wrist, and supination.
                        • Innervated by the radial nerve (C5,6,7,8 T1).
                    • The ulna is always medial to the radius, in terms of orientation.
                      • When we pronate or supinate our arm, the ulna remains fixed, while the radius moves over top it.

                    A. Bones of the Forearm

                    • Interosseous Membrane: separates the anterior and posterior compartments. Made of connective tissue, increasing the rigidity of ulna and radius together.
                    • Head of Ulna/Radius
                    • Styloid Process of Radius
                    • Styloid Process of Ulna
                    • Ulnar Notch
                    • Distal Radioulnar Joint
                    • Proximal Radioulnar Joint
                    • Clinical Note: The styloid process of radius is more distal than that of ulna. However, if there’s a fracture of either the radius or the ulna, then the relative positions of the two styloid processes can shift.

                    B. Elbow Joint

                    • Radial Annular Ligament → the radial head pivots within the annular ligament axis during movements of pronation and supination
                    • Medial (Ulnar) Collateral Ligament.
                    • Lateral (Radial) Collateral Ligament.
                    • Tendon Bicep Brachii.
                    • Olecranon Bursa → The olecranon bursa lies between the olecranon and the skin; inflammation of this is colloquially known as Student’s elbow (students spend lots of time resting on their elbows).

                    C. Anterior Muscles of the Forearm

                    • Innervation → median nerve (with a few exceptions).
                    • Function → flexion and pronation.
                    • Blood Supply → the radial and ulnar arteries.
                      • Recall that the brachial artery branches into a radial and ulnar branch at the level of the forearm.
                    • Broadly speaking, the anterior muscles of the forearm are classified into:
                      • Superficial layer.
                      • Middle layer.
                      • Deep layer.

                    Superficial Muscles

                    • 4 main muscles comprise the superficial layer of anterior muscles:
                      1. Pronator teres
                      2. Flexor carpi radialis
                      3. Flexor carpi ulnaris
                      4. Palmaris longus.
                    • Pronator teres is a wrist pronator; the rest are wrist flexors.
                    • All are innervated by the median nerve except the flexor carpi ulnaris, which is innervated by the ulnar nerve.
                    • The superficial muscles share a common attachment; the medial epicondyle. This is known as the common flexor origin (CFO).
                    • The bicipital aponeurosis covers the heads of these superficial muscles.

                    1. Pronator Teres

                    • O → medial EPICONDYLE of humerus and CORONOID process of ulna
                    • I → middle lateral surface of RADIUS
                    • A → PRONATES and FLEXES forearm at elbow
                    • N → MEDIAN NERVE (C7,8)
                    • Notes:
                      • This is the only anterior superficial muscle that is not a wrist flexor (doesn’t cross the wrist).
                      • Since it attaches on the medial side of the humerus, and then crosses laterally to the radius, when it contracts, it pronates the arm.

                    2. Flexor Carpi Radialis

                    • O → medial EPICONDYLE of humerus
                    • I → base of 2nd METACARPAL
                    • A → FLEXES and ABDUCTS the hand at wrist
                    • N → MEDIAN NERVE (C7,8)
                    • Notes:
                      • From the name (flexor carpi radialis), it flexes the wrist from the radial side.
                      • Since it attaches to the side of the hand, it doesn’t result in true flexion, but rather radial deviation (ie. abduction).

                    3. Flexor Carpi Ulnaris

                    • O: Differs depending on the head:
                      • Humeral Head: medial EPICONDYLE
                      • Ulnar Head: OLECRANON and post. ULNA
                    • I → PISIFORM, HAMATE and 5th METACARPAL
                    • A → FLEXES and ADDUCTS hand at wrist
                    • N → ULNAR NERVE (C8).
                    • Notes:
                      • This is analogous to the flexor carpi radialis, except that it attaches to the other side of the hand.
                      • Hence, it results in ulnar deviation (adduction).
                      • Note that this was an exception to the rule that all anterior muscles are innervated by the medial nerve; it’s innervated by the ulnar nerve (this makes sense; it has ulnaris in it’s name).

                    4. Palmaris Longus

                    • O → medial EPICONDYLE
                    • I → distal FLEXOR RETINACULUM and PALMAR APONEUROSIS
                    • A → weak FLEXOR and TIGHTENS PALMAR aponeurosis
                    • N → MEDIAN NERVE(C7,8)
                    • Notes:
                      • Not everyone has a palmaris longus; it’s thought that primates had a more pronounced palmaris longus, so the more distantly related you are, the less likely you are to have one.
                      • It has a small belly and a long tendon, which then attaches to the palmar aponeurosis.
                      • Flexor Retinaculum: the band of connective tissue at wrist which prevents bow stringing of tendons of the wrist
                      • Palmar Aponeurosis: sheet of connective tissue over the palm of hand that helps provide structural support

                    Middle Muscle

                    1. Flexor Digitorum Superficialis

                    • This is the only muscle of the middle layer.
                    • O → Depends on the head:
                      • Humeroulnar Head: medial EPICONDYLE of humerus (CFO), CORONOID process of ulna, ulnar COLLATERAL LIGAMENT
                      • Radial Head: superior anterior RADIUS.
                    • I → MIDDLE PHALANGES (2-5)
                    • A → FLEXES MIDDLE PHALANGES at proximal interphalangeal joints (4 fingers).
                    • N → MEDIAN NERVE (C7,8)
                    • Notes:
                      • Just like all of the other anterior muscles, this has an origin at the medial epicondyle.
                      • The tendons of these muscles insert into the middle phalanges of the 4 fingers.
                      • However, before they get there, they split (like a snake’s tongue).
                      • The reason for this is that the tendons of the flexor digitorum profundus (a deeper muscle) actually go further, into the distal phalanges.
                      • As a result, the superficial tendons have to split in order to let the deep tendons get by.

                    Deep Muscles

                    • 3 main muscles comprise the deep layer of anterior muscles:
                      1. Flexor Digitorum Profundus
                      2. Flexor Pollicis Longus
                      3. Pronator Quadratus
                    • Like most of the other anterior muscles, these are innervated by the median nerve. However, the deep muscles in particular are innervated by a particular branch of the median nerve, the anterior interosseous branch.
                    • Unlike most of the other anterior muscles, these don’t originate at the medial epicondyle.

                    1. Flexor Digitorum Profundus

                    • O → proximal medial anterior ULNA and INTEROSSEUS membrane
                    • I → distal phalanges 2-5 (deep is distant!!!)
                    • A → FLEXES distal phalanges at distal interphalangeal joint (2-5)
                    • N → this is the 2nd exception (in addition to the flexor carpi ulnaris) to the median nerve innervation rule; only part of it is innervated by a branch of the median:
                      • Medial Part: ULNAR NERVE
                      • Ulnar Part: anterior interosseous branch of MEDIAN NERVE
                    • Notes → as mentioned previously, the tendons of the profundus muscle run past the tendons of the superficialis where they split.

                    2. Flexor Pollicis Longus

                    • O → anterior RADIUS and INTEROSSEUS membrane
                    • I → DISTAL PHALANX of thumb
                    • A → FLEXES THUMB
                    • N → anterior interosseous branch of MEDIAN NERVE.
                    • Notes: Pollicis = thumb.

                    3. Pronator Quadratus

                    • O → anterior ULNA.
                    • I → anterior RADIUS.
                    • A → PRONATES forearm.
                    • N → anterior interosseous branch of MEDIAN NERVE
                    • Notes: this is the second anterior muscle that isn’t a flexor; the pronator quadratus, along with the pronator teres, are the two pronators of the arm.

                    D. Posterior Muscles of the Forearm

                    • Innervation → ALL via the radial nerve.
                      • More specifically, this is via a specific branch of the radial nerve: the posterior interosseous branch (with 1 exception).
                      • Contrast this with the anterior interosseous branch off the median nerve, which innervates the deep muscles of the anterior compartment.
                    • Function → extension and supination.
                    • Blood Supply → the posterior interosseous artery
                      • Recall that this was a branch of the common interosseous, which came off the ulnar artery.
                    • Broadly speaking, the posterior muscles of the forearm are classified into:
                      • Superficial layer.
                      • Deep layer.
                    • There’s no middle layer like with the anterior muscles!

                    Superficial Muscles

                    Usually, when you see a brevis and longus muscle, the longus one will actually be smaller than the brevis one. The longus and brevis refers to the length of the tendon, not the size of the muscle.

                    • 5 main muscles comprise the superficial layer of posterior muscles:
                      1. Extensor Carpi Ulnaris
                      2. Extensor Carpi Radialis Longus
                      3. Extensor Carpi Radialis Brevis
                      4. Extensor Digitorum
                      5. Extensor Digiti Minimi.
                    • The superficial muscles share a common attachment; the lateral epicondyle. This is known as the common extensor origin (CEO).
                      • Irritation of the lateral epicondyle due to extensor action is colloquially known as Tennis Elbow.

                    1. Extensor Carpi Ulnaris

                    • O → lateral EPICONDYLE of humerus
                    • I → base of 5th metacarpal
                    • A → Extends and Adducts hand at wrist
                    • N → Posterior Interosseous branch of radial nerve (C5, 6, 7, 8, T1)
                    • Notes: This is the extensor analag to the flexor carpi ulnaris in the anterior compartment.

                    2. Extensor Digiti Minimi

                    • O → lateral EPICONDYLE of humerus
                    • I → Proximal phalanx of 5th digit (pinky)
                    • A → Extends wrist and 5th digit (pinky)
                    • N → Posterior Interosseous branch of radial nerve (C5, 6, 7, 8, T1)
                    • Notes: as the name suggests, this extends the little finger (digiti minimi).

                    3. Extensor Digitorum

                    • O → lateral EPICONDYLE of humerus
                    • I → Extensor Expansion of medial 4 digits
                    • A → Extends fingers at metacarpalphalangeal joints and Extends hand at wrist
                    • N → Posterior Interosseous branch of radial nerve (C5, 6, 7, 8, T1)
                    • Notes:
                      • Unlike the anterior compartment, where there was both a flexor digitorum superficialis and a flexor digitorum profundus, there’s only one muscle that controls digit extension.
                      • The tendons of the extensor digitorum are interconnected; this makes it harder to independently extend the fingers (unlike with the flexor digitorum muscles).

                    4. Extensor Carpi Radialis Brevis

                    • O → lateral EPICONDYLE of humerus
                    • I → Base of 3rd metacarpal
                    • A → Extends and Abducts hand at wrist
                    • N → Posterior Interosseous (Deep branch) of Radial Nerve (C5, 6, 7, 8, T1)
                    • Notes: Note that there are 2 muscles that extend and adduct the wrist, and only 1 (flexor carpi radialis) that flex and adduct it.

                    5. Extensor Carpi Radialis Longus

                    • O → Lateral supracondyle ridge of humerus
                    • I → Base of 2nd metacarpal
                    • A → Extends and Abducts hand at wrist
                    • N → Radial Nerve (C6)
                    • Notes: This is an exception to the rule that all the posterior compartment muscles are innervated by the posterior interosseous branch of the radial.
                      • Since it originates higher on the humerus, it actually gets innervation from the radial nerve itself before it gives off the posterior interosseous branch.

                    Deep Muscles

                    • 5 main muscles comprise the deep layer of the posterior muscles:
                      1. Supinator
                      2. Abductor Pollicis Longus
                      3. Extensor Pollicis Longus
                      4. Extensor Pollicis Brevis
                      5. Extensor Indicis
                    • Since there are extra muscles attaching to the index finger and pinky (the extensor indicis and extensor digiti minimi respectively), they have more independent movement than the other fingers.
                    • The thumb itself has a number of muscles attaching to it, so it can also easily move independently.
                    • These muscles come out from under the superficial muscles, so they’re often referred to as the outcropping group.

                    1. Supinator

                    • O → lateral EPICONDYLE of humerus, RADIAL COLLATERAL and ANNULAR LIGAMENTS, SUPINATOR FOSSA, and CREST OF ULNA
                    • I → lateral, posterior, and anterior of proximal radius
                    • A → Supinates forearm
                    • N → Posterior Interosseous branch of radial nerve (C5, 6, 7, 8, T1)
                    • Notes → opposes the action of the pronators (pronator teres, pronator quadratus).

                    2. Abductor Pollicis Longus*

                    • O → posterior surface of ULNA, RADIUS, INTEROSSEOUS MEMBRANE
                    • I → base of 1st metacarpal
                    • A → Abducts thumb and extends carpo-metacarpal joints
                    • N → Posterior Interosseous branch of radial nerve (C5, 6, 7, 8, T1)

                    3. Extensor Pollicis Longus*

                    • O → posterior middle ULNA and INTEROSSEOUS MEMBRANE
                    • I → base of DISTAL PHALANX of thumb
                    • A → Extends thumb at carpometacarpal and interphalangeal joints
                    • N → Posterior Interosseous branch of radial nerve (C5, 6, 7, 8, T1)

                    4. Extensor Pollicis Brevis*

                    • O → posterior RADIUS and INTEROSSEOUS MEMBRANE
                    • I → base of PROXIMAL PHALANX of thumb
                    • A → Extends proximal phalanx at carpometacarpal joint
                    • N → Posterior Interosseous branch of radial nerve (C5, 6, 7, 8, T1)

                    5. Extensor Indicis

                    • O → posterior ULNA and INTEROSSEOUS MEMBRANE
                    • I → Extensor expansion of 2nd Digit
                    • A → Extends index finger and extends hand
                    • N → Posterior Interosseous branch of radial nerve (C5, 6, 7, 8, T1)
                    • Notes → allows the index finger to have extension independent of the extensor digitorum (which is why you can point).

                    *: These muscles comprise the anatomical snuffbox:

                    • Extensor policus longus.
                    • Extensor policus brevis.
                    • Abductor policus longus.

                    Summary of Anterior vs Posterior Forearm Muscles

                      Anterior Compartment Posterior Compartment
                    Innervation Median Nerve (2 exceptions) Radial Nerve
                    Action Flexion and Pronation Extension and Supination
                    Blood Supply Radial and ulnar arteries Posterior interosseous artery
                    Often attach to Medial epicondyle Lateral epicondyle
                    • Median nerve = every anterior forearm muscle, except flexor carpi ulnaris and half of flexor digitorum profundus.
                    • Ulnar nerve = flexor carpi ulnarus and half of flexor digitorum profundus.
                    • Radial nerve = all posterior forearm muscles (most as the posterior interosseous nerve branch).

                    E. Anterior Compartment Arteries

                    • Brachial artery branches into radial (lateral) and ulnar (medial)
                      • Both supply the anterior compartment.
                    • The 2 arteries form arches in the palm:
                      • The ulnar artery forms the superficial palmar arch.
                      • The radial artery forms the deep palmar arch.
                      • The two anastamose with one another to provide dual circulation.
                    • The common interosseous artery branches off the ulnar artery, and branches further into anterior interosseous artery and posterior interosseous artery.
                      • The common interosseous aptly runs between the radius and ulna.
                      • The anterior interosseous and posterior interosseous arteries run anterior and posterior to the interosseous membranes respectively.
                      • This is important because the only blood supply to the posterior forearm is through this posterior interosseous artery.
                    • Finally, radial and ulnar recurrents come back up the arm and anastamose with the ulnar collaterals we saw in the previous lecture.
                      • As mentioned before, this helps maintain circulation to the elbow joint.

                    F. Components of the Forearm

                    G. Carpal Tunnel

                    • Guyon’s Canal → small canal that carries the ulnar artery and ulnar nerve.
                      • Between palmar carpal ligament (superficial), flexor retinaculum and hypothenar muscles (deep), pisiform and pisohamate ligament (medial), and hook of hamate (lateral).
                    • Carpal Tunnel → the carpal bones of the wrist are concave with respect to the palmar side, creating a space through which the carpal tunnel runs.
                      • The superficial side is covered by the flexor retinaculum to create tunnel.
                      • Contents of carpal tunnel (1 nerve, 9 tendons):
                        1. Median Nerve
                        2. Flexor Pollicis Longus Tendon
                        3. Flexor Digitorum Profundus (4 tendons)
                        4. Flexor Digitorum Superficialis (4 tendons)
                    • Carpal Tunnel Syndrome → median nerve entrapment neuropathy that causes paresthesia, pain, numbness and other symptoms in the distribution of the median nerve due to compression of the carpal tunnel

                    H. Colle’s Fracture

                    • Colle’s Fracture → fracture of the distal radius (distal neck) with dorsal (posterior) and radial displacement of the wrist and hand
                    • Is described as a bayonet or dinner fork configuration of the bones.
                    • Often associated with osteoporosis.
                    • It is due to a trauma which hyperextends the wrist.
                    • Smith’s Fractures – similar to Colle’s fracture but it is due to a hyperflexion of the wrist.
                    • Can result in damage to median nerve either via hematoma in the carpal tunnel, hyperextension at the time of trauma, or direct injury.
                    • Scaphoid Fractures can also be associated with this.
