                                      Anatomy of the Arm

                                      Andrew Zaleski Friday, January 23, 2015

                                      Introduction

                                      • Arm → shoulder to elbow.
                                      • Forearm → elbow to wrist.
                                      • The arm, like the forearm, is split into 2 compartments.
                                        1. Anterior Compartment → involved in flexion of the shoulder and elbow, and supination.
                                          • Innervated by the musculocutaneous nerve (C5,6,7).
                                        2. Posterior Compartment → involved in extension of the shoulder and elbow.
                                          • Innervated by the radial nerve (C5,6,7,8 T1).
                                      • In the lateral arm runs the cephalic vein; in the medial arm runs the basilic vein.

                                      A. Bones of the Arm

                                      • Lateral Epicondyle
                                      • Medial Epicondyle
                                      • Olecranon Process of Ulna
                                      • Olecranon Fossa
                                      • Coronoid Fossa
                                      • Radial Fossa
                                      • Coronoid Process of Ulna
                                      • Capitulum Trochlea
                                      • Radial Tuberosity
                                      • Ulnar Tuberosity
                                      • Greater Tubercle
                                      • Lesser Tubercle
                                      • Head of humerus
                                      • Bicipital Groove
                                      • Deltoid Tuberosity

                                      B. Anterior Arm Muscles

                                      • The 3 muscles of the anterior compartment comprise of:
                                        1. Biceps Brachii (aka. biceps)
                                        2. Brachialis
                                        3. Coracobrachialis
                                      • All of them are supplied by the brachial artery.
                                      • All of them are innervated by the musculocutaneous nerve.
                                        • Note that the musculocutaneous nerve provides motor innervation to the muscles of the anterior compartment, and then continues as the lateral cutaneous nerve of the forearm and provides sensory innervation.
                                        • Since it innervates the muscles, and then the cutaneous forearm, it’s aptly referred to as the musculocutaneous nerve.
                                      • In addition to these 3 muscles, there’s another ‘pseudo’ anterior arm muscle called the brachioradialis muscle.
                                        • This muscle is located in the anterior compartment, but is innervated like a posterior compartment muscle (ie. by the radial nerve).

                                      1. Biceps Brachii

                                      • O: Differs depending on the head:
                                        • Short Head: CORACOID process of scapula.
                                        • Long Head: SUPRAGLENOID tubercle of scapula/glenoid labrum.
                                      • I → RADIAL TUBEROSITY.
                                      • A → Supinates forearm (major) and flexes elbow
                                      • N → Musculocutaneous nerve (C5,6,7)
                                      • Clinical → the long tendon ruptures easily with trauma in the elderly; tapping the biceps tendon briskly elicits the biceps jerk tendon reflex to check C5/C6 spinal cord levels.
                                      • Blood Supply → Brachial artery and branches.
                                      • Notes:
                                        • In the arm, the median nerve is most medial, then the brachial artery, and then the biceps brachii is most lateral.
                                        • A sheet-like tendon near the base of the biceps called the bicipital aponeurosis protects the brachial artery and median nerve as they continue into the forearm.

                                      2. Brachialis

                                      • O → Distal anterior humerus.
                                      • I → CORONOID process of ULNAR TUBEROSITY
                                      • A → MAJOR flexor of forearm (thumbs up), flexes forearm in all positions.
                                      • N → Musculocutaneous nerve (C5,6,7) and radial nerve.
                                      • Notes:
                                        • Note that this attaches to the ulnar tuberosity as opposed to the radial tuberosity.
                                        • Hence, the biceps brachii attaches to the radial tuberosity, and the brachialis attaches to the ulnar tuberosity.

                                      3. Coracobrachialis

                                      • O → CORACOID process of scapula
                                      • I → Medial HUMERUS
                                      • A → Weak flexor and adducts arm
                                      • N → Musculocutaneous nerve (C5,6,7)
                                      • Notes:
                                        • Small muscle; does not cross the elbow (starts from the coracoid, and only goes to the medial humerus). As a result, it only flexes the arm at the glenohumeral joint.
                                        • Good landmark for the musculocutaneous nerve, because you can see the nerve pierce the muscle!

                                      4. Brachioradialis

                                      • O → Proximal lateral SUPRACONDYLAR ridge of humerus
                                      • I → lateral surface of distal RADIUS (styloid process).
                                      • A → FLEXES the forearm
                                      • N → Radial nerve (C5,6,7)
                                      • Clinical → tapping elicits the brachioradialis jerk tendon reflex used to test for C5, C6, and C7 spinal cord levels
                                      • Notes:
                                        • Not really an anterior arm muscle, since it’s innervated by the radial nerve, as opposed to the musculocutaneous nerve.
                                        • Forms the bulk of the lateral side of your forearm.
                                        • Good landmark to distinguish between the anterior and posterior compartments.
                                        • Since it crosses the elbow, it does elbow flexion.
                                        • It’s strongest when the radius is upwards (the muscle is least stretched), so it’s used most when in a ‘hammering’ or ‘drinking’ position.
                                        • Hence it’s known as the drinking or thumbs-up muscle.

                                      5. Summary of Anterior Arm Muscles

                                      • The biceps brachii crosses both the shoulder and elbow so it can flex both.
                                      • The brachialis and brachioradialis only cross the elbow so they only flex the elbow.
                                        • Since the brachialis attaches to the ulnar tuberosity, and the brachioradialis attaches to the distal radius, they’re stronger in different positions.
                                      • The coracobrachialis only crosses the shoulder, so it only flexes the shoulder (and adducts it).

                                      C. Posterior Arm Muscles

                                      1. Triceps

                                      • O → varies for each of the 3 heads (hence tri-ceps):
                                        • Long Head: INFRAGLENOID TUBERCLE of scapula
                                        • Medial Head: posterior surface of HUMERUS BELOW radial groove
                                        • Lateral Head: posterior surface of HUMERUS ABOVE radial groove
                                      • I → proximal OLECRANON process and FOREARM FASCIA via triceps tendon
                                      • A → MAJOR extensor of forearm and shoulder
                                      • N → Radial nerve (C5,6,7,8, T1)
                                      • Blood Supply → profunda brachial artery and branches.
                                      • Clinical → tapping triceps tendon used to elicit the triceps jerk tendon reflex to test C7 and C8.
                                      • Notes:
                                        • The radial nerve runs along a groove on the posterior humerus; this is the radial groove (aka. spiral groove).
                                          • The lateral head attaches above this groove; the medial head attaches below it.
                                        • The long head of the triceps runs between the teres major and teres minor.
                                        • The lateral head of the triceps merges with the long head to form the triceps tendon. This then attaches to the olecranon of the elbow.
                                        • Between the long head and the lateral head lies the muscle’s blood supply and innervation: the radial nerve and profunda brachii artery.

                                      Reflex Testing Nerve Roots

                                      • Biceps reflex → C5,6. (C5,6 pick-up sticks)
                                      • Brachioradialis reflex → C6.
                                      • Triceps reflex → C7,8 (C7,8, lay them straight).

                                      D. Arterial Supply of the Arm

                                      • Subclavian → Axillary → Brachial → Ulnar/Radial Arteries.
                                      • The axillary artery becomes the brachial artery past the lower border of the teres major.
                                      • Brachial Artery Branches:
                                        1. Profunda Brachii → recall that this supplies triceps.
                                        2. Superior Ulnar Collateral
                                        3. Inferior Ulnar Collateral
                                        4. Ulnar Artery (medial)
                                        5. Radial Artery (lateral)
                                      • The superior and inferior ulnar collaterals anastamose with ascending recurrent branches to help maintain blood flow to the elbow.
                                      • Musculocuntaneous nerve usually pierces coracobrachialis.
                                      • Brachial artery can be injured with midshaft or supracondylar fractures of the humerus.
                                      • Median nerve is medial to brachial artery near elbow.
                                      • Ulnar nerve passes behind the medial epicondyle (Funny Bone); more severe injury can lead to claw hand deformity and numbness.
                                      • Radial nerve emerges between brachialis and brachioradialis muscles.

                                      E. Common Fracture Sites of the Arm

                                      1. Surgical Neck
                                        • Most common in adults from a fall on an outstretched arm.
                                        • Concerned with the axillary nerve, since it passes by the surgical neck.
                                        • Hence, you would test the (1) deltoids and (2) teres minor.
                                      2. Midshaft Fracture
                                        • Concerned with the radial nerve (runs along spiral groove).
                                      3. Supracondylar fracture
                                        • Common in children.
                                        • Concerned with the ulnar nerve (runs behind the medial epicondyle) and the median nerve.
