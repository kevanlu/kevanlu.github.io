              Approach to the Arm and Elbow

              Kevan Lu Friday, January 23, 2015

              Lecture Objectives

              • Apply anatomy to distinguish the common areas of elbow pathology:
                • Joint arthropathy (arthritis)
                • Joint stability (Fracture, Dislocation)
                • Other structures: eg. Bursa, Capsule, Ligament, Tendon, nerve.
              • Some useful acronyms:
                • FOOSH → Falling on an outstretched hand (common MSK mnemonic).
                • ORIF = Open Reduction, Internal Fixation.

              Arthritis

              • In general, arthritis can be categorized as either inflammatory or non-inflammatory:
                • Non-Inflammatory Arthritis
                  • Post traumatic.
                  • Osteoarthritis.
                • Inflammatory Arthritis
                  • Rheumatoid.
                  • Psoriatic.
                  • Crystalline (Gout, CPPD).
              • The inflammatory causes will be discussed during the rheumatology week.
              • Today we’ll be focusing primarily on osteoarthritis of the elbow joint.

              Elbow Osteoarthritis

              • Progressive pain, stiffness, and loss of RoM of the elbow joint.
              • This is due to progressive cartilage loss.
                • It’s unusual to see the elbow as the first presenting joint with degenerative conditions, unless there’s a history trauma.
                • Typically, the weight-bearing joints are the first to go → the knees and hips.
              • Clinical Features:
                • Deep aching quality of elbow (vs superficial).
                • Loss of both passive and active ROM.
                • Exacerbated by activity; relieved with rest.

              Here, note (1) the loss of joint space, and (2) sclerosis (increased density) of the surrounding bone.

              • If a person presented with medial elbow pain, stiffness and a loss of ROM of the elbow, and also began to complain of numbness in the 4th and 5th finger, what structure would you think of?
                • Ulnar nerve.
                • The ulnar nerve runs along the medial epicondyle of the humerus.
              • What are treatment options for osteoarthritis?
                • Activity modification.
                • NSAIDS, analgesics.
                • PT.
                • Surgery → debridement, replacement.
              • What is joint debridement?
                • Debridement is when you go into the joint and ‘clean out the joint space’.
                • Not curative; it doesn’t magically restore cartilage. It simply aims to eliminate any damaged tissue that’s causing the elbow to ‘catch’.
                • Hence this reduces pain and improves function.
                • Reserved for younger patients → a replacement only lasts around 10 years, so it wouldn’t be a good first line choice in a younger patient.
              • What is joint replacement?
                • Where you replace components of the joint.
                • For the elbow, this procedure is called Total Elbow Arthroplasty.
                • Very good results with inflammatory conditions where the cause is not wear and tear.
                • Poor results with osteoarthritis or post-traumatic arthritis → the replacement joint easily wears down, so here the joint will have poor survival.

              Remember

              • Total elbow arthroplasty is used for inflammatory conditions, but not for osteoarthritis.

              Tendon Problems

              • Tendonitis → tendon inflammation.
              • Tendonosis → tendon degeneration.
              • Tendonitis and tendonosis are often used synonymously, but technically, the majority of issues are due to degeneration as opposed to inflammation.
              • With respect to the elbow, we’ll mainly be dealing with the most common tendon issue; inflammation of the tendons attaching to the lateral epicondyle.
              • Pathological tendon findings are categorized by whether there’s pain with active or passive movement:
                • Tendonitis → pain on resistive activity (eg. lateral epicondylitis).
                • Tenosynovitis → pain with passive movement (eg. De Quervain’s Tenosynovitis).

              Lateral Epicondylitis

              • aka. Tennis Elbow
              • ‘Insertionitis’ of the extensor origin group (recall that all the wrist extensors attach to the lateral epicondyle).
              • Known as ‘tennis elbow’ because you need strong wrist extensors to hit a steady backhand in tennis.
              • Hence people playing tennis put more stress on the muscles inserting into the lateral epicondyle.
              • Clinical Features
                • Latearl epicondylar region is warm, occasionally swollen
                • Tender to palpation.
                • Pain on resisted activity of attachment group.
                  • Recall that this included wrist extension, finger extension, and forearm supination. EPL = extensor pollicis longus, EPB = extensor pollicis brevis, APL = abductor pollicis longus.
                  • This does not include thumb extension (EPL, EPB, APL do not attach at the elbow, but further down the radius and ulna).
              • Treatment of Epicondylitis:
                • Modified activities.
                • PT.
                • NSAIDS, Analgesics.
                • Local steroid injection (cortisone)
                • Surgery.

              Tenosynovitis

              • De Quervain’s Tenosynovitis → inflammation of the fluid filled sheath that surrounds the tendons of the first extensor compartment.
                • Because this affects the smooth movement of the tendon, pain will be present with passive movement.
              • The first extensor compartment (most medial) contains 2 tendons:
                • APL → Abductor Pollicis Longus.
                • EPB → Extensor Pollicis Brevis.
                • EPL is not part of the first compartment; recall that it wraps around Lister’s tubercle to form the other side of the snuff box. Hence, it’s more lateral than APL and EPB.
              • Treatment of Tenosynovitis:
                • Cortisone injections (know complications and contraindications).

              Ligament Problems

              • Ligament is more irregularly arranged and contains more elastin fibres than tendons.
              • Ligaments provide strong tensile force to help maintain joint stability.
              • With respect to the elbow joint, it’s effectively a hinge; unlike the shoulder, it’s fairly stable with good osseous support.

              Little Leaguer’s Elbow

              • Little Leaguer’s Elbow → elbow injury commonly seen in the skeletally immature.
                • Due to the fact that the medial epicondylar growth plate is weaker, and is therefore more susceptible to trauma when pulled by the MCL.
                • Since the MCL attaches to the medial epicondyle, a strong MCL pull can result in an avulsion fracture of the medial epicondyle.
                • Avulsion fracture → fracture of the piece of bone attached to a ligament or tendon.
              • Typically seen when the growth plate is vulnerable, between 11-13.
                • This is when the growth plate is beginning to close.
                • That’s also why you’re not allowed to throw curve balls when you’re a kid; it puts too much pressure on the medial epicondyle.
              • Treatment Options for Little Leaguer’s Elbow:
                • Rest, modified activity at least 6-8 weeks/year.
                • PT.
                • Surgery if epicondyle is displaced more than 1 cm.
                • Consider ulnar neuropathy or joint instability.

              Nerve Problems

              • Cubital Tunnel Syndrome → ulnar nerve trapping/pinching in the cubital tunnel.
                • The cubital tunnel lies between the two heads of the FCU (flexor carpi ulnaris); between the olecranon and medial epicondyle.
              • Findings with Cubital Tunnel Syndrome:
                • Sensory Exam → decreased sensation in ulnar nerve distribution (4th and 5th fingers).
                • Motor Exam:
                  • Weakness with grasping, pinching, and dexterity.
                  • Recall the DAB/PAD mnemonic → as we’ll see later, the dorsal interossei of the hand abduct the fingers (hence DAB), and the plantar interossei of the hand adduct the fingers (hence PAD).
                  • With more severe injury, an ulnar claw might also be seen.
                • Other features:
                  • Positive Tinel’s sign at the ulnar nerve at the elbow (ie. right below the medial epicondyle).

              • Causes of Cubital Tunnel Syndrome
                • Increased external pressure → anesthesia, bed mobility, wheelchair, trauma.
                • Pathology in the groove itself → fracture, stretch, osteophytes with osteoarthritis.

              Practice Quiz

              1. A 29 year old school teacher complains of sharp lateral elbow pain for the past 4 days. He recalls chipping ice from the front steps this weekend. The pain is brought out by activities involving gripping and lifting especially with a fist and wrist extended.
                • What’s the diagnosis?
              2. A 24 year old woman complains of medial right elbow pain, sometimes extending down the forearm and is accompanied by tingling/numbness in the 4th and 5th fingers. This is particularly true when she is playing volleyball.
                • What’s the diagnosis?
              3. A 31 year old man slipped on the sidewalk, striking his elbow. Now he has pain and swelling of the elbow and is unable to bend or move the elbow.
                • What diagnostic test would you like to order?
              4. An X-Ray was taken for this patient, and the following was seen:
                • Given this X-Ray, what’s the most likely diagnosis?

              Answers

              1. Lateral epicondylitis.
              2. Ulnar neuropathy.
              3. X-ray.
              4. Anterior elbow dislocation + fracture of the radial head/neck.
