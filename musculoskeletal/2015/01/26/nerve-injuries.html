          Nerve Injuries

          Kevan Lu Monday, January 26, 2015

          Lecture Objectives

          1. Differentiate the types of pathophysiology in a nerve injury and how that affects outcome.
          2. Recognize the clinical features of common neuropathies.
          3. Apply this to clinical anatomy.

          Lecture Overview

          1. Nerve Anatomy and Pathophysiology.
          2. Nerve Injury (3 examples):
            1. Carpal Tunnel Syndrome.
            2. Foot drop.
            3. Radial Nerve Palsy.

          Nerve Anatomy and Pathophysiology

          • The peripheral nervous system (PNS) is classified the somatic and autonomic nervous system.
            1. Somatic Nervous System → motor fibres to skeletal muscle, sensory fibres from skin, viscera, muscles, and tendon receptors.
            2. Autonomic Nervous System → generally travel with nerves or vessels and control sweating, blood flow, and end organ regulation.
          • As mentioned before, the cell body of the nerve root (eg. the dorsal root ganglion) lies distal to where the nerve attaches to the spinal cord.
            • As a result, if the nerve gets avulsed at the root, the cell body is still alive, but it’s not connected to the spinal cord.
          • Motor Unit → the key functional unit of muscle motion; comprises of:
            1. Anterior horn cell.
            2. Motor Nerve
            3. Neuromuscular Junction and the end muscle

          ##The Spinal Cord

          • Protruding from the spinal cord are both dorsal and ventral roots.
          • These then combine to form a spinal nerve, which then splits again to form dorsal and ventral rami.
          • The sympathetic trunk communicates with the spinal nerves via rami communicantes.

          ##Anatomy of a Nerve

          • A nerve is comprised of a bundle of fascicles.
          • Surrounding each nerve is a lining called the perineurium.
          • Surrounding each fascicle is a lining called an epineurium.
          • In each fascicle are a series of neuron axons.
            • These can be either motor or sensory, and myelinated or unmyelinated.
            • Recall that myelinated axons allow for faster conduction, because the signal can jump from one node of Ranvier to another.
          • When nerve reconstruction is done, it doesn’t just involve reconnecting the nerve itself, but actually involves connecting each fascicle (each of which is about the size of a hair).
            • Because each nerve has multiple fascicles, a partial nerve injury that doesn’t damage all the fascicles will still allow for some retained function.

          • Some classes of nerve fibres include:
            • A-alpha fibres → motor, myelinated, very fast
            • A-delta fibres → sensory, unmyelinated, slower.
            • C fibres → pain/temperature sensors, slow.

          ##Classification of Nerve Disorders

          • Radiculopathies → nerve disorders affecting the roots.
          • Plexopathies → nerve disorders affecting the plexuses.
          • Mononeuropathies → lesions of individual peripheral nerves (usually compressive/traumatic).
          • Multiple mononeuropathies (mononeuropathy multiplex) → multiple focal lesions of individual peripheral nerves (usually inflammatory/vascular).
          • Polyneuropathies → generalized processes affecting the peripheral neuron cell body, the axon, and/or myelin.
          • How are multiple mononeuropathies different from polyneuropathies?
            • Multiple mononeuropathy typically affects only a few nerves, often in different areas of the body.
            • In contrast, polyneuropathy affects many nerves, usually in about the same areas on both sides of the body (hence, it’s a generalized process).
            • Extensive multiple mononeuropathy may be difficult to distinguish from polyneuropathy.

          ##Nerve Injury

          • Wallerian Degeneration → this refers to a degenerative process that occurs after a nerve has been crushed or cut.
            • What happens is that any part of the nerve fibre that is separated from the neuron’s cell body due to the injury ends up degenerating.
            • Nerve regeneration does occur, where the axons that remain connected to the cell body can regrow. However, this is a slow process:
              • Peripheral nerves regrow at a rate of 1 mm per day, or 1 inch a month.
          • How does a nerve respond to being injured?
            • A transient insult (such as ischemia from sleeping on your arm) is quickly reversible.
            • A more significant insult (nerve stretch or transection) can result in Wallerian Degeneration.
          Insult Terminology Etiology Examples
          Minimal Reversible Conduction Block (CB) Ischemia, mild compression Crossed legs, slept on it
          Intermediate Prolonged CB Focal demyelination CTS
          Severe Wallerian (Axonal) Degeneration Loss of axon and myelin sheath Stretch, transection, prolonged ischemia (eg. with an infarction)

          ####Remember - Nerves grow at approximately 1 mm/day or 1 inch/month.

          ##Classification of Nerve Injury

          • Peripheral nerve injuries follow Seddon Classification, as follows:
            1. Neurapraxia (Class I) → myelin damage (ie. bruised nerve).
            2. Axonotmesis (Class II) → axon damage.
            3. Neurotmesis (Class III) → nerve is completely transected.
          • These classifications are important because they guide treatment and prognosis.
            • For instance, neurotmesis will never recover naturally; you have to sew the two pieces back together.
            • Neurapraxia → complete recovery.
            • Axonotmesis → variable recovery.
            • Neurotmesis → no chance of recovery.

          ####Peripheral Nerve Injury Classification 1. Neurapraxia (Class I) → myelin damage (ie. bruised nerve). 2. Axonotmesis (Class II) → axon damage. 3. Neurotmesis (Class III) → transected nerve.

          ##Assessing Nerve Function

          • Nerve function is assessed with EMG/NCS:
            • EMG → Electromyography.
              • This measures the electrical activity of the muscles, both at rest and when contracting.
              • Involves sticking a needle into the muscle.
            • NCS → Nerve Conduction Studies.
              • This measures the electrical activity of the nerve, such as how well and how fast it can send signals.
          • EMG/NCS let you assess:
            • SNAP → Sensory Nerve Action Potential, which reflects sensory function.
            • MUAP → Motor Unit Action Potential, which reflects motor unit function.
          • EMG/NCS helps you identify the severity and extent of any neuromuscular problem.
            • One example of what you can see with EMG is shown below.
            • Here, note the spontaneous muscle activity seen with needle insertion into the muscle. This is bad; it suggests denervation, which brings the muscle membrane potential closer to zero.
            • The nerve typically provides inhibition to the motor unit, so without this, you see muscle fibrillations and positive sharp waves.

          Nerve Injury

          ##1. Carpal Tunnel Syndrome

          • Carpal Tunnel Syndrome → neuropathy caused by the compression of the median nerve as it passes through the carpal tunnel.
          • What are the contents of the Carpal Tunnel?
            • 10 things; 1 median nerve, 9 tendons.
            • Of the 9 tendons, 4 are the flexor digitorum superficialis tendons, 4 are the flexor digitorum profundus tendons, and 1 is the flexor pollicis longus. Recall that MCP = Metacarpophalygeal joint, PIP = Proximal Interphalyngeal joint, DIP = Distal Interphalyngeal Joint.
            • Recall that the FDS flexes the MCP and PIP joints, while the FDP does the DIP joints (the superficial tendons split to let the deep tendons attach further along the fingers).
          • Special Tests for Carpal Tunnel Syndrome:
            1. Tinel’s Sign → tapping on the carpal tunnel.
              • Poor sensitivity and specificity; positive in 45% of people with CTS, but also 29% of normal people.
            2. Phalen’s Sign → wrist flexion as shown to the right. A positive sign will result in paresthesia in the distribution of the median nerve (medial palmar side of hand).
              • Somewhat better sensitivity (40-60%).
          • Other Signs of CTS:
            • Weakness with thumb abduction, opposition, and flexion. L = Lumbricals 1/2, O = Opponens Pollicis, A = Abductor Pollicis Brevis, F = Flexor Pollicis Brevis.
              • Recall that the recurrent branch of the median nerve innervates the LOAF muscles; (Lumbricals 1/2), Opposition, Abduction, and Flexion.
              • Hence, if the median nerve is compressed, the LOAF muscles will have reduced function.
            • If there’s complete denervation, there may also be atrophy of the thenar eminence (thenar wasting).
          • Causes of CTS:
            • Most are idiopathic.
            • Some are anatomical (reduced space in the carpal tunnel), or due to diseases that cause nerve damage (diabetes, polyneuropathies).
            • One mnemonic for CTS causes is PRAGMATIC:
              • P → Pregnancy (fluid retention is compressive)
              • R → Rheumatoid Arthritis (and other forms).
              • A → Acromegaly.
              • G → Glucose (Diabetes)
              • M → Mechanical.
              • A → Amyloid.
              • T → Thyroid (under-activity).
              • I → Infection.
              • C → Crystals (Gout, pseudogout).
          • Treatment of CTS:
            • Non-surgical:
              • Avoid activity, vibratory tools, and high force (duh).
              • Splinting.
              • Steroid injection into the carpal tunnel.
              • NSAIDs
            • Surgery:
              • Surgical decompression of the carpal tunnel → common, very effective.
              • Can be open or endoscopic.

          ##2. Foot Drop

          • Foot Drop → inability to dorsiflex the foot.
          • What is the nerve that’s damaged if there’s a foot drop?
            • The peroneal nerve (aka. the common fibular nerve).
            • This is the nerve that runs near the fibular neck, and is hence often damaged with a fibular fracture.
            • The sensory distribution of the peroneal nerve is the dorsum of the foot.
          • Why does a peroneal nerve injury cause a foot drop?
            • As the sciatic nerve goes from the thigh to the leg, it branches into a tibial and common fibular (peroneal) nerve.
            • The peroneal nerve then goes in front of the tibia and fibula, and the tibial nerve goes behind the tibia and fibula. The two branches are thus separated by the membrane separating the two bones (the interosseous membrane).
            • As a result, the tibial nerve innervates the posterior compartment muscles of the leg, while the common peroneal nerve innervates the anterior compartment (as well as the lateral compartment).
            • Since the anterior compartment is responsible for dorsiflexion, while the posterior compartment is responsible for plantar flexion, damage to the peroneal nerve will result in unapposed plantar flexion → a foot drop.
          • When should surgery be considered?
            • If the nerve is intact, but isn’t functioning, it’s best to wait a few months before considering surgery to see if it’ll recover on its own.
            • Surgery is indicated if there’s no recovery by 3 months.
          • What’s the treatment for Foot Drop?
            • AFO → Ankle Foot Orthosis. This is a splint that helps lift the foot up.
            • Surgery → some form of nerve repair, if due to a peroneal nerve problem.

          ##3. Radial Nerve Palsy

          • Radial Nerve Palsy → injury to the radial nerve.
          • What are expected findings with a radial nerve palsy?
            • Numbness over the back of the hand.
            • Inability to extend the wrist or digits (the radial nerve, namely the posterior interosseous branch of the radial nerve, innervates the extensor muscles of the forearm).
            • Possible wrist drop due to unopposed flexion of the wrist.
          • What are common causes of radial nerve palsy?
            • Typically due to entrapment of the radial nerve as it’s in a very vulnerable position.
            • Recall that the radial nerve runs along the ‘radial groove’ of the humerus, which lies between the medial and lateral heads of the triceps. Saturday Night Palsy is named as such because it reflects a common cause of compression; putting your arm over the armrest of an adjacent chair, such as when you’re making a move on a girl at the movie theatre.
            • Hence, anything that damages this area can cause a radial nerve palsy.
              • Fracture of the Humerus
              • Saturday Night Palsy → ie. compression of the radial nerve.
          • Path of the Radial Nerve (with Muscle Acronyms):
            • BR → Brachioradialis.
            • ECRL → Extensor Carpi Radialis Longus..
            • ECRB → Extensor Carpi Radialis Brevis.
            • Supin → Supinator.
            • EDC → Extensor Digitorum (Communis).
            • ECU → Extensor Carpi Ulnaris.
            • APL → Abductor Pollicis Longus.
            • EPL → Extensor Pollicis Longus.
            • EPB → Extensor Pollicis Brevis.
            • EIP → Extensor Indicis (Proprius).

          Summary

          • Nerve injury can be classified into 3 main categories:
            • Neurapraxia → myelin damage.
            • Axonotmesis → axon damage.
            • Neurotmesis → nerve transection.
          • Knowing these categories helps you determine prognosis and outcome:
            • For instance, neurotmesis will not improve unless surgery is done.
          • Imaging is used to assess anatomy.
          • EMG/NCS is used to assess physiology/function.
