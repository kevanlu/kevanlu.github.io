                      Nerve Surgery & Reconstruction

                      Kevan Lu Monday, January 26, 2015

                      Lecture Objectives

                      • How are nerves injured?
                      • How can we tell if/where the nerve is injured? (Anatomy!)
                      • How can we tell if nerves are going to recover spontaneously?
                      • If they are not recovering, when and how should we fix them?

                      Objective

                      • How are nerves injured?
                      • Acute Causes
                        • Penetrating injuries → knife, gun shot wound.
                        • Blunt injuries → nerve stretching, or crush.
                          • Common causes of nerve stretching include motorcycle, ATV, and snowmobile accidents, which can result in stretching of the brachial plexus.
                      • Chronic Causes
                        • Compression → eg. Carpal Tunnel Syndrome.
                        • Metabolic → B12 deficiency, DM.
                        • Infectious → leprosy (most common cause of nerve injury worldwide).
                        • Radiation

                      Objective

                      • How can we tell if/where a nerve is injured?
                      • History → areas of numbness/paresthesiae.
                      • Physical Exam → focus on anatomy; which nerve(s) are at risk?
                        • Motor → assess which muscles have lost function.
                        • Sensory → assess which dermatomes have lost sensation.
                      • Adjunctive Tests
                        • EMG.
                        • Imaging.

                      Case Example

                      • A patient presents with the following:
                        • 0/5 power to biceps.
                        • 0/5 power to supraspinatus.
                        • 0/5 power to deltoid.
                        • 4/5 power to triceps.
                        • 5/5 power to thenar eminence.
                      • What nerves are involved?
                        • Biceps → musculocutaneous nerve, and primarily C5/C6.
                        • Supraspinatus → suprascapular nerve, which comes off the superior trunk, which forms from C5/C6.
                        • Deltoid → axillary nerve, off the radial nerve. Although the radial nerve has contributions from C5,6,7,8 and T1, the axillary nerve only has contributions from C5/C6.
                        • Triceps → Radial nerve, primarily C7/C8.
                        • Thenar Eminence → recurrent branch of median nerve.
                        • Note that the muscles with 0 power are all innervated by the C5/6 nerve roots.

                      • What could be causing his findings?
                        • We have 3 possibilities:
                          1. Triple nerve injury to the (1) musculocutaneous nerve, (2) axillary nerve, and (3) suprascapular nerve.
                          2. Nerve root avulsion at C5,6.
                          3. Neurotmesis of the superior trunk.
                        • By Occam’s razor, it’s more likely to be a single injury than 3 injuries, so 2 or 3 are most likely.
                        • To distinguish between 2 or 3, you could assess the function of the dorsal scapular nerve, or the long thoracic nerve, since these come off of the roots.
                          • Recall that the dorsal scapular nerve innervates the levator scapulae, and the rhomboid major and minor.
                          • Recall that the long thoracic nerve innervates the serratus anterior.
                        • If it was a trunk injury, the action of these two nerves would be preserved; if it was a root avulsion, it wouldn’t be.

                      Objective

                      • How can we tell if nerves are going to recover spontaneously?

                      • As we saw in the previous lecture, peripheral nerve injuries can be classified as:
                        1. Neurapraxia (Class I) → myelin damage (ie. bruised nerve).
                        2. Axonotmesis (Class II) → axon damage.
                        3. Neurotmesis (Class III) → nerve is completely transected.
                      • This was referred to as the Seddon classification system.
                      • In addition to this, there is a newer, Sunderland classification, that more precisely stratifies nerve injuries, as shown below.

                      Classification of Peripheral Nerve Injuries

                      Seddon Classification Sunderland Classification Injury
                      First Degree Neurapraxia Myelin injured; intact axons
                      Second Axonotmesis Axons injured; intact stroma
                      Third   Endoneurium disrupted
                      Fourth   Epineurium disrupted
                      Fifth Neurotmesis Nerve transection

                      • Nerve recovery can be predicted by the type of injury:
                        • Neurapraxia → complete recovery.
                        • Axonotmesis → variable recovery.
                        • Neurotmesis → no chance of recovery.
                      • Clinically, neuropraxia by definition will recover within 3 months, but axonotmesis and neurotmesis may look very similar.
                        • In fact, although an EMG may distinguish neuropraxia from the other two, it can’t distinguish between axonotmesis and neurotmesis.
                      • So practically, if we’re not sure if it’s axonotmesis of neurotmesis, we follow the patient:
                        • Serial examinations looking for evidence of recovery.
                        • We base our expectations on the size of the injury, and the expected rate of recovery (1 mm/day)
                        • If no recovery by 3-4 months, consider reconstruction.

                      Objective

                      • If they are not recovering, when and how should we fix them?
                      • As was mentioned above, if there’s a possibility of recovery (ie, it isn’t obvious that the nerve has been transected), we only consider surgery if there’s no recovery by 3-4 months.
                      • Surgical options include:
                        1. Neurolysis (not very useful, not used)
                        2. Direct repair
                        3. Nerve grafting → began use in the 1980s.
                        4. Nerve transfers → began use in the 1990s.

                      Direct Repair

                      • If possible, this is the best option.
                      • However, this is only possible for the first 1-2 weeks (before Wallerian degeneration occurs), and only if the gap is very small.
                        • Because the nerves are normally under a state of mild tension, when they’re cut, the nerves actually retract.
                        • This makes it difficult to directly reattach the nerves unless the distance is very small.
                        • Attempting to repair nerves at longer distances using more tension will produce poor results.

                      Nerve Grafting

                      • Also known as nerve splicing.
                      • The idea here is to excise the damaged section of nerve, and to cut out a piece of another nerve to repair the gap.
                        • We cut out nerves that are considered to be expendable; often this is the sural nerve that provides sensory innervation to the lateral leg and foot.
                      • However, since the axons of the graft undergo Wallerian degeneration once its excised, it only functions as a scaffold through which the damaged nerve can grow through.
                        • Since the axons in the graft itself die out, the nerve must still grow through the scaffold until it reaches the target, which can be slow (recall, 1 mm/day).
                      • Why not use something else for a scaffold, instead of taking another nerve?
                        • We’ve attempted to use veins, or man-made scaffolds, but there’s some factor in the connective tissue of the nerve itself that facilitates regrowth.
                        • Hence, at the moment, we have to use a graft.
                      • What factors limit the success of nerve grafting?
                        1. Age (>30 years).
                        2. Length of gap (>6 cm).
                        3. Distance from injury to motor endplate.
                        4. Mismatch of motor and sensory fascicles.
                      • Since the nerve still has to grow through the graft, if it has to go a long way, the success is lower.
                        • This is why the length of the gap, and the distance from the injury to the endplate matters.
                      • Furthermore, if a motor fascicle is connected to a sensory fascicle, it won’t work.
                        • To resolve this issue, we can actually use electrophysiological testing or histochemical staining to identify each fascicle before we reattach them.
                        • However, this is only feasible if the number of fascicles is small. If it was an upper brachial plexus injury, this would be unrealistic.

                      Nerve Transfers

                      • This is a very new technique, that was introduced only 20 years ago (1994).
                        • It’s only been used routinely in the last decade.
                        • Results in remarkably better recovery over a nerve graft.
                        • This was what was used in the Case of the Week patient.
                      • The way a nerve transfer works is to reconnect the damaged nerve to another ‘spare’ nerve that’s closer to the end muscle.
                        • For instance, if we used a nerve graft for an ulnar nerve injury at the level of the axilla, it would take years for the nerve to grow through the graft and reach the hand. By that point, the muscles would have atrophied anyway.
                        • But if we instead connected a nerve that was nearby the damaged distal nerve, the amount of time needed for regrowth would be much shorter.
                      • Example:
                        • A patient has an upper ulnar nerve injury at the level of the axilla.
                        • Since the motor endplate is too far away, a nerve graft is infeasible.
                        • Instead, we can redirect the nerve to the pronator quadratus (the anterior interosseus nerve), and use it to reconnect the distal ulnar nerve.
                        • Since the pronator teres also pronates the hand, the pronator quadratus is somewhat redundant.
                        • Now, since the distance to the end-plate is much shorter, the recovery time will be shorter (months vs years), and muscle atrophy will be reduced.
                      • Problems with nerve transfers:
                        • Since you’re attaching a different nerve, it takes a long time to relearn how to do certain actions.
                        • In the above example, since you’re using the nerve to the pronator quadratus, the patient will have to pronate their wrist in order to adduct or abduct their fingers.
                      • Benefits of Nerve Transfers:
                        • Greatly decreases the time for new axons to reach the muscle.
                        • You can be certain that you’re connecting fascicles ‘motor-to-motor’.

                      Nerve Reconstruction Summary

                      Advantages Disadvantages
                      Direct Repair Provides the best functional result
                      • Can only be done in the first 1-2 weeks.
                      • Can only be done if the gap is very very small
                      Nerve Graft Allows for reconstruction with larger injuries or longer timeframes versus direct repair
                      • Less effective if age > 30
                      • Less effective if gap > 6 cm
                      • Less effective if large distance to motor end-plate
                      • Might mismatch motor and sensory fascicles
                      • Must sacrifice another nerve for the graft
                      Nerve Transfer
                      • Greatly reduces time for new axons to reach muscle
                      • Can be certain that you're connecting motor to motor
                      • Must redirect the nerve from another muscle
                      • Function is somewhat dictated by the nerve that was transfered.
