                            Case of the Week – Brachial Plexus Injury

                            Kevan Lu Monday, January 26, 2015

                            Lecture Objectives

                            1. Outline the anatomy of the brachial plexus
                              • See The Brachial Plexus.
                            2. Discuss and Highlight clinical features of nerve root avulsion
                              • Nerve root avulsion → pulling of the nerve roots out from the spinal cord.
                            3. Introduce the concepts of neuropathic pain and the Rehabilitation in Nerve injury.

                            Nerve Root Avulsion

                            • What clinical features make you think of nerve root avulsion?
                              1. Horner’s Syndrome (T1).
                              2. Severe Pain.
                              3. Dermatomal/myotomal weakness of C8/T1 segments.

                            Horner’s Syndrome

                            Recall from ITM that the sympathetic trunk interacts with the spinal nerves or their ventral rami by way of rami communicantes.

                            • The sympathetic trunk runs from T1-L2, so when there’s a brachial plexus injury, Horner’s syndrome is a sign that the T1 nerve root has been avulsed.
                              • Horner’s Syndrome is characterized by decreased pupil dilation, eyelid retraction, and sweating on the side of the nerve root lesion.
                                • Can be caused by damage at any point along the sympathetic pathway.
                              • This makes sense; if the sympathetic trunk is damaged on one side, you’ll have unopposed parasympathetic activity. This results in the clinical findings described.
                              • The other side will have a normal sympathetic trunk, so that side will have exhibit sympathetic activity in a person that just experienced an injury.

                            Dermatomal Weakness

                            • Good ways to assess each dermatome are:
                              1. Thumb = C6 (median & radial nerve innervation).
                              2. Middle finger = C7 (median & ulnar nerve innervation).
                              3. Little finger = C8 (ulnar nerve innervation).
                            • Although the thumb and middle finger have multiple nerves innervating them, they each mainly have 1 involved root.

                            Finger Dermatomal Associations

                              Dermatome Nerves
                            Thumb C6 Radial + median
                            Middle Finger C7 Median + ulnar
                            Little Finger C8 Ulnear

                            Patient Case

                            Case Summary

                            • 33 year old male, MCA (motorcycle accident) May 2013.
                            • Left clavicle fracture, right arm weakness.
                            • Problems:
                              • Left shoulder weakness.
                              • Severe right arm pain.
                              • Right hand and forearm weakness/clumsiness.
                            • P/E Findings
                              • Left Hand
                                • Abnormal Clavicle at SC joint.
                                • Weakness of Deltoid (shoulder Abduction) and Shoulder ER (infraspinatus)
                                • Triceps 4/5.
                                • Good Elbow Flexion (biceps/brachialis).
                              • Right Hand Hyperalgesia → increased sensitivity to pain.
                                • Horners → miosis, hypohydrosis, ptosis, enopthalmous,
                                • 4th 5th finger allodynia, hyperalgesia.
                                • Absent sensation in medial arm and medial hand C7/C8/T1 dermatome.
                                • Good shoulder, Elbow function.
                                • Weak ie. no hand intrinsic function, or thumb Abduction, 0/5 thumb extension.

                            Left Sided Injuries

                            • What caused his deltoid weakness?
                              • Damage to the axillary nerve, which innervates deltoid and teres minor.
                            • Where is the corresponding sensory region?
                              • The deltoid is innervated mainly by the C5 nerve root. (C5)
                              • The corresponding dermatomal region is the lateral upper side of arm (since it’s where a military badge would be placed, it’s sometimes called the Sergeant’s patch).

                            Right Shoulder Injuries

                            • What nerve root are you worried about with his Horner’s Syndrome?
                              • T1 → where the sympathetic trunk overlaps with the brachial plexus.
                            • What muscle(s) are affected if he can’t abduct his thumb?
                              • Abductor pollicis longus (extrinsic).
                              • Abductor pollicis brevis (intrinsic).
                            • What muscle(s) are affected if he can’t extend his thumb?
                              • Extensor pollicis longus (extrinsic).
                              • Extensor pollicis brevis (extrinsic).
                            • An MRI revealed a pseudomeningocele, as shown to the right (axial MRI at the T1 level):
                              • Pseudomeningocele → it looks like an meningocele, but it’s not contained within the dura, but rather by the surrounding soft tissues.
                              • In other words, the dura has actually been damaged, and the CSF is leaking out. Note the outpouching of CSF seen to the right.
                              • This is often seen with a nerve root avulsion; when the nerve root gets pulled out, the CSF ends up oozing out.

                            Nerve Conduction Studies (NCS)

                            NCS Findings

                            • NCS revealed the following:
                            • Right Sided: APB = Abductor Pollicis Brevis, ADQ = abductor digiti quinti, ie. abductor digiti minimi.
                              • Severe axonal loss to the hand muscles, thumb abductors, and thumb extensors (ie. no motor units).
                              • Absent median and ulnar function (median assessed by APB, ulnar assessed by ADQ)
                              • Median antebrachial cutaneous nerve conduction present SNAP, but no sensation. (SNAP = Sensory Neuron Action Potential)
                            • Left Sided:
                              • Left Deltoid Denervation with no deltoid MUAPs (MUAP = Motor Unit Action Potential).
                            • How is it possible that nerve conduction tests showed present SNAPs, but the patient was asensate?
                              • In other words, nerve conduction was normal, but the patient still had no sensation.
                              • This fact that conduction was normal suggests that the dorsal root ganglion (where the cell body lives) and everything distal to that is okay.
                              • However, the fact that the patient is still asensate suggests that there’s nothing that attaches the cell body to the spinal cord.
                              • In otherwords, this is a sign that there’s a nerve avulsion.

                            Remember

                            • Nerve Conduction Studies showing present SNAPs but no corresponding sensation suggests a nerve root avulsion.

                            Treatment

                            • In summary, this patient had:
                              • Severe Right Brachial Plexopathy with Nerve Root Avulsion.
                              • Left Axillary Nerve Palsy.
                              • Left Clavicle Fracture.
                            • As a result, he has lost the ability to effectively (1) abduct his left arm (L deltoid), and (2) use his right hand (due to lower nerve root contributions to the median/ulnar nerve).
                              • The radial nerve (C5,6,7) and the musculocutaneous nerve (C5,6,7) have preserved function, because the avulsion was at the level of T1.
                              • Hence, the action of his right upper arm is largely intact.
                            • What can you do to treat this patient?
                              • Nerve repair surgery.
                              • In general, these types of repairs must be done shortly after the injury (within a year), otherwise the muscle will atrophy and the outcomes of nerve repair will be poor.
                              • Because you need to reattach the disconnected nerve to something, repair will involve rerouting a functional nerve.
                            1. Improving his L Arm Abduction
                              • This was done with a left axillary nerve repair.
                              • Involves redirecting a branch of the functioning left radial nerve to reconnect the axillary nerve.
                              • In this case, a branch of the radial nerve that attached to one of the heads of the tricep was used.
                                • As a result, the patient then ends up with 2/3 heads of his left triceps innervated; his triceps will still work, but it’ll be weaker.
                              • Because his deltoid is now innervated by a branch of radial nerve involved in arm extension, whenever he now wants to abduct his arm, he has to think of extending his arm!
                                • However, eventually, the brain figures out that it doesn’t have to do this, and relearns how to naturally abduct the arm (neuroplasticity).
                            2. Improving the use of his R Hand
                              • Unfortunately, it’s impossible to reconnect an avulsed nerve root.
                                • The nerve root is like a coaxial cable; it contains thousands of cell bodies that are impossible to reconnect individually.
                              • Hence, there’s no way to directly re-innervate the hand.
                              • Instead, just as before, we can redirect other functional nerves.
                                • Redirect the branch of the right MCN to brachialis (musculocutaneous nerve) to AIN (anterior interosseus nerve).
                                • Redirect the branch of the right radial to supinator to PIN (posterior interosseus).
                              • Why is it okay to lose innervation to these muscles?
                                • The biceps brachii can still do the functions of the brachialis and supinator by itself
                                • Recall that the brachialis crosses the elbow joint and is involved in elbow flexion, while the supinator is involved in supination.
                                • Hence, although flexion and supination will be weaker, the biceps will still be able to cover the slack from losing the action of the other 2.
                              • Why is it important to gain innervation to the AIN and PIN?
                                • These two muscles innervate the deep anterior muscles and posterior muscles of the forearm, so reconnecting them will help restore some of his ‘pinching’ capacity.
                                • By being able to use his right arm to pinch to a certain degree, his functional capacity improves tremendously.
                                  • He can grasp objects, button things up, etc.

                            Neuropathic Pain

                            • Neuropathic pain is pain arising from abnormal neural activity secondary to disease or injury of the nervous system.
                              • It remains persistent without ongoing disease.
                            • What are the symptoms of neuropathic pain?
                              • Shooting pain, burning pain, tingling and numbness.
                              • Dyesthesia → abnormal (often painful) sensation.
                              • Hyperalgesia → increased pain response to a painful stimulus.
                              • Allodynia → increased pain response to a non-painful stimulus.
                            • What are features of neuropathic pain?
                              1. Often experienced in parts of the body that otherwise appear normal.
                              2. Generally chronic, severe and resistant to over-the-counter analgesics.
                              3. It is further aggravated by allodynia (touch-evoked pain), and hyperalgesia (increased pain to a painful stimulus).
                            • How is neuropathic pain treated?
                              • Oftentimes it’s highly persistent and difficult to treat.
                              • Narcotics are a poor choice for neuropathic pain → they don’t really help, but make you feel drowsy and out-of-it, and also cause unpleasant GI side-effects (constipation).
                              • Depression medications are more effective.
