            Cervical Soft Tissue Injuries

            Kevan Lu Friday, January 30, 2015

            Lecture Objectives

            1. Identify red flags that may suggest serious cervical injuries.
            2. List common mechanisms that may injure the soft tissues of the c‐spine.
            3. Outline a management plan to treat soft tissue injuries of the c‐spine.

            What is Soft Tissue?

            • Some definitions:
              • “The soft parts of the human body as distinct from bone and cartilage”.
              • “Body tissue except bone, teeth, nails, hair, and cartilage”.
            • Soft tissue includes tendons, ligaments, fascia, skin, fibrous tissues, fat, muscles, nerves, and blood vessels.
            • Soft tissue is important; hust because you don’t have a fracture doesn’t mean it isn’t a serious injury.
              • Soft tissues play an essential role in the stability of the c-spine.

            How Common is C-Spine Pain?

            • Prevalence of neck pain is 9-18%; a more common complaint than LBP.
            • The lifetime incidence of C-spine pain is about 1/3.
            • Has a significant impact on disability and return to work.

            Objective

            • Identify red flags that may suggest serious cervical injuries.
            • Red Flags:
              • Altered mental status.
              • Abnormal motor examination.
              • Abnormal sensory examination.
              • Abnormal or absent reflexes.
              • Paralysis.
              • Neck pain at rest or with isometric neck motion.
              • Decreased cervical ROM.
              • Posterior midline cervical tenderness.
              • Muscle spasm of cervical muscles.
              • Bilateral symptoms.
              • Lower extremity symptoms.
              • Hypotension with bradycardia (neurogenic shock).
              • Diaphragmatic breathing without retractions (recall that C3,4,5 innervate the diaphragm).
              • Autonomic dysreflexia/hyperreflexia.
              • Hypothermia or hyperthemia.

            Objective

            • List common mechanisms that may injure the soft tissues of the c‐spine.
            • Strain → injury to muscle/tendon.
            • Sprain → injury to ligament.
            • Cervical sprains and strains collectively account or 85% of acute, subacute, and chronic neck complaints.

            Remember

            • Strain → injury to muscle/tendon.
            • Sprain → injury to ligament.
            • MNEMONIC → Strain has a T in it, hence it’s a TENDON injury.
            • Common mechanisms of C-Spine Injury:
              1. Whiplash-like injury (flexion/extension).
              2. Repetitive muscle strain → poor biomechanics and posture.
              3. Direct Trauma → remember possibility with concurrent head trauma.

            Whiplash Associated Disorder (WAD)

            • WAD → neck pain after whiplash (eg. after an MVC).
              • Acceleration-deceleration mechanism.
              • The shoulders and lower c-spine are forced forward; upper c-spine and head then extend back and forward.
              • Results in injury to the neck extensors when they’re contracting eccentrically to stop the head from flying forwards after hyperextension.
              • Eccentric contraction → when the muscle is contracting while it’s elongating.
            • Associated Injuries:
              • Partial and complete muscle tears are the most common.
              • ‘S’-shape deformity → when, at some point during the whiplash, the c-spine is put into an ‘s-shape’.
                • This can result in anterior longitudinal ligament injuries and facet joint injuries (C5/6).
            • Findings with WAD:
              • The majority of patients will have neck pain as the primary symptom; a subset will demonstrate other signs.
              • If you’re not sure if it’s WAD, do an MRI to rule it out (recall that MRIs are best for looking at soft tissue).
              • The majority improve by 2-3 months; 82% are symptom free by 2 years.
              • Less than 5% are still severely affected by 2 years.
            • Classification of WAD (not too important):
              • Grade 0: no neck pain, stiffness, or any physical signs are noticed
              • Grade 1: neck complaints of pain, stiffness or tenderness only but no physical signs are noted by the examining physician.
              • Grade 2: neck complaints and the examining physician finds decreased range of motion and point tenderness in the neck.
              • Grade 3: neck complaints plus neurological signs such as decreased deep tendon reflexes, weakness and sensory deficits.
              • Grade 4: neck complaints and fracture or dislocation, or injury to the spinal cord.
            • Management of WAD:
              • Acute:
                • PT, simple mobilization, ‘act as usual’.
                • Avoid C-spine collars; immobilization seems to worsen long-term outcomes.
              • Subacute (weeks - months):
                • Progressive strengthening programs may actually be detrimental.
                • It’s better to take it easier at this point.
              • Chronic (months - years):
                • Best evidence is for modest exercise programs.
                • If not responding to conventional treatment, radiofrequency neurotomy may be used to burn the medial branches of the dorsal rami that innervate the facet joint.

            Repetitive Muscle Strain

            • Repetitive Strain Injuries (RSIs) → injuries to the MSK and/or nervous systems that may be caused by repetitive tasks, forceful exertions, vibrations, mechanical compression, or sustained or awkward positions:
              • Seen often in medical students and residents, as they hunch over their computers.
              • Everyone gets it; some people have persistent pain.
            • Poor baseline head and neck posture may be a contributing factor.
              • ‘The classic hunchback’.
              • This type of posture can either be the root cause of an RSI, or exacerbate an existing one.
              • The reasoning for this is that, by hunching forward, you have a larger moment about the axis of the spine.
              • Because the head isn’t resting directly above the axis of the spine, it applies more torque, putting more strain on the muscles of your neck.
            • Management of Repetitive Strain Injuries:
              • Acitivity modication.
              • Ergonomic assessment.
              • Think of an approach to manage pain: eg. NSAIDs.
              • Consider trigger point injections if appropriate.
            • Trigger Point Injections:
              • Trigger points → hyperirritable areas of tissue that are tender when compressed and can give rise to referred pain.
              • Often associated with myofascial pain syndromes.
              • Can be treated with direct injection of a local anesthetic and/or corticosteroid.
              • More recently, botulinum toxin has begun to be used (botox).
              • Literature → safe, may improve pain, but evidence is generally poor.

            Direct Trauma

            • Association with head trauma common with high cervical spine injuries
              • Increasing recognition of overlap of “Concussion‐like” symptoms and those that may stem from soft tissue of neck, especially regarding headache.
              • The classic example of this is with Sidney Crosby.

            Cervicogenic Headache

            • Cervicogenic Headache → Unilateral head pain of fluctuating intensity that is increased by movement of the head.
              • ie. headache caused by a neck problem.
              • Precipitated by neck movement or sustained awkward head positioning.
              • The headache does not shift from side to side.
              • Typically non-throbbing, non-lancinating (not stabbing), moderate to severe intensity.
            • Neck-strengthening exercises and retraining may play a useful role.
            • Epidemiology and Management:
              • Low prevalence overall, but represents up to 1/3 of all headache complaints.
              • Various pain generators proposed, including facets (C1-4), IVDs, muscles, nerves, and ligaments (basically, everything in the neck ahahaha).
              • May often be associated with occipital neuralgia
                • For instance, with greater occipital neuralgia, there’s irritation to the greater occipital nerve that travels around the back of the neck, and sends sensation to the front of the head.
                • It can often be irritated due to neck muscle injury, which can cause pseudo-entrapment that shoots pain to the front of the head.
              • Treatments → PT, injections possible (including trigger points, nerve blocks).

            General Management Approach to Soft Tissue Injuries

            Objective

            • Outline a management plan to treat soft tissue injuries of the c‐spine.
            • THE GAME PLAN → mnemonic for a general approach for any soft tissue injury.
              • T → Therapy (Physio, OT).
              • HE → Home Exercise.
              • G → Gait Aids (canes, crutches, walkers).
              • A → Adaptive Equipment (braces, splints).
              • M → Modalities (ice, heat, massage).
              • E → Education.
              • P → Pain medications.
              • L → Local treatments (topical or injections).
              • A → Anti-inflammatories.
              • N → Neuropathic and other pain medications.
            • Also consider other sources of stress:
              • Sleep, mood, litigation, work satisfaction, school.

            Summary

            • Take a good pain history (KEY).
            • Rule out serious injury with “RED FLAGS”.
            • Identify the mechanism of injury.
            • Look for concurrent injuries (Facet joints, Radiculopathy, head, shoulder injuries).
            • Formulate “T.H.E. G.A.M.E. P.L.A.N.” and use an evidence informed approach.
            • Recognize impact on patient’s mood, sleep, home, work and recreational activities.
