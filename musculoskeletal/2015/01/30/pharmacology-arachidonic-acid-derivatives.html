            Pharmacology of Arachidonic Acid Deriatives – NSAIDs

            Kevan Lu Friday, January 30, 2015

            Lecture Objectives

            1. To understand the basis for inflammation particularly as it pertains to arachidonic acid metabolism and the enzymatic formation of pro- inflammatory eicosanoids (prostaglandins).
            2. To understand the mechanistic bases for the therapeutic and toxic effects of NSAIDs.

            Inflammation

            • Inflammation is a reaction in the tissues to infection, irritation or injury.
            • It’s characterized by the 5 cardinal signs:
              • Rubor (redness).
              • Calor (heat).
              • Dolor (pain).
              • Tumor (swelling).
              • Functio leasa (loss of function).
            • Functionally speaking, inflammation is a defense mechanism against infection and tumours; it brings immune cells to the vicinity of an injurious process.
              • It’s also important in repair and regeneration after an injury.

            Proposed Mediators of Inflammation

            • There are many proposed mediators of inflammation, including clotting factors, bradykinin, complement proteins, cytokines, etc.
            • The ones that are relevant for today’s lecture are the arachidonic acid metabolites (eicosanoids).
              • Cyclooxygenase products → prostaglandins.
              • Lipoxygenase products → leukotrienes.
              • Epoxygenase products → epoxides.

            Prostaglandins

            • Of these, prostaglandins are the target of NSAIDs.
              • They’re 20C fatty acid derivatives, and modulate a number of key biological processes, including gut integrity and renal function.
            • Prostaglandins are produced from Arachidonic Acid:
              • Arachidonic acid is a polyunsaturated fatty acid (cis-5,8,11,14-eicosatetraenoic acid).
              • Arachidonic acids are located in cell membranes, particularly in the phospholipids.
              • They can either be derived directly from our diet, or can be synthesized from linoleic acid.

            • Effects of Prostaglandins:
              • Gut integrity (cytoprotection of gastric mucosa) → this is why long-term NSAID use can cause gastric ulcers.
              • Haemostasis
              • Vasoconstriction
              • Vasodilation
              • Renal homeostasis → recall that prostaglandins dilate the afferent arterioles, so NSAIDs block them.
                • We saw this in GFR Physiology.
                • This was also why giving an ACEI with an NSAID could lead to a dramatic drop in the GFR.
              • Labour and parturition (giving birth) → this is why misoprostol (a prostaglandin analogue), is an abortifacient.
            • Different prostaglandins mediate these different effects, depending on their distribution in your body.

            Cyclooxygenases

            • Cyclooxygenases → convert ARACHIDONIC ACIDS into PROSTAGLANDINS (as well as Thromboxanes and Prostacyclins).
            • There are 2 isoforms that have been identified: COX-1 and COX-2.
              • COX-1 → constitutively expressed.
              • COX-2 → inducible.

            1. Cyclooxygenase 1 (COX-1)

            • Also known as Prostaglandin-endoperoxide synthase 1
            • Constitutively expressed.
            • Expressed in platelets, gut and kidney
              • In platelets, converts AA to TXA2.
              • In the gut, converts AA to cytoprotective prostaglandins.
            • Recall from Blood that the mechanism of ASA was to block COX-1 mediated conversion of AA to TXA2 in the platelets!
            • Recall from Digestive System & Nutrition that the blocking of cytoprotective prostaglandins by NSAIDs could lead to PUD.
            • The activity of COX-1 can be induced under certain conditions such as inflammation.

            2. Cyclooxygenase 2 (COX-2)

            • Also known as Prostaglandin-endoperoxide synthase 1
            • Inducible; induction is enhanced by inflammation.
            • Present in synovial membranes in arthritis, although it also seems to have a physiological role.

            Non-Steroidal Anti-Inflammatory Drugs (NSAIDs)

            • Since they play a key role in inflammation and inflammation-mediated pain, prostaglandins are an attractive therapeutic target.
            • The classic application of this is with NSAIDs, drugs which inhibit pain and inflammation by inhibition of prostaglandin synthesis.
            • There are 3 classes of NSAIDs
              1. Salicylates.
              2. Traditional NSAIDs.
              3. COX-2 Selective Drugs.

            1. Salicylates

            • Example → Aspirin (acetylsalicylic acid).
            • ASA irreversibly inhibits COX-1 and alters COX-2 activity to preferentially synthesize lipoxins (anti-inflammatory) instead of prostinoids (pro-inflammatory).
              • Because it binds irreversibly, although its half life is very short, it’s biologic effect is quite long.
            • Pharmacology of ASA:
              • Absorption → readily absorbed in the small intestine.
              • Distribution → 50-80% protein bound, VD 0.2 L/kg.
              • Metabolism → primarily hepatic, metabolism can easily be saturated with supra-therapeutic doses (bad).
              • Excretion → renally excreted.
            • Indications:
              • Analgesic, antipyretic, anti-inflammatory (high-dose ASA), anti-thrombotic (low-dose ASA).
            • Contraindications:
              • Hypersensitivity to NSAIDs.
              • Children with viral illness (risk of Reye’s Syndrome, which causes fatty liver and cerebral edema).
              • Reye’s syndrome is potentially fatal, so don’t give aspirin to sick children!
            • Adverse Drug Reactions:
              • GI bleeding.
              • Tinnitus (in high doses).
              • Hypersenstivity
              • Prolonged bleeding.
            • Toxicity → our body’s conjugation systems rapidly saturate.
              • Drugs that must be conjugated in order to be excreted generally have this behaviour, of rapid saturation.
              • As a result, the kinetics change quickly from first order to zero order, resulting in substantial increases in serum drug concentration after a certain point.
              • ASA toxicity associated with significant mortality (2% with an acute overdose, 25% with a chronic overdose).

            2. Traditional NSAIDs

            • Example → Ibuprofen.
            • Developed because aspirin proved to be so useful and popular, so people began to look for other drugs (especially once the side effects of steroids became better known).
            • Ibuprofen is a proprionic acid derivative, with an efficacy and adverse effects profile similar to ASA.
              • However, there’s NO CONNECTION between non-ASA NSAIDs and Reye’s Syndrome.
              • As a result, Ibuprofen, and other non-aspirin NSAIDs are safe to use with sick children!
            • Examples of traditional NSAIDs:
              • Ibuprofen
              • Indomethicin
              • Naproxen
              • Diclofenac
              • Ketorolac
            • Problems with Traditional NSAIDs:
              1. Afferent arteriolar vasoconstriction:
                • NSAIDs reduce GFR, so they’re very bad in patients with renal damage, or who are already dehydrated and have a low GFR.
              2. GI Bleeding
                • Can cause PUD (typically gastric ulcers; recall that H. pylori is the more common cause of duodenal ulcers).
                • In Canada, about 400 deaths/year, and 4000 hospitalizations arise due to GI bleeding.
                • As a result, some people have opted to give patients a PPI or H2 antagonist concomitantly, as protection.
                • This also prompted research into more selective prostaglandin inhibitors → the COX-2 inhibitors.

            3. COX-2 Inhibitors

            • Recall that COX-1 was constitutively expressed, while COX-2 was inducible.
            • The idea was thus to produce a COX-2 specific inhibitor, which would avoid some of the side-effects caused by constitutive cyclooxygenase inhibition.
            • Examples of COX-2 Selective Inhibitors:
              • Rofecoxib (Vioxx)
              • Celecoxib (Celebrix)
              • Valdecoxib (Bextra)

            • However, it was found amidst a great deal of media controversy that Vioxx increased the risk of MIs.
              • There are a number of competing theories as to why this might be the case:
                • Selective inhibition of PGI2 (prostacyclin) without TXA2 inhibition (which is COX-1 specific) might have lead to increased thrombosis.
                • COX-2 may produce cardioprotection following ischemia.
                • COX-2 inhibitors may cause hypertension.
              • But regardless of the cause, Vioxx and Bextra were taken off the market.
              • The only remaining COXIB is Celecoxib (Celebrix).
            • Health Canada now recommends that COX-2 selective inhibitors can still be used, but patients on it must be carefully selected and monitored.

            Summary of NSAIDs

            1. Salicylates (eg. ASA):
              • These have diverse uses, including as an anti-thrombotic agent.
              • However, they carry the risk of Reye’s syndrome is used in children with viral illnesses.
              • They also carry the risk of GI bleeding and reduction of renal blood flow.
            2. Traditional NSAIDs (eg. Ibuprofen)
              • These don’t have the risk of Reye’s syndrome.
              • However, they still carry the risk of GI-bleeding, and afferent arteriolar vasoconstriction.
            3. COX-2 Inhibitors (eg. Celecoxib)
              • These were developed to avoid the risk of GI bleeding.
              • However, it was found that one of them (infamously, Vioxx), resulted in an increased risk of MIs.

            Acetaminophen

            • Acetaminophen (aka. Paracetamol outside of Canada and the US) is an analgesic-antipyretic.
              • Trade name: Tylenol.
              • Acetaminophen is less potent than NSAIDs, but also carries fewer side effects.
              • Acetaminophen has no anti-inflammatory effects:
                • Hence, it’s not an NSAID.
                • It’s mechanism of action is still under debate; some believe that it inhibits a ‘3rd cyclooxygenase’ called COX-3; others believe that it blocks COX (hehe), but also blocks their inflammatory activity.
            • Pharmacology of Acetaminophen:
              • Absorption → excellent GI absorption.
              • Distribution → widely distributed, VD 1.0 L/kg.
              • Metabolism → metabolized in therapeutic doses by glucuronic acid conjugation.
              • Excretion → metabolites eliminated in the urine.
            • Indications → analgesia, anti-pyretic.
              • Not an anti-inflammatory.
            • Contraindications:
              • Hypersensitivity to acetaminophen.
              • Use with caution in the presence of liver disease or alcoholism.
            • Adverse Drug Reactions:
              • Very infrequent.
              • GI discomfort is probably the most common.
            • Drug Toxicity
              • Conjugation pathways are easily saturated.
              • After this point, metabolism switches to oxidative (Phase I) pathways, which result in a more toxic metabolite.
              • This can result in hepatic injury, and may progress to hepatic failure.
              • Currently the most common cause of acute liver failure in the UK and US.
              • N-acetylcysteine (NAC) can be used to bind these toxic metabolites, and treat this hepatic injury.

            Summary of Acetaminophen

            • Can be used as an analgesic or anti-pyretic, but it has no anti-inflammatory activity.
            • Hence, it’s less potent than NSAIDs, but also carries fewer side effects or adverse drug reactions.
            • However overdose leads to hepatic toxicity; Acetaminophen overdose is the most common cause of acute liver failure.
