####Objective - Apply the principles of resuscitation to prioritize management and investigations in Paediatric patients utilizing the Paediatric Assessment Triangle.

Initial Assessment

  • Always start with the ABC’s; it’s in the order of urgency!
    • A closed airway will kill you before impaired breathing, which will kill you before circulatory compromise.
  • Pediatric Assessment Triangle (PAT):
    • Provides you with clues as to what’s abnormal.
    • The 3 parts of the PAT are:
      1. Appearance, rather than airways.
      2. Breathing.
      3. Circulation.
  • The idea with the PAT is that, if you look at what’s abnormal, you get a hint at what you’re dealing with, as seen below.

Pneumonia Normal Abnormal Normal
Sepsis Abnormal Abnormal Abnormal
Intoxication Abnormal Normal Normal

##Basic Life Support Differences

  • CPR will be done faster in a child than in an adult.
  • Rescue breathing:
    • Adult → RR 12 (1 breath/5 seconds).
    • Child → RR 16/min.
    • Infant → RR 20/min.
  • CPR hand position:
    • Adults → 2 hand technique (> 5 cm).
    • Children → 1 hand technique (5 cm).
    • Infants → 2 finger technique (4 cm).

##Important Physiologic Differences

  • Children don’t get hypotension, unless they’re about to die.
  • In children, they can’t effectively raise stroke volume, so they increase CO by increasing HR.
    • , so if SV can’t increase, HR has to.
  • Corrollaries:
    1. HYPOTENSION IS A LATE SIGN OF SHOCK.
    2. TACHYCARDIA IS WORRISOME IN CHILDREN.
  • The same thing applies with respiration, where children can’t easily change their tidal volume:
    • , so if the TV can’t increase, then the RR has to.
  • Broadly speaking, in order to compensate, children just do things faster.

####Important - Children cannot effectively change their stroke volume or tidal volume. - So when they have circulatory or respiratory problems, the only way they can compensate is by changing their HR or RR. - Hence TACHYCARDIA or TACHYPNEA are worrisome signs in children.

WHAT DO YOU NEED FOR INTUBATION?

T - 8 min.

  • Initial needs for intubation: – Suction – Oxygen – Airway Equipment – Pharmacology – Monitoring Equipment
  • Mnemonic → SOAP ME

T - 5 min.

  • Preoxygenation.
  • Preoxygenate them before the procedure itself (which puts them to sleep and reduces their ventilatory drive).
  • Prevents the procedure from exacerbating the problem.
  • Especially important due to decreased pulmonary capacity of the child and the negative effects of hypoxia

T - 2 min.

Pre-medications - Premediate, sedate, paralyze them empirically. - Drugs that may be useful for the process of intubation. Atropine. - Protects against reduced vagal tone with paralysis; prevents patient from going bradycardic when you sedate them. - Dose: 0.02 mg/kg. - Minimum dose of 0.1 mg/kg. Lidocaine. - Blunt increased ICP when you manipulate the airway.

T - 1 min.

Sedate the patient before you intubate them: - It’s humane. - It prevents them from struggling and making it difficult to establish and airway. Sedatives -> Etomidate, fentanyl, morphine, thiopental, ketamine, midazolam. - Each has +s and -s. Paralytics -> Depolarizing, non-depolarizing.

Cricoid pressure. BURP = backwards upwards retrograde pressure. INTUBATE.

• Check Tube Placement – Due to short trachea – Rt mainstem bronchus intubations common • Radiograph • Sedation - Make sure air entry is bilateral and equal on both sides to make sure this doesn’t happen.

Circulation

  • Poor Feeding
  • Lethargy
  • Irritiability
  • Decreased LOC
  • Sunken fontanelle
  • prolonged capillary refill time
  • Tachycardia
  • mottling of the skin
  • cool extremities
  • decreased urine output

Most common causes of shock: 1. HYPOVOLEMIC = most common. 2. DISTRIBUTIVE = most worrisome. - SEPSIS.

Circulating blood volume – 80 cc/kg • For acute fluid resuscitation – Isotonic Solution – Normal Saline – Ringer’s Lactate – Acute bolus of 20cc/kg over 30min-1 hour

Difficult IV Access • Common problem in Pediatrics • Often patients transported to CHWO without IV access • Frequently multiple attempts required • In resuscitations, often compounded level of difficulty given status of patient - Use of central lines - Femoral, subclavian, or even jugular vein. • Use of umbilical lines - If very young infant, within first week or 2, you can still use umbilicus. • Use of different anatomic sites - Periabdominal, scalp veins, etc. • Use of an Intra-osseous - In traumatic situations, where you need an IV within 90 seconds, and other things aren’t working. - Huge ass needle that you stick into a bone, typically anterior tibia. 1-3 cm below tibial tuberosity. - But into bone marrow, and infuse medications into bone marrow. - IO-gun = needle attached to screwdriver. - O__O.

  • You know it’s in when you feel a lack of resistance after passing through the bony cortex.
  • If the needle stands up straight w/o support.
  • The ability to aspirate bone marrow.
  • The free flow of fluid without s/q infiltration.

Contraindications:

  • Open fracture.
  • Open wound at insertion site.
  • Existing increased compartment pressures; worried about compartment syndrome.

Complications:

• Extravasation of fluids or medications into subcutaneous tissue • Subcutaneous abscess, osteomyelitis, bacteraemia • Epiphyseal injury, Fracture • Fat Embolus

Tips for Clerkship

  1. Don’t be a bird (regurgitate everything); think as it comes about what it means.
  2. Be focused and think of ‘what’.
    • Imagine you could only check 2 things, and DO THEM well!
    • Don’t just regurgitate your P/E approach.
  3. Come up with a plan
    • Pretend as if the consultant doesn’t exist.
    • You learn so much more if you try to come up with investigations and a plan.
  4. Don’t cop out.
    • You only have a few times to acquire a certain skill.
    • Once you become a senior resident, no one double-checks!
    • Don’t do “I don’t want to look because I don’t want to put the patient through this”.
    • Do everything!
  5. Put the patient first.
    • Stay if it helps; don’t just ditch when you can!
  6. Do a little bit every day!
    • Don’t just leave everything till the last minute!
    • Some people start off amazing and end up being disappointing; others are the opposite!
    • Just do a little bit every day!
  7. Find role-models.
    • Aspire to be like them!
  8. Don’t let the work and abuse detract from the fact that you’re doing great things!
