General Approach

####Objective - Describe a general approach to the recognition and initial management of an undifferentiated patient presenting to the ED with an acute toxic ingestion.

  • General approach:
    1. Assess the ABCs.
    2. Resuscitate.
    3. Ascertain what ingestion or exposure has occurred → try to find out what toxidrome is involved.
    4. Decontaminate/eliminate.
    5. Antidote.
  • Risk Factors with the ABCs:
    • Airway → toxic risk factors include:
      • Ingestion of corrosives (highly acidic or basic).
      • Inhalation of toxic substances.
      • Drugs causing CNS depression.
      • COMA → if the patient is comatose, remember DON’T for treatment: dextrose, oxygen, narcan and thiamine.
      • This is known as the COMA cocktail; a series of drugs that have minimal harm, and can be given to the undifferentiated coma patient.
    • Breathing → immediate threats to breathing are: You can see respiratory fatigue as a rise in the PCO2 even when the bicarbonate is low.
      • Metabolic acidosis causing hyperventilation (compensatory respiratory alkalosis). This is a threat because it causes respiratory fatigue over time, depending on the degree of acidosis.
      • Hypoventilation → respiratory depression.
      • CNS depression or excitation.
      • CVS depression → cardiac failure.
    • Circulation → immediate threats to circulation are:
      • HR, BP.
      • QT prolongation → risk of Torsades.
      • QRS prolongation → VT or PEA arrest (PEA = pulseless electrical activity).

####DON’T Mnemonic - DON’T → Dextrose, oxygen, narcan, thiamine. - These have minimal harm, and potential gain (eg. even if the patient wasn’t hypoglycemic, giving them dextrose won’t hurt).

  • What should you ask on history?
    • Interview the patient, friends, family, police and/or paramedics.
    • AMPLE History → Allergies, Medications, PMHx, Last meal, Events (surrounding the presentation, ie. HPI).
      • Rather than a full history, here, we only focus on the emergent issues.
    • Figure out the W’s → who, what, when, where, why, how much?
    • Co-ingestants? Emesis?
    • Call POISON CENTRE for information and management advice.
  • What should you look for on Physical Exam?
    • Airway → if not patent, intubate early!
    • Breathing → look for hypoventilation or hyperventilation:
      • Hypoventilation (mostly sedatives) → ethanol, sedative hypnotics, carbon monoxide, cyanide, opiates.
      • Hyperventilation → amphetamines, salicylates, cocaine, drug withdrawal.
    • Circulation → brady/tachycardia, hyper/hypotension. Generally intuitive; things that activate the SNS or inhibit the PSNS have excitatory effects, and vice versa.
      • Bradycardia → beta-blockers, CCBs, digoxin, mushrooms.
      • Tachycardia → anticholinergics (look like symphathomimetics, but with NO SWEATING), amphetamines, antihistamines, cocaine, stimulants, drug withdrawal. Note that TCAs (tricyclic antidepressants) are listed as both hypotensive and hypertensive drugs; this is because they have > 1 mechanism of action.
      • Hypotension → antihypertensives (duh), antipsychotics, TCA, ethanol, AV node blockers, opiates, sedatives.
      • Hypertension → amphetamines, cocaine, PCP, TCA, sympathomimetics.
    • Temperature → hypothermia or hyperthermia.
      • Hypothermia → antipsychotics, ethanol, hypoglycemia, opioids, sedative hypnotics.
      • Hyperthermia → amphetamines, anticholinergics, cocaine, symphathomimetics, withdrawal, antihistamines, TCA.
    • Eye Findings:
      • Mydriasis (pupil dilation) → anticholinergics, sympathomimetics, Demerol (an older opiate that paradoxically dilates the eyes), withdrawal states.
      • Miosis → (pupil constriction) → cholinergics, narcotics, phenothiazine (an antipsychotic).
      • Nystagmus → alcohol, barbituates, dilantin, PCP, carbamazepine.
    • Abnormal Odours (don’t need to remember):
      • Garlic → arsenic, organophosphate.
      • Bitter almonds → cyanide, apricot pits.
      • Rotten eggs → hydrogen sulfide.
      • Acetone → isopropyl alcohol, ethanol.
      • Shoe polish → nitrobenzene.
      • Violets → turpentine.
      • Pears → chloral hydrate.
      • Peanuts → vacor.
    • Skin bullous lesions → could suggest a bite, caustic chemicals, etc.

The Poison Control Centre

####Objective - Explain the role of the Poison Control Centre.

  • The Poison Control Centre gives you the following information:
    1. Is the drug they took toxic?
    2. Pharmacokinetics
      • The drug solubility helps you determine how to eliminate the toxin.
        • Water soluble drugs can be eliminated via the urine or through dialysis.
        • Protein bound drugs have to be out-competed by a competitive agonist.
        • Lipophilic bound drugs will ‘hide’ in fat stores, and will be more toxic to the CNS.
      • The half-life determines how long you have to watch the patient before you can rule out toxicity. This can often vary based on dosage if the drug changes pharmacokinetics (eg. it changes from 1st to zero-order kinetics).
    3. Decontaminate? Eliminate?
    4. Labs and diagnostics.
    5. Indicated treatments and therapeutic contraindications.

Toxidromes

####Objective - Describe and recognize the following toxidromes, and identify at least one drug capable of causing the toxicity: - Anticholinergic. - Cholinergic. - Sympathomimetic. - Opiate. - Sedative/hypnotic.

  • Here, you take all the findings listed previously on Hx and P/E, and you integrate them.
  • Put together your assesment of the pulse, BP, respiration, temperature, bowel sounds, skin, mentation, pupils, and start to evaluate if a toxidrome exists.
  • Toxidrome → a toxidrome is a group of signs and symptoms that tend to consistently result from a particular toxic exposure.
    • Recognizing the toxidrome aids in the diagnosis and management of the unknown poisoning.
    • Not all features of a toxidrome are consistently present, and partial presentations do not suggest a less severe disease.
    • The ‘tox physical’ → vitals, mental status, pupils, GI, skin assessment, muscous membranes, GU.

Anticholinergic Toxidrome

  • Recall from Cholinergic Pharmacology II back in Cardio, that the features of this were:
    • Hot as a hare → people are no longer able to sweat, can’t thermoregulate.
    • Dry as a bone → also because they can’t sweat anymore. Also, since we block salivation (the S in SLUDGE), we have a dry mouth.
    • Red as a beet → flushing due to systemic vasodilation. (recall that the SNS causes systemic vasoconstriction and skeletal muscle vasodilation)
    • Blind as a bat → pupil dilation affects the reflex of accomodation.
    • Mad as a hatter → if the muscarinic antagonist passes the BBB, it can cause CNS effects.
  • How do you differentiate this from a sympathomimetic toxidrome?
    • Most of the features (↑ HR, BP) are shared by drugs that stimulate the SNS.
    • The key difference is the ABSENCE OF SWEATING.
    • Generally, blocking PSNS (by blocking Ach) gives you similar effects as stimulating the SNS, with one exception → sweating!
    • With a sympathomimetic toxidrome you have sweating; with an anticholinergic toxidrome you don’t.

####Remember - The diference between a sympathomimetic toxidrome and an antimuscarinic toxidrome is the ABSENCE OF SWEATING.

  • Is ‘antimuscarinic’ the same as ‘anticholinergic’?
    • Although they’re used interchangeably, technically, antimuscarinic is the more accurate term, since not all acetylcholine receptors are blocked by an anticholinergic toxin.
    • Recall that there are 2 types of cholinergic receptors:
      1. Muscarinic.
      2. Nicotinic.
    • Anticholinergic toxins competitively block the post-ganglionic muscarinic receptors, but have little/no influence on the pre-ganglionic nicotinic receptors.
    • See Cholinergic Pharmacology I for review.
  • What antimuscarinic drugs are there? Note that atropine, the prototypical anticholinergic drug, is derived from Jimson weed.
    1. Tricyclic Antidepressants.
    2. Antipsychotics.
    3. Antihistamines.
    4. Antiarrhytmics (Quinidine).
    5. Plant alkaloids (Jimson Weed).
    6. Mydriatic ophthalmic drops.
    7. Meperidine.
    8. Carbamazepine.
    9. Cyclobenzaprine.
  • MEMORY TIP → these are all the anti- drugs: tricyclic anti-depressants, anti-psychotics, anti-histamines, anti-arrhytmics.

####Remember - Most antimuscarinic drugs are the anti- drugs: - Tricyclic antidepressants. - Antipsychotics. - Antihistamines. - Antiarrhytmics.

  • Management:
    1. ABCs as always.
    2. FLUIDS → since everything is ‘turned on’ for these patients.
    3. BENZODIAZEPINES → since everything is turned on, and these people are very agitated, you need to calm them down.
      • Benzodiazepines are the safest drug in any toxic patient, so it’s the go-to drug to use if a patient is agitated.
      • DO NOT GIVE THEM AN ANTIPSYCHOTIC; they’re antimuscarinic, so this could actually worsen their symptoms.
    4. Call the poison centre.

####Antimuscarinic Management 1. ABCs. 2. Fluids. 3. Benzodiazepines. 4. Call the poison centre.

  • Clinical Pearl → Benzodiazepines are the safest drug in any toxic patient, so it’s the go-to drug to use if a patient is agitated.

Cholinergic Toxidrome

70 + 2 x age for lower limit of normal in a pediatric patient. 50th %ile 90 + 2 x age Pouring fluids out of every orifice.

FOR THIS ONE, be careful: Decontaminate the patient and protect from exposure first. As soon as you expose yourself and your staff, you guys could get sick. ABCs. CALL POISON CENTRE!! DUMBELs mnemonic: - Diarrhea. - Urination. - Miosis - Bradycardia + Bronchospasm. - Emesis. - Lacrimation. - Salivation. - FLUIDS POURING OUT OF EVERY ORIFICE.

  • Acetylcholine acts on BOTH nicotinic and muscarinic here.
  • As opposed to anticholinergic, which is just muscarinic.
  • Some drugs cause toxicity by inhibiting acetylcholine esterase, which breaks down Ach. Examples: ORGANOPHOSPHATES (PESTICIDES) Carbamates Some muscarinic mushrooms (Clitocybe and Inocybe)

Management; 1. Decontaminate Antidote: An antimuscarinic = ATROPINE. Call poison centre

Sympathomimetic Toxidrome

  • What is the drug of choice immediately? BENZOS!
  • What do you load with them if they don’t stop seizing? Anti-epileptics/anti-convulsant.
    • NEVER USE PHENYTOIN!!!!!!!!!!!!!
    • AVOID IN TOXIC SEIZING PATIENT? SEE laTER.
    • V-tach.
  • Review alpha-1, alpha-2, beta-1, beta-2. Drugs: Alpha-1: NE, phenylephrine.

Drugs of abuse: Cocaine - Blocks NE too. - Blocks sodium channels -> widened QRS. - Dopamine -> delusions. - Reuptake of serotonin -> SSRI side effects. MDMA.

Support ABCs. Cooling measures, IV’s, fluids. BENZOS. Avoid use of phenytoin (dilantin), in seizing patient. Blocks Na channels -> widened QRS, risk for v-fib.

Management: Cocaine toxicity with wider QRS, treat with bicarb infusion. - Alpha blockade first to avoid unopposed alpha effect (phentolamine). - If you use a beta blocker, it should be used after an alpha blocker.

Opiates

  • To intubate or not to intubate?
  • Coma cocktail:
    • Dextrose, Oxygen, NARCAN, Thiamine.
  • Opiates bind to opioid receptors, mu, kappa, delta.
    • Antagonized by naloxone (Narcan)
    • Cause vasodilation and can increase ICP.
  • Most cause histamine release from mast cells causing urticaria.
  • Tolerance develops rapidly.
  • Everything is turned down.
    • But no fluids pouring out of every orifice as with cholinergic.
  • Dependence acutely interrupted can lead to WITHDRAWAL.
  • But not life threatening.

Supportive care - ABCs, cardiac monitor, iv fluids… • Management of toxicity = antidote Naloxone (Narcan) • Management of withdrawal = BZD, clonidine, ibuprofen, suboxone (naltrexone) • COWS Clinical Opiate Withdrawal Scale

Sedative-Hypnotic

ALCOHOL and BENZOS.

Sedative = medication that produces a calming effect • Hypnotic = agent that promotes sleep • Sedative hypnotics are drugs administered solely for their sedative effects ie. BZD, Zolpidem, Propofol • Ethanol is a sedative-hypnotic

• Mechanism of action is to be antimuscarinic and increase GABA activity • CNS sedation, slurred speech, ataxia, respiratory depression, hypothermia • BZD overdose EKG is normal, CNS sedation • Antidote - benzos: Flumazenil (do not use if chronic BZD dependent patient, because of withdrawal leading to potential seizures and epilepsy)

Glutamate turns on GABA turns off.

Specific Toxicities

Carbon Monoxide Acetaminophen ASA Ethylene Glycol Methanol Tricyclic Antidepressants

TCA = amitriptyline (not used much for depression anymore, largely supplanted by SSRIs, but now is also used largely for neuropathic pain). - Pathognomonic finding for TCA overdose: terminal deflection in aVR is upwards. - TCAs block sodium channels, widens QRS. - Risk are seizures. - Risk are ventricular dysrhythmias.

TCAs = diverse effects. - Most common thing is sinus tachycardia. - Widened QRS is a late finding.

Antidote for TCA is sodium bicarbonate, to donate sodium back to help with sodium blockade. Works for any wide QRS.

Toxic alcohol High osmolar gap.

Expected = 2 salts plus a sugar bun. Actual. Difference = osmolar gap. If there’s a gap, think toxic alcohols (which can contribute). - Methanol, ethylene glycol. - Elevated gap, >10. - Metabolic acidosis + elevated osmol gap. - Classic story = BLIND DRUNK. Often ‘snowstorm apperance to patient’. - Toxic alcohols water soluble, amenable to dialysis if severe.

Toxic effects due to metabolite = formic acid. Block alcohol dehydrogenase = fomepizole. Outcompete methanol = ethanol.

Ethylene Glycol: Positive bifringent Calcium oxalate crystals in urine.

Salicylates. Recall that ASA toxicity was unique. Primary metabolic acidosis with overcompensation due to actions on the respiratory centre. Hence you actually have a normal or even alkalotic pH.

Salicylate ion is created, trap it in urine, so it can’t cross tubule back into blood stream. Treatment is sodium bicarb to alkalinize urine. Need it to have pH 7.5 or greater, or it won’t be effective.

Any CNS deysfunction in the presence of a mixed acid base disturbance = ✔ASA level

Done nomogram for 6h post-ingestion to determine toxicity.

Call Poison Centre • Low serum pH allows unionized salicylate into brain • Urine alkalinization to trap ion in urine • Glucose supplementation because neuroglucopenia results despite normal plasma levels
