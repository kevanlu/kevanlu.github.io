    Acid Base Physiology

    Kevan Lu Friday, April 11, 2014

    Acid-Base Independent Learning Module

    • See GU Module 1.

    Lecture Objectives

    • Should be able to examine an arterial blood gas and electrolyte panel and recognize the presence of the acid-base disorder and describe pathophysiology.
    • Provide a differential diagnosis.
    • Describe appropriate management.
    • See the previous lecture from Respiration & Airways on Blood Gas Analysis for the respiratory component of blood gas analysis.

    Why is Acid-Base Homeostasis Important?

    • Respiration generates CO2, which has to be gotten rid of, as it generates carbonic acid as it combines with acid.

    • Food ingestion (especially proteins) also increases acids in the blood.

    • pH is maintained in a narrow range between 7.35 and 7.45.

    • pH outside 6.8 to 7.8 results in permanent cell damage and death.

      • Recall that enzymatic function is highly pH dependent.
      • So a pH far outside of the normal range can have far reaching consequences!

    Acid-Base Terminology

    Some labs report the total CO2 or tCO2 instead of the HCO3. The total is the combination of HCO3 and dissolved CO2 which is quite trivial, so these values are usually very close to one another and can be used interchangeably.

    • Arterial Blood Gas (ABG), provides the PaCO2, the pH and the HCO3.

    • Acidosis is a process that tends to lower the pH.

      • PaCO2↑, or HCO3↓

    • Alkalosis is a process that tends to raise the pH.

      • PaCO2↓, or HCO3↑

    • Acidemia is a low pH.

    • Alkalemia is a high pH.

    • Don’t confuse acidosis/alkalosis with acidemia/alkalemia!

    The Anion Gap

    • The anion gap (AG) is the difference between the predominant cation (Na+), and the two predominant anions (Cl- and HCO3-) of the ECF.
    • We don’t include K+, because it’s actively pumped out of the ECF, and has a low concentration in the plasma (< 5 mmol/L).

    The Anion Gap

    $$ \text{Anion Gap} = [\text{Na}^+] - \left( [\text{Cl}^-] + [\text{HCO_3}^-] \right)$$
    • The normal anion gap is 12 → this is due to the presence of negatively charged proteins, especially albumin.
    • The difference between the observed and expected anion gap is the \( \Delta AG \), and should usually be 0.
    • If the \( \Delta AG \) is elevated, this means that there are lots of unaccounted for anions! This is worrisome.

    Correcting the Anion Gap

    • Since the normal anion gap is mainly due to the presence of negatively charged albumin, you have to correct the anion gap when the albumin levels deviate from normal.

      • This could be seen with liver disease, where the liver isn’t producing proteins properly.
      • Or with protein malnutrition (Kwashiorkor).

    • The normal serum albumin is 40 g/L. For each 10 g/L↓ in albumin, you have to decrease the expected anion gap by 3.

      • In other words, the fractional change in the albumin corresponds to the fractional change in the expected anion gap.
      • If the albumin was half of 40, the expected anion gap would be half of 12, or 6.

    Remember

    • Normal albumin = 40 g/L.
    • For a 10 g/L fall in albumin, drop the expected anion gap by 3.

    How does the Kidney regulate Acid-Base Levels?

    • Remember that the proximal tubule has carbonic anhydrase in its brush border which facilitates reabsorption of filtered bicarbonate.

    • The proximal tubule then senses acidosis → retains bicarbonate.

      • It also increases ammonia secretion, which traps the H+ that’s been secreted further down in the tubule.
      • This is because excreted acid is in the urine as ammonium (NH4+).

    • Also, recall that the α-intercalated cells of the collecting duct secrete H+ driven by aldosterone.

    Remember

    • Excreted acid appears in the urine as ammonium (NH4+).

    • Loss of filtered bicarbonate is called proximal renal tubular acidosis (RTA), also called type II RTA.

      • This makes sense, because bicarbonate reabsorption and loss occurs in the brush border of the proximal renal tubule.
    Why are the RTA numbers so weird? Because they were named in the order they were discovered! Also, Type III RTA doesn't exist → it was discovered, found to be wrong, and then thrown out!

    • Failure to secrete H+ distally is called distal RTA.

      • Remember that this is the role of the α-intercalated cell, which also depends on the function of the principal cell.
      • If it’s just a problem with the α-intercalated cell, then the principal cell will be functioning normally, so there will be normal K+ excretion.
      • This will be termed hypokalemic distal RTA → Type I RTA.
      • If there’s a problem with aldosterone, then neither the principal cell nor the α-intercalated cell will work properly (since the latter relies on the K+ export of the former).
      • This causes hyperkalemic distal RTA → Type IV RTA.

    • Type I RTA, where there’s a problem with the α-intercalated cell only, is pretty rare! Generally, aldosterone problems are more common.

    Renal Tubular Acidosis Types

    • Proximal Renal Tubular Acidosis

      • Excess bicarbonate excretion from the proximal convoluted tubule.
      • This is Type II RTA.

    • Distal Renal Tubule Acidosis

      1. Type I RTA → also known as hypokalemic distal RTA.

        • A problem with the α-intercalated cell only → spares the function of the principal cell, leading to normal potassium excretion.

      2. Type IV RTA → also known as hyperkalemic distal RTA.

        • A problem with aldosterone, which affects both the principal cell and the α-intercalated cell. This affects K+ excretion, leading to hyperkalemia.

    Remember

    • Type I = hypokalemic distal RTA = α-intercalated cell only.
    • Type II = proximal RTA = proximal tubular cells.
    • Type III = doesn’t exist.
    • Type IV = hyperkalemic distal RTA = principal cell and α-intercalated cells.

    Bicarbonate Absorption

    Remember we saw carbonic anhydrase function in the conversion of bicarbonate in RBCs in the Gas Transport & Diffusion lecture!
    • Carbonic anhydrase in the brush border converts the HCO3 into H2O and CO2, which can then diffuse into the proximal tubular cell.
    $$HCO_3^- + H^+ \rightarrow H_2CO_3 \rightarrow H_2O + CO_2$$
    • To get the bicarbonate from the proximal tubular cell into the blood, the reverse reaction happens.
    • Carbonic anhydrase in the proximal tubular cell converts the H2O and CO2 into HCO3, which can then get symported into the blood.
    $$H_2O + CO_2 \rightarrow H_2CO_3 \rightarrow HCO_3^- + H^+$$

    • As a result, carbonic anhydrase inhibitors, cause sodium bicarbonate loss, because they block re-absorption of bicarbonate from the proximal convoluted tubule.

      • One example of a carbonic anhydrase inhibitor is Acetazolamide.
      • It’s used to deal with altitude sickness, by causing a metabolic acidosis which would lead to a respiratory alkalosis.
      • This would cause the body to naturally hyperventilate, which would prepare people when they would climb mountains.
    Again, not to be confused with Fanconi Anemia, which we saw in Blood. They just happened to be named after the same guy.

    Remember

    • Fanconi’s Syndrome = spectrum of symptoms associated with proximal tubular cell disorders.

      1. Renal Tubular Acidosis
      2. Glucosuria, phosphaturia, aminoaciduria

    Remember

    • Carbonic anhydrase inhibitors lead to bicarbonate loss at the proximal convoluted tubule.
    • One example is Acetazolamide.

    Acid-Base Compensatory Responses

    1. Metabolic Acidosis

    • In metabolic acidosis, acid gain or bicarbonate loss should result in a 1:1 equivalent reduction in PCO2, called compensatory respiratory alkalosis.

      • Why is it a complete match? Because you can always breathe more heavily! So this compensatory response is swift and complete.
      • So if you ↓HCO3 by 10 → ↓PCO2 by 10.

    • Note that a breathing pattern that increases both the rate and depth of breathing is called Kussmaul Respiration.

    Metabolic Acidosis

    • With metabolic acidosis, there is a 1:1 reduction.
    • Memory Tip → you can always breathe faster.

    2. Metabolic Alkalosis

    • In metabolic acidosis, the increase in bicarbonate should result in an increase in PCO2, called compensatory respiratory acidosis, but it is not quite equivalent → there’s a 10:7 ratio.

      • Why is this not quite equivalent? Although you can theoretically always breathe slower, this is limited by your oxygen demand.
      • So if you ↑HCO3 by 10 → ↑PCO2 by 7.

    Metabolic Alkalosis

    • With metabolic alkalosis, there is a 10:7 ratio.
    • Memory Tip → you can breathe a bit slower, but this is limited by your need for oxygen, so it’s not quite 1:1.

    3. Respiratory Acidosis

    • In respiratory acidosis, there is an acute response (hours), where the cells act as a buffer to control the acidosis to a small extent, about 10:1.

      • So short term, you have a trivial cell buffering effect.
      • So if you ↑PCO2 by 10 → ↑HCO3 by 1.

    • There is also a chronic response (days), which is mediated by an increase in renal acid secretion, which is stronger than cell buffering → 10:4.

      • This is 4 times stronger than the cell-buffering effect!
      • So if you ↑PCO2 by 10 → ↑HCO3 by 4.

    Respiratory Acidosis

    With respiratory acidosis there is both an acute and chronic response.

    • Acutely, there will be a 10:1 ratio.

    • Memory Tip → Cell buffering is weak.

    • Chronically, there will be a 10:4 ratio.

    • Memory Tip → renal buffering is 4× stronger than cell buffering.

    4. Respiratory Alkalosis

    • Similarly, in respiratory alkalosis, there is an acute response (hours), where the cells can shift their buffers back to the ICF, which is an approximately 10:2 response.

      • Just as before, the effect of the cell buffer shift is pretty trivial.
      • So if you ↓PCO2 by 10 → ↓HCO3 by 2.

    • The chronic response (days), functions to the same degree as with respiratory acidosis (10:4), and works by preserving renal acids and reducing bicarbonate loss.

      • This renal response is twice as strong as the cellular response, and the ratio is the same as with respiratory alkalosis.
      • So if you ↓ PCO2 by 10 → ↓HCO3 by 4.

    • Chronic respiratory alkalosis is pretty rare → patients won’t chronically hyperventilate.

      • One exception for this is when you’re pregnant → then you’re minute ventilation increases physiologically, and you have a slight respiratory alkalosis.

    Respiratory Alkalosis

    With respiratory alkalosis there is both an acute and chronic response.

    • Acutely, there will be a 10:2 ratio.

    • Memory Tip → Cell shift is small.

    • Chronically, there will be a 10:4 ratio.

    • Memory Tip → renal response is 2× stronger than with cells. The ratio is the same as with respiratory acidosis.

    Summary of Compensatory Responses

    1. Metabolic Acidosis → 10:10 ratio.

    2. Metabolic Alkalosis → 10:7 ratio.

    3. Respiratory Acidosis

      • Acute → 10:1 ratio.
      • Chronic → 10:4 ratio.

    4. Respiratory Alkalosis

      • Acute → 10:2 ratio.
      • Chronic → 10:4 ratio.

    Acid-Base Disorders

    • With an acid-base disorder, there is typically a primary disorder, folloved by a compensatory response.

    • Occasionally, two or more disorders may exist.

    • Some examples include:

      1. Metabolic acidosis superimposed on metabolic alkalosis, leading to a ‘normal’ pH and HCO3, but with an elevated anion gap.
      2. Salicyate Poisoning (ASA toxicity), leading to metabolic acidosis, and a overcompensatory respiratory alkalosis. This leads to an alkalemic pH.

    • Why does an ASA overdose cause overcompensation?

      • ASA gives you a metabolic acidosis by uncoupling oxidative respiration, resulting in lactic acidosis.
      • However, it also affects the respiratory centre in the brain, causing it to overcompensate.
      • This causes a stronger respiratory response than expected, leading to an alkalemic pH.

    • This is one of the rare cases where the primary response is weaker than the compensation.

    Remember

    • This will be on the exam!
    • ASA toxicity causes a primary lactic acidosis, and then a overcompensatory respiratory alkalosis because of effects on the respiratory centre of the brain.

    Metabolic Acidosis

    Anion Gap Metabolic Acidosis

    • Also known as AGMA.
    • This is where the anion gap is higher than expected.
    • This is due to an increase in H+ coupled with an increase in an unmeasured anion, that’s leading to the increase in the anion gap.
    • Examples of unmeasured anions include lactate, ketoacids, salicyates, ethylene glycol, or methanol metabolites.
    • It can me remembered with the MUDPILES acronym:

    MUDPILES Acronym

    The differential diagosis for an AGMA is:

    1. M → Methanol.
    2. U → Uremia.
    3. D → Diabetic Ketoacidosis.
    4. P → Phenformin (Replaced with Metformin).
    5. I → Iron poisoning.
    6. L → Lactic Acidosis.
    7. E → Ethylene Glycol.
    8. S → Salicylate Poisoning or Starvation Ketoacidosis.
    • If you find a wide AG, you must find its source.
    The reason it's 2Na + Glucose + BUN, is because the Na is the predominant cation in the blood, and the number of anions in the urine should match! So instead of using all those, we just multiply the Na by two!
    • One way is to measure the osmolality and compare it with the expected osmolality, \(\text{Osm} = 2 \times Na + \text{urea} + \text{glucose}\).
    • An increase in this gap is suggestive of toxic alcohols → methanol, ethylene glycol.

    The Expected Osmolarity

    $$\text{Osm} = 2 \times Na + \text{urea} + \text{glucose}$$
    • Can be remembered as two salts plus a sugar bun.
    • An bigger osmolal gap than expected is suggestive of methanol or ethylene glycol

    Non Anion Gap Metabolic Acidosis

    • Also known as NAGMA.

    • This is when the anion gap is not wider than expected, and we have a metabolic acidosis.

    • In this case, it’s not because of the presence of some hidden anions, it would just be due to less bicarbonate or more H+.

      1. Bicarbonate Loss:

        • Loss from the proximal tubule (Type II RTA).
        • GI tract losses (diarrhea).

      2. Failure to secrete H+

        • Problem with the α-intercalated cell (Type I RTA).
        • Problem with aldosterone affecting both the α-intercalated cell and the principal cell (Type IV RTA).

    • To diagnose NAGMA, you have to take a history.

      • Focus on diarrhea, drugs such as carbonic anhydrase inhibitors (Acetazolamide), aldosterone inhibitors or K-sparing diuretics.
      • ACEI, ARBs, Heparin, etc, can also affect the RAAS, affecting aldosterone release.

    • Examine the urine net charge (the urine anion gap), and the urine pH.

      • If urine [Na+] + [K+] is much less than urine [Cl-], then this implies that there is significant urinary [NH4+] and the distal nephron is doing its job
      • If urine pH is <5.5, this implies that distal nephron is maximally acidifying the urine

    Urine Anion Gap/Net Charge

    1. If Cl >> Na + K, then this implies that NH4 is present.

      • This means that H+ is properly being excreted from the α-intercalated cells.
      • This suggests that it’s not a failure to secrete H+ → it’s more likely to be due to bicarbonate loss (proximal tubule, GI problems).

    2. If Cl ≤ Na + K, then this implies that NH4 is absent.

      • This means that the distal tubule isn’t properly acidifying the urine.
      • This suggests that it’s more likely to be a failure to secrete H+, and not a problem of bicarbonate loss.

    • This is because the ‘hidden’ cation that makes up the difference is the ammonium!

      • This would be simpler if we could measure the urine ammonium.
      • But unfortunately, ammonium dissociates rapidly, so it’s extremely difficult to measure.
      • As a consequence, we have to measure ammonium indirectly using the anion gap!
    Caveat
    • Note that we assume that Na, Cl, K, and NH4 are the only ions in the urine.
    • In cases where there are other anions, we may have an artificially low anion gap, because there are other unmeasured anions that are not contributing.
    • This would make it look as if there’s not enough NH4, making it look like a failure to secrete H+.
    • Some examples include sulfates, hippurate, bicarbonate, and ketoacids.

    Remember

    • NAGMA can be due to:

      1. Bicarbonate Loss.
      2. Failure to Secrete H+.

    • The urine anion gap or the net charge helps us differentiate between them.

      1. If Cl >> Na + K, then it’s more likely to be bicarbonate loss, because there’s plenty of H+ in the urine.
      2. If Cl ≤ Na + K, then it’s more likely to be a failure to secrete H+, because there isn’t much H+ in the urine.

    Treatment of Acidosis

    1. Treat the Underlying Cause

      • Methanol and ethylene glycol can be treated with fomepizole or IV ethanol.
      • These outcompete them for binding to alcohol dehydrogenase.
      • Hemodialysis is often required to remove the toxic metabolites.

    Treating Methanol/Ethylene Glycol Poisoning

    1. Fomepizole → used now instead of IV ethanol as a competitive inhibitor of alcohol dehydrogenase!
    2. IV ethanol → now replaced with fomepizole because fomepizole doesn’t make patients drunk (damn). Still used when fomepizole is unavailable.
    3. Hemodialysis → remove the toxin quickly because metabolism of the toxic metabolites takes a while.

    • Remember that providing buffer (sodium bicarbonate) generates more CO2 and if the patient can’t breathe properly, they will retain CO2 and potentially worsen intracellular acidosis.

      • Don’t give IV bicarbonate to patients that can’t breathe properly!

    • Also remember that sodium bicarbonate contains sodium. This goes straight to the ECF, pulling water into the ECF.

      • This may be a problem in critically ill patients with ECF volume overload, poor cardiac function, etc.
      • So don’t give IV bicarbonate in patients with ECF overload.

    Remember

    • Don’t give sodium bicarbonate to patients who:

      1. Can’t breathe → can’t get rid of CO2.
      2. Have ECF Overload. → can’t handle volume overload.

    • However, if you have a bicarbonate in the single digits, you’re at a high risk for having a rapid drop in pH, so you should give it anyway!

    • However, when the pH < 7.10 or the HCO3 < 10-12 mmol/L, you’re at the point where a further drop in HCO3 or a rise in PCO2 will lead to a rapid drop in pH.

    • At this point, bicarbonate is indicated.

      • So if you see a bicarbonate in the single digits, you would err towards giving it, even with the above caveats.

    Metabolic Alkalosis

    • Metabolic alkalosis is typically not a disease by itself. It’s usually caused by too much mineralicorticoid activity → typically too much aldosterone.

      • This can be primary, as with Conn’s syndrome, adrenal hyperplasia, reninoma (tumour producing renin), etc.
      • This can also be secondary, associated with ECF volume contraction and stimulation of RAAS, or with diuretics, vomiting, or Bartter’s/Gitelman’s.

    • Urine lytes are helpful:

      • Urine [Cl-] < 10, suggests a secondary cause (unless on diuretics).

        • If you’re vomiting and you’re alkalotic, your body tries to get rid of the bicarbonate, and it brings sodium with it.
        • So when you’re volume depleted, and you have metabolic alkalosis, usually the urine calcium will be a better indicator than the urine sodium of volume contraction.

      • High urine [K+], confirms that aldosterone is working.

    • Often associated with hypokalemia, because excess aldosterone function will increase K+ out, leading to potassium wasting.

    Remember

    1. Low Urine Cl is better than urine Na when looking at volume contraction with metabolic alkalosis.
    2. High Urine K confirms the action of aldosterone.

    Treatment of Alkalosis

    • Treat the underlying cause.

      • If it’s primary hyperaldosteronism, then you’d give an aldosterone antagonist such as spironolactone.
      • If you are volume contracted, trigging the RAAS response which jacks up aldosterone activity, then give isotonic saline!

    Complication

    • If you have ongoing low potassium, because of the action of aldosterone, this can actually trick the kidney into thinking that you’re acidotic, and not alkalotic.

      • This is because K+ and H+ can exchange for one another in the proximal tubular cell.
      • So when you’re hypokalemic, K+ exchanges for H+, causing the proximal tubular cell thinks you’re acidotic.
      • This means that you need to replace the K as well, and not just replace the volume!

    Remember

    • If you’re volume depleted, you have to correct the low K as well as correcting the volume, to fix the abnormal kidney response (It thinks that you’re acidotic and not alkalotic).

    Summary

    1. Examine the pH to determine if it’s acidosis or alkalosis.

      • If the pH is low (<7.35), then is this primary metabolic acidosis or respiratory acidosis.
      • If the pH is high (>7.45), then is this primary metabolic aklalosis of respiratory alkalosis.

    2. Determine the compensation. Is it acute or chronic? Is it sufficient? Is there a mixed disorder? (eg. with ASA toxicity).

    3. Examine the anion gap, and compare it to what’s expected, factoring in albumin.

    4. If metabolic acidosis, determine if it’s AGMA or NAGMA.

      • If AGMA, look at the osmolal gap. If elevated, consider Methanol/Ethylene glycol poisoning.
      • If NAGMA, either consider bicarbonate loss or a failure to excrete H+.

    5. If it’s alkalosis, is the ECF expanded (primary mineralicorticoid problem), or contracted (secondary mineralicorticoid problem).
