    Acute Kidney Injury II

    Kevan Lu Tuesday, April 15, 2014

    Acute Kidney Injury Mini Case 2

    You evaluate a 60 yo man who undergoes fem-pop bypass for severe PVD. His is hypotensive post-op and now has oliguria and rapidly increasing BUN, Cr. Brown ‘muddy’ casts are seen on urinalysis.

    Diagnosis?
    1. ATN
    2. Acute Glomerulonephritis.
    3. Contrast induced nephropathy.
    4. Post-obstructive uropathy.

    • This is an example of a patient with Acute Tubular Necrosis.

    • If you’re acute pre-renal state is too prolonged, it’ll transform into ATN, (which is a renal AKI).

      • With ATI, the tubules undergo necrosis and will slough off into the urine.
      • This are seen as muddy brown casts → aka. granular casts.
      • These are pathognomonic for ATN.

    • If you read the case, he sounds prerenal!

    • However, you should suspect transformation to ATN if you see:

      1. Granular Casts.
      2. No response to IV resuscitation.

    • So if you gave a patient IV fluids, but his creatinine and BUN continued to rise, this would be suggestive of ATN.

    • A pre-renal state is reversible. A renal state is acutely irreversible.

    • Long-term, the tubules can regenerate, which takes up to 6 weeks.

    • NEVER BIOPSY ATN. Clinical Dx.
    • But if you did, this is what you see.
    • Look at the funky tubule in the middle with shit in it!
    • That’s what forms the granular casts in the urine.
    • Blah blah blah.

    Renal Causes of Acute Kidney Injury

    Renal Causes

    • Renal causes of AKI can be categorized by the compartment that they affect:

      1. Acute Tubular Necrosis (ATN) → affects the tubules.
      2. Acute Interstitial Nephritis (AIN) → affects the interstitium.
      3. Glomerulonephritis → affects the glomeruli.
      4. Atheroemboli → affects the blood vessels.

    • Of the kidney problems, the most common one is ATN → the tubules are affected.

    • These conditions can be further subdivided:

    1. Tubular → Acute Tubular Necrosis.

      • Ischemic ATN → necrosis due to ischemic insult.
      • Nephrotoxic ATN → necrosis due to cytotoxic insult.

    2. Interstitial → Acute Interstitial Necrosis.

      • Drug Hypersensitivity.
      • Infection.
      • Idiopathic.
      • Autoimmune.

    3. Glomerular → Glomerulonephritis.

      • Nephritic Syndromes

    4. Vascular → Renal atheroembolic disease.

    1. Acute Tubular Necrosis

    ARF = Acute Renal Failure. Don't forget that this was what Acute Kidney Injury used to be called!
    • Acute Tubular Necrosis* is the most common cause of AKI/ARF in hospitalized patients.
    • There are three phases of ATN:

    1. Initiation Phase.

      • This is the pre-renal state.
      • Renal injury lasting hours to days.

    2. Maintenance Phase.

      • Lasts days to weeks.
      • GFR and urine output at lowest.

    3. Recovery Phase.

      • Postacute tubular necrosis diuresis.
      • The renal tubules will begin to slowly regenerate at this point, so the GFR and urine output will slowly return to baseline.
      • YOu can still experience uremia and hypovolemia as tubular function is not completely restored.

    1. Ischemic Acute Tubular Necrosis

    • Ischemic Acute Tubular Necrosis → ATN caused by extended ischemia.

    • Since Ischemic ATN is caused by an extended prerenal state, it can be considered as part of the prerenal ‘spectrum’.

    • This also means that ischemic ATN has the same causes as pre-renal AKI:

      • True volume contraction → hemorrhage, diarrhea, diuretics etc.
      • False volume contraction (with CHF).
      • Relative volume contraction (with sepsis).
      • Renal vasocontriction → hepatorenal syndrome.
      • Medications → NSAIDs, tacrolimus, cyclosporine, for instance.

    1. Nephrotoxic Acute Tubular Necrosis

    • Contrast and aminoglycosides most often associated with nonischemic ATN.
