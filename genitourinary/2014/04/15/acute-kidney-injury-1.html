    Acute Kidney Injury I

    Kevan Lu Tuesday, April 15, 2014

    Acute Kidney Injury Independent Learning Module

    • See GU Module 2.

    Lecture Objectives

    • Acute Kidney Injury is one of the most common diagnoses you’ll see in internal medicine.

    • By the end of this lecture, you will be able to:

      1. List a differential diagnosis for acute kidney injury.
      2. List diagnostic tests that help flush out the cause of acute kidney injury.
      3. Outline the management of patients with acute kidney injury based on the underlying cause.

    Definition of Acute Kidney Injury (AKI)

    • Acute Kidney Injury, formerly known as acute kidney failure, is defined as satisfying any of the following 3 criteria:

      1. Increase in serum creatinine of more than 44 mEq/L.

      2. An increase in serum creatinine of more than 50% from baseline.

      3. A reduction in the calculated creatine clearance of 50%.

        • This one is listed in textbooks, but it’s actually not very accurate, because the creatinine clearance assumes that you’re in a steady state, and your GFR isn’t changing rapidly.
        • In acute kidney injury, this isn’t the case!

    • One example of when you wouldn’t be in a steady state is the following:

      • Say you’re an 80 y/o man with a huge prostate.
      • This blocks the urinary outflow tract, making the patient anuric.
      • As a result, there’s no creatinine clearance, so the serum creatinine has gone from 100→200.
      • In this case, the GFR would be zero!
      • However, the calculated creatinine clearance (using the Cockcroft Gault equation, or the MDRD equation), would be non-zero!

    • So the calculated creatinine clearance is a poor measure of kidney function when there is an acute problem.

    Remember

    • Don’t use the Cockcroft Gault Equation or the MDRD Equation in patients with AKI, because they’re not in a steady state!

    Acute Kidney Injury

    • Acute Kidney Injury (AKI) is defined as having any of the following 3 criteria:

      1. Increase in serum creatinine of over 44 mEq/L.
      2. Increase of serum creatinine of more than 50% from baseline.
      3. Decrease in the calculated creatinine clearance of 50%.

    • Don’t use the creatinine clearance because it isn’t a good measure of acute injury → it assumes that the patient is in a steady state.

    • Generally, AKI is suspected when serum creatinine goes up quickly, in the span of days to weeks.

    Epidemiology of AKI

    • AKI is a common problem:

      • 5% of hospitalized patients develop AKI.
      • Of these, 0.5% will require dialysis.
      • 20% of critical care admissions develop AKI.

    • AKI usually doesn’t present by itself → it’s usually in the context of multi-system organ failure!

      • The archetypical example would be with severe sepsis, where there can be end-organ damage in multiple systems.

    Etiology of AKI in the Hospital Setting

    AKI can be split into 3 causes → prerenal, renal, and postrenal causes:

    1. Prerenal Causes → before the kidney.

      • These are due to impaired blood flow to the kidney.
      • So true volume contraction can cause this.
      • CHF can cause this because there’s alse volume contraction.
      • Sepsis can cause this because there’s relative volume contraction due to systemic vasodilation.

    2. Renal (Intrinsic Causes) - There are 4 compartments of the kidney that can be damaged, leading to AKI.

      1. The tubules → Acute Tubular Necrosis (ATN).
      2. The interstitium → Acute Interstitial Nephritis (AIN).
      3. The glomeruli → Glomerulonephritis.
      4. The blood vessels → Atheroemboli.

    3. Postrenal Causes

      • These are due to obstruction.
      • To have an obstruction causing an AKI, it has to block both kidneys, because otherwise, your other kidney will compensate to maintain a normal serum creatinine.
      • Bilateral kidney stones, swollen prostates, and tumours are obvious examples.
      • To rule out obstruction, you gotta do a renal ultrasound, to look for signs of hydronephrosis.

    • The most common causes are prerenal and renal causes.

    • You want to rule out prerenal and postrenal causes immediately, because these are the ones that can be fixed immediately.

      • If you miss these, and there’s resultant permanent damage, then you’re in deep shit!
      1. Rule out pre-renal causes → do a full physical exam for volume depletion!
      2. Rule out post-renal causes → do a renal ultrasound looking for hydronephrosis!

    Remember

    • The Etiology of AKI can be conceptually divided into 3 categories:

      1. Prerenal → something wrong with blood flow.
      2. Renal (Intrinsic) → something wrong with the kidney.
      3. Postrenal → something wrong with obstruction.

    • Prerenal and postrenal causes should be ruled out first, because those are the ones that are most easily treated.

    • To rule out a prerenal etiology, do a physical exam.

    • To rule out a postrenal etiology, do a renal ultrasound.

    Morbidity & Mortality of AKI

    • Mortality rates range from 7-80% depending on patients other co morbidities.

    • This rate has remained unchanged since the advent of dialysis because of increasing age and co morbid conditions.

    • Most common cause of death associated with ARF are sepsis, cardiac failure and respiratory failure.

    • Mortality rates are lower for nonoliguric AKI (>400ml/day) then with oliguric AKI (<400 ml/day).

      • So mortality is associated with the severity of the kidney damage!
      • Patients that have low urine output do worse than those with normal urine output.

    Pathophysiology of AKI

    As we’ve said before:

    1. Prerenal → a decrease in effective flow.

    2. Renal → damage to the kidney.

      • This damage can be to (1) tubules, (2) interstitium, (3) glomerulus, or (4) blood vessels.

    3. Postrenal → due to obstruction distal to the kidney.

    Patient History

    • A history will give you the etiology 80% of the time! So it’s useful to be able to take a good history.

    • Look for past nausea, vomiting, or diarrhea, suggestive of volume loss.

    • Hx of heart disease, liver disease, previous renal disease.

    • Do a Hx geared towards obstruction → kidney stones, benign prostate hyperplasia.

      • Kidney stones will often present with flank pain.
      • Prostate hyperplasia will result in the patient feeling like they need to pee, but they can’t.

    • Recent illnesses, changes in medication.

    • Edema, change in urination → symptoms of kidney injury.

    • Recent radiology studies → dyes used in radiology can sometimes be nephrotoxic.

    • Rashes → could be suggestive of autoimmune dysfunction.

    Physical Exam

    1. Look at the ECF Volume Status

      • Blood pressure, heart rate.
      • JVP, lung sounds, mucous membranes, peripheral edema, skin turgor, etc.

    2. Look for signs of possible causes

      • Rash or synovitis suggestive of an autoimmune response.
      • Swollen prostate suggestive of distal obstruction.

    Workup for AKI

    1. Renal Ultrasound

      • Probably the most important test with an AKI, because you need to rule out hydronephrosis (due to obstruction).
      • This is going to be on the exam! Don’t forget it.

    2. Urinalysis

      • Urine sediment (U/A) → looking for casts, cells, protein.

      • Urine electrolytes (UNa) and Urine Cr → to calculate FeNa/FeUrea.

        • UNa = Urine Sodium.
        • FeNa = Fractional Excretion of Sodium (remember SU/P over CU/P).
        • FeUrea = Fractional Excretion of Urea (same idea as with sodium).

      • Urine Osmolarity.

      • Urine eosinophils → remember that this were suggestive of interstitial nephritis.

    Acute Kidney Injury Mini Case 1

    A 75 yo man, NH resident, with Alzheimer’s dementia admitted for aspiration pneumonia. HR 120 beats/min, BP 90/60, BUN 50 and Cr 180.

    Diagnosis?
    1. ATN from Abx.
    2. Glomerulonephritis.
    3. Dehydration.
    4. AIN from NSAIDs.

    • His creatinine a week ago was 80, so going from 80→180 is an increase of > 50% from baseline → AKI.

    • The patient is tachycardic, hypotensive, suggestive of a prerenal cause.

      • His dementia supports a possible cause of the dehydration.
      • Looking at their BUN:Creatinine Ratio, it’s 50,000/180 = 277!
      • This is > 80, so this is further suggestive of volume contraction.

    • Hence, dehydration is the most likely cause for the AKI.

    • Note: Hyaline casts are normal → they’re just empty casts that everyone pees out. They’re just Tamm-Horsfall protein surrounding nothing.

    Prerenal Causes of Acute Kidney Injury

    1. Decreased Effective Circulating Volume

    Remember from the GFR physiology lecture that NSAIDs block prostaglandins, resulting in afferent arteriolar vasoconstriction, reducing renal flow.

    • This is where we have Impaired renal blood flow. This can be due to:

      • True volume contraction → hemorrhage, diarrhea, diuretics etc.
      • False volume contraction (with CHF).
      • Relative volume contraction (with sepsis).
      • Renal vasocontriction → hepatorenal syndrome.
      • Medications → NSAIDs, tacrolimus, cyclosporine, for instance.

    • Hepatorenal Syndrome → Where patients with liver cirrhosis don’t clear certain cytokines that contrict the renal blood vessels.

    • Hyaline casts can be seen, but are not abnormal.

      • As mentioned before, hyaline casts are empty casts of Tamm-Horsfall protein.

    • Treat with fluid boluses → eg. isotonic saline.

    Urine Indices in AKI

    Recall that the FENa is normally around 1%, and if it's less than that, then it's suggestive of Na reabsorption due to RAAS → volume contraction.

    • These are all indices for volume contraction that we’ve seen before!

      1. Urine [Na] < 20 mmol/L.
      2. BUN:Creatinine > 80:1.
      3. FENa < 1%.

    Postrenal Causes of Acute Kidney Injury

    1. Post-Obstructive Uropathy

    • This is effectively the only cause of postrenal AKI.

    • This is where we have bilateral obstruction distal to the kidney.

      • We can also have a unilateral obstruction, if the patient only has one kidney, and that’s blocked off.

    • Patients with sudden onset anuria are highly suggestive of post-obstructive uropathy.

    • Primary obstructive causes include:

      • BPH → Benign Prostate Hyperplasia.
      • Prostate & Cervical Cancer.
      • Kidney stones (bilateral).
      • Retroperitoneal fibrosis → AI phenomenon that scars the retroperitoneum and the ureters.

    • You will almost always see hydronephrosis on a renal ultrasound.

    • The rare times you don’t is when the patient is extremely volume contracted.

      • The patient will have no flow out, but they’ll also have no flow in!

    • Note: Be careful when you fix an obstruction.

      • You want to monitor for a severe post obstructive diuresis → where all the backlogged urine gushes out.
      • Patients can sometimes end up peeing out a tremendous amount of fluid after the obstruction clears, and end up volume contracted.
      • Then they’ll go from a postrenal AKI to a prerenal AKI!

    Causes of a Post-Obstructive Diuresis

    1. Natriuresis

      • This is what we described before. After the obstruction is cleared, the body will naturally try to excrete the backlogged urine.

    2. Nephrogenic DI

      • Often times, the diuresis persists past when the patient is euvolemic.
      • This is sometimes because of tubular damage resulting in acute nephrogenic diabetes insipidus, affecting the tubular response to ADH.
      • This keeps the aquaporins open even past when the patient is euvolemic, resulting in volume contraction.

    3. Osmotic Diuresis

      • During an AKI, there will be a build-up of urea.
      • Since urea is osmotically active, it’ll draw water out into the urine, resulting in increased urine flow out.
