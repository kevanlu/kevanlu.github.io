    Glomerulonephritis & UTIs Small Group

    Kevan Lu Tuesday, April 29, 2014

    Case 1

    A 7 year old male is brought to your office by his mother who informs you that her son’s urine is clear but that he has some puffiness around his eyes.

    1. What is the simplest screening test for renal cause of edema in this patient?

      • Suspicious of nephrotic syndrome → specifically Minimal Change Disease.
      • In that case, you would want to look for macroalbuminuria first and foremost → a urine dip-stick would work. (Recall, sensitive for > 300 mg/day albumin).

    2. What is the best test to measure the patient’s kidney function?

      • The gold standard is the renal clearance of inulin, but no one does it.
      • The serum creatinine is very good for estimating kidney function, but isn’t sensitive to falls in kidney function of < 50%.
      • The MDRD is the best equation for estimating the GFR, and would probably also be a good test to use here.

    3. What is the most likely cause and how would you manage it?

      1. High dose prednisone for 4 weeks, > 80% response rate.
      2. Kidney biopsy only if no response to prednisone.
      3. Cyclosporine/Cyclophosphamide only if the kidney biopsy confirms the diagnosis of minimal change disease, and it’s steroid resistant.

    Case 2

    A 65 year old male complains of recent onset symmetrical swelling in his lower extremities, fatigue and shortness of breath. Creatinine is 180 µmol/L, urinalysis shows protein and blood and microscopy shows red cell casts. Ultrasound shows normal sized kidneys.

    1. Pathologically, what type of renal disease is this likely to be?

      • Glomerulonephritis → the RBC casts are pathognomonic.

      • Note that glomerulonephritis can actually be categorized as:

        • Proliferative Glomerulonephritis → nephritic syndrome. Where there’s proliferation of the parietal cells in the glomerular basement membrane.
        • Non-Proliferative Glomerulonephritis → nephrotic syndrome. Where there’s no proliferation of cells.

      • So in this case, the RBC casts suggests nephritic syndrome.

    2. What is the differential diagnosis here?

      • Type I → If α-GBM antibodies are found.

        1. Anti-GBM disease
        2. Goodpasture Syndrome → this is actually Anti-GBM disease where you also have hemoptysis as well.

      • Type II → If you see immune complexes.

        1. IgA nephropathy → if you see α-IgA antibodies.
        2. Henoch-Schonlein Purpura → if you have IgA nephropathy + a rash.
        3. Post-infectious Glomerulonephritis → if you see ASOT.
        4. SLE → If you see ANA, α-DNA, low complement.
        5. Cold Abs → For cryoglobuminemia. Also Hep B surface antigen and α-Hep C antibodies.
      Wegener was a German pathologist who also happened to be a nazi, so the name of the disease was changed to a more politically-correct one!

      • Type III → Pauci-immune GN, aka the classic vasculitities.

        1. Wegener’s Granulomatosis → now known as Granulomatosis with Polyangiitis. Would expect to see c-ANCA.
        2. Microscopic Polyangitis → if you see p-ANCA.
        3. Churg-Strauss Syndrome → if you see p-ANCA & c-ANCA.

    3. What additional history and physical signs would you look for?

      • Prior illnesses and infections:

        • Post-infectious Glomerulonephritis → would have been sick 2 weeks ago.
        • IgA Nephropathy → onset can be concomitant with the disease.

      • Joint pain, pleuritis, chest pain, rash, if suspicious of lupus.

      • Possible HepB/HepC infection → signs of IVDU, purple rashes, sexual history, etc.

      • Possible allergic symptoms would be more suggestive of Churg-Strauss.

      • Past history → eg. you need to know a past serum creatinine to know what his baseline was!

      • Allergic symptoms → Churg-Strauss will often present with concomitant allergic symptoms.

    4. What noninvasive and invasive tests would you arrange?

      • Immunology screening. As said before:

        • Anti-GBM antibodies for Anti-GBM Disease or Goodpasture.
        • α-IgA antibodies for IgA Nephropathy.
        • ASOT for post-infectious glomerulonephritis.
        • ANA, α-DNA, C3, C4 for lupus.
        • Cold Abs for Cryoglobuminemia.
        • c-ANCA, p-ANCA for the pauci-immune causes.

      • Biopsy → scarring and crescentic glomeruli suggest a worse prognosis. Active inflammation suggests a better one.

    5. What treatments might you institute?

      1. High dose steroids → Prednisone, Solu-Medrol (Methylprednisolone).
      2. Cyclophosphamide.
      3. Plasmaphoresis.

    Remember

    With the treatment of rapidly progressive glomerulonephritis, you generally need:

    1. High Dose Steroids.
    2. Cyclophosphamide.
    3. Plasmaphoresis.

    Case 3

    A 40 year old male presents to your office complaining of weight gain and symmetrical pitting edema. He denies orthopnea and PND and has no stigmata of liver disease. His urinalysis reveals occasional RBC’s, with no cellular casts and > 3 g/L protein.

    1. What general type of glomerular pathology is this patient likely to have?

      • Generalized edema and macroalbuminuria is suggestive of nephrotic syndrome.
      • Since the level of proteinuria is the biggest prognostic factor for the progression of kidney disease, this is pretty serious!

    2. What simple noninvasive investigations would you order?

      • Urine Albumin:Creatinine → has supplanted 24h urine collection as the best way to assess proteinuria.
      • Renal U/S → use this to assess kidney size, for biopsy. Useful if a biopsy is need later on.
      • Serum Lipids → hyperlipidemia is part of the definition of nephrotic syndrome.
      • Serum glucose → diabetes is a possible cause of secondary nephrotic syndrome.
      • Protein electrophoresis → in case multiple myeloma is causing his proteinuria (light chains).

    3. How do you make a specific diagnosis in this patient?

      • Renal Biopsy.

    4. What is the differential diagnosis of this patient?

      • Primary Causes:

        1. Minimal Change Disease → most common in children.
        2. Membranous Nephropathy → most common in caucasians.
        3. Focal Segmental Glomerulosclerosis → most common in blacks.

      • Secondary Causes:

        1. Diabetes.
        2. Amyloidosis.
        3. Lupus.

      • Note: (1) Lupus, (2) Membranoproliferative Glomerulonephritis aka. MPGN, and (3) IgA nephropathy can present as either a nephritic syndrome, or a nephrotic syndrome.

    5. What would be your management?

      • You have general treatment, and you have treatment specific to the diagnosis.

      • General Treatment → aimed towards the edema and proteinuria.

        • Salt restriction → non-pharmacological.
        • Loop diuretics → Lasix.
        • ACE Inhibitors → proteinuria.
