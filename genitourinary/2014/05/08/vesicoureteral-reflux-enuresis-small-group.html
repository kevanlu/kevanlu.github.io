Vesicouretal Reflux & Enuresis Small Group

Kevan Lu Thursday, May 08, 2014

Lecture Objectives

  • By the end of the session students should be able to:
  1. Describe the presenting signs and symptoms of urinary tract infections (UTI) in an infant, preschooler and school aged child.
  2. State how a UTI is diagnosed in children.
  3. Describe the treatment approach to UTI in children.
  4. List the investigations indicated for a child with a documented UTI.
  5. Define vesicoureteral reflux and outline the principles for treatment.
  6. Describe an approach to the child with diurnal enuresis.
  7. State the classification of nocturnal enuresis.
  8. Describe the natural history of nocturnal enuresis.
  9. Outline the treatment approach to nocturnal enuresis.

Case 1 – Maria

Maria is a one-year-old girl who is brought in to your office today by her mother with a chief complaint of fever, vomiting and loose stools. It was thought to be a viral gastroenteritis. She has had a low grade fever for three days. She vomited 6 times on the first day of her illness, and has had several episodes of liquid stools.

  • What else do you want to know in the history?

    • Diet, sick contacts, their intake/hydration status.
    • Has it happened before.
    • Activity level, voiding problems.
    • Signs of infection: cough, runny nose, difficulty breathing, family history.
    • Perinatal screening problems → pregnancy on time, any problems near the pregnancy?

Additional Information

Maria is at your office now because of persistent fever. The vomiting and diarrhea have resolved, but she is breastfeeding less well than usual. Her mother notes that her urine seems to have a “strong” odour, and that she is not as playful as usual. She hasn’t had any known sick contacts. Maria has no cough, URI symptoms or rash. Past history is unremarkable and she is on no medications.

  • How would you characterize Maria’s symptoms and what diagnoses would you consider?

    • Strong odour urine + fever → UTI (cystitis, pyelonephritis).

    • No upper respiratory problems doesn’t completely rule out pneumonia!

    • Could be gastroenteritis → still has the liquid stools and vomiting.

    • What if it’s not an infection?

      • Malignancy.
      • Juvenile rheumatoid arthritis.
      • Kawasaki’s.
      • Thyrotoxicosis → extremely unlikely.

  • What is the difference between cystitis and pyelonephritis and how would you make this distinction?

    • Cystitis = lower urinary tract.

      • Strong smelling urine would be more suggestive of cystitis.

    • Pyelonephritis = upper urinary tract.

      • More systemic symptoms → fever, vomiting, flank pain.
    A DMSA scan is a radionucleotide test that measures dimercaptosuccinic acid uptake into the kidney. Reduced uptake is suggestive of pyelonephritis vs cystitis.
    • However, in general, it’s very difficult to tell → a DMSA scan should be done to differentiate between the two.

Case 2 – Melanie

Melanie is a 6-year old girl who is brought to your office with the problem of enuresis.

  • What is enuresis and how is it classified?

    • Enuresis → inability to control voiding.
    • Can be classified as diurnal/nocturnal, or primary/secondary.
    • Primary → never able to achieve dryness.
    • Secondary → enuresis after being dry for 6-months.

  • What factors suggest readiness for toilet training/learning

    • When they’re able to tell you that they’re ready to urinate.
    • If they can sit up straight.

  • What additional information would you like about Melanie?

    • Details on her voiding problem → when, how, etc.
    • Is she a primary or secondary case?
    • Consider her stool as well.
    • Any holding behaviours (squeezing legs together, clutching, etc) → suggesting that they’re holding it in forcibly.

Melanie has been dry at night consistently since age 4 years. She began toilet training at age 2 1/2 years.
