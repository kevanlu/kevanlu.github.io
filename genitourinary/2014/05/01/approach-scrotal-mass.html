    Approach to the Scrotal Mass

    Kevan Lu Thursday, May 01, 2014

    Scrotal Anatomy

    • The epididymis feeds to the vas deferens, and draws sperm from highly convoluted semniferous tubules.

    • There is a tunica vaginalis on the outside of the testis, and a tunica albuginea just surrounding the testis itself.

      • There is a potential space between the tunica vaginalis and the tunica albuginea!

    • The testes arises from around L1 (recall, this was the transpyloric plane), around where the renal vessels are.

    • The gubernaculum (from gubernatorial → governing) governs the path that the testicle goes down. It brings with it a layer of tunica vaginalis.

    On the right, the tunica vaginalis is reflected, revealing the tunica albuginea. The gap between the two is a potential space.

    Differential Diagnoses for a Scrotal Mass

    • Benign or Malignant?

    • Congenital or Acquired?

    • Most people use the VITAMIN-C acronym, or VINDICATE (from rearranging the letters):

      • V → Vascular.
      • I → Infectious.
      • T → Traumatic.
      • A → Auto-immune.
      • M → Metabolic.
      • I → Iatrogenic.
      • N → Neoplastic.
      • C → Congenital.

    • The differential diagnosis for a scrotal mass is:

      1. Hernia → protrusion of an organ past a fascial wall.
      2. Hydrocele → fluid-filled sac.
      3. Varicocele → abnormal expansion of the pampiniform venous plexus.
      4. Hematocele → blood-filled sac.
      5. Testicular Cancer
      6. Testicular Torsion → twisting of the testicles.
      7. Epididymitis/Orchitis → inflammation of the epididymis or testes.

    Hydrocele

    • Hydrocele → fluid-filled scrotal sac.

    • There are two forms of a hydrocele → one in adults, and one in babies:

      • Babies: Actually a hernia due to incomplete closure, causing the scrotal sac to fill with peritoneal fluid. So this is technically both a hernia and a hydrocele. This is fluid build-up in an open space.
      • Adult: This is fluid build-up in an enclosed space → typically in the potential space between the tunica vaginalis and the tunica albuginea.

    • In babies, because the hydrocele is due to a hernia, the amount of fluid will vary with abdominal pressure:

      • When the baby cries, the abdominal pressure increases, and fluid will go into the scrotal sac.
      • When the baby is calm, the abdominal pressure falls, and fluid will flow back out of the scrotal sac.

    Differentiating a Hernia from a Hydrocele

    • Both are spherical bulges in the inguinal/scrotal area.

    • One way of differentiating between them is with trans-illumination → if you shine a light through the bulge, if it’s fluid-filled, then it should glow!

      • The problem with this is that many hernias sacs will be fluid-filled too.

    • If it’s a hernia of the bowel, then you might be able to hear bowel-sounds.

      • The problem with this is that often the hernia may be strangulated, and will convey no bowel sounds.

    • The foolproof differentiating feature is that you cannot get above a hernia but you can with a hydrocele!

      • Since the hydrocele is fluid filled, you can move the fluid around.
      • But with a hernia, you can’t feel the end, because it’s a protrusion from another cavity!

    Remember

    • You can get above a hydrocele, but you cannot get above a hernia.

    Varicocele

    • A varicocele is a dilated network of gonadal veins → ie. the pampiniform plexus.

    • Often because of back-flow. For instance, this could be due to high abdominal pressure, or defective valves in the gonadal veins.

    • Presentation: - Often asymptomatic, or presents with a dull ache.

      Infertility is probably due to the increase in scrotal temperature with the improper venous drainage. There are also more blood toxins due to improper clearance.
      • The most common presenting symptom is infertility, or abnormal semen.

    • Physical Exam:

      • Standing Valsava → the Valsalva maneuver increases the abdominal pressure, so it should exacerbate the varicocele.
      • The scrotum will feel like a bag of worms.

    Management of a Varicocele

    • If they’re asymptomatic → leave them alone.

    • If they have infertility, eliminate the soruce of the excessive venous back-flow source by performing a Varicocelectomy:

      • You don’t just cut out the veins → instead we ligate the veins that feed the varicocele.

    • A right-sided varicocele is more concerning:

      • This is because the right gonadal vessel feeds directly into the IVC, so if there’s backflow, it’s something more serious causing retroperitoneal IVC obstruction (tumours, lymph node enlargement, etc).
      • With a left-sided varicocele, the left gonadal vessel feeds into the left renal vein, which is often compressed by the SMA, so this is a less surprising finding.

    Spermatocele

    • Spermatocele → semen-filled scrotal sac.

      • Originates from seminal fluid leak from the epididymis/rete testis.
      • This can get so big that it’ll seem like a third testicle!

    • Seldom symptomatic → will sometimes have pain if it’s large.

    • These are often difficult to differentiate from a hydrocele because they will also trans-illuminate.

    • However, they won’t illuminate as well, because semen is murkier.

    • If it’s bothersome, then a spermatocelectomy should be done.

      • Incision and drainage is not the best way to go, because it’ll just fill up again.

    Hematocele/Hematoma

    • Hematocele → blood-filled sac.
    • Hematoceles often occupy the potential space between the tunica vaginalis and the tunica albuginea.
    • The key finding on history is a history of direct trauma.
    • May have underlying ruptured testis as well → rupture of the tunica albuginea.
    • External signs of bleeding, ecchymosis.
    • If it’s small, you can watch and wait.
    • However, if it’s continuously enlarging or the testes is ruptured, then you should surgically intervene.

    Testicular Torsion

    • Testicular Torsion → caused by a congenital problem with a lack of anchoring of the testis by the gubernaculum.

      • With an insufficient/absent gubernaculum, the testis will lie horizontally, and swing and twist freely.
      • This is often referred to as a bell-clapper deformity.

    • This twisting has numerous consequences:

      1. Cutting off of venous blood supply → this happens first, since the venous system is lower pressure. This causes congestion.
      2. Cutting off of arterial blood supply → this causes ischemia, and evential testicular necrosis!

    • Will present with a sudden onset of severe pain. This differs from the previous conditions we mentioned, which will often have mild/no pain.

    • Generally seen in younger patients (adolescents, young adults).

    • May have had a previous history.

    • Physical Exam:

      • If there’s congested venous flow only, you’ll have a rock-hard painful testis, with continued arterial inflow.
      • If there’s congested arterial flow, then that’s a surgical emergency → there’s no blood flow, so you gotta save the balls!

    Remember

    • Testicular Torsion is a medical emergency! You have 6-8 hours to save the testicle.

    Epididymo-orchitis

    • Epididymitis → inflammation of the epididymis.

    • Orchitis → inflammation of the testis.

    • This can cause a scrotal mass because the inflammation can cause significant swelling.

    • Usually seen in sexually active patients, or those with obstructive urinary problems.

    • Will often have constitutional symptoms, obviously.

    • Epididymitis and Orchitis will also have pain, similarly to testicular torsion:

      • With epididymo-orchitis, this will usually have a gradual onset.
      • With testicular torsion, this will have an acute onset.

    • Another distinguishing factor with these is increased blood flow.

      • The inflammatory response seen in epididymo-orchitis will result in increased blood flow.
      • This contrasts with testicular torsion, which will result in decreased blood flow.
      • The increased blood flow can be seen via dopper ultrasound, as shown on the right.

    Differentiating Torsion and Epididymitis

    TorsionEpididymitis
    Age GroupAdolescents/Young adultsAdults
    OnsetSuddenMore Gradual
    Previous HistoryMore likely (congenital)Not likely
    Associated S/SNoneS/S of infection
    P/EPainful, hard testisPainful, swollen epididymis

    • Also, note that in testicular torsion, the position of the testicle may differ!

      • It may lie more horizontally.
      • This is often difficult to note with a swollen testis, so you can instead check the contralateral testis.
      • If there’s a congenital problem with the gubernaculum, you should note the abnormality on the contralateral testis as well!

    • If you’re still unsure, then you should perform a doppler ultrasound.

    TorsionEpididymitis
    Blood Flow (on doppler)NoneIncreased
    TreatmentSurgeryAntibiotics
    • If you still can’t tell the difference, or you’re in a rural area where a doppler U/S isn’t available, then err on the side of caution and proceed with surgical exploration.

    Testicular Cancer

    • Painless enlargement is most common → archetypically the painless lump.

    • This will often present as a firm lump/nodule.

    • Heavy, aching sensation, but acute pain is only seen in 10% of cases.

    • Lymphatic Spread → will often spread to L1 lymph nodes → the embryologic origin of the testes.

      • This is counter-intuitive! Testicular tumours don’t spread to the nearby lymph nodes, they spread to where their embryological origin is!
      • Spread to the L1 lymph nodes will often result in an abdominal mass.

    • Hematologic Spread → lungs, liver, brain.

    Classification of Germ Cell Tumours

    • Germ Cell Tumours can be classified into:

      1. Seminomas
      2. Non-Seminomas → teratomas, embryonal cancers, yolk-sac tumours, choriocarcinoma.

    Epidemiology of Testicular Tumours

    • Most commonly seen in young men → second to lymphoma as the most common tumour seen in young men.
    • Annual incidence of 3-4% in 100,000.
    • 1-2% of all male malignancies.
    • 12% of all cancer deaths between ages 20-35.
    • Rare in Asians and Africans, highest in Scandinavia.

    Etiology of Testicular Tumours

    • Cryptorchidism → undescended testicle.

      • Increases the risk of testicular cancer by up to 40-fold compared to normal.
      • This increased risk persists, even if you repair the undescended testicle early on. On the plus side, if it’s repaired, it makes it easier to monitor!

    • Testicular Atrophy.

    • Prior contralateral tumour.

    • Trauma is not a factor!

    Management of Testicular Tumours

    1. Baseline CXR, αFP (α-fetal protein), βHCG (β-HCG), LDH.

      • αFP and βHCG are tumour markers.
      • If you see increased β-HCG in a male, then he obviously isn’t pregnant! So this is suggestive of a tumour.
      • These are specific, but not completely sensitive.
      • So if they’re there, they’re confirmatory, but if not, then it doesn’t tell you much.

    2. Radical orchiectomy

      • Take out the testicle!

    3. Serial markers.

    4. Chest and abdominal CT.

      • Recall that lymphangitic spread goes to the L1 lymph nodes first, so you should do a abdominal CT to look for metastases.
      • It also often spreads to the lungs, so a chest CT should be done as well.

    Treatment Post Orchidectomy

    • After you take out the testicle, the treatment depends on the stage and cell type (seminoma vs non-seminoma).

    • Some options include:

      • Surveillance.
      • Retroperitoneal lymph node dissection.
      • Radiotherapy to RP nodes.
      • Chemotherapy.

    Outcomes of Testis Cancer

    • Excellent if treated early and aggressively.

    • Surgery +/- Chemo +/- Radiation.

      • Stage 1 → 99% Cure.
      • Stage 2 → 90-95% Cure.
      • Stage 3 → up to 80% Cure.
