                                  Ascending Sensory Pathways

                                  Kevan Lu Friday, March 20, 2015

                                  Lecture Objectives

                                  • This lecture should integrate the material we’ve seen in previous lectures.
                                  • Name and describe the pathways for transmission of sensory information from the periphery to the termination in the spinal cord and brain.
                                  • Describe the sensory deficits due to lesions in the spinal cord, brain stem, diencephalon and cerebrum.

                                  Three main Ascending Pathways 1. Dorsal Column, aka. the Medial Lemniscus Sensory System. 2. Spinothalamic and Spinoreticular Pathways. 3. Spinocerebellar Pathways.

                                  FOCUSING MAINLY ON INNERVATION OF BODY; sensory to head involves cranial nerves, not spinal cord.

                                  Dorsal Column Pathway

                                  Dorsal Column = discriminative touch (aka. fine touch), conscious - Pressure sense - Vibration sense - Proprioception.

                                  Enters dorsal funiculus, without synapsing, enters spinal cord. Where does it first synapse is IMPORTANT differentiating feature. Medial meniscal system does not synapse, just turns and ascends spinal cord. Organization of nerves remains intact all the way to the cortex; this is why there’s a sensory homunculus in your primary somatosensory cortex. Lower body information remains more medial in gracile fasciculus, upper body remains more lateral, in cuneate fasciculus. *

                                  SOMATOTOPY.

                                  Sensory information from Head and Neck goes into Ventral Posterior Medial. Sensory information from the rest of body goes into Ventral Posterior Lateral.

                                  What happens in brainstem? Cuneate and gracile fasciculi terminate in gracile and cuneate nuclei in medulla. Large sensory nuclei and in medulla, project as gracile and cuneate tubercles.

                                  Doesn’t cross at spinal cord, crosses after synapsing in gracile and cuneate nuclei, at the decussation of the medial lemniscus.

                                  Why called this? Because fibre pathway in medulla, pons and midbrain = medial lemniscus. L side medial meniscus damage = R side loss of sensation. L side spinal cord damage = L side loss of sensation.

                                  second order sensory neuron continues, terminates in thalamus, in ventral posterior lateral nucleus of thalamus. third order sensory neuron continues to innervate the postcentral gyrus (primary somatosensory cortex).

                                  1. Sensory nerve to cuneate/gracile nuclei
                                    • Gracile = medial = lower body.
                                    • Cuneate = lateral = upper body.
                                  2. Cuneate gracile nuclei -> VPL
                                  3. VPL -> Postcentral gyrus.
                                    • Crossing occurs as the decussation of the medial lemniscus.

                                  Neuroplasticity in somatosensory Blind vs sighted, righties. Braille readers. Extended use of somatosensory cortex in those blind people who rely on touch to read braille.

                                   Discriminative touch, pressure, vibratory sensation, and conscious joint position enter the dorsal aspect of the spinal cord.  The axons destined to carry information to the cerebral cortex do not synapse in the spinal cord, but turn and ascend ipsilaterally through the spinal cord in the dorsal funiculus.  However, these sensory axons can also have collaterals that can synapse in the spinal cord, especially in the dorsal and ventral horns. These collaterals can participate spinal reflexes.  The gracile fasciculus contains axons with information from the lower half. The cuneate fasciculus contains the axons from the upper half of the body. Thus, there is a map of the body in the dorsal funiculus such that the lower to upper body is represented from medial to lateral.  The first order neurons of the dorsal column pathway terminate in the gracile and cuneate nuclei in the medulla.  The second order neurons decussate (internal arcuate fibres) in lower medulla and ascend to thalamus (VPL). The fiber bundle in the brain stem containing these second order sensory axons is known as the medial lemniscus.  Third order neurons from thalamus enter the cortex via the internal capsule to terminate in primary somatosensory cortex on the postcentral gyrus.  A topographic map of the body is maintained at all levels of the neuraxis, from the spinal cord to the cerebral cortex.

                                  Spinothalamic and Spinoreticular Pathways

                                  Pain + temperature Difference is that, as soon as it gets into spinal cord, it synapses AT DORSAL HORN

                                  Recall ventral horn = motor, dorsal horn = sensory. Decussation then occurs at same level of spinal cord. GOes across white matter at midline called anterior white commisure. Right below grey matter in middle.

                                  Pain + temperature then ascents in the lateral and ventral spinothalamic/spinoreticular tract.

                                  ??? The spinothalamic tract consists of two adjacent pathways: anterior and lateral. The anterior spinothalamic tract carries information about crude touch. The lateral spinothalamic tract conveys pain and temperature. ???

                                  COROLLARY - Say you injured your spinal cord on the L side, below the gracile and cuneate nuclei in the medulla. - Then you’d have affected ipsilateral dorsal column sensation (proprioception, vibration sense, fine touch) - You’d also have affected contralateral spinothalamic column sensation (pain + temperature) BELOW THAT LEVEL ONLY.

                                  Some of these second order neurons information will terminate in reticular formation of brainstem already know that periaqueductal gray is responsible for modulating pain, so it’s obviously involved!?!?!

                                  THALAMUS Some terminate in VPL. Some terminate in Pulvinar or intralaminar nuclei. (association nuclei)

                                  Third order neurons distribute to primary and secondary/association sensory cortices (not just primary) Also cingulate & insular cortex, why? Pain is PAINFUL, unpleasant. So gets integrated in different region of cortex. Cingulate & insular, part of limbic (emotional) system. Discriminate as pleasant/unpleasant.

                                  . The neurons of the reticular formation all play a crucial role in maintaining behavioral arousal and consciousness.

                                  Afferents in the spinothalamic and spinoreticular pathways convey pain (nociception) and temperature to the brain stem and the cerebral cortex.  First order pain & temperature afferents terminate in the dorsal horn at the same level that they enter.  Second order neurons cross the midline in the anterior white commissure of the spinal cord and ascend to the brain in the lateral and ventral (anterior) spinothalamic and spinoreticular pathways.  Some second order neurons terminate in the reticular formation in the brain stem and the periaqueductal gray for autonomic responses and reflex suppression of pain.  The second order neurons terminate in the thalamus in the VPL as well as association nuclei of the thalamus.  Third order neurons from the VPL ascend to the cerebral cortex and terminate in primary and secondary somatosensory cortex to convey conscious awareness of pain.  Third order neurons from the association nuclei of the thalamus also terminate in the cingulate and insular cortices to convey the affective (unpleasant) component of pain.

                                  Spinocerebellar Pathways

                                  ROLE: Subconscious proprioception. Limb position sense, that never reaches level of consciousness. Important in cerebellar smoothing and coordinating of movement. This isn’t CONSCIOUS PROPRIOCEPTION (yup, my thumb it sticking up), but it’s the unconscious knowledge of where your body is in space that mediates our movements.

                                  Spinal cord, terminates at DEEPER LEVEL of dorsal horn than spinothalamic, then there are 2 possibilities: 1. 1 portion immediately turns and ascends through dorsal/posterior spinocerebellar tract.

                                  Corticopontocerebellar tract, left cerebrum, terminated in pons, crossed the midline into cerebellum. Already tells us that cerebellum not ordered like cerebellum. R CEREBELLUM = RIGHT BODY. L CEREBELLUM = LEFT. IPSILATERAL vs CONTRALATERAL. Results in difference in ascending pathways. Hence, you want ascending info here to rise in ipsilateral side, WITHOUT crossing over. Dorsal (posterior) spinocerebellar tract = NORMAL, stays on ipsilateral.

                                  Second branch = VENTRAL SPINOCEREBELLAR TRACT (GREEN), actually crosses over, and then ascends. So this information is on the CONTRALATERAL SIDE, which is the opposite of the cerebellar organization.

                                  Dorsal column enters at inferior cerebellar peduncle (#3 feature, info about self), proprioceptive information goes directly in.

                                  On ventral spinal cerebellar tract, instead goes through SUPERIOR cerebellar peduncle, and then crosses the midline, and terminates on ipsilateral side. DOUBLE CROSSING.

                                  WHYYYY!?!?! Correlations; some spinocerebellar routes are on ipsilateral side in spinal cord, others are on contralateral side.

                                  MIDDLE CEREBELLAR PEDUNCLE takes crossed info from PMC into contralateral side. (what was the order?) INFERIOR CEREBELLAR PEDUNCLE takes info about proprioception in from ipsilateral branches of spinocerebellar pathways (where are you right now?) SUPERIOR CEREBELLAR PEDUNCLE takes info about priorioception in from contralateral branches of spinocerebellar pathways. - aka ventral spinocerebellar tract fibres. - Otherwise, mostly efferent fibres?!

                                  Feedback from thalamus?

                                  Summary (spinocerebellar pathways)  Afferents in the spinocerebellar pathways convey subconscious proprioception from the trunk and lower limbs to the cerebellum.  First order neurons enter the spinal cord and synapse in the intermediate zone of the spinal cord.  Many second order neurons (above L3) ascend ipsipalerally in the dorsal (posterior) spinocerebellar tract and enter the cerebellum via the inferior cerebellar peduncle.  Other second order neurons cross the midline in the spinal cord and ascend the spinal cord in the ventral (anterior) spinocerebellar tract. These axons enter the cerebellum via the superior spinocerebellar tract and then decussate a second time to end up on the ipsilateral cerebellum.  This proprioceptive information from both tracts the spinal cord terminates in the vermis and intermediate hemisphere of the ipsilateral cerebellum.  A similar pathway exists for proprioceptive information from the upper limbs with the first order neurons terminating in the ipsilateral spinal cord and medulla (the rostral spinocerebellar tract).

                                  Afferents summary.

                                  Afferent neuronal signals from skin and deeper structures are segregated in the spinal cord; eg. spinothalamic tract vs. dorsal column pathway.  First order axons of discriminative touch and conscious proprioception ascend ipsilaterally to medulla; after synapsing in the medulla the axons cross midline.  Second order axons of pain and temperature cross midline in the spinal cord.  The second order neurons to the cerebellum ascend ipsilaterally or cross the midline in the spinal cord and then cross again in the cerebellum.
