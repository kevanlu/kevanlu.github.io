                                      Brainstem & Cranial Nerves

                                      Kevan Lu Thursday, March 19, 2015

                                      Lecture Objectives

                                      • Identify the 3 major subdivisions of the brainstem and describe the anatomic relationships of the major motor and sensory nuclei and the major descending and ascending tracts in the brainstem.
                                      • Know the twelve cranial nerves, where they exit the brain and their normal function.
                                      • Be able to determine the major symptoms and signs of dysfunction resulting from lesions in the brain stem.

                                      Subdivisions of the Brainstem

                                      Objective

                                      • Identify the 3 major subdivisions of the brainstem and describe the anatomic relationships of the major motor and sensory nuclei and the major descending and ascending tracts in the brainstem.
                                      • The brainstem, as the name suggests, sits at the base of the brain and connects it to the spinal cord.
                                        • The cranial nerves largely come out at the level of the brainstem.
                                      • Subdivisions of the brainstem:
                                        1. Midbrain.
                                        2. Pons.
                                        3. Medulla (Oblongata).
                                      • The midbrain is located inferior to the thalamus, and posterior to the pituitary.
                                        • Running through the midbrain is the cerebral aqueduct (of Sylvius), which connects the third ventricle to the fourth ventricle.
                                        • The third ventricle is the space between the two lobes of the thalamus.
                                        • The fourth ventricle is the space between the pons/medulla and the cerebellum, and is continuous with the spinal canal.
                                      • The pons is a bulbous projection off the ventral surface of the brainstem.
                                      • The medulla lies below the pons, and is continuous with the spinal cord.

                                      Anatomical Features of the Brainstem

                                      Ventral Surface of the Brainstem

                                      Olfactory tract -> uncus (temporal lobe). Base of hypothalamus, optic chiasm, where the optic nerves meet, and then branch off into the optic tract. Partially covers midbrain = mammillary bodies of the hypothalamus (mentioned before as related to limbic system). Most prominent feature are these two large cerebral peduncles; large fibre pathways running from a rostral to caudal direction. Continuation of fibres from internal capsule (recall that these were the collection of projection fibres to and from the deep gray matter) -> basal ganglia, thalamus. Cerebral peduncles = descending fibres from the cerebral cortex. Carrying info down; a lot of info will terminate/synapse in pons. The rest will continue down to spinal cord, carrying motor commands.

                                      PONS Lots of medial -> lateral fibres covering pons. Information that started in motor cortex, ending up in cerebellum, hence why it’s wrapping around the pons. Traevls through middle cerebellar peduncle.

                                      Motor info comes back at medulla, goes down spinal cord as pyramid. (corticospinal tract).

                                      L hemisphere controls R side of body, and vice versa, this motor output pathway crosses midline. This crossing happens at the level of medulla at pyramidal decussation.

                                      Olivary nucleus = large nucleus, gives input to cerebellum.

                                      Dorsal Surface of the Brainstem

                                      Lateral geniculate (visual relay). Medial genicular (auditory relay). Large pulvinar nucleus of thalamus. Dorsal surface of midbrain largely covered by superior and inferior colliculi. Receives direct visual information. Reflex tracking of moving object, can be involuntary. SUPERIOR COLLICULUS.

                                      INFERIOR COLLICULUS = auditory reflexes. Clap hand by ear as loud as you can – wake them up. Direct auditory information, reflexes.

                                      Tracts, responsible for connecting cerebellum to rest of brain. Midline, sitting out there, almost w/o any connection. 3 large fibre tracts responsible for connecting cerebellum to rest of brain: 1. Superior cerebellar peduncle to midbrain & diencephalon. 2. Middle cerebellar peduncle (pons), much larger, connects cerebellum with pontine nuclei, recall that this was motor information to cerebellum. 1 synapse from motor cortex. 3. Inferior cerebellar peduncle (medulla + spinal cord).

                                      Gracile and cuneate tubercle, sensory relay nuclei from spinal cord. Fibre tract carrying them = cuneate + gracile fasciculus

                                      Olfactory nerve + optic tract above brainstem. III, IV, VI IV comes off dorsal surface, and reflects to ventral side. Lateral pons = trigeminal nerve.

                                      6, 7, 8 come off medial to lateral at interface b/w pons and medulla. 9, 10, 12 come off medulla. 11 comes off near beginning of spinal cord.

                                      Cross-Sections of the Brainstem

                                      Cross Section of the Midbrain

                                      Dorsal = inferior colliculus Ventral = cerebal peduncle, extention of internal capsule. CN III exiting brain steam (III, IV, above pons, brainstem) Substantia nigra = PART OF MIDBRAIN, recall that these project dopamingergic info to striatum of basal ganglia, inhibits striatum. Parkinson’s.

                                      Periaqueductal gray, important in pain suppression at level of spinal cord, surrounds cerebral aqueduct, connects III and IV ventricles.

                                      Red nucleus, minor role in motor control. Lots of other small nuclei related to cranial nerve input and output.

                                      Cross Section of the Pons

                                      Sup, connecting medulla + spinal cord. Middle = motor info from motor cortex to cerebellum. Cerebral peduncle from midbrain (large, carries motor info from cerebral cortex), shrinks down to the corticospinal tract. A lot of the fibres of the cerebral peduncle fibres have thus terminated. At the PONTINE NUCLEI.

                                      Pontine nuclei, cross and go into middle cerebellar peduncle. Origin in cortex, synapse in pons, termination in cerebellum = corticopontalcerebellar pathway.

                                      MEDULLA Gracile and cuneate nucleus = point of termination for somatosensory information entering the spinal cord. Also have visceral sensory and visceral motor nuclei, important for control of heart + gut. Visceral sensory = vagus.

                                      Inferior olivary nucleus, input to cerebellum. XII, not along row with 9, 10, 11.

                                      Corticospinal tract (pyramid). Above pyramidal decussation at this point, so this info is on SAME SIDE as motor cortex at the moment. Crosses at pyramidal decussation.

                                      DEFICITS CORRESPOND TO EACH LOCATION.

                                      Deficit = pyramid + hypoglossal nerve = paralysis on contralateral side of body because pyramid is there + problem with tongue.

                                      2 ways to activate BP: 1. Subject = gripping cylinder, gripping as hard as possible, baroreceptor activation. 2. Phenylephrine (vasoconstrictor)

                                      Baroreceptor activation => UP HR and BP. Phenylephrine = reflex decrease in BP. Activity in insular cortex (autonomic control).

                                      Need to know (brain stem cross sections)  At all levels of the brain stem multiple motor and sensory nuclei associated with cranial nerves can be seen. It is not necessary to learn the location and names of all of these cranial nerve nuclei.  Scattered throughout the brain stem is a large collection of neurons known as the reticular formation. The reticular formation does not form discrete nuclei. Based on the location in the brain stem, the reticular formation can participate in several functions, including control of movement, modulation of pain, autonomic reflexes and arousal.  There is also a large amount of white matter in the brain stem consisting of pathways for brain stem connections as well as pathways for axons connecting the forebrain (cerebrum and diencephalon) with the spinal cord.

                                      The inferior and superior colliculi (auditory and visual reflexes) are seen as large nuclei in the dorsal region of the midbrain.  The periaqueductal gray is a large collection of neurons surrounding the cerebral aqueduct (connects the III and IV ventricles). The periaqueductal gray is involved in suppression of pain and autonomic responses.  The red nucleus is another large group of neurons observed ventrally in the midbrain and is involved in flexion movement of the limbs.  Immediately dorsal to the cerebral peduncles is the substantia nigra that contains the dopamine neurons that terminate in the striatum.

                                      Each of the inferior, middle and superior cerebellar peduncles can be seen in pontine cross sections.  The pontine nuclei are seen as a very large collection of nuclei in the ventral pons.  The corticospinal tract is located within the pontine nuclei. It is much smaller than the cerebral peduncle in the midbrain since many of the axons have terminated in the pontine nuclei.  The corticopontocerebellar pathway is an important pathway for motor control. One set of neurons originates in the motor region of the cerebral cortex and project to the pontine nuclei via the internal capsule (diencephalon) and then via the cerebral peduncles (midbrain). In the pontine nuclei there is a synapse onto a second set of neurons that first decussates (crosses the midline) and then enters the cerebellum via the middles cerebellar peduncle. This pathway sends information regarding motor commands to the cerebellum.

                                      The gracile and cuneate nuclei are located in the dorsal region of the medulla. They relay somatosensory information from the spinal cord to the thalamus.  Autonomic control nuclei (visceral sensory and motor) are located near the midline in the medulla. Other autonomic and respiratory control nuclei are located in the pons and midbrain.  The inferior olivary nucleus is located in the ventral part of the medulla. It has connections with the cerebellum for proprioception (limb position in space).  The pyramids containing the corticospinal axons for motor control are located at the very ventral aspect of the medulla
