                                      Anatomy of the Orbit

                                      Kevan Lu Thursday, March 19, 2015

                                      Lecture Objectives

                                      1. Name the bones that form the walls of the orbit and state susceptibility to fracture
                                      2. Describe the basic components of the eye, state its position in the orbit and name the structures involved in maintaining its position
                                      3. Name the extraocular muscles and state their innervation and individual functions
                                      4. Rationalize the eye movements patients are asked to perform in order to test the function of individual muscles.
                                      5. Name the motor and sensory nerves of the orbit and describe their function
                                      6. State the general distribution of the ophthalmic artery and the clinical significance of occlusion of its main branch to the retina
                                      7. Outline the general distribution of the veins of the orbit, stating the clinical significance of anastomoses of ophthalmic veins with the cavernous sinus and extracranial veins

                                      Introduction to the Orbit

                                      • Orbit → the cavity or socket of the skull in which the eye and its appendages are situated. “Orbit” can refer to the bony socket, or it can also be used to imply the contents.
                                      • The eye is largely held in the bony orbit by a large amount of fat.
                                      • Muscles also help keep the eye in place (the extra-ocular eye muscles).

                                      Bones of the Orbit

                                      Objective

                                      1. Name the bones that form the walls of the orbit and state susceptibility to fracture.
                                      1. Frontal Bone → roof.
                                      2. Maxilla → floor.
                                        • The maxillary sinus lies right below the orbit; hence the floor of the orbit is quite thin.
                                        • Hence, the floor of the orbit is highly susceptible to fracture.
                                      3. Ethmoid Recall that this was in the anterior cranial cavity, and included the cribriform plate and the crista galli.
                                        • Forms part of the medial wall of the orbit, and is also highly susceptible to fracture. This part is called the lamina papyracea (paper thin), aka the orbital lamina of the ethmoid.
                                      4. Sphenoid
                                        • Both the lesser and greater wing of the sphenoid are represented on the lateral wall, with a slit between them called the superior orbital fissure.
                                        • Recall from Anatomy of the Cranial Cavity 2 that the CN III, IV, V1, and VI travel through here. That is, the 3 motor nerves to the eye + the sensory innervation to the ophthalmic region.
                                        • We also see a hole in the lesser wing of the sphenoid, the optic canal; this is where the optic nerve goes through.
                                      5. Zygomatic Bone → Forms the lateral-most margin of the orbit.
                                      6. Lacrimal
                                        • A tiny bone on the medial side of the orbit with a hole that drains into the nose (the nasolacrimal duct).
                                        • Drains into the inferior meatus along the lateral nasal wall.
                                        • This is why your nose gets stuffy when you cry!

                                      Bones of the Orbit

                                      • Roof → frontal bone.
                                      • Floor → maxillary bone.
                                      • Medial wall → ethmoid (lamina papyracea), lacrimal.
                                      • Lateral wall → zygomatic bone, sphenoid (greater + lesser wing).
                                      • Weak Points in the Orbit:
                                        1. Maxilla → thin due to the presence of the maxillary sinus immediately below.
                                        2. Lamina Papyracea → thin bone, part of ethmoid, on medial side of orbit.

                                      Blowout Fractures

                                      • Direct trauma to the orbital bones is rare; more often, fractures occur due to indirect trauma to the head.
                                      • Indirect blows that displace the orbital walls will often result in a fracture of the weak parts of the orbit.
                                      • This can result in:
                                        1. Medial wall blowout fracture (fracture at the lamina papyracea).
                                        2. Inferior wall blowout fracture (fracture of the maxilla above the maxillary sinus).

                                      Usually don’t get direct blow to medial wall of orbit, but do often get indirect blows. Indirect blow that displaces orbital walls. Medial (lamina papyracea) and inferior (maxilla) walls of orbit are relatively weak. Inferior wall blowout fracture Fat from orbit often seen herniating into maxillary sinus = teardrop sign. MEDIAL or INFERIOR wall blowout fracture

                                      Lacrimal apperatus almond shaped = tear gland. Lateral part of orbit, underneath frontal bone, overtop eyeball. Produces lacrimal fluid (tears) Blinking spreads fluid over the cornea from lateral to medial. Drains into nasal cavities via nasolacrimal duct (in lacrimal bone)

                                      Puncta -> sup and inf canaliculi to lacrimal sac in bone.

                                      Muscles of the Orbit

                                      • 7 extraocular muscles:
                                        • 1 eyelid muscle → levator palpebrae superioris.
                                        • 4 recti muscles → superior, inferior, medial, lateral.
                                        • 2 oblique muscles → superior, inferior.
                                      • These function to move the eye within the orbit, or elevate the eyelid (levator palpebrae superioris).

                                      Movements of the Eye

                                      • Elevation – look up • Depression – look down • ADduction – eye looks towards nose • ABduction – eye looks away from nose Internal & external rotation.

                                      Superior and inferior oblique go through a trochlea? pulley. reverse direction of fibres.

                                      You’d think that the superior rectus would pull directly up or down, but that isn’t the case. Origin on orbit, attachment on eyeball, so different function as what you think. So superior rectus not only elevates, but also medially rotates.

                                      Because of pulley at trochlea, direction of muscle action changes. As a result, the superior oblique goes in the OPPOSITE direction of what you’d expect. Instead of elevating and medially rotating like the superior rectus, it depresses and laterally rotates the eye. ANTAGONIST TO SUPERIOR RECTUS.

                                      How to eliminate actions of muscles that pull in sim directions?

                                      • In isolation SO depresses and aBducts eye • Inferior rectus (IR) also depresses eye (and aDducts eye) To eliminate downward pull of IR: • Patient is asked to aDduct eye which aligns axis of SO with axis of eyeball • When eye is aDducted the medial pull of IR overrides its downward pull

                                      IR can’t both medially pull and inferiorly pull well at the same time. NOW CAN TEST SUPERIOR OBLIQUE. Now, can’t look down, is superior oblique.

                                      Levator palpebrae superioris attaches to tarsal plate (cartilage of eyelid), goes exactly what you think. Neat part, conjoint tendon between superior rectus and levator palpebrae. So when you look up, you’re also activating the levator palpebrae That’s why you can’t see the back of your eyelid!!!

                                      Nerves of the Orbit Sight = Optic CN II

                                      Motor to Extraocular Muscles Oculomotor CN III Trochlear CN IV Abducens CN VI Ophthalmic branch of trigeminal (V1).

                                      Everything by ocular motor except for IV superior oblique (connection, superior oblique goes through trochlea). Lateral rectus VI Makes sense, called ABducens.

                                      CN V = 3 branches = opthalmic, maxillary, submandibular. V1 branches into 3 main branches. frontal, lacrimal and nasociliary branches.

                                      PALSY Eye in down and out position OCULOMOTOR NERVE PALSY. Unopposed action of trochlear nerve (down and out) + abducens (out). Hence this finding. ALso ptosis of affected side (no levator palpebrae superioris).

                                      TROCHLEAR NERVE PALSY Up and in position due to loss of superior oblique. Commonly injured by trauma.

                                      Abducens Nerve Palsy Lateral rectus, so unable to ABduct; adducted. can’t abduct, characteristic head turn to avoid double vision. Often injured by bad falls (increased intracranial pressure).

                                      Arteries of Orbit Internal Carotid enters carotid cavity through carotid canal, goes through cavernous sinus. Pops up just medial to clinoid processes (bed posts off for pituitary). First branches it gives off here is ophthalmic. Goes through optic canal with optic nerve. Bunch of branches, correspond with branches of the ophthalmic nerve.

                                      Central retinal artery (tiny little thing), goes into dural sheath of optic nerve, intermingles with optic nerve fibres. Same with vein. What happens if something goes wrong with this artery? YOU GO BLIND ONLY BLOOD SUPPLY TO THE EYEBALL!!!!!!!!!!!! WTF! No collateral circulation!! :O :O :O

                                      Veins. Intstead of just 1 ophthalmic artery that branches, here we have a superior and inferior ophthalmic vein. Superior ophthalmic vein through superior orbital fissure, inferior ophthalmic vein through inferior orbital fissure. Recall from previous lecture that they connect to facial vein, drain into cavernous sinus. !!!DANGER TRIANGLE!!!

                                      CAVERNOUS SINUS AGAIN! artery runs through vein. Abducens right inside cavernous sinus, III, IV, V1, V2 on lateral walls. Infection can affect all of these. Abducens often affected first (adduction of eye = cross-eyed). Then will proceed to oculomotor & trochlear nerve -> paralysis or spasticity of eye. REALLY BAD, can even affect optic nerve. Super worrisome!! Hence the cavernous sinus is hugely clinically important!!
