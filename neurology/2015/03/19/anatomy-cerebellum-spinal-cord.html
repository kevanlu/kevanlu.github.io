                                  Anatomy of the Cerebellum & Spinal Cord

                                  Kevan Lu Thursday, March 19, 2015

                                  Lecture Objectives

                                  • Know the main structures and connections of the cerebellum.
                                  • Know the cross-sectional anatomy of the spinal cord, including the differences at each level and the sensory and motor input and output organization.
                                  • Describe monosynaptic and polysynaptic reflexes.

                                  Review from Previous Lectures

                                    • Spinal reflexes.
                                  • The cerebellum is what we’ll be focusing on today.

                                  Commands from motor cortex -> striatum (putamen + caudate nucleus) -> direct/indirect striopallidal pathways. - Direct = feed-forward. - Indirect = inhibitory. - Ensured that motor cortex was always in a state of readiness; important for starting actions and making sure the motor cortex is appropriately tuned for the object. START + STRENGTH. - eg. picking up eggshells vs picking up a rock. - Cerebellum plays a role in SMOOTHING and COORDINATING MOVEMENT. - Largely the role of the cerebellum. - No cerebellum = missing object, descending oscillations, etc. - Motion that you want to do isn’t smooth and coordinated. - So what connections does it need to do this? 1. What is the command (information from PRIMARY MOTOR CORTEX). Pathway from motor cortex -> internal capsule -> cerebral peduncle. Recall that this was the CORTICO-PONTO-CEREBELLAR PATHWAY. REVIEW PATH FROM PREV. LECTURE. 2. Proprioception (how is your body moving in response to itself) -> comes from SPINAL CORD. - Conscious proprioception needs to go through cortex (ie. where you close eyes and know where you hand is). - Unconscious proprioception doesn’t need to. (ie. you don’t ‘know’ where you hand is here, but your cerebellum does). 3. Vestibular (how is your balance?)

                                  Reciprocal Motor Cortex and Cerebellar Pathways. Since cerebellum is smoothing movements, then you not only need motor info going in, you also need to send it OUT. ie. need FEEDBACK TO THE CEREBRAL CORTEX.

                                  Just like with thalamus, input from cerebellum to ventral posterior region of thalamus!?!?!??!?! Pathways, (inferior cerebellar peduncle) = INPUT TO PROPRIOCEPTION.

                                  The spinocerebellum is the vermis and intermediate hemispheres on the midline of the cerebellum whose primary input is from the spinal cord.  The cerebrocerebellum is the lateral hemisphere that receives input from the cerebral cortex via the corticopontocerebellar tract.  The vestibulocerebellum is the flocculonodular lobe that receives vestibular information from brain stem nuclei.  To coordinate and refine movements the cerebellum receives the necessary information from the motor cortex on what the command is (cerebrocerebellum). It can then compare this with where the body is in space, including limb position (spinocerebellum) as well as equilibrium and balance (vestibulocerebellum). Information is then sent back to the cerebral cortex to provide a correction to the command on a continuous basis.

                                  The Spinal Cord

                                  Gross anatomy of the spinal cord Spinal cord = where spinal nerves enter and exit. Cervical, thoracic, lumbar, sacral. Leave between vertabra (intervertebral foramina).

                                  Ends at L2 ish, filum terminale REVIEW.

                                  Cauda equina.

                                  See MSK lectures. Filum terminale, attaches end of spinal cord to coccyx so it doesn’t float arround.

                                  The spinal cord lies within the vertebral canal. However, it only extends as far lumbar vertebra L1 or L2. The tapered end of the spinal cord is known as the conus medullaris.  The filiium terminale is a long filament of connective tissue that anchors the spinal cord to the coccyx.  There are 31 spinal nerves, including 8 cervical, 12 thoracic, 5 lumbar, 5 sacral and 1 coccygeal nerve. These spinal nerves leave the spinal canal through the corresponding intervertebral foramina. Since the spinal cord is not as long as the vertebral column, the spinal nerves descending below the conus medullaris are known as the cauda equina. Cerebrospinal fluid can be sampled in the region of the cauda equina below the conus medullaris.  The cervical and lumbar enlargements of the spinal cord are related to the innervation of the limbs.

                                  Cervical and lumbar enlargement = where nerves going out to limbs originate (more output to limbs than trunk).

                                  Gray matter is H shaped structure in middle Everything on outside is FIBRES; white matter, both ascending and descending. Connect spinal cord to other parts of spinal cord, and to brain.

                                  Sensory enter spinal nerves via DORSAL ROOTS; sensory only. Before it enters, splits into multiple dorsal root filaments. Terminates at dorsal horn. Dorsal root ganglion, comprised of cell bodies of all the unipolar sensory neurons.

                                  Below that ON IMAGE = ventral root, ventral root filaments, connected to ventral horn of spinal cord. Somatomotor + visceral motor info. Ventral root = MOTOR INFO only.

                                  The dorsal and ventral roots combine to form the SPINAL NERVES. These then split into dorsal and ventral rami; unlike the roots, these contain a mix of sensory and motor innervation. - Dorsal rami -> dorsal part of body. - Ventral rami -> ventral part of body, much thicker since includes limbs.

                                  White matter on lateral side = lateral funiculus. White matter on ventral side = anterior funiculus?

                                  Cross section of thoracic spinal cord.

                                  SYMPATHETIC TRUNK. H in thoracic spinal cord is different. At this level, there’s a little bump of grey matter into white matter. (bumps on side of H)

                                  The intermediolateral nucleus (IML) = LATERAL HORN is a region of grey matter found in one of the three grey columns of the spinal cord, the lateral grey column. This is Rexed lamina VII.

                                  Location for cell bodies of sympathetic outflow. ONLY HERE SINCE SYMPATHETIC CHAIN ONLY RUNS FROM T1 to L2. Visceral motor, act like motor neurons, join ventral roots, go out to innervate organs and blood vessels of skin and muscles. Sympathetic outflow does not go directly to organ; synapses (preganglionic neuron and postganglionic neuron). Leaves spinal cord in ventral root, goes through rami communicantes into sympathetic ganglia. Synapses there, then goes back and REJOINS DORSAL AND VENTRAL RAMI, then provides sympathetic output to skin + muscle.

                                  Skeletal muscle; only the axon that leaves the spinal cord that innervates skeletal muscle, cell body remains within CNS.

                                  HENCE THERE’S NO VENTRAL ROOT GANGLION. Why isn’t there a ventral root ganglion? The dorsal root ganglion is not for protection. It is the site where the cell bodies of afferent (sensory) neurons are found. The cell bodies of efferent (motor) neurons, the axons of which form the ventral root, are located within the spinal cord, thus no enlargement. (Ganglion derives from the term for knot.)

                                  Spinal cord transection? Above sympathetic outflow, big issues with things like urination, defecation, etc.

                                  The spinal cord is the entry point for sensory information in spinal nerves. The sensory information enters by the dorsal root. The dorsal roots separate into dorsal root filaments before entering the spinal cord.  The cell bodies of the sensory neurons are contained in the dorsal root ganglion in the peripheral nervous system.  The spinal cord contains somatic motor neurons whose axons extend to skeletal muscle via spinal nerves. It also has contains visceral (sympathetic and parasympathetic) motor neurons with axons in the spinal nerves.  The somatic and visceral motor neurons exit the spinal cord via the ventral roots. The motor neuron axons exit the spinal cord as ventral neuron filaments before forming the ventral root.  The dorsal and ventral roots join immediately distal to the dorsal root ganglion. At this point the spinal nerve is a mixed nerve containing both sensory and motor fibres.  Each mixed spinal nerve divides into a dorsal and ventral ramus. The dorsal rami innervates the skin and muscles of the back. The ventral rami innervate the skin and muscles of the ventral trunk and limbs.  Sympathetic motor neurons seen in spinal segments T1-L2, project to sympathetic ganglion via rami communicantes and rejoin the spinal nerve via a separate rami communicantes.

                                  Dorsal funiculus Ventral funiculus Dorsal horn for dorsal roots. Ventral horn for motor neurons leave through ventral roots. Intermediate zone = interneurons doing connections between destinations in spinal cord. Lateral horn = ONLY BY SYMPATHETIC CHAIN. Preganglionic, have to synapse in ganglia (eg. synapse in sympathetic chain).

                                  Sections associated with the limbs have greater amount of gray matter (cervical and lumbar enlargements)  White matter decreases from rostral to caudal: fewer descending and ascending fiber fibers  Gray matter of T1 to L2 has a lateral horn: the lateral horn or the intermedial lateral cell column, the origin of sympathetic preganglionic neurons

                                  = MAKES SENSE, less stuff going up or down! C1 necessary contains all fibres going from brain to C1 down to bottom, or back up. Sensory information at sacral joins lumbar, etc.

                                  C5 and L3 have larger size; eg. example of location where innervation to limbs take place. Note their really big ventral horns for controlling arms and hands.

                                  Anatomists (they have no life!) have looked within the spinal cord, and divided them anatomically similar to the cortex (broddman’s areas). 10 laminae of rexed; different nuclei. Not important; just know that motor out through ventral horn, sensory in from dorsal horn, etc.

                                  The cuneate and gracile fasciculi together are known as the dorsal or posterior funiculus.  The fibre tracts at the base of the spinal cord are the ventral or anterior funiculus.  Sensory input terminates in the dorsal horn of the gray matter of the spinal cord.  Somatic motor neurons are located in the ventral horn of the gray matter.  Interneurons are located in the intermediate zone of the gray matter.  The central canal contains the CSF made in the ventricles of the brain and is continuous with the IV ventricle.  The white matter contains multiple ascending and descending sensory and motor pathways to the brain, both crossed and uncrossed.

                                  Spinal canal continuous with 4th ventricle.  There is decreasing white matter caudally in the spinal cord. Some of the descending pathways are terminating at higher levels and there are more ascending pathways joining the white matter fibre tracts.  There are some specialized nuclei in the spinal cord. In addition, the spinal cord gray matter has been divided into ten lamina of Rexed.
