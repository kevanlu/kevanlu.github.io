                    Hypertension in Pregnancy

                    Kevan Lu Thursday, December 11, 2014

                    Lecture Objectives

                    • Describe the age distribution of hypertension in the general and female population.
                    • Define and classify hypertensive disorders of pregnancy.
                    • Describe the pathophysiology of hypertensive disorders in pregnancy.
                    • State the symptoms and signs of these disorders.
                    • Provide the principles of management and maternal and fetal surveillance.
                    • State the maternal and fetal complications and the risk of recurrence in subsequent pregnancy.

                    Objective

                    • Describe the age distribution of hypertension in the general and female population.
                    • Hypertension increases in the general population as they age.
                    • In women in particular, hypertension is less common than in men before menopause, but is more common than in men after menopause (see below).
                    • Since maternal age has been increasing, the incidence of chronic hypertension with pregnancy has been increasing as well.
                      • Recall from the previous lecture that any sort of hypertension in pregnancy increases the risk for IUGR.

                    Objective

                    • Define and classify hypertensive disorders of pregnancy.
                    1. Pre-existing hypertension
                      • ie. chronic hypertension.
                      • Known history of hypertension.
                      • OR Hypertension < 20 weeks, or > 6 weeks postpartum.
                    2. Gestational hypertension
                      • Mild isolated increase in BP >140/90, usually near term.
                      • 15-25% will progress to pre-eclampsia.
                    3. Preeclampsia
                      • Gestational hypertension (BP > 140/90) + proteinuria +/- other adverse conditions diagnosed after 20 weeks gestation.
                        • If this is before 20 weeks, it’s technically considered chronic hypertension (see above.)
                      • This is a multi-system disease, rather than a pregnancy variant of chronic hypertension.
                      • It’s the leading cause of both maternal and perinatal morbidity and mortality.
                    4. Preeclampsia superimposed on chronic hypertension
                      • Preeclampsia + chronic hypertension.
                      • This is usually more difficult to diagnose, because you’re not completely sure whether a high blood pressure is due to chronic hypertension or due to new-onset pre-eclampsia.

                    Objective

                    • Describe the pathophysiology of hypertensive disorders in pregnancy.

                    Pathophysiology of Gestational Hypertension

                    • The normal response in pregnancy is for the blood pressure to drop early on, as vasodilation occurs faster than the rise in blood volume.
                    • By the 28-32 week mark, this will usually rise back to baseline.
                    • With gestational hypertension, this late rise in blood pressure goes past the baseline.

                    • It’s thought that gestational hypertension is due to abnormalities in the normal changes in vascular sensitivity.
                      • Normally, vascular sensitivity (ie. the vessel sensitivity towards vasoconstrictors or vasodilators) will drop off with increasing gestational age.
                      • In patients with gestational hypertension, this drop off is blunted.

                    Pathophysiology of Preeclampsia

                    • Preeclampsia is thought to be due to an abnormality in the remodelling of the spiral arteries, due to impaired extravillous trophoblast invasion.
                    • Failure of remodelling then leads to higher pressures feeding into the placenta.
                    • This causes microvascular placental damange.
                    • In response, the placenta then releases some unknown factor that causes endothelial dysfunction and vasosplasm.
                    • This leads to the classic symptoms of pre-eclampsia:
                      • Hypertension
                      • Proteinuria
                      • Seizures
                      • Hepatic, Renal dysfunction
                      • Thrombocytopenia
                      • Placental dysfunction
                      • Fetal growth restriction

                    Objective

                    • Provide the principles of management and maternal and fetal surveillance.

                    Management of Chronic Hypertension

                    • Continue antihypertensives (except diuretics, or ACE inhibitors).
                      • Diuretics are ineffective because blood volume increases physiologically during pregnancy.
                      • ACE inhibitors are teratogenic.
                    • Rule out underlying renal/cardiovascular complications.
                    • Follow closely for worsening htn/superimposed preeclampsia
                    • Follow fetal growth, placental function by ultrasound

                    Management of Gestational Hypertension

                    • Usually happens near term, and there’s a 15-25% risk of progression to pre-eclampsia.
                    • Patients with gestational hypertension are monitored closely.
                    • Delivery is induced by their due date, to minimize the risk of complications.

                    Management of Preeclampsia

                    • Delivery remains the only effective therapy.
                      • Since the cause is the placenta, you have to take it out!
                      • Symptoms will usually resolve within days.
                      • Delivery will often exacerbate symptoms in the short term (it’s thought that this is due to a transient burst of the placental ‘factor X’ as the placenta is removed).
                    • Management differs depending on the severity of the pre-eclampsia:
                    • Mild pre-eclampsia
                      • Defined as BP > 140/90 + proteinuria (≥1+ or 0.3 g/24h).
                      • Managemement involves close fetal & maternal monitoring, with the goal of delivering at term.
                    • Severe pre-eclampsia
                      • Defined as BP > 160/110 + proteinuria (≥3+ or 3 g/24h) + any of the following signs of end-organ damage:
                        • ↑LFTs > 2x normal.
                        • Plts < 100,000 cells/mm3</sup.
                        • Oliguria (<30 mL/h).
                        • Headache.
                        • Visual disturbances.
                        • Epigastric pain.
                        • Seizures.
                      • Here, management involves the following:
                        • Delivery regardless of gestational age.
                        • Antihypertensives (to prevent CVAs).
                        • Seizure prophylaxis (MgSO4).

                    Management of any Hypertension

                    • Regardless of the type of hypertension, it’s critical to keep the BP < 160/110.
                      • This is protective for cerebrovascular events.
                      • Labatelol, hydralazine, and nifedipine can be used as antihypertensives.
                    • Preventative measures
                      • There seems to be no good way to prevent pre-eclampsia or gestational hypertension.
                      • No evidence for salt restriction, dietary restriction, or antioxidants.
                      • Some (controversial) evidence exists for low dose ASA.

                    Objective

                    • Describe the risk factors for pre-eclampsia.
                    • Demographics
                      • Age < 20 or > 35 years
                      • Obesity → BMI ≥ 35.
                    • Past History
                      • Medical Conditions
                        • Hypertension, renal disease, diabetes, connective tissue disease
                      • Previous preeclampsia.
                      • First pregnancy, pregnancy interval > 10 years.
                      • Multiple Gestation.
                        • Since something in the placenta seems to be causing the preeclampsia, the more placenta you have, the higher the risk!

                    Objective

                    • State the maternal and fetal complications and the risk of recurrence in subsequent pregnancy.
                    • Maternal Complications:
                      • Hypertensive crisis
                      • Seizures, CVA
                      • Proteinuria/oliguria
                      • Hepatic dysfunction
                      • Thrombocytopenia
                      • LV dysfunction
                      • Pulmonary edema
                    • Fetal Complications
                      • Placental abruption
                      • IUGR / Placental insufficiency
                      • Fetal hypoxia
                      • Stillbirth
