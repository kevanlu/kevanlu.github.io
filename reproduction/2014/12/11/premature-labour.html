                    Premature Labour

                    Kevan Lu Thursday, December 11, 2014

                    Lecture Objectives

                    • List clinical risk factors and discuss the burden prematurity places on our health care system.
                    • Review the varied mechanisms involved in the pathophysiology.
                    • Outline the complications of prematurity and the prognosis based on gestational age.
                    • Discuss risk factor modification, prediction and strategies to prevent prematurity.
                    • Describe the use of tocolytic agents and corticosteroids.
                    • Investigate (self study) the role of progesterone in prevention of preterm birth.

                    Objective

                    • List clinical risk factors and discuss the burden prematurity places on our health care system.
                    • Definition: Premature birth = birth at 20 to 37 completed weeks.
                    • Incidence: increased 30% since early eighties.
                    • Importance:
                      • Leading cause of neonatal death in 1st month (24%).
                      • Long term morbidity in survivors: cerebral palsy, cognitive disability, chronic lung disease, visual and hearing loss.
                      • Estimated cost to health care system is 18.1 billion (in the US).

                    Clinical Risk Factors

                    • Maternal Risk Factors
                      • Previous preterm birth
                      • PROM (Premature Rupture of Membranes).
                      • Second trimester bleeding
                      • Cervicovaginal and intraamniotic infection
                      • Uterine/cervical abnormities (congenital/acquired)
                      • Socioeconomic (nutrition, smoking, drugs)
                      • Indicated preterm inductions (preeclampsia, severe IUGR).
                    • Fetal/Placental Risk Factors
                      • Multiple gestation.
                      • Congenital abnormalities (uterine/cervical).
                      • Placental abruption/previa.
                    • Bolded elements are major contributors.
                    • Even still, 50% of patients with premature labour will have no recognizable identifiable cause.

                    Objective

                    • Review the varied mechanisms involved in the pathophysiology.
                    • There’s no one mechanism through which preterm labour occurs.
                    • But they all lead to an intermediate decidual response to uterotonins and proteases.
                    • These uterotonins and proteases then lead to uterine contractions and cervical changes respectively, leading to preterm delivery.

                    Objective

                    • Outline the complications of prematurity and the prognosis based on gestational age.
                    • Fetal mortality:
                      • With markedly premature babies, there is a significant increase in mortality.
                      • As the gestational age increases, mortality decreases.
                      • At around 36 weeks, mortality starts to plateau.
                    • Fetal morbidity:
                      • Similar to the mortality, the risk of fetal complications is highest with more substantially premature babies.
                      • These complications all drop over time (see right), and are mostly similar to those of a term baby at around 36 weeks.
                      • Fetal complications include:
                        • Respiratory distress.
                        • Sepsis.
                        • Intraventricular hemorrhage.

                    Objective

                    • Discuss risk factor modification, prediction and strategies to prevent prematurity.
                    • Predictive Values:
                      • There are 2 effective predictive tools that can be used to stratify risk for preterm labour, in addition to the risk factors obtained from Hx:
                        1. Cervical Length
                          • Studies show that if the cervix is shorter than 2.5 cm, we see an increased risk of preterm delivery.
                          • The shorter it is, the higher the risk.
                          • Additionally, whether the cervix is beginning to open, or whether it remains closed, is another important predictive factor.
                        2. Fetal Fibronectin
                          • Not very sensitive, but very specific.
                          • Hence, it’s not so good for ruling in pre-term labour, but it’s very effective for ruling it out.
                    • Preventative Strategies
                      • Progesterone therapy has been found to reduce the risk of preterm delivery.
                      • This can be administered with vaginal tablets or intramuscularly (17-hydroxyprogesterone caproate).
                      • High-risk patients with progesterone therapy will have a risk that’s similar to the baseline.

                    Objective

                    • Describe the use of tocolytic agents, corticosteroids, GBS prophylaxis, and MgSO4.
                    • Tocolytic Therapy
                      • The goal with tocolysis is to stop labour from happening.
                      • True active labour is generally very difficult to stop. However, weaker uterine contractions are often responsive to tocolytic agents.
                      • Examples include indomethacin, and Ca channel blockers.
                      • These agents are usually administered for ~48-72 hours. If labour continues past this point, there are no further attempts to delay delivery.
                    • Corticosteroids
                      • Used to reduce fetal complications upon delivery:
                        • 50% reduction in perinatal mortality.
                        • 50% reduction in RDS (Respiratory Distress Syndrome).
                        • 50% reduction in IVH (Intraventricular Hemorrhage).
                      • Betamethasone is most commonly used (12 mg IM, repeat again in 24h).
                      • Maximum benefit is seen in 24h to 7 days after the 1st dose; multiple courses are controversial.
                        • Serial steroid use actually lead to more complications than they prevented.
                        • The idea is that steroids shift fetal priorities from organ growth to organ maturation.
                          • So when they’re used transiently, they’ll improve outcomes by allowing for more maturation in utero.
                          • However, when they’re used for longer periods of time, the negatives (ie. slowed organ growth) outweigh the benefits.
                    • GBS Prophylaxis
                      • 30% of women are colonized with GBS.
                      • Common cause of neonatal sepsis, pneumonia, and meningitis.
                      • To avoid fetal complications, intrapartum antibiotic therapy is administered:
                        • Penicillin G 5 million units IV, then 2.5 million q4h.
                    • MgSO4 for Neuroprotection
                      • Magnesium sulphate reduces the risk for cerebral palsy in premature births.
                      • For women with imminent preterm birth (≤ 31+6 weeks), antenatal magnesium sulphate administration should be considered for fetal neuroprotection. (I-A)

                    Premature Rupture of Membranes

                    • Premature Rupture of Membranes (PROM) → rupture of membranes prior to labour (more specifically, > 1h before contractions).
                      • This can often be diagnosed on history, but examination can often help:
                        • Speculum (fluid looking in vagina/cervix).
                        • Nitrazine (pH changes due to amniotic fluid).
                        • Cytologic (Ferning → a ‘fern-shaped’ crystallization of amniotic fluid seen with ruptured membranes).
                        • U/S (is the fluid level around the fetus normal or not?)
                    • PROM can be classified into 2 categories:
                      • Preterm (PPROM).
                      • Term.
                    • Management of PROM differs for these categories:
                      • With a term PROM, management is straightforward: just deliver the baby.
                      • With a PPROM, management decisions are more difficult:
                        • Do we leave the baby inside to prevent fetal complications (eg. prematurity, infection, cord prolapse, malpresentation)?
                        • Do we deliver the baby to prevent maternal complications (eg. chorioamnionitis, sepsis)?
                    • In general, management is as follows:
                      • Term PROM:
                        • Allow spontaneous labour.
                        • Induction of labour if at ~6h with Oxytocin.
                        • GBS Prophylaxis.
                      • PPROM:
                        • Antibiotic therapy to decrease sepsis risk.
                          • Ampicillin/Erythromycin x 7d.
                        • Antenatal steroids (< 34 weeks).
                        • Tocolysis is controversial with PPROM.
                        • Aim to induce labour at ~36 weeks.
