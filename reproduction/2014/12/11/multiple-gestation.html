                    Multiple Gestation

                    Kevan Lu Thursday, December 11, 2014

                    Lecture Objectives

                    • Multiple gestation rates are increasing greatly. Why and what are the consequences?
                    • Define and review etiology of monozygotic and dizygotic twins.
                    • Outline the risks of twin pregnancies.
                    • Describe importance of chorionicity, and the management of monochorionic and dichorionic twins.
                    • Discuss the impact of multiple gestation, particularly higher order multiples, on families and supports for families.

                    Objective

                    • Multiple gestation rates are increasing greatly. Why and what are the consequences?
                    • Multiple births are on the rise (since 1980):
                      • 65% increase in twins; 500% increase in triplets and quadruplets.
                      • Now the overall incidence is ~1/90.
                    • What are the reasons behind this increase?
                      • Due to increasing fertility therapy.
                      • As evidence, when you look at the incidence of monozygotic vs dizygotic twins, the incidence of monozygotic twins has remained stable, but the incidence of dizygotic twins has risen significantly
                      • This is because dizygotic twins are increased with fertility therapy (since there are multiple eggs), but monozygotic twins are not.
                    • What are the consequences?
                      • As seen before:
                        • Increased risk for preeclampsia.
                        • Increased risk for premature labour.

                    Objective

                    • Define and review etiology of monozygotic and dizygotic twins.
                    • Dizygotic (Non-identical).
                      • Caused by the fertilization of multiple eggs.
                      • Always dichorionic and diamniotic: ie, each fetus has its own chorionic and amniotic sac.
                      • They each have their own placenta as well.
                      • More common with fertility therapy.

                    • Monozygotic (Identical).
                      • Caused by division of a single fertilized egg.
                      • Can present in many different ways:
                        • If the embryo split early on, it can behave like 2 separate zygotes, and be dichorionic and diamniotic, as with a dizygotic gestation.
                        • If the embryo splits after the formation of the chorionic sac, but before the formation of the amniotic sac, it will be monochorionic and diamniotic.
                        • If the embryo splits after the formation of both the chorionic and amniotic sac, it will be monochorionic and monoamniotic.
                        • Finally, in extremely rare cases, where division occurs after some fetal development, you may end up with conjoined twins.
                    • The incidence of these conditions are listed below:
                      • Dizygotic:
                        • (100%) dichorionic, diamniotic.
                      • Monozygotic:
                        • (30%) dichorionic, diamniotic (day 1-3)
                        • (70%) monochorionic, diamniotic (day 3-8)
                        • (1%) monochorionic, monoamniotic (day 8-13)
                        • (rare) conjoined twins (> 13 days)
                    • This is important because morbidity and mortality when division occurs later.
                    • Thus, intuitively, with respect to risk:
                      • Dichorionic + diamniotic < monochorionic + diamniotic < monochorionic + monoamniotic < conjoined.

                    Objective

                    • Describe the diagnosis of multiple gestation.
                    • Multiple gestation is diagnosed based on:
                      • Clinical risk factors.
                      • Large size for dates.
                      • Ultrasound.
                    • In particular, a few key findings on U/S help not only diagnose multiple gestation, but also help characterize it (monozygotic vs dizygotic, mono- vs di-chorionic, mono- vs di-amniotic).
                    • These U/S findings include:
                      1. Fetal Sex:
                        • This is usually not medically important in a singleton pregnancy (unless there are genetic concerns).
                        • With twins however, looking at fetal gender is critical, because if the twins are of opposite gender, then you can rule out monozygosity.
                      2. Number of placenta:
                        • “Do you see 1 placenta or 2?”
                        • If you see a single placenta, then it must be monozygotic, because all dizygotic twins have separate placentas, amnions, and chorions.
                        • As mentioned above, complication rates are higher with a shared placenta.
                      3. Membrane thickness:
                        • Useful if it’s hard to distinguish if it’s 1 placenta or 2.
                      4. Twin peak sign:
                        • This involves looking at how the membranes around the two twins interact (see right).
                        • Lambda sign = dichorionic + diamniotic.
                        • T-sign = monochorionic + diamniotic.
                          • This makes sense → you don’t see invagination of the chorion here because there’s only one chorion!

                    Objective

                    • Outline the risks of twin pregnancies.
                    • In all twin pregnancies, regardless of their type, there are some fetal and maternal risks that are in common:
                    Maternal Risks Maternofetal Risks Fetal Risks
                    Hyperemesis (since 2x β-hCG) Preterm Labour Congenital abnormalities
                    Anemia Polyhydramnios Prematurity
                    Gestational DM   IUGR, discordant growth
                    Preeclampsia   Malpresentation
                    C-section   Fetal demise (1 twin)
                    Placental Abruption   TTS (monochorionic only)
                    Post-partum Hemorrhage    

                    Objective

                    • Describe importance of chorionicity, and the management of monochorionic and dichorionic twins.
                    • As mentioned previously, twins that are monochorionic will have increased complication rates than dichorionic twins.
                    • These risks include:
                      • Increased risk fetal anomalies
                      • Increased perinatal mortality
                      • Placental vascular communications
                    • Monochorionic twins can also get a condition called Twin/Twin Transfusion Syndrome (TTS), that is specific to fetuses that share a placenta:
                      • It happens in up to 15% of pregnancies with a shared placenta.
                      • TTS is caused by an imbalance in how blood is distributed through anastamoses between the two umbilical cords.
                      • As a result, one fetus actually ends up getting blood from the other fetus!
                      • The ‘donor’ fetus will then lose blood into the ‘recipient’ fetus: one twin will be bleeding out, and the other one will receive excessive fluid.
                        • The donor will be at risk of hypoxic issues.
                        • Recipient will be at risk for fluid overload issues (eg. cardiac issues).

                    Management of Twin Gestation

                    • Surveillance
                      • Watch for a difference in fetal weights of >20%.
                        • If it exists, determine the etiology (most commonly placental).
                      • Increased surveillance:
                        • Assess growth q 3 weeks, and fetal wellbeing and placental function weekly.
                    • Delivery
                      • Aim to deliver at term (38 weeks).
                      • The route of delivery depends on the presentation:
                        • (40%) Vertex/Vertex. (1st/2nd out).
                        • (20%) Vertex/Breech.
                        • (10%) Breech/Breech.
                        • (10%) Breech/Vertex.
                      • As long as the first baby out is vertex, then we aim for a vaginal delivery.
                        • As seen above, about 60% of twin pregnancies are like this.
                        • However, with further complications, the total rate of C-section is ~50%.

                    Objective

                    • Discuss the impact of multiple gestation, particularly higher order multiples, on families and supports for families.
                    • Emotional stress, physical exhaustion.
                    • Financial stress due to increased clothing/diaper/stroller costs.
                    • Relationship stress.
