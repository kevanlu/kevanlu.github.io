                    Labour & Birth

                    Kevan Lu Friday, December 12, 2014

                    Lecture Objectives

                    • Define labour and distinguish from false labour and identify the theories of the initiation of labour.
                    • Define the stages of labour and average timelines.
                    • Define the cardinal movements.

                    Objective

                    • Define labour and distinguish from false labour and identify the theories of the initiation of labour.
                    • True Labour → regular uterine contractions causing progressive cervical dilation.
                    • False Labour → where uterine contractions do NOT cause progressive cervical dilation.
                      • These are cases where women feel as if they’re in labour because they’re contracting, but if there isn’t cervical change, then it doesn’t count!

                    Theories on the Initiation of Labour

                    • Labour is mainly initiated by 2 changes:
                      • Oxytocin stimulation → Oxytocin receptors in the uterine muscles are quiescent through pregnancy, but are activated at term.
                      • Progesterone withdrawal → there are high levels of P throughout pregnancy, but this decreases relative to estrogen at term.
                    • There are some other minor triggers as well:
                      • Fetal triggers → fetal adrenal and HPA signals.
                      • Prostaglandins
                      • Withdrawal of Inhibitors → progesterone, relaxin, prostacyclin, parathyroid hormone, nitric acid.
                    • The body then undergoes a few changes to prepare for labour:
                      1. Braxton-Hicks Contraction
                        • Painless tightenings, no cervical change.
                        • Differs from true labour in that these contractions will stop, while labour contractions will not until the baby is delivered.
                        • Starts at around 28 weeks.
                      2. Cervical Effacement
                        • The cervix starts as a long, thick closed tube.
                        • As the body gets closer to labour, the cervix shortens and softens.
                      3. Engagement
                        • Where the baby’s head begins to ‘settle down into the pelvis’.
                        • Around 2 weeks prior to labour in primip.

                    Fetal Heart Rate Monitoring

                    • Heart rate monitoring should be done in all pregnancies.
                    • This monitoring can either be intermittent or continuous.
                      • Intermittent auscultation
                        • Done for a completely healthy, normal pregnancy.
                        • q15 min in 1st stage of labour / q5 min in 2nd stage of labour.
                      • Continuous auscultation
                        • Done with high risk patients → preeclampsia, bleeding, abnormal FHR.
                        • Also done with anyone with an induced labour.
                        • Also done in women undergoing a VBAC (Vaginal Birth after Caesarian).
                    • Findings with FHR monitoring can be classified as follows:
                      • Normal FHR
                      • Atypical FHR
                      • Abnormal FHR
                    • Normally, the FHR should vary between 120-160, with accelerations above the baseline.
                      • Decelerations in the fetal heart rate should coincide with contractions (as a response to the fetus being squeezed).
                    • When these decelerations get deeper, or the decelerations occur after the peak of the contraction, then you begin to worry.
                      • When the deceleration occurs after the peak of the contraction, this is called a late deceleration.
                      • One deceleration isn’t a big deal; multiple decelerations is worrisome, and suggests the need for more urgent intervention.

                    • In these cases, prompt operative delivery may be necessary.

                    Friedman Curves

                    • Friedman Curves show the typical progression of labour. They plot cervical dilation with respect to time during labour.
                    • These are not strict rules on how labour will progress, but they provide a good guideline.
                    • Importantly, note that the curve is much steeper with multiparous patients, than with primips.
                      • The idea here is that the bodies of women with previous vaginal births are more acclimated to the process of labour.

                    Objective

                    • Define the stages of labour and average timelines.

                    First Stage of Labour

                    • Definition: onset of labour → full dilation.
                    • The first stage of labour is divided into 2 stages:
                      • Latent phase: 0-4 cm cervical dilation.
                      • Active phase: 4-10 cm cervical dilation.
                    • In many cases, the birthing unit waits until the patient is in the active phase before admitting them.
                    • Average Duration (and Friedman)
                      • 6 - 18 hrs if primip. If active phase expect 1.2 cm/hr dilation.
                      • 2 – 10 hrs if multip. If active phase expect 1.5 cm/hr dilation.

                    Second Stage of Labour

                    • Definition: full dilatation → delivery of fetus.
                    • Average Duration (and Friedman)
                      • 30 min - 3 hrs if primip.
                      • 5 min - 30 min if multip.
                    • In the second stage of labour, progress is monitored by the distance the head is down in the pelvis. This is quantified as follows:
                      • The level at the ischial spines is used as ‘zero’.
                      • Anything above that is -1, -2, -3.
                      • Anything below that is +1, +2, +3.
                      • +3 = head on perineum.
                    • This 1,2,3 method is actually the traditional method for grading the progress of second stage labour.
                      • The ACOG tried to convert people to using cm measurements for greater interobserver reliability, but this hasn’t been taken up well due to convenience and familiarity.

                    Third Stage of Labour

                    • Definition: delivery of fetus → expulsion of placenta.
                    • Average Duration: 2-30 min.
                    • All major obstetrical bodies in the world recommend active management for the 3rd stage of labour, including:
                      • Uterotonic agents (Syntocinon, Ergot).
                      • Gentle traction on cord.
                      • Fundal massage.
                    • Active management helps deliver the placenta when the mother has a tired uterus + reduced oxytocin immediately post-partum.
                    • Why is the delivery of the placenta important?
                      • The blood supply is massive in the placental bed. If it comes off, and the uterus does not contract, it’ll pour a lot of blood into the uterus, leading to post-partum hemorrhage.
                    • How do you know if the placenta is separating?.
                      • This is a classic clerkship question. There are 3 main features we look for.
                        1. New-onset brighter blood → there’s lot of blood at birth, but you’ll know that the placenta is beginning to separate if you see new, brigher red blood.
                        2. Cord gets longer.
                        3. Fundus begins to ball up → because the muscle begins to contract.
                    • What is the mechanism behind the expulsion of the placenta?
                      • It isn’t because we’re pulling on it. Rather, it’s because of the uterine contractions.
                        • During pregnancy, the shape of the placenta and the shape of the uterus are well matched. When the uterus starts contracting, and the muscle balls up, the two no longer match.
                        • This causes the placenta to shear off.
                      • As the uterus tightens, it has the added effect of constricting the spiral arteries, decreasing the risk of PP hemorrhage.

                    Fourth Stage of Labour

                    • Definition: First hour after birth.
                    • During this time, there is autotransfusion of blood from the uteroplacental unit back into the systemic circulation.
                      • This causes a hemodynamic shift, which may cause issues in women with pre-existing cardiac compromise.
                    • Maternal Management:
                      • Watch for complications.
                      • Post-partum hemorrhage.
                    • Fetal Management:
                      • Watch for fetal accommodation to air.
                      • Breastfeeding.

                    Pain Management During Labour

                    • In the first stage of labour, pain travels along T10, 11, 12.
                    • As the head descends, this moves to S2, 3, 4.
                    • Options for Pain Management include:
                      • Epidural
                        • Most common, since they’re the most effective at taking pain away.
                        • They also don’t introduce narcotic to the baby, since the anesthesia isn’t systemically distributed.
                      • Spinal
                        • Similar placement to an epidural, but a different medication.
                        • Used as a local anesthetic, and unlike an epidural, it’s a one-shot deal.
                        • Typically used for C-sections, but not labour (since labour is ongoing, while C-section isn’t).
                      • Pudental
                        • Local anesthesia, that’s useful for the birth itself, especially if forceps or vacuum needed.
                        • Recall that the pudendal nerve innervates the perineum.
                      • Lamaze
                        • “The goal of Lamaze is to increase a mother’s confidence in her ability to give birth; classes help pregnant women understand how to cope with pain in ways that both facilitate labor and promote comfort”
                        • Involves breathing and focal point exercises.
                      • PCA (Patient Controlled Analgesia)
                        • Generally only used transiently to avoid narcotizing the baby.
                        • IV narcotic, patient controlled.
                        • Usually used in patients who have contraindications for an epidural.
                      • General Anesthesia
                      • Nitrous Oxide

                    Objective

                    • Define the cardinal movements.
                    • These are the movements that the baby needs to do in order to squeeze it’s way out.
                    • These movements (in order) are:
                      1. Engagement
                      2. Descent
                      3. Flexion
                      4. Internal Rotation
                      5. Extension
                      6. External Rotation
                      7. Expulsion

                    1. Engagement
                      • Discussed previously. Where the fetus’ head descends into the pelvis.
                    2. Descent
                      • Where the head continues to descend further into the pelvis.
                    3. Flexion
                      • Where the baby flexes its head.
                      • Normally the baby’s head is in a neutral position. As contractions force the baby’s head down, it reacts by putting its head into a flexed position, where its chin is brought to its chest.
                      • This has the ancillary benefit of shifting the orientation of the baby’s head so that the widest part of the head (the biparietal diameter) can go through the widest part of the pelvis.
                    4. Internal Rotation
                      • Refers to the internal rotation of the head only, not the rest of the body.
                      • As the baby is forced out of the pelvis, it rotates its head internally: either down towards the ground, or up towards the sky.
                        • If it rotates its head upwards, this is called occiput posterior.
                        • If it rotates its head downwards, this is called occiput anterior.
                      • With occiput posterior, the widest part of the head then meets the narrowest part of the pelvis. This is the ‘wrong direction’, as it’s then harder for the baby’s head to get out.
                      • With occiput anterior, the rotation of the head follows the curve of the pelvis, making labour easier.
                    5. Extension
                      • When baby reaches end of curve, it can then crane its neck out and get its head out.
                      • This is the extension phase.
                    6. External Rotation
                      • The head is now outside the pelvis, so the baby can rotate it back to a neutral position.
                    7. Expulsion
                      • That baby pops out!

                    Remember

                    • Occiput posterior = more difficult labour.
                    • Occiput anterior = easier labour.

                    Pelvic Architecture

                    • There are 4 general pelvis shapes:
                      • Gynecoid → most common pelvic shape, and the best pelvis for vaginal birth (ie. ‘birthing hips’).
                      • Android → the pelvis is a little more heart shaped.
                      • Platypelloid → flat pelvis, not much vertical descent. Makes internal rotation more difficult.
                      • Anthropoid → narrow, longer front to back than side to side.
                    • Generally, gynecoid and android pelvises have the best outcomes.
                    • Platypelloid and anthropoid pelvises may inhibit cardinal movements.
                    • Nonetheless, a trial of labour is the only true test of pelvic adequacy
                      • This is because both the shape of the baby’s head and the pelvis are dynamic during labour, so it’s difficult to predict whether the head will fit or not.
